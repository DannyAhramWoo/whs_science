Concept

Pinholes, Shadows and Image Size

Because light travels in straight lines, a small hole makes an upside-down image and a blocked light makes a shadow, and the size of both depends only on distances.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

Close the curtains on a bright day and leave one small gap. On the wall opposite you may see a faint picture of the street outside — cars, trees, sky. It is dim, and it is upside down. No lens, no glass, no electricity. Just a hole.

Everything here follows from one fact: light travels in straight lines. A single narrow path of light is called a ray, and we draw rays as straight arrows because that is what light does when nothing bends it. Every point on a sunlit object throws rays off in all directions. Normally rays from every point land everywhere on a wall, all mixed up, giving a patch of light with no picture in it.

A small hole sorts them out. Of all the rays leaving one point on the object, only the few aimed at the hole get through, and they land on one small spot on the screen. Rays from a different point land on a different spot. Point by point, the object is rebuilt. That is an image: a picture formed by light. The hole is also called an aperture, meaning an opening that light passes through.

2

How it works

Why the image is upside down. Trace two rays. Take a candle in front of a pinhole. A ray leaves the top of the flame, travels straight to the hole, and because a straight line cannot bend it carries on downwards beyond it, landing near the bottom of the screen. A second ray leaves the bottom of the candle, aims up at the hole, and keeps going upwards to land near the top. The two rays crossed at the hole. Every other ray does the same, so the whole picture arrives flipped, left to right as well. This is not a strange property of pinholes — it is straight lines crossing at a point, and nothing more.

Making the hole bigger. Widen the hole and two things change together, in opposite directions. More light gets through, so the image is brighter. But rays from a single point no longer squeeze through one tiny opening: a spread of them gets through and lands as a small patch. Each point becomes a blob, and neighbouring blobs overlap, so the image is blurrier. Shrink the hole and the reverse happens — sharper, but dimmer. You cannot have both, and this trade-off is the whole idea. It applies to a camera aperture and to your own eye: the pupil is an aperture. In dim light it widens to let more light in, and your vision is genuinely a little less sharp. In bright light it narrows, and you see more crisply.

Moving the screen back. Move the screen further from the hole and the cone of light spreading out behind the hole has more room to widen, so the image gets bigger. But the same amount of light is now spread over a larger area, so it is dimmer. Again, a trade.

The size rule. The image size follows a simple proportion. It is set by two distances: how far the object is in front of the hole, and how far the screen is behind it.

image height / object height = screen distance / object distance

Try it with numbers. A candle 20 cm tall stands 100 cm in front of a pinhole, with the screen 25 cm behind. Then image height = 20 × (25 / 100) = 5 cm. Now walk the candle in to 50 cm from the hole, changing nothing else: image height = 20 × (25 / 50) = 10 cm. Halving the object distance doubled the image. So moving the object closer makes the image bigger, and moving it further away makes it smaller — which is exactly why distant things look small.

Shadows. A shadow is the same geometry, with the object blocking light instead of a hole letting it through, so the same rule sets its size. Move a lamp closer and the shadow on the wall behind grows rapidly; slide the lamp far away and the shadow shrinks towards the object's own size, because the rays arriving are nearly parallel. Plot shadow height against lamp-to-object distance and the curve falls steeply at first, then flattens towards a level it never quite reaches.

A very small source gives one sharp shadow. A broad source gives two regions. The umbra is the full shadow at the centre, where the object blocks the whole source and no light arrives. Around it is the penumbra, a partial shadow where only part of the source is blocked, so some light still reaches it. The wider the source, the wider the penumbra — which is why shadows under a small bright lamp have crisp edges and shadows on a cloudy day barely have edges at all.

The heart of it

A smaller aperture gives a sharper but dimmer image, and a larger one gives a brighter but blurrier image, because light travels in straight lines and each point can only be rebuilt as accurately as the hole is narrow.

3

Where the idea came from

This is one of the oldest recorded observations in physics. The Chinese philosopher Mozi and his followers described an inverted image formed through a small opening around the 4th century BC, and reasoned it was upside down because light travels in straight lines and crosses at the hole. Aristotle in Greece noticed at around the same period that light coming through gaps in leaves cast round patches on the ground during a partial eclipse, and was puzzled that the patches were round when the gaps were not. Each patch is an image of the Sun, not a picture of the gap.

The clearest early account came from the scholar Ibn al-Haytham, working in Cairo around 1020, who studied images made by light passing through small apertures into darkened rooms and set out why the image must be inverted. He also argued, against the common view of his time, that we see because light travels from objects into the eye — not because rays shoot out of the eye. Much of what he wrote about light was built on for centuries. The darkened-room device later became known in Latin as the camera obscura, "dark chamber", and by the 1500s artists were using it to trace scenes.

Some claims about camera obscura use are hard to pin down. Precise dates for early devices, and how far particular painters relied on them, are still argued over by historians, so treat confident stories about them with a little caution.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is believing a bigger opening gives a better image. It gives a brighter one, and people hear "more light" as "better". Sharpness and brightness pull in opposite directions here.

The version of this that catches people out is the transfer to the eye, an angle IMSO used in 2017. In dim conditions the pupil widens, and the natural answer is that vision improves. What actually improves is brightness. The wider aperture also makes the image on the back of the eye slightly less sharp, exactly as with a camera.

Safety. A pinhole is the safe way to watch a solar eclipse, but only if used correctly. You make a hole in card, hold it so sunlight passes through onto a second surface behind, and you look at that surface. Never put your eye to the hole and look through it towards the Sun. A pinhole does not weaken sunlight enough to make direct viewing safe, and looking at the Sun can damage the eye permanently and painlessly, so you may not notice until it is too late. Watch the projected patch of light, never the source.

Commonly missed

A pinhole camera makes a clear but faint image. A student widens the pinhole to make the picture easier to see. What happens to the image, and why?

It is a trap because "easier to see" sounds like an improvement, and the answer that everything gets better is very tempting. It also does get genuinely brighter, so half the intuition is right.

How to work it: Handle the two effects separately. First, brightness: a wider hole lets more light through each second, so the image is brighter — that part of the plan works. Second, sharpness: think about the rays leaving one single point on the object. With a tiny hole, only one narrow bundle gets through and lands on one small spot. With a wide hole, a spread of rays from that same point gets through at slightly different angles and lands spread across a small patch. Every point becomes a patch, and the patches from neighbouring points overlap, so detail is lost. The image is brighter but blurrier. The full answer names both effects and explains the blurring in terms of straight-line rays. The same reasoning applied to a widened pupil in dim light gives brighter but less sharp vision.

5

Connected ideas

Everything here rests on light travelling in straight lines, which is also why eclipses happen and why they show umbra and penumbra on the same scale as a shadow on a wall. Reflection and refraction are the two ways that straight-line rule gets bent or turned, and lenses use refraction to gather far more light than a pinhole can while keeping the image sharp. Scattering explains why the light arriving at your pinhole from a shaded object exists at all.

In one line

Light travels in straight lines, so rays cross at a small hole to make an upside-down image whose size depends only on distances, and widening the hole always buys brightness at the cost of sharpness.
