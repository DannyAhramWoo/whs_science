Concept

Eclipses

An eclipse happens when the Sun, Earth and Moon line up so that one body's shadow falls on another.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Light travels in straight lines. When an opaque object — one light cannot pass through — sits in a beam of light, it blocks the light and casts a shadow behind it. Both the Earth and the Moon are opaque balls sitting in sunlight, so both trail long shadows pointing away from the Sun. An eclipse is what happens on the rare occasions when one of those shadows lands on the other body.

There are two kinds, and the names describe what gets hidden. In a solar eclipse the Sun is hidden: the Moon passes between the Earth and the Sun, and the Moon's shadow falls on the Earth. In a lunar eclipse the Moon is hidden: the Earth passes between the Sun and the Moon, and the Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.

Because a solar eclipse needs the Moon between us and the Sun, it can only happen at new moon. Because a lunar eclipse needs the Earth in between, it can only happen at full moon. That gives you a way to work out which eclipse is which without memorising anything.

2

How it works

The obvious question is why we do not get an eclipse every month, given that new moon and full moon come round every 29.5 days. The answer is that the Moon's orbit is tilted by about 5 degrees compared with the Earth's orbit around the Sun. Most months the Moon passes a little above or a little below the exact line, and its shadow misses the Earth entirely, or the Earth's shadow misses it. Only when a new or full moon happens to fall near one of the two points where the two orbital planes cross do the three bodies line up properly. That works out at roughly two to five solar eclipses a year, of which any one place on Earth sees a total eclipse only about once in 375 years on average.

Shadows have two parts, and this explains what an eclipse looks like. The umbra is the inner cone where the light source is completely blocked; the penumbra is the outer region where it is only partly blocked. Stand in the Moon's umbra and you see a total solar eclipse — the sky darkens, the temperature drops, and the Sun's faint outer atmosphere becomes visible. Stand in the penumbra and you see a partial eclipse, with a bite taken out of the Sun.

The Moon's umbra where it reaches the Earth is small, at most about 270 km across, and it sweeps across the surface at over 1,500 km per hour. That is why totality lasts only a few minutes and is visible from a narrow track. A lunar eclipse is quite different: the Earth is much larger than the Moon, so the Earth's shadow at the Moon's distance is roughly two and a half times the Moon's width. The Moon takes over an hour to cross it, and everyone on the night side of the Earth sees the same thing at the same time. This is the main reason lunar eclipses seem more common — they are not, but far more people see each one.

One remarkable coincidence makes total solar eclipses possible at all. The Sun is about 400 times wider than the Moon, and it also happens to be about 400 times further away. So the two appear almost exactly the same size in our sky, and the Moon can just cover the Sun's disc. There is no deep reason for this; it is chance, and it is temporary, since the Moon drifts about 3.8 cm further from Earth each year.

The heart of it

An eclipse is a shadow, and which body is in the middle decides which kind you get.

3

Where the idea came from

Eclipses show something important about how science develops: you can predict a thing long before you understand it. Babylonian astronomers, keeping careful records on clay tablets from roughly 750 BC onwards, noticed that eclipses repeat in a cycle of about 18 years and 11 days — later called the Saros cycle. They did not know the Earth was a ball orbited by a Moon. They simply had centuries of data and spotted the pattern, and it was enough to predict when eclipses would come. Prediction and explanation are genuinely different achievements, and the Babylonians had the first without the second.

The Greeks supplied the explanation. Anaxagoras in the fifth century BC understood eclipses as shadows, and Aristotle used the shape of the Earth's shadow on the Moon as evidence that the Earth is a sphere: the edge of the shadow is always curved, no matter what time of night or year, and only a ball casts a round shadow from every direction.

For most of history eclipses were frightening rather than interesting, and were widely read as warnings or as some creature devouring the Sun. A famous story says the Greek thinker Thales predicted a solar eclipse in 585 BC that stopped a battle between the Lydians and the Medes. The battle and the eclipse appear to be real, but historians doubt Thales could have had a method capable of predicting it, so the story may well be embroidered.

The most consequential eclipse in modern science came on 29 May 1919. Albert Einstein's theory of general relativity predicted that gravity bends light, so stars appearing close to the Sun should seem slightly shifted. Normally the Sun's glare makes those stars invisible — but not during totality. Expeditions led by Arthur Eddington photographed the eclipse from Príncipe off West Africa and from Sobral in Brazil, and the measured shift matched Einstein's prediction. The results were more marginal than the newspapers suggested, and have been argued over since, but later measurements confirmed the effect. It made Einstein world-famous almost overnight.

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent mix-up is naming the wrong body as the one in the middle. The way to avoid it is to read the name as saying what is being eclipsed. Solar eclipse: the Sun is what disappears, so something must be in front of it, and that is the Moon. Lunar eclipse: the Moon is what darkens, so something must be blocking its light, and that is the Earth.

The other error is assuming eclipses ought to happen monthly. They would, if the Moon's orbit lay in the same flat plane as the Earth's — but it is tilted about 5 degrees, so the alignment is usually not quite exact.

Commonly missed

Explain why a lunar eclipse can be seen by far more people at once than a total solar eclipse, even though both are caused by shadows.

The trap is answering that lunar eclipses happen more often. They do not, particularly. The question is about how many people see each one.

How to work it: Compare the sizes of the shadows against the object they land on. In a solar eclipse the small Moon casts a shadow on the much larger Earth, so the dark patch covers only a narrow strip of the surface, and only people inside that strip see totality. In a lunar eclipse the large Earth casts a shadow on the smaller Moon, so the shadow swallows the Moon completely — and since the darkened Moon is then simply less bright in the sky, anyone on the night side of the Earth can see it at once.

Connected ideas

Eclipses rest on light travelling in straight lines and being blocked by opaque bodies, and they occur only at the new moon and full moon points of the phase cycle. The alignments themselves come from the rotation and revolution of the Earth and Moon, held in place by gravity.

In one line

An eclipse is one body's shadow falling on another: the Moon blocking the Sun from us at new moon, or the Earth blocking sunlight from the Moon at full moon.
