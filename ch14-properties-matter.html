Chapter 14

Properties of Matter

Everything around you is made of tiny particles, and how those particles are arranged explains almost every property you can test. The one idea most often muddled: stirring makes sugar dissolve faster, but it does not make more sugar dissolve.

14.1

The three states and the particle model

All matter is made of extremely small pieces called particles. They are far too small to see, even with a school microscope. But if you know how they are packed and how much they move, you can predict how the material behaves.

In a solid, the particles are packed closely together in a fixed pattern. They still move — they vibrate on the spot — but they cannot swap places. That is why a solid keeps its own shape and its own volume. A brick stays brick-shaped wherever you put it.

In a liquid, the particles are still close together, but they are not locked into a pattern. They can slide past one another. So a liquid keeps a fixed volume, but it flows and takes the shape of whatever container it is in. Pour 200 ml of water into a tall glass or a wide bowl and you still have 200 ml.

In a gas, the particles are far apart and move fast in random directions. Nothing holds them together. A gas has no fixed shape and no fixed volume — it spreads out until it fills its whole container.

The one table to know

Solid: fixed shape, fixed volume. Liquid: no fixed shape, fixed volume. Gas: no fixed shape, no fixed volume. The liquid row is the one that gets mixed up, because a liquid is "half solid, half gas" in its behaviour.

Commonly missed

A student says a liquid has no fixed volume because water spreads out flat when spilled on a table. Is the student correct?

The trap is confusing shape with volume. The spilled water certainly changes shape — it spreads into a puddle. But if you could scoop every drop back up, you would have exactly the same amount as before.

How to work it: ask whether the amount of space the material occupies has changed, or only its outline. A puddle has a new outline and the same volume. So the student is wrong: liquids do have a fixed volume.

14.2

Why gases squash and float

Press the end of a bicycle pump with your finger over the outlet. The handle moves in. Now try the same with a syringe full of water. It will barely budge. Why the difference?

Squashing something means pushing its particles closer together. In a gas there is plenty of empty space between the particles, so they have somewhere to go. A gas is compressible. In a liquid or a solid the particles are already touching, so there is nowhere left to push them. Liquids and solids are almost impossible to compress.

The very same gaps explain a second property. Density means how much mass is packed into a given volume. A litre of air contains only a scattering of particles; a litre of water contains particles packed shoulder to shoulder. So air has a far lower density than water — roughly a thousand times lower. That is why a gas rises through a liquid as bubbles.

Say the gaps, not the state

When a question asks why a gas can be compressed, or why a gas has low density, the mark is for mentioning the large spaces between the particles. Writing "because it is a gas" earns nothing — that is the question, not the answer.

14.3

Diffusion: particles spread themselves out

Someone frying onions in the kitchen can be smelled from the bedroom, with no fan and no draught. The smell travels because gas particles are always moving and always colliding. Over time they wander away from the crowded place near the pan and out into the rest of the house.

This spreading of particles from where they are crowded to where they are not is called diffusion. Nobody has to push them. It happens on its own, simply because the particles never stop moving.

Diffusion happens in liquids too. Drop a spot of ink into still water and watch it slowly blur outwards until the whole glass is evenly tinted. But it is far slower than in a gas, because liquid particles are packed close and keep bumping into their neighbours. In a solid the particles cannot swap places at all, so effectively there is no diffusion.

Commonly missed

A bottle of perfume is opened in the corner of a sealed room with no air movement. Why can it be smelled at the far wall a few minutes later?

The trap is answering "because the air carries it". The question has already told you there is no air movement, so an answer about wind or draughts contradicts the stem.

How to work it: when a question rules out the obvious mechanism, it wants the particle explanation. Perfume particles are always moving randomly, and they spread from the crowded corner into the emptier parts of the room by diffusion.

14.4

Pure substances, mixtures and dissolving

A pure substance contains only one kind of matter — distilled water, or a lump of copper. A mixture contains two or more substances jumbled together but not joined. Sea water, air and a bowl of muesli are all mixtures.

Two things are true of every mixture, and questions test both. First, the parts keep their own properties: the salt in sea water still tastes salty, and iron filings mixed into sand are still attracted to a magnet. Second, a mixture can be separated again by physical means — filtering, evaporating, using a magnet — with no chemical reaction needed.

When a solid seems to vanish into a liquid, it has dissolved. The solid is the solute, the liquid it dissolves in is the solvent, and the clear mixture they form is a solution. The solute has not disappeared. Its particles have simply spread out between the solvent particles, which is why a solution weighs the same as the two parts you started with.

If the solid does not dissolve and instead stays as visible bits floating or settling, you have a suspension. Sand stirred into water is a suspension: it looks cloudy and it settles if you leave it alone. A true solution stays clear and never settles.

Dissolved is not destroyed

Sugar dissolved in water has not gone. If you weigh the water, weigh the sugar, then weigh the solution, the mass is the same. Boil the water away and the sugar reappears.

14.5

Solubility, saturation and separating mixtures

Solubility means how much of a substance will dissolve in a given amount of solvent. It is an amount, measured in grams — not a speed. Two things change that amount: the temperature (usually more solid dissolves in a hotter solvent) and the type of solvent (chalk will not dissolve in water however hard you try, but some substances dissolve readily in alcohol and not in water).

When no more solute will dissolve at that temperature, the solution is saturated. Add another spoonful of sugar to a saturated cup and it just sits at the bottom. Heat the cup, though, and it will take more.

Choosing a separation method is easier if you first ask one question: did the solid dissolve? If it did not, the bits can be trapped in filter paper. If it did, the particles slip straight through the paper and you must remove the liquid instead.

Two more methods complete the set. Magnetic attraction pulls magnetic materials, such as iron filings, out of a mixture with non-magnetic ones, such as sand. Chromatography separates coloured dyes: a spot of ink on paper is touched to water, and as the water creeps up, each dye travels a different distance and appears as its own band.

Commonly missed

A student stirs 10 g of sugar into a cup of water and it all dissolves in 40 seconds. She repeats it with sugar ground into a fine powder and it dissolves in 15 seconds. She concludes that grinding the sugar increased its solubility. Is she right?

This is the classic trap. Stirring, grinding and warming all speed dissolving up, so students group all three together. But they do not all do the same job.

How to work it: separate the two questions — how fast versus how much. Stirring and grinding into smaller pieces only change the SPEED; the same 10 g would eventually have dissolved either way. Only temperature and the choice of solvent change the final AMOUNT that will dissolve. So she is wrong: the solubility was unchanged, only the rate.

14.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

14.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A bicycle pump full of air can be squashed, but a syringe full of water cannot. Explain the difference using particles.

Show answer

In air, the particles are far apart with large empty spaces between them. Pushing the handle forces those particles closer together into the empty space, so the volume gets smaller.

In water, the particles are already touching one another. There is almost no space left to push them into, so the water will not compress and the plunger stops.

2

A cup of tea is saturated with sugar. What could you do to make more sugar dissolve, and what would only make it dissolve faster?

Show answer

To make more dissolve, heat the tea. A hotter solvent generally holds more solute, so the solution stops being saturated and takes another spoonful. Changing to a different solvent would also change the amount, though that is not practical for tea.

Stirring, or grinding the sugar into finer grains, would only make the sugar dissolve faster. Neither changes the maximum amount the tea can hold at that temperature, so if it is already saturated, no extra sugar dissolves at all.

3

A mixture contains sand, iron filings and salt, all stirred into water. Describe how you would separate all three, in order.

Show answer

Use a magnet first, before adding anything else, to attract the iron filings out. Iron is magnetic and the other two materials are not.

Then filter the remaining mixture. Sand does not dissolve, so it is trapped on the filter paper. The salt has dissolved, so its particles pass straight through with the water.

Finally evaporate the water from what came through the filter. The water turns to vapour and leaves the salt behind as solid crystals.

4

Two glasses look identical: both are colourless and liquid. One is salt water, one is muddy water that has been left to stand. How would you tell which is a solution and which is a suspension?

Show answer

Look at the bottom of each glass. A suspension settles, so muddy water left standing shows a layer of sediment and the liquid above will be at least slightly cloudy. A solution never settles, because the dissolved particles are spread evenly between the water particles.

You could also filter both. The suspension leaves solid on the filter paper; the solution passes through completely unchanged, because dissolved particles are far too small to be caught.

14.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 14.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about a liquid are correct?

  • 1It can be compressed as easily as a gas.
  • 2It takes the shape of its container.
  • 3It has a fixed volume.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. The particles in a liquid are already touching, so there is no empty space to squash them into. And the moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: liquid particles slide past each other, so the liquid flows into the shape of the container — statement 2 is correct. The particles stay just as close together whatever the container, so the volume does not change — statement 3 is correct. Consistent with C.

2

Salt is stirred into a beaker of water until no more will dissolve. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The solution is saturated.
  • 2Heating the water would allow more salt to dissolve.
  • 3Stirring for longer would allow more salt to dissolve.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. "No more will dissolve" is the definition of a saturated solution. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Stirring changes only the speed of dissolving, never the maximum amount. The solution is already at its limit, so stirring achieves nothing. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Solubility usually rises with temperature, so hotter water holds more salt. So the answer is B.

Watch for this pair: statements 2 and 3 look like the same kind of claim. One changes the amount, the other only the rate. Sort every such statement into "how much" or "how fast" before judging it.

3

Which statements about mixtures are correct?

  • 1The substances in a mixture keep their own properties.
  • 2A mixture can be separated by physical methods such as filtering.
  • 3Sand stirred into water forms a solution.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Iron filings mixed with sand are still magnetic; salt in water still tastes salty. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Sand does not dissolve. It stays as visible grains that settle out, so that is a suspension, not a solution. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Because the parts are only jumbled and not joined, physical methods such as filtering, evaporating or using a magnet will separate them. So the answer is B.
