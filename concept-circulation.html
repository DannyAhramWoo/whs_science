Concept

Circulation

Circulation is the constant one-way journey of blood around a closed loop of vessels, driven by the heart, that delivers what cells need and carries away what they produce.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Every cell needs oxygen and food, and every cell produces waste. A cell on the sole of your foot is a long way from your lungs and your gut, and nothing could travel that far quickly enough by drifting. Circulation solves this: a network of tubes reaching every part of the body, with a pump keeping the contents moving.

The name is doing real work. Blood does not flow out and get used up, like water from a tap; it goes round and comes back, the same blood passing the same places again and again. It is a loop, not a supply line, and that fact was the hardest thing here for anyone to work out.

Three parts make the system. The heart is the pump. The blood vessels are the pipes: arteries carrying blood away from the heart, veins carrying it back, and capillaries, so narrow that red cells pass almost single file. The blood is no simple liquid — it carries red cells for oxygen, white cells for defence, and dissolved substances including digested food, carbon dioxide and heat.

2

How it works

The heart is two pumps side by side. The right receives blood returning from the body, low in oxygen, and pushes it to the lungs; the left receives it back, now loaded with oxygen, and pushes it to the whole body. So blood passes through the heart twice each circuit. This double circulation exists because of pressure: squeezing through the lungs costs the blood most of its push, and returning to the heart restores it.

That is also why the left side has a much thicker muscular wall. The right only pushes blood as far as the lungs beside it; the left pushes it to your toes and your brain. More distance and more resistance need more force, and more force needs more muscle.

Blood goes one way only because of valves, inside the heart between its chambers and along the larger veins. A valve is a flap that opens if the flow pushes one way and is pressed shut if the flow tries to reverse, so the flow itself does the deciding and no control is needed. Veins need them badly because their blood has already spent most of its pressure and, in your legs, must travel upwards.

The exchange itself happens only in the capillaries; arteries and veins are transport, not delivery. A capillary wall is one cell thick, so oxygen and dissolved food pass out to the surrounding cells and carbon dioxide passes in. This is why capillaries are so numerous and so fine: substances cross by diffusion, which is only fast over very short distances, so splitting an artery into thousands of capillaries brings the blood within a few cell-widths of every cell.

The heart of it

Blood goes round a closed loop rather than being used up, and everything useful happens in the capillaries, where the wall is thin enough for substances to cross.

3

Where the idea came from

For centuries the accepted picture, inherited from the ancient world, was that blood was made in the liver from food, travelled outwards, and was consumed by the body — a supply line, not a loop. It fitted intuition, since you eat and the food seems to go somewhere.

In 1628 the English physician William Harvey broke it with arithmetic. He estimated how much blood the heart pushed out with each beat, multiplied by the beats in an hour, and found a quantity far greater than the whole body contains — vastly more than any food could make in that time. If blood were manufactured and consumed, the body would have to produce its own weight in blood repeatedly through the day, which is absurd. The only escape was that the same blood must be going round and being used again.

There was a serious gap in his case, and Harvey knew it. If blood leaves through arteries and returns through veins, something must connect the two, and he could not see any such connection, because it is too small for the naked eye. He argued it must exist because the arithmetic demanded it. Capillaries were finally seen by Marcello Malpighi, using a microscope, after Harvey had died. So for a time Harvey's conclusion ran ahead of the visible evidence, resting on a calculation rather than a sighting.

Unsurprisingly, he met resistance. Overturning an authority that had stood for well over a thousand years is not comfortable, and asking people to accept an invisible link on the strength of a sum is a genuine demand. The episode shows that reasoning can be sound before it can be shown, and that being right is not the same as being believed.

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent error is defining arteries and veins by whether their blood carries oxygen. That works nearly everywhere and fails at the lungs, where the artery to the lungs carries blood low in oxygen and the vein from the lungs carries blood rich in it. The reliable definition is direction: arteries carry blood away from the heart, veins carry it back, whatever they contain.

The second is imagining blood is blue in the veins and turns red on contact with air. Blood low in oxygen is simply a darker red; the blue you see is your skin altering the appearance of the vessel underneath.

Commonly missed

Why does blood need to pass through the heart twice for one full circuit of the body, rather than going from the lungs directly to the rest of the body?

The trap is answering that it needs to collect oxygen, which the lungs did already. The question is about pressure, not oxygen.

How to work it: Blood loses most of its pressure squeezing through the very fine vessels of the lungs, so if it continued straight to the body it would arrive too slowly and weakly to reach the furthest tissues. Returning to the heart lets the thick-walled left side give it a second, stronger push, so oxygen-rich blood reaches every part of the body quickly enough to be useful.

5

Connected ideas

Circulation links the systems that take things in to the cells that use them. Breathing loads the blood with oxygen; digestion loads it with food; respiration consumes both and produces the carbon dioxide the blood carries away. Exchange across capillary walls happens by diffusion, the same spreading that moves a smell across a room. Blood also spreads heat from busy muscles, one of the ways you stay at a steady temperature.
