Concept

Circulation

Circulation is the constant one-way journey of blood around a closed loop of vessels, driven by the heart, delivering what cells need and carrying away what they produce.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Every cell needs oxygen and food, and every cell produces waste. A cell on the sole of your foot is far from your lungs and gut, and nothing could travel that far quickly enough by drifting. Circulation solves this: a network of tubes reaching every part of the body, with a pump keeping the contents moving.

The name is doing real work. Blood does not flow out and get used up, like water from a tap; it goes round and comes back, the same blood passing the same places again and again. It is a loop, not a supply line.

Three parts make the system. The heart is the pump. The blood vessels are the pipes: arteries carrying blood from the heart, veins carrying it back, and capillaries, so narrow that red cells pass almost single file. The blood carries red cells for oxygen, white cells for defence, and dissolved food, carbon dioxide and heat.

2

How it works

The heart is two pumps side by side. The right receives blood from the body, low in oxygen, and pushes it to the lungs; the left receives it back, loaded with oxygen, and pushes it round the body. Blood therefore passes through the heart twice each circuit. This double circulation exists because of pressure: squeezing through the lungs costs the blood most of its push, and returning to the heart restores it. The left side has the thicker wall because it drives blood to your toes and brain, not just to the lungs alongside it, and more distance needs more force.

Blood goes one way only because of valves, inside the heart and along the larger veins. A valve is a flap that opens if the flow pushes one way and is pressed shut if it reverses, so the flow itself decides and no control is needed. Veins need them because their blood has already spent most of its pressure and, in your legs, must travel upwards.

The exchange happens only in the capillaries; arteries and veins are transport, not delivery. A capillary wall is one cell thick, so oxygen and dissolved food pass out to surrounding cells and carbon dioxide passes in. Capillaries are so numerous and fine because substances cross by diffusion, which is only fast over short distances: splitting an artery into thousands of capillaries brings blood within a few cell-widths of every cell.

The four parts of blood

Blood is often described as a red liquid, but it is really four things mixed together, and a question asking you to name its components wants all four.

Plasma is the liquid itself, straw-coloured and mostly water. Everything else floats in it, and the dissolved cargo — food from the gut, carbon dioxide, urea, salts, hormones and heat — travels in the plasma rather than in any cell. Red blood cells carry oxygen. They are shaped like discs pressed in on both sides, which gives more surface for oxygen to cross, and in humans they lose their nucleus as they mature, leaving more room for the red substance that binds the oxygen. White blood cells defend against disease, some by engulfing bacteria whole, others by making antibodies. They are far less numerous than red cells but they can leave the vessels and enter the tissues, which red cells cannot. Platelets are fragments of cells, not whole ones, and they start the clotting that seals a cut. Without them a small wound would keep bleeding.

One distinction catches people out. Plasma and serum are not the same thing. Serum is plasma with the clotting substances removed, which is what is left after blood has clotted. If a question asks for the liquid part of whole blood, the answer is plasma.

The proportions are worth holding: a little over half of blood by volume is plasma, and most of the rest is red cells, with white cells and platelets together a very thin layer. A fuller treatment, including what each part looks like under a microscope, is on Blood Composition.

The heart of it

Blood goes round a closed loop rather than being used up, and everything useful happens in the capillaries, whose walls are thin enough for substances to cross.

3

Where the idea came from

For centuries the accepted picture, inherited from the ancient world, was that blood was made in the liver from food, travelled outwards, and was consumed by the body — a supply line, not a loop. It fitted intuition, since you eat and the food seems to go somewhere.

In 1628 the English physician William Harvey broke it with arithmetic. He estimated the blood the heart pushed out with each beat, multiplied by the beats in an hour, and found a quantity far greater than the whole body contains — vastly more than any food could make in that time. If blood were manufactured and consumed, the body would have to produce its own weight in blood repeatedly each day, which is absurd. The only escape was that the same blood goes round and is used again.

There was a serious gap in his case, and Harvey knew it. If blood leaves through arteries and returns through veins, something must connect the two, and he could not see it, being too small for the naked eye. He argued it must exist because the arithmetic demanded it. Capillaries were finally seen by Marcello Malpighi, using a microscope, after Harvey had died. So for a time Harvey's conclusion ran ahead of the visible evidence, resting on a calculation rather than a sighting.

Unsurprisingly, he met resistance. Overturning an authority that had stood for over a thousand years is not comfortable, and asking people to accept an invisible link on the strength of a sum is a genuine demand. The episode shows that reasoning can be sound before it can be shown, and that being right is not the same as being believed.

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent error is defining arteries and veins by whether their blood carries oxygen. That works nearly everywhere but fails at the lungs, where the artery carries blood low in oxygen and the vein blood rich in it. The reliable definition is direction: arteries carry blood away from the heart, veins carry it back, whatever they contain.

The second is imagining blood is blue in the veins and turns red on contact with air. Blood low in oxygen is simply a darker red; the blue is your skin altering how the vessel looks.

Commonly missed

Why must blood pass through the heart twice for one full circuit, rather than going from the lungs directly to the rest of the body?

The trap is answering that it collects oxygen, which the lungs did already. The question is about pressure.

How to work it: Blood loses most of its pressure squeezing through the fine vessels of the lungs, so if it continued straight on it would arrive too weakly to reach the furthest tissues. Returning to the heart lets the thick-walled left side give it a second, stronger push, so oxygen-rich blood reaches every tissue quickly enough.

5

Connected ideas

Circulation links the systems that take things in to the cells that use them: breathing loads the blood with oxygen, digestion loads it with food, and respiration consumes both and produces the carbon dioxide the blood carries away. Exchange across capillary walls happens by diffusion, the spreading that also moves a smell across a room. Blood spreads heat from busy muscles too, helping keep your temperature steady.
