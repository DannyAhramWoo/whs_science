Concept

Combustion

Combustion is a chemical change in which a substance reacts with oxygen, giving out heat and usually light.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Everyone knows what burning looks like. What is less obvious is that burning is not the fuel falling apart on its own: it is the fuel joining up with oxygen from the air. Without oxygen there is no combustion, however hot you make things.

You can prove this with a jar. Put a burning candle under an upturned glass and it goes out within seconds. The candle has not run out of wax, nor the jar out of air. It has run out of usable oxygen, the gas making up about a fifth of the air around us. The flame stops when the reacting partner is gone.

So combustion needs three things at once: fuel, oxygen, and enough heat to start. Take away any one and the fire stops. That is why blowing out a candle works, why a fire blanket works, and why clearing dry wood from a bush fire's path works. These are not three separate tricks but three ways of breaking the same requirement.

2

How it works

Most everyday fuels — wood, wax, petrol, natural gas — are mainly carbon and hydrogen. When they burn completely, the carbon joins with oxygen to make carbon dioxide and the hydrogen joins with oxygen to make water. The water leaves as invisible vapour, which is why you rarely notice it. Hold a cold spoon in a gas flame and it mists over. That mist is genuinely new water, made in the flame from hydrogen locked in the gas.

Why does combustion give out heat? Because the new arrangement holds less energy than the old one of fuel and oxygen, and the leftover has to go somewhere. It leaves as heat and light. This also explains why a fire keeps going once lit: the heat released supplies the starting heat for the next bit of fuel, so the reaction feeds itself until fuel or oxygen runs short.

If oxygen is in short supply you get incomplete combustion. There is not enough to convert every carbon atom to carbon dioxide, so some ends up as soot and some as carbon monoxide, a colourless and very dangerous gas. This is why a well-adjusted gas flame is blue and a starved one yellow and sooty. The yellow is glowing carbon particles that did not finish reacting, and such a flame is cooler because less of the available energy is released.

Combustion is a chemical change, not a physical one. Melted wax is still wax and cools back into a block; burnt wax is gone as wax. Nothing is destroyed, though: the atoms are all still there, recombined and mostly drifting away as gas.

The heart of it

Burning is fuel combining with oxygen, and the heat you feel is the energy left over as the atoms settle into a lower-energy arrangement.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of the 1700s, educated people explained burning with the phlogiston theory. The idea was that flammable things contained a substance called phlogiston, and burning was the process of releasing it. Air was needed because air could soak up only so much phlogiston, which is exactly why a candle in a sealed jar goes out — the air had become saturated.

It is important to see that this was a serious theory, not a silly one. It explained why some things burn and others do not, why fires need air, and why a fire in a closed space dies, and it made predictions that could be tested. Being wrong is not the same as being foolish. Phlogiston lost to something better, not to common sense.

What defeated it was careful weighing. In the 1770s the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier burned metals in sealed vessels and measured the masses precisely. If burning released phlogiston, the burnt metal should weigh less. It weighed more. Lavoisier showed the extra mass had come from the air: the metal had combined with oxygen rather than lost anything. Lavoisier did not work alone — others, including Joseph Priestley, had already isolated the gas we now call oxygen. His contribution was recognising what it meant and treating the balance as the deciding instrument.

4

What people get wrong

The biggest misunderstanding is that burning destroys matter, because ash weighs so much less than the log. It does not. Almost all of the log has left as invisible gas, and if you captured every gas and weighed it with the ash, the total would exceed the original log by exactly the mass of oxygen taken from the air.

The second is treating melting, boiling and burning as one family because all three involve heat. Melting and boiling are physical and reversible; burning is chemical and is not.

Commonly missed

A piece of magnesium ribbon of mass 2.0 g is burned completely in air. The white powder left behind is weighed and found to have a mass greater than 2.0 g. Explain why.

It is a trap because burning is so strongly associated with shrinking and disappearing that a gain in mass feels impossible.

How to work it: Combustion is the fuel combining with oxygen, not losing anything. The magnesium has reacted with air's oxygen to form magnesium oxide. Unlike wood, the product is a solid and stays in the dish rather than escaping as gas. So the powder contains both the original magnesium and the oxygen it took in, and must weigh more than 2.0 g. No matter was created — the extra mass came out of the air.

5

Connected ideas

Combustion is the clearest example of a chemical change, so it is the natural companion to that idea. It releases stored chemical energy as heat and light, tying it to energy and its conservation. Almost all the fuels we burn at scale are fossil fuels, and the carbon dioxide they produce feeds the carbon cycle and the greenhouse effect. Respiration is a quieter relative: it releases energy from food using oxygen, without a flame.
