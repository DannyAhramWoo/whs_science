Concept

Centre of Mass

The centre of mass is the single point where all of an object's weight can be treated as acting.

Introduced in Chapter 2: Mass and Weight

1

What it means

Balance a ruler on one finger. There is exactly one place where it sits level. Slide your finger either way and it swings down. That balance point is the ruler's centre of mass.

Gravity pulls on every part of the ruler at once. The centre of mass is a shortcut: rather than track thousands of separate pulls, treat the whole weight as acting downwards at one point.

For an even, regular shape it sits at the geometric centre — the middle of a ruler, the centre of a disc. For odd shapes it shifts towards the heavy end. A hammer's centre of mass is near the head, not halfway along the handle.

The centre of mass need not be inside the object. For a ring or a horseshoe it sits in the empty space in the middle, where there is no material at all.

2

How it works

The centre of mass explains stability. An object's base is the area of ground it stands on. Imagine a line dropped from the centre of mass to the floor. If it lands inside the base, the object settles back down. If it lands outside the base, the object topples.

Tilt a mug and watch. As it leans, the centre of mass swings sideways. While the line still falls inside the rim, letting go returns the mug upright. Tilt further and gravity finishes the job.

Two design rules follow. A wide base helps, because the line has further to travel before it escapes. A low centre of mass helps, because a low point swings sideways less for the same tilt. A racing car is broad and squat; a tall bus carries heavy machinery low in its floor.

The centre of mass also explains turning. Push a book through its centre of mass and it slides straight. Push it off to one side and it does two things at once: it slides, and it rotates. A push left of the centre swings it one way; a push right swings it the other.

You can find the centre of mass of a flat, irregular shape by experiment. Hang the shape from a pin near its edge. Gravity brings the centre of mass to rest directly below the pin, so hang a weighted string from that pin and draw the vertical line it marks. Repeat from a different point. Where the two lines cross is the centre of mass.

A tightrope walker carries a long pole because it adds mass far out to the sides. That makes the combined centre of mass easier to keep over the wire, and tipping slow enough to correct. Drooping the pole ends lowers it further.

The heart of it

An object topples the moment a vertical line from its centre of mass falls outside its base, which is why wide and low means stable.

3

Where the idea came from

The oldest surviving treatment belongs to Archimedes of Syracuse, in the third century BC. In his work on the balancing of planes he showed how levers balance and how to locate what he called the centre of gravity of flat shapes. He treated it as the point at which a shape could be supported without tipping — the definition still used today.

Archimedes worked by pure geometry, with no notion of gravity as a force. The famous remark about moving the Earth given a place to stand is attributed to him by later writers, so we cannot be sure of his words.

When Isaac Newton published his laws of motion in 1687, the centre of mass became something deeper: the point that moves as though all the mass and all external forces acted there. That is what lets us draw a complicated object as a single dot with one weight arrow.

Centre of mass and centre of gravity are used interchangeably at this level, and on Earth they sit at the same point. They differ only where gravity's strength varies across the object.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is assuming the centre of mass is always the middle of the object. It is the middle only when the mass is spread evenly. Load a bookcase's top shelf with heavy books and the centre of mass rises above the midpoint, which is why it tips forward so easily.

The second is judging stability from height alone. A tall object with a wide base and its mass low down can be very stable. The test is always the same: does the vertical line from the centre of mass stay inside the base?

One IMSO paper asks not about toppling but about turning, giving a force applied away from the centre of mass and asking which way the object spins.

Commonly missed

A block rests on a smooth table. A horizontal push is applied near its top edge rather than through its centre of mass. Describe the block's motion.

The trap is answering "it slides forwards" and stopping there. That is only half right. A force off the centre of mass never produces pure sliding.

How to work it: split the effect in two. First, there is an unbalanced force on the block, so its centre of mass accelerates in the direction of the push — it slides forwards. Second, the push does not pass through the centre of mass, so it also has a turning effect about that point. The push is above the centre of mass, so the top is driven forwards while the lower part lags, and the block rotates forwards as well as sliding. If the push were low instead, the rotation would go the other way.

5

Connected ideas

The centre of mass is where the weight arrow is drawn in any force diagram, so it connects mass, weight and gravity. A balanced object is one whose forces and turning effects cancel, and pressure explains what the base does against the ground.

In one line

The centre of mass is the one point where an object's whole weight can be taken to act, and an object stays upright as long as a vertical line from that point lands inside its base.
