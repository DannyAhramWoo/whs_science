Concept

Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a proposed explanation stated so clearly that it tells you in advance what result would prove it wrong.

Introduced in Chapter 20: How Scientists Work

1

What it means

A hypothesis is often described as "a scientific guess", which is not quite wrong but misses the part that does the work. Anyone can guess. What turns a guess into a hypothesis is that it sticks its neck out: it commits you to a particular result before you look, so that the experiment can genuinely go against you.

Compare two statements about a plant. "Light affects how plants grow" is true, vague and useless — almost any result would fit it. "A bean seedling kept in the dark for two weeks will grow taller but paler than an identical seedling in daylight" is a hypothesis. It names what you will change, what you will measure, and which way the measurement should go. If the dark seedling comes out shorter and greener, the hypothesis has failed, and you will know it immediately.

The word testable means exactly this: there is some observation you could make that would settle the matter. Not necessarily an easy observation, but a possible one. The word falsifiable is the sharper version — it means there is a specific result that would count as failure. A hypothesis that cannot fail cannot succeed either, because nothing you find out will have told you anything.

It is worth saying plainly that a hypothesis is not a theory. In ordinary speech "theory" means a hunch, but in science a theory is the opposite: a large explanation that has already survived a great deal of testing, like the germ theory of disease. A hypothesis is what you have before the testing; a theory is what may survive after a great deal of it.

2

How it works

The practical shape of a good hypothesis is a prediction with a reason attached. "If I increase the number of cells in the circuit, the bulb will get brighter, because more cells push a bigger current through the same bulb." The first half can be checked by looking. The second half is what makes it science rather than fortune-telling, because it says which underlying idea is on trial.

That matters when the test fails. If the bulb does not get brighter, you have learned something specific — the reasoning about current is wrong, or something else in the setup is interfering — rather than simply learning that a guess was unlucky. A prediction with no reason attached teaches you nothing when it fails.

Notice what a successful test does and does not do. If the bulb does get brighter, your hypothesis has survived. It has not been proved. It survived one test, and some other explanation might fit equally well; perhaps handling the circuit tightened a loose connection. This is why scientists talk about a hypothesis being "supported" rather than "proved", and it is not false modesty. Every hypothesis that was eventually overturned had been supported by evidence first.

Failure, on the other hand, is decisive in a way that success is not. One clean, well-controlled experiment in which the dark seedling comes out greener is enough to sink the seedling hypothesis. A thousand agreeing experiments cannot make it certain, but one disagreeing experiment can make it false. That asymmetry is the engine of the whole method: you cannot climb to certainty, but you can reliably eliminate.

This also explains why "it did not work" is not a wasted experiment, though it feels like one. A hypothesis eliminated is a genuine result. The only genuinely wasted experiment is one designed so loosely that no outcome would have counted against it.

The heart of it

A hypothesis earns its name by naming, in advance, the result that would prove it wrong.

3

Where the idea came from

There is no single inventor, and any page that names one is simplifying. But two figures shaped the idea more than most.

The first is the English lawyer and philosopher Francis Bacon, writing in the early 1600s. Bacon attacked the scholarly habit of his day, which was to start from the authority of ancient writers and reason downwards. He argued for the reverse: gather observations first, look for patterns, then deliberately seek out the cases that would break the pattern. His insistence that nature had to be interrogated by experiment rather than consulted in books is the root of the modern habit. Bacon himself got a good deal wrong — he was dismissive of the mathematical approach that turned out to be enormously fruitful, and he never produced much experimental science of his own — but the shift in attitude was real.

The second is Karl Popper, an Austrian-born philosopher working mainly in the twentieth century. Popper asked a sharp question: what separates a scientific claim from a non-scientific one? His answer was falsifiability. A claim is scientific, he argued, not because it can be confirmed — it is easy to find confirmations for almost anything if you go looking — but because it forbids something. A claim that is compatible with every possible observation tells you nothing about the world.

Popper's criterion is not the last word, and philosophers still argue over its edges, since real scientists rarely abandon a good idea on a single failed test. But as a rule of thumb for judging whether your own hypothesis is worth testing, it remains the most useful single question you can ask: what result would make me give this up?

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent error is writing a hypothesis that cannot lose. "Temperature will affect the rate of dissolving" cannot lose, because faster, slower and unchanged are all covered by the word "affect". Commit to a direction: faster or slower. Being wrong in a stated direction is far more useful than being safely unfalsifiable.

A second error is treating a supported hypothesis as a settled fact and stopping. The reason to keep testing is that agreement is cheap — it is disagreement that carries information.

A third is confusing a hypothesis with a method. "I will put one plant in the cupboard and one on the windowsill" is a plan, not a hypothesis. It says what you will do, not what you expect or why.

Commonly missed

A student writes: "My hypothesis is that adding sugar to water will change how long it takes to freeze." Explain why this is a weak hypothesis and rewrite it.

The trap is that the sentence looks scientific. It names a variable, it names an outcome, and it sounds cautious. But "change" covers every possible result, so no experiment could ever fail to support it. Caution here is a disguise for saying nothing.

How to work it: ask what result would count as the hypothesis being wrong. Under the original wording, none would — that is the fault. Fix it by committing to a direction and supplying a reason: "Water with sugar dissolved in it will take longer to freeze than pure water, because the dissolved sugar particles get in the way of the water particles forming an ice structure." Now a result showing the sugary water freezing first, or at the same time, clearly contradicts it, and the experiment has become capable of teaching you something.

5

Connected ideas

A hypothesis usually begins life as an inference from something you observed and could not explain. Turning it into a real test means controlling variables, since a hypothesis about one factor can only be tested if the other factors are held still. How much you can trust the outcome depends on the accuracy and precision of your measurements — a difference smaller than your measurement error cannot support or sink anything. Many of the big ideas elsewhere in this book, from the particle model to photosynthesis, are hypotheses that survived so much testing that they graduated into theories.
