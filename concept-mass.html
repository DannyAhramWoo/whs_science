Concept

Mass

Mass is how much matter an object contains — and, just as importantly, how hard it is to get that object moving or to stop it.

Introduced in Chapter 2: Mass and Weight

1

What it means

Pick up an empty backpack, then pick it up again with six textbooks inside. Something has been added, and you can feel it. That something is matter — the stuff an object is made of, the atoms in it. Mass measures how much of that stuff is there, in grams and kilograms.

The chapter's definition — mass is the amount of matter in an object — is true, but it hides something, because you cannot count the atoms in a backpack. So what is really being measured when a balance reads 2 kg?

Mass shows itself in two completely different ways. First, an object with more mass is pulled harder by gravity — that is the one the chapter used. Second, an object with more mass is harder to accelerate: harder to start moving, harder to stop, harder to swerve. This second property is called inertia, and it has nothing whatsoever to do with gravity.

That second meaning survives everywhere. Take a toolbox into deep space and it no longer presses on your hand at all. But shove it and it still resists, and once drifting it still takes work to stop. The stuff is still there, so the mass is still there.

2

How it works

Think about an empty shopping trolley and one loaded with bags of rice, both rolling on the same smooth floor. The loaded one is harder to get going and much harder to stop. Nothing about the floor changed. What changed is the mass, and mass is exactly the thing that resists a change in motion.

This is why two very different instruments both claim to measure mass. A beam balance — the kind with a pan on each side — compares your object against known masses, and gravity pulls on both sides equally, so it cancels out of the comparison. A bathroom scale instead measures the force pressing down on it. Two mechanisms, and only one travels well.

Mass and size are also not the same thing. A duvet is enormous and has very little mass; a steel bolt is tiny and has a surprising amount. What links them is density — how much mass is packed into each unit of space, which is why "bigger" and "heavier" are not the same claim.

The heart of it

Mass measures matter, and matter resists being pushed around — so mass is the same everywhere in the universe, whether or not any gravity is acting on it.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of history nobody separated mass from weight, and it would be unfair to call that a stupid mistake. On the surface of the Earth the two always change together: double the matter and you double the pull. Nothing in ordinary life ever forces them apart, so there was no reason to invent two words.

The split became sharp only with Isaac Newton, in his book Principia, published in 1687. Newton needed a quantity describing how strongly an object resisted a push, separately from how strongly the Earth pulled it, because his laws of motion did not work otherwise. It is a good example of a distinction that existed all along but that nobody needed until the mathematics demanded it.

The kilogram itself has a stranger history. From 1889 until 2019 a kilogram was defined by a single physical object: a cylinder of platinum and iridium kept near Paris, called the International Prototype of the Kilogram. It was the kilogram, by definition, and copies were checked against it every few decades.

That system had a flaw. Over the years the prototype's mass appeared to drift very slightly compared with its own copies, and nobody could say which had changed, because the master cylinder could not be wrong — it was the definition. In 2019 it was replaced by a definition built on the Planck constant, a fixed constant of nature. The reasoning is worth remembering: an object can be scratched or contaminated, but a constant of nature cannot change.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is saying "mass" when you mean weight, or writing kilograms for a force. Everyday English is the culprit, because "heavy" refers to weight while shop labels are printed in kilograms. In a science answer the two are not interchangeable, and this question format often tests exactly that.

The second error is subtler: believing mass changes when an object changes shape or state. Crush a drinks can flat and its mass is unchanged, because no atoms left. Melt an ice cube in a sealed container and the mass is unchanged too — the same particles are still inside, only arranged differently. Mass tracks matter, not appearance.

Commonly missed

A beam balance and a bathroom scale both read 2 kg for the same object on Earth. Both are carried to the Moon and used again. What does each read now?

The trap is assuming that two instruments showing the same number must work the same way. They do not, and the Moon exposes the difference.

How to work it: Ask what each instrument senses. The beam balance compares the object against known masses; the Moon's weaker gravity pulls on both pans equally, so they still balance and it reads 2 kg. The bathroom scale senses the downward force alone, about six times smaller on the Moon, so it reads roughly 0.33 kg. The real mass never changed. Only one instrument was ever measuring it directly.

5

Connected ideas

Multiply mass by the strength of gravity and you get weight, which is why the two are so easily confused. Mass is also what gravity responds to: more massive objects pull harder. The resistance to being pushed is inertia, and mass shared over a volume gives density. Measuring mass is fiddly, making it a natural place to think about accuracy and precision — the difference between a reading close to the truth and one merely repeatable.

In one line

Mass is the amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms, and it is the same on Earth, on the Moon and in empty space, because moving an object somewhere does not add or remove any of its stuff.
