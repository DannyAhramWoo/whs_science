Concept

The Kidneys and the Nephron

The kidneys keep the blood's composition correct by filtering out almost everything small and then taking back whatever the body still needs; urine is what is left over.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

It is natural to say the kidneys make urine, the way a factory makes a product. That is not their job. Their job is to keep your blood right: the correct water, the correct salt, no build-up of urea. Urine is the leftovers.

Think of tidying a very full drawer. You could hunt through it for the rubbish, which is slow and easy to get wrong. Or you could tip it out and put back only what you want. That sounds wasteful, but it is quicker and far more reliable, and it is the design your kidneys use.

Each kidney holds around a million tiny working units called nephrons: a filter joined to a long, thin tube. Everything below happens inside one nephron, a million times over. This goes beyond what primary papers usually ask about, but it is here because it does come up.

2

How it works

The order matters more than any single name, so follow it as a sequence: filter, then reclaim, then concentrate.

Step one — the glomerulus filters. The glomerulus is a tight knot of capillaries, like a ball of tangled wool. Blood arrives under high pressure, which squeezes the small things out through the capillary walls: water, glucose, amino acids, salts and urea. Blood cells and large proteins are too big to pass. The glomerulus is on the blood side, and filtering is all it does.

Step two — Bowman's capsule catches. Wrapped around that knot is a cup called Bowman's capsule. It filters nothing; it collects the liquid squeezed out and leads into the tubule. The two are not alternatives: the glomerulus is the sieve, Bowman's capsule the bowl underneath. The liquid collected there is the filtrate.

Step three — the tubule reabsorbs the useful things. The filtrate runs down a long, winding tube surrounded by capillaries, and here the body takes back what it should never have lost: glucose and amino acids completely, along with most of the water and the salt still needed. This is reabsorption, the step people forget.

Steps four and five — the loop concentrates, the collecting duct carries away. A hairpin bend, the loop of Henle, pulls back yet more water, so the remaining liquid becomes concentrated. That is why urine is dark when you have drunk little. What leaves is mostly water with urea and surplus salts. Urea stays behind on purpose, being waste; glucose should never appear at all, since reabsorption reclaims every bit, which is why doctors test for it.

The path out is kidney, ureter, bladder, urethra. The two similar words are easy to muddle. The ureter runs from kidney to bladder, and there are two; the urethra is the single tube from the bladder out of the body.

The heart of it

The glomerulus filters almost everything small out of the blood, Bowman's capsule catches it, and the tubule then takes back everything still useful — so urine is what nobody claimed.

3

Where the idea came from

The Italian anatomist Marcello Malpighi, using an early microscope in the 1660s, saw small round tufts of vessels scattered through the kidney. He could describe them but not explain them. They are still sometimes called Malpighian corpuscles in his honour.

In 1842 the English surgeon William Bowman described the capsule surrounding each tuft and how it connects to the tubule. Only then did the shape make sense: a filter inside a cup draining into a tube. Around 1844 the German physiologist Carl Ludwig proposed how it must work — pressure forces fluid out at the glomerulus, and useful substances are taken back along the tubule. That was reasoning rather than observation, and it was argued over for decades.

The argument was settled in the 1920s by A. N. Richards and colleagues. They pushed an extremely fine glass pipette into a single nephron of a frog, drew out the fluid and analysed it. The fluid in Bowman's capsule contained glucose; the fluid further along the tubule did not. Filtration first, reabsorption afterwards, measured directly rather than inferred. The technique is called micropuncture.

4

What people get wrong

Two mistakes dominate, both from reading a nephron diagram without holding the sequence in mind.

The first is swapping the glomerulus and Bowman's capsule, drawn on top of one another, so a label line can seem to point at either. Separate them by what they are made of. The glomerulus is blood vessels, part of the circulation. Bowman's capsule is no vessel; it is the start of the tubule, a cup round the outside. Vessels inside, cup outside.

The second is assuming any labelled point along the tubule is where urine is concentrated. Concentration happens late, at the loop and collecting duct; the part just after Bowman's capsule reabsorbs glucose and amino acids.

Commonly missed

On a diagram of a nephron, one label points to the knot of capillaries and another points to the winding tube just after it. What happens at each?

The trap is that both sit close together at the top of the diagram and both deal with the filtrate, so it is easy to answer with whichever name comes first to mind.

How to work it: Walk the sequence rather than guessing at a label. Blood under pressure reaches the knot of capillaries — the glomerulus — and small substances are squeezed out; that is filtering, and it is all the knot does. The cup around it, Bowman's capsule, catches the filtrate. The winding tube just after reclaims glucose, amino acids and most of the water. Only later, at the loop and collecting duct, is the liquid concentrated. So the knot filters, and the tube after it reabsorbs — not concentrates.

5

Connected ideas

The kidney handles urea, which the liver made from surplus amino acids supplied by digestion. It depends on circulation, since it can only clean blood brought to it, and its filtering is driven by the pressure the heart creates.

In one line
