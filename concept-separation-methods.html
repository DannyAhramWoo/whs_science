Concept

Separating Mixtures

A mixture can be pulled apart again by taking advantage of some property in which its parts differ.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Pick the stones out of a bowl of rice and you have separated a mixture. Nobody had to teach you the method, because the stones are bigger and a different colour, and either difference is enough to work with. Every separation method in science is the same trick done more carefully.

A mixture is two or more substances that are together but have not joined up, so each keeps its own properties. Salt stirred into water is still salt, still salty, still able to be got back. Because nothing has joined, the parts can be separated by physical means: sorting, warming, pouring, straining.

That is the first thing to fix. You can only separate a mixture. If the substances have genuinely joined to make a compound, a single new substance, then simple physical methods will not touch it. Rust is not iron with something sprinkled on it, so you cannot shake or filter the iron back out of it.

2

How it works

Choosing a method is always the same question: what is different about the parts? Name the difference, and the method names itself.

Using a magnet works when one part is magnetic and the rest is not: iron filings come cleanly out of sand because sand is unaffected. Using a sieve works when the pieces are different sizes and both are solids, as with stones from soil. Decanting, which means carefully pouring off the liquid, works when a heavy insoluble solid has settled and can be left behind at the bottom.

Filtration also uses size, but between a solid and a liquid: the holes in the filter paper let the liquid pass and are too small for undissolved lumps. Evaporation uses a difference in how easily things turn to gas — warm salt water and the water leaves as vapour while the salt is left in the dish. Sublimation uses the same idea between two solids, when one turns straight to gas on warming and the other does not.

Now a worked case: a mixture of sand, salt and water, from which you must obtain all three. Sand is insoluble, salt is dissolved, water is the liquid. Filter first, so the sand is caught in the filter paper and the salty water passes through. Then evaporate that liquid, and dry salt is left behind.

The order matters. Evaporating first would leave salt and sand jumbled together as one dry solid, and you would have to add water again to get anywhere. Filtering first works because filtration removes only the undissolved part, so each step takes out exactly one thing without spoiling the next. Iron filings, sand and salt follow the same logic: magnet first, then add water and filter out the sand, then evaporate to recover the salt.

The heart of it

You separate a mixture by finding one property in which its parts differ, and every method is just a way of exploiting one such difference.

3

Where the idea came from

Separation is one of the oldest pieces of practical science, and it has no single discoverer. Straining wine through cloth, letting muddy water settle, and panning for gold are all ancient, and Egyptian and Greek writers describe such methods thousands of years ago. These people were not testing a theory of matter; they wanted clear wine and clean water.

The methods were sharpened by the alchemists. One of the earliest named figures is Maria the Jewess, also called Mary the Prophetess, an alchemist associated with Alexandria. Her dates are genuinely uncertain — sources place her somewhere around the first to third century AD, and we cannot be sure. She is credited with distillation apparatus including the tribikos, and with the water bath still called the bain-marie after her. Her own writings do not survive, so some of what is attributed to her may be embroidered.

What the alchemists lacked was the idea that a mixture differs in kind from a compound. Without it, a failed separation was as likely to be blamed on bad luck as on the substances being genuinely combined. Only when chemists began to weigh carefully did that line become clear, and only then could you predict whether a separation would work at all.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is reaching for filtration whenever a solid and a liquid are mentioned. Filtration only removes solid that is not dissolved; anything dissolved slips straight through. The second error is choosing methods in the wrong order, usually by evaporating too early and re-mixing two solids that had been conveniently apart. A good habit is to label each part of the mixture as magnetic or not, dissolved or not, solid or liquid, and only then pick a method. The labels decide the order.

Commonly missed

A student is given a mixture of sand and salt, both dry. She decides to filter it to separate them. Explain why this will not work, and describe a method that does.

It is a trap because sand and salt look like exactly the sort of thing a filter should deal with.

How to work it: Filtration separates an insoluble solid from a liquid. With two dry solids there is nothing to pass through the paper, so both stay on top and nothing is separated. Instead use the difference that matters: salt dissolves in water and sand does not. Add water and stir, filter so the sand is caught while the salty water passes through, then evaporate that liquid to leave the salt. Three steps, each using one difference.

5

Connected ideas

Every separation method rests on the particle model, since it is the size and behaviour of particles that make one part of a mixture behave unlike another. Filtration and sublimation are worth meeting in their own right, and understanding what a solution is explains why dissolved substances refuse to be filtered out. Magnetism supplies the cleanest separation of all, and density explains why a settled solid can be decanted from above.

In one line

You can separate a mixture but not a compound, and you do it by finding a property in which the parts differ and choosing the method that uses that difference.
