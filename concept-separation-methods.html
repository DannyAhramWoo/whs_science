Concept

Separating Mixtures

A mixture can be pulled apart again by taking advantage of some property in which its parts differ.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Pick the stones out of a bowl of rice and you have separated a mixture. Nobody had to teach you the method, because the stones are bigger and harder and a different colour, and any one of those differences is enough to work with. Every separation method in science is the same trick done more carefully.

A mixture is two or more substances that are together but have not joined up. Each keeps its own properties. Salt stirred into water is still salt, still salty, still able to be got back. Because nothing has joined, nothing has to be undone, and the parts can be separated by physical means — sorting, warming, pouring, straining. No new substance is made or destroyed along the way.

That is the first thing to fix. You can only separate a mixture. If the substances have genuinely joined to make a compound, a single new substance, then simple physical methods will not touch it. Water is not a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen you could strain or warm apart; the two have combined, and getting them back needs a chemical process, not a sieve. Rust is the same. It is not iron with something sprinkled on it, so you cannot shake or filter the iron back out.

2

How it works

Choosing a method is always the same question: what is different about the parts? Name the difference, and the method names itself.

Using a magnet works when one part is magnetic and the rest is not. Iron filings mixed with sand come out cleanly on a magnet because sand is unaffected. Using a sieve works when the pieces are different sizes and both are solids — stones from soil, lumps from flour. Decanting, which just means carefully pouring off the liquid, works when a heavy insoluble solid has settled and you can leave it behind at the bottom.

Filtration also uses size, but between a solid and a liquid. The holes in the filter paper are large enough for the liquid to pass and too small for the undissolved solid lumps, so the solid stays behind. Evaporation uses a difference in how easily things turn to gas: warm salt water and the water leaves as vapour while the salt, which does not become a gas at those temperatures, is left in the dish. Sublimation uses the same idea between two solids, when one of them turns straight to gas on warming and the other does not.

Now a worked case. You are given a mixture of sand, salt and water and asked to obtain all three. Sand is insoluble, salt is dissolved, water is the liquid. Filter first: the sand is caught in the filter paper and the salty water passes through. Then evaporate the salty water: the water goes off as vapour and dry salt is left. If you want the water itself rather than just wanting it gone, you would have to catch and cool the vapour instead of letting it escape.

The order matters, and this is where reasoning earns its keep. Evaporating first would leave salt and sand jumbled together as one dry solid, and you would have to add water again to get anywhere. Filtering first works because filtration removes only the undissolved part and leaves the dissolved part untouched — so each step takes out exactly one thing and does not spoil the next. A mixture of iron filings, sand and salt follows the same logic: magnet first for the iron, then add water and filter to take out the sand, then evaporate to recover the salt.

The heart of it

You separate a mixture by finding one property in which its parts differ, and every method is just a way of exploiting one such difference.

3

Where the idea came from

Separation is one of the oldest pieces of practical science, and it has no single discoverer. Straining wine through cloth, letting muddy water stand until the silt settled, and panning for gold in a river are all ancient. Egyptian and Greek writers describe cloth straining and settling for wine and drinking water thousands of years ago. These people were not testing a theory of matter. They wanted clear wine and clean water, and they noticed what worked.

The methods were sharpened by the alchemists, who spent centuries heating, cooling and purifying substances in the hope of transforming them. One of the earliest named figures is Maria the Jewess, also called Mary the Prophetess, an alchemist associated with Alexandria. Her dates are genuinely uncertain — sources place her somewhere around the first to third century AD, and we cannot be sure. She is credited with distillation apparatus including the tribikos, and with the gentle water bath still called the bain-marie after her. Her own writings do not survive; we know her mainly through later writers quoting her, so some of what is attributed to her may be embroidered.

What the alchemists lacked was the idea that a mixture is different in kind from a compound. Without it, a separation that failed was as likely to be blamed on bad luck or bad timing as on the substances being genuinely combined. Only when chemists began to weigh things carefully and to see that some substances could not be split by any amount of warming or straining did the line between mixture and compound become clear, and only then could you predict in advance whether a separation would work at all.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is reaching for filtration whenever a solid and a liquid are mentioned. Filtration only removes solid that is not dissolved. Anything dissolved slips straight through with the liquid, because dissolved particles are spread out among the liquid particles and are far too small to be caught.

The second error is choosing methods in the wrong order, usually by evaporating too early and re-mixing two solids that had been conveniently apart. A good habit is to write down what is in the mixture, label each part as magnetic or not, dissolved or not, solid or liquid, and only then pick a method. The labels decide the order.

Commonly missed

A student is given a mixture of sand and salt, both dry. She decides to filter it to separate them. Explain why this will not work, and describe a method that does.

It is a trap because sand and salt look like exactly the sort of thing a filter should deal with, and both are solids of different sizes.

How to work it: Filtration separates an insoluble solid from a liquid. With two dry solids there is nothing to pass through the paper, so both stay on top and nothing is separated. Instead, use the difference that matters: salt dissolves in water and sand does not. Add water and stir, so the salt dissolves and the sand does not. Filter, and the sand is left in the filter paper while the salty water passes through. Evaporate that liquid, and the salt is left behind as the water turns to vapour. Three steps, each using one difference.

5

Connected ideas

Every separation method rests on the particle model, since it is the size, spacing and behaviour of particles that make one part of a mixture behave unlike another. Filtration and sublimation are worth meeting in their own right. Evaporation is the method behind salt recovery, and understanding what a solution is explains why dissolved substances refuse to be filtered out. Magnetism supplies the cleanest separation of all, and density explains why a settled solid can be decanted from above.
