Chapter 7

Light, Sound and Colour

Light and sound are both ways energy travels, and they behave in almost opposite ways. The single biggest mistake is treating them as the same kind of thing — light can cross empty space, sound cannot.

7.1

Light travels in straight lines

Light travels in straight lines. It does not curve round corners on its own. Everything in this section follows from that one fact.

A shadow forms when something blocks the light. The light that would have reached that patch of wall was stopped, and because light cannot bend round the blocker, nothing arrives to replace it. The shadow is simply the shape of the missing light.

Materials are sorted by how much light they let through. A transparent material lets nearly all the light straight through, so you can see clearly through it — clear glass, still water. A translucent material lets some light through but scatters it, so you see a blur of light but no sharp shape — frosted glass, tracing paper, a plastic milk bottle. An opaque material lets none through — wood, metal, a book. Only opaque objects make a full dark shadow.

Why shadows exist

A shadow forms because light cannot bend round an opaque object. If light could curve, there would be no shadows at all.

7.2

Reflection and mirror images

When light hits a smooth surface it bounces off. This is reflection. The law of reflection says the angle the light arrives at equals the angle it leaves at, measured from a line drawn at right angles to the mirror.

A flat mirror is called a plane mirror, and the image it makes follows four rules every time:

The image is the same size as the object. It is the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front. It is laterally inverted — left and right are swapped, which is why the word AMBULANCE is printed back-to-front on the front of the vehicle so a driver reads it correctly in a rear-view mirror. And it is a virtual image: the light does not actually go behind the mirror, so you could not catch the image on a screen held there.

Commonly missed

A girl stands 2 m in front of a plane mirror. She then walks 0.5 m towards it. What is now the distance between her and her image?

The trap is answering 1.5 m, or 2 m, or 3 m — all of which come from forgetting that the image moves too. The image is never fixed to the wall. It always sits the same distance behind the mirror as the girl is in front, so when she moves, it moves the same amount in the opposite direction.

How to work it: First find her new distance to the mirror: 2 − 0.5 = 1.5 m. Her image is therefore 1.5 m behind the mirror. The distance between them is 1.5 + 1.5 = 3 m.

Say the rule out loud every time: object-to-image distance is twice the object-to-mirror distance. A 1.5 m gap to the mirror always means a 3 m gap to the image. Notice also that she and her image approach each other at twice her walking speed.

7.3

Refraction: light bending between materials

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one material into another — from air into water, or from air into glass. It happens because light does not travel at the same speed in every material, and the change of speed at the boundary tips the ray to a new direction.

The bending is real, but what you notice is the effect it has on where things seem to be. Your brain assumes light always came in a straight line, so it traces the bent ray backwards and puts the object in the wrong place.

Three classics come up over and over. A straw in a glass of water looks broken or bent at the water surface. A swimming pool looks shallower than it really is, which is why it can be dangerous to judge depth by eye. And words under a thick glass block appear nearer the top than they are.

Commonly missed

Which of these is not an effect of refraction? (1) A straw in water appears bent. (2) The word AMBULANCE appears the right way round in a mirror. (3) A swimming pool appears shallower than it is.

The trap is that all three are "things looking odd", so they feel like the same family. They are not. Refraction happens when light passes through a boundary into a new material. A mirror is not a boundary light passes through — the light bounces straight back off it.

How to work it: ask one question about each option — did the light go into a different material, or did it bounce? Straw in water: light went from water into air, so refraction. Pool: light went from water into air, so refraction. Mirror writing: light bounced off a shiny surface, so reflection, not refraction.

Lateral inversion belongs to mirrors and reflection. It is the standard wrong option in this question and it catches people every year.

7.4

Colour, filters and how we see

White light looks colourless but it is really a mixture of all the colours. A prism or a raindrop can spread it out into red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo and violet — that spread is a rainbow.

A filter is a coloured transparent sheet. It lets its own colour through and absorbs everything else. Shine white light through a red filter and only red comes out; all the other colours were soaked up. That is why the world looks dim through coloured plastic — most of the light has been removed.

Put two different filters one behind the other and you usually get darkness. A red filter passes only red. A blue filter then absorbs red, because red is not its colour. Nothing survives the second filter, so no light comes out at all.

Seeing colour works the same way. An apple looks red because its skin reflects red light into your eyes and absorbs the other colours. The redness is not stored in the apple; it is a statement about which light bounces off. So a red apple under pure blue light looks black — there is no red light present for it to reflect, and it absorbs the blue.

Reflects, not contains

An object looks red because it reflects red light and absorbs the rest. Take away the red light and it has nothing left to reflect, so it looks black.

7.5

Sound: vibrations that need a material

Sound is made by vibrations. A guitar string shakes, a drum skin shakes, your vocal cords shake. The vibrating object pushes the air next to it back and forth, that air pushes the air beyond it, and the disturbance travels outwards until it reaches your ear.

That chain is the important part. Sound needs a medium — a material to travel through. Solids, liquids and gases all work. A vacuum, which is space with no material in it at all, does not. Ring an electric bell inside a jar and pump the air out: the hammer still moves, but the sound fades to nothing. Light from the same jar keeps coming out perfectly well. This is why space is silent even though it is not dark.

Two properties of a sound can change independently. Pitch is how high or low a note sounds, and it depends on frequency — how many vibrations happen each second. Faster vibration means higher pitch. Loudness depends on amplitude — how big each vibration is. Hitting a drum harder makes a bigger vibration and a louder sound, but the pitch does not change.

Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), and at this level you find it by dividing:

frequency = number of vibrations ÷ time in seconds

So an object making 20 vibrations in 5 seconds has a frequency of 20 ÷ 5 = 4 Hz. If a different object makes 60 vibrations in 5 seconds, that is 12 Hz — three times the frequency, so a higher pitch.

Light versus sound

Light can travel through a vacuum. Sound cannot, because it needs particles of a material to pass the vibration along. If a question mentions space, an airless jar, or the Moon, this is almost always the point being tested.

7.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

7.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A boy stands 1.2 m in front of a plane mirror. How far is he from his image? He then steps back 0.3 m. How far apart are they now?

Show answer

The image is always the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front. So the image starts 1.2 m behind the mirror, and the distance between the boy and his image is 1.2 + 1.2 = 2.4 m.

After stepping back he is 1.2 + 0.3 = 1.5 m from the mirror. His image moves back the same amount, to 1.5 m behind. The new separation is 1.5 + 1.5 = 3.0 m.

The pattern is worth memorising: the object-to-image distance is always twice the object-to-mirror distance, so it changes by twice as much as the person moves.

2

An astronaut on the Moon can see an explosion but hears nothing. Explain why, and say what this tells you about the difference between light and sound.

Show answer

There is almost no air on the Moon, so there is no medium for the sound to travel through. Sound moves by making particles of a material vibrate and pass the vibration on. With no particles, the vibration cannot be passed anywhere, so no sound reaches the astronaut.

Light does not need particles. It can travel through a vacuum, which is why the astronaut still sees the flash — and why sunlight reaches Earth across empty space.

3

A ruler is held over the edge of a desk and twanged. It makes 45 vibrations in 3 seconds. Find the frequency. The ruler is then pushed further in so a shorter piece sticks out, and it makes 90 vibrations in 3 seconds. What has happened to the pitch?

Show answer

Frequency is the number of vibrations divided by the time in seconds. First case: 45 ÷ 3 = 15 Hz. Second case: 90 ÷ 3 = 30 Hz.

The frequency has doubled, and pitch depends on frequency. So the note is higher the second time. A shorter length of ruler vibrates faster, which is why it sounds higher.

Note that nothing here tells you about loudness — that would depend on how hard the ruler was twanged, not how fast it vibrates.

4

A green leaf is placed in a room lit only by red light. What colour does it appear, and why?

Show answer

It appears black. A leaf looks green in white light because it reflects green light into your eyes and absorbs the other colours, including red.

In a room lit only by red light there is no green light present for the leaf to reflect. The red light that does land on it is absorbed. Almost no light comes back off the leaf, so it looks black.

7.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 7.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about the image formed in a plane mirror are correct?

  • 1The image is larger than the object.
  • 2The image is the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front.
  • 3The image is laterally inverted.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. A plane mirror image is always exactly the same size as the object, never larger. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer must be C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is the standard equal-distance rule, and statement 3 is lateral inversion, which is why AMBULANCE is printed back-to-front. Both correct, so C fits.

2

Which statements about sound are correct?

  • 1Sound is produced by vibrating objects.
  • 2A sound of higher frequency has a higher pitch.
  • 3Sound can travel through a vacuum.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Every sound starts with something vibrating. The answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. A vacuum has no particles, so there is nothing to pass the vibration along. Light can cross a vacuum, sound cannot. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Pitch depends on frequency: more vibrations per second means a higher note. So the answer is B.

3

A red book is viewed under different conditions. Which statements are correct?

  • 1In white light, the book reflects red light and absorbs the other colours.
  • 2Under blue light only, the book appears black.
  • 3Placing a red filter and a blue filter one behind the other lets most of the light through.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. That is exactly what "looking red" means. The answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. The red filter passes only red; the blue filter then absorbs that red. Two different filters in a row give darkness, not brightness. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Under blue light there is no red light for the book to reflect, and it absorbs blue, so it looks black. The answer is B.
