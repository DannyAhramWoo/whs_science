Concept

Gravity

Gravity is an attraction between any two objects that have mass — every object pulls on every other one, always, across empty space.

Introduced in Chapter 2: Mass and Weight

1

What it means

Drop anything and it falls. That is so ordinary it takes effort to notice how odd it is. Nothing touches the object — no string pulls it, no wind blows it — yet it accelerates towards the ground the moment you let go. Gravity is a non-contact force, one that acts across a gap with nothing in between.

The chapter said gravity acts between any two objects with mass, and that is meant literally: you are pulling on this page and the page is pulling on you. Nobody notices because gravity is an extraordinarily weak force, so weak that it only becomes obvious when one object is planet-sized. The Earth has a mass of about six thousand billion billion tonnes, which is why its pull is the only one you ever feel.

Two things set the strength of the pull. Mass: more massive objects pull harder, so Earth pulls harder than the Moon. Distance: the further apart two objects are, the weaker the pull, and it fades quickly — double the distance and the pull drops to a quarter, not a half. That sharp falling-off is why gravity dominates near a planet and becomes almost nothing far away.

2

How it works

Start with the strangest fact about falling. Drop a heavy stone and a light pebble together and they land at the same moment. This feels wrong, because the stone is pulled much harder. But the stone also has more mass to shift: doubling the mass doubles the pull and also doubles the resistance to being accelerated, so the two effects cancel exactly and every object falls at the same rate. A feather ruins the demonstration, but that is air resistance, not gravity — on the airless Moon in 1971, a dropped hammer and feather landed side by side.

Now the question that makes gravity click: why does the Moon not fall down? Imagine throwing a ball horizontally. It curves and lands. Throw it harder and it lands further away, because the Earth's surface curved away beneath it while it fell. Throw it hard enough and the surface curves away exactly as fast as the ball drops, so it never lands — it circles. That is an orbit, and it is why the space station stays up and why the Earth circles the Sun.

The heart of it

Gravity does not switch off in space; it is what holds every orbit together, and things float in orbit because they are falling alongside their spacecraft, not because the pull has gone.

3

Where the idea came from

The famous story is Isaac Newton and the falling apple, and it is worth knowing how well it is evidenced.

The account comes from William Stukeley, who wrote his Memoirs of Sir Isaac Newton's Life in 1752, describing a conversation with Newton in 1726 in which Newton recalled an apple falling and wondering why it always fell straight down — an event supposed to have happened around 1666. The chain is long: second-hand, written down roughly eighty-six years later, from an old man's memory of his own youth. Newton may well have polished it, and we cannot be sure of the details. But Newton himself used the story to explain his thinking, so it is not an invention of later writers.

The idea behind it was the real achievement. Newton's step was to propose that the force pulling the apple down is the same force holding the Moon in its orbit — that earthly and heavenly motion obey one law. Before this, the sky and the ground were widely thought to work by different rules entirely. In the Principia of 1687 he set out a law predicting the strength of that pull from the masses involved and the distance between them, and it matched the observed motions of the planets.

Here is the part most often left out. Newton described how gravity behaves, but explicitly declined to claim he knew what it is or why it exists. He could calculate the pull accurately and could not say what caused it, and he said so rather than inventing an explanation. That is a fair model of how science works: a law that predicts reliably is a real achievement even when the underlying reason is unknown.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest misunderstanding is that there is no gravity in space. If that were true, nothing would orbit anything. Gravity weakens with distance but never reaches zero, and at the space station's altitude it is still nearly as strong as at ground level.

The second is thinking gravity only pulls downwards. "Down" is not built into the universe; it is simply the direction towards the centre of the Earth. Someone on the opposite side of the planet is pulled the opposite way in space, and feels perfectly normal. Down is local.

Commonly missed

Why does the Moon stay in orbit around the Earth instead of falling into it or flying away?

The trap is that both wrong answers sound sensible. If gravity pulls the Moon, it should crash into us; if it does not, the Moon should escape. Many reach for a balance of two forces, but there is no second force pushing the Moon away.

How to work it: Separate the pull from the motion. Gravity pulls the Moon towards the Earth, and that is the only force acting. But the Moon is also travelling sideways at high speed, and gravity bends that motion into a curve rather than stopping it. The Moon falls towards the Earth continuously and continuously misses, because the surface curves away just as fast. In one sentence: the Moon is falling, but moving sideways fast enough to keep missing.

5

Connected ideas

Gravity is a force, so everything true of forces applies to it. Acting on an object's mass, it produces that object's weight — mass is what gravity grips, weight is how hard it grips. Because gravity holds orbits, it explains why the phases of the Moon repeat and why an eclipse can be predicted years ahead: the geometry stays reliable only because the orbits do. And lifting something against gravity stores potential energy, released as kinetic energy when it falls.

In one line

Gravity is the attraction between any two objects with mass, stronger for larger masses and weaker with distance, and it never switches off — which is exactly why the Moon keeps circling the Earth instead of drifting away.
