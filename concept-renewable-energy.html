Concept

Renewable Energy

An energy source is renewable if nature refills it about as fast as we use it, so that using it today does not leave less of it for tomorrow.

Introduced in Chapter 9: Where Energy Comes From

1

What it means

Think about a tap and a bucket. Draw water from a bucket and it empties, until one day there is nothing left. Draw from a running tap and you can take a glass every hour for the rest of your life without the tap being any worse off. Non-renewable sources are buckets; renewable sources are taps.

The precise meaning is about rate, not amount. A source is renewable when nature makes new supply at least as fast as we take it away. Sunlight arrives every morning whether or not anyone collects it, wind keeps blowing, trees grow back in decades. Coal took millions of years to form from buried plants and we burn it in seconds, so the refilling is hopelessly slower than the emptying.

Notice what this definition does not say. It says nothing about how much there is, whether the source is clean, or whether it is cheap. Those are separate questions asked about the same list of sources, which is exactly why they get muddled.

2

How it works

The phrase to hold on to is replenished on a human timescale. To replenish means to refill. A human timescale means within years or decades, the sort of stretch a person actually lives through, rather than the millions of years that made coal. Wood passes the test; oil does not, because nothing we do puts it back within any lifetime.

It follows that renewable does not mean unlimited. If a village cuts firewood faster than its woodland regrows, that woodland will disappear even though wood is on the renewable list. Renewability is not a property stamped permanently on a substance; it is a comparison between how fast nature supplies and how fast we consume.

Nor does renewable mean clean. Biomass — fuel made from wood, straw or crops — is renewable, because another field of sugar cane grows next season. But it still has to be burnt, and burning anything containing carbon releases carbon dioxide. Nuclear power, meanwhile, releases no carbon dioxide at all and yet is firmly non-renewable, because uranium is a fixed amount of metal dug from rock. Those two sit in the corners nobody expects.

One more pattern hides underneath. Almost every renewable source is the Sun in disguise. The Sun heats some patches of the Earth more strongly than others, warm air rises, cooler air rushes in beneath it, and that moving air is wind. The Sun also evaporates seawater, which falls as rain on high ground; gravity then pulls it back down through a dam's turbines, so the Sun did the lifting for hydroelectric power. Biomass is solar energy captured by photosynthesis a few months ago. Only two renewables escape: geothermal, which is heat left inside the Earth, and tidal, which comes chiefly from the Moon's gravitational pull on the oceans.

The heart of it

Renewable means refilled fast enough to keep up with us — it is a promise about supply, never a promise that the source is unlimited, clean or free.

3

Where the idea came from

The technology is ancient. Watermills were turning grindstones in the Greek and Roman world more than two thousand years ago, and windmills were at work in Persia by roughly the ninth century, later spreading across medieval Europe to grind grain and pump water from low-lying fields. None of this was new by the time anyone thought to give it a name.

What is genuinely modern is the category. For nearly all of human history essentially every source of energy was renewable — wind in sails, water in mills, wood on fires, and the muscles of people and animals. Nobody sorted energy into renewable and non-renewable because there was almost nothing to put in the second pile. The age of coal and oil is the exception in the story, not the normal state of affairs.

The word itself became common only after the oil shocks of the 1970s, especially the embargo of 1973, when fuel supplies were suddenly cut and prices leapt. Petrol queues made a previously dull question urgent: which of our energy sources can actually run out, and which cannot? Sorting sources by that question is what created the label. No single person coined it, and you should be suspicious of any account that hands the credit to one named inventor.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating "renewable" as a general badge of goodness. The word sounds friendly, so people hear it as meaning clean, endless and harmless all at once. It means one narrow thing: nature refills it fast enough. Biofuel is the standing proof, since it is renewable and still puts carbon dioxide into the air.

The cure is to answer two questions separately every time. First: will it run out? That is renewability. Second: what comes out when it is used? That is pollution. Keeping them apart takes deliberate effort, because the two lists overlap so heavily that it feels as though one answer should cover both.

Commonly missed

Which of these renewable energy sources releases carbon dioxide when it is used?

The trap is that "renewable" tugs your mind towards "clean", so you hunt for the non-renewable option — and there is not one, because the question already told you every option is renewable.

How to work it: ask which source is set on fire. Solar, wind, hydroelectric, tidal and geothermal are never burnt; they capture movement or heat already there. Only biomass or biofuel is burnt, and burning carbon produces carbon dioxide, so that is the answer.

5

Connected ideas

Renewable energy is best understood against its opposite, so fossil fuels are the natural companion. Underneath both lies energy itself and the law that it is never created or destroyed, only shifted between forms — which is why every source is a store being tapped rather than a factory making something new. The water cycle explains how hydroelectric power is the Sun at second hand, and the greenhouse effect is why anyone cares about the difference at all.
