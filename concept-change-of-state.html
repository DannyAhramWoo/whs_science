Concept

Changes of State

A change of state is a substance moving between solid, liquid and gas as heat is gained or lost, with the particles rearranging but never changing into anything else.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Ice left in a warm room turns to water. Water left in a pan on a hot ring turns to steam. Steam meeting a cold window turns back to water. Each of these is a change of state, and every one of them can be reversed by putting the heat back or taking it away again.

The changes have names worth getting exactly right. Melting is solid to liquid. Freezing is liquid to solid — the same process running backwards, and it happens at the same temperature. Boiling and evaporation are both liquid to gas. Condensation is gas to liquid. And sublimation is solid straight to gas without passing through liquid, which is less familiar but real: solid carbon dioxide, sold as dry ice, sublimes at ordinary pressure, and this is why it produces fog rather than a puddle.

The essential point is that no new substance is ever made. The particles in steam are the same particles as in the ice it came from. They have only moved further apart and started moving faster. That is why every change of state is a physical change and can be undone.

2

How it works

The particles in a solid are held to one another by attractive forces. To melt the solid you must give the particles enough energy of motion to overcome those attractions well enough to slide past one another. To boil the liquid you must give them enough to break away from their neighbours altogether. Heating supplies that energy; cooling removes it and the process runs the other way.

Now here is the part that surprises almost everyone. Put a beaker of crushed ice at −10 °C on a steady heater and record the temperature every minute. At first the temperature rises, as you would expect. When it reaches 0 °C, the ice starts melting — and the temperature stops rising. It stays at 0 °C, sometimes for a considerable time, even though the heater is still going full blast. Only when the last of the ice has melted does the temperature begin to climb again. The same thing happens at 100 °C: while the water is boiling, the temperature holds steady, however hard you heat it.

Where is the energy going? Not into making the particles move faster, because temperature is a measure of how fast the particles move and the temperature is not changing. It is going into pulling the particles apart against their mutual attraction. That energy is called latent heat, from a Latin word meaning hidden — hidden because it goes in without showing up on the thermometer.

The amounts are large. Melting a gram of ice takes roughly as much energy as heating a gram of liquid water by 80 °C. Boiling a gram of water away takes about seven times more still. This has practical consequences you already know. Ice cubes cool a drink far better than the same mass of water at 0 °C, because as they melt they absorb all that latent heat from the drink. Steam scalds far worse than boiling water, because when it condenses on your skin it releases that same enormous quantity of energy.

Latent heat also runs in reverse. Freezing water releases heat rather than absorbing it. Fruit growers exploit this, spraying water on blossom before a frost: as the water freezes it gives out heat and holds the buds at around 0 °C rather than letting them fall lower.

The heart of it

While a substance is changing state its temperature stays fixed, because all the heat is going into rearranging the particles rather than speeding them up.

3

Where the idea came from

The behaviour of melting ice was well known long before anyone understood it. What was missing was the idea that heat and temperature are different things.

Joseph Black, working in Glasgow in the 1760s, made the crucial measurements. He noticed something that puzzled him: a large block of ice in a warm room takes hours to melt, whereas if you simply warm a similar quantity of cold water by a few degrees it happens in minutes. If melting merely meant reaching 0 °C, ice should melt almost the instant it got there. Instead heat kept flowing in for hours with nothing to show for it on the thermometer.

Black concluded that a large quantity of heat is absorbed during melting without producing any rise in temperature, and he named it latent heat. He went on to measure the same effect for boiling water and found it larger still. He was careful and quantitative about it, which is why the result stuck.

This was genuinely surprising at the time, and it is worth understanding why. Heat and temperature were not clearly distinguished. Most people took it as obvious that adding heat raises temperature, in the way that adding water to a bucket raises the level. Black's result showed that assumption is simply false during a change of state. Black himself explained heat using the caloric theory — the idea that heat is an invisible weightless fluid flowing between bodies — which was later abandoned in favour of heat as particle motion. His theory was wrong; his measurements and the concept of latent heat survived that change intact and are still used unchanged.

There is a practical postscript. Black's student and colleague James Watt was working on improving the steam engine at the time, and understanding how much heat was consumed and released in turning water to steam and back mattered directly to that work.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is believing the temperature keeps rising during melting or boiling. It does not, and this is the single most examined fact in the topic. If a heating curve is drawn, the flat sections are exactly where the changes of state occur.

The second is confusing melting with dissolving. Melting needs heat and involves one substance; dissolving needs a solvent and needs no heating at all.

The third is thinking the particles themselves change during a change of state. They do not change size, mass, or identity. Only their spacing and speed change, which is why the mass of a sealed container is the same whether its contents are frozen, liquid or gaseous.

Commonly missed

A mixture of ice and water at 0 °C is heated steadily. For several minutes the thermometer stays at 0 °C. A pupil says the heater must be faulty. Is the pupil right, and what is happening to the energy being supplied?

The trap is the assumption that supplied heat must always show as a temperature rise. That is only true when no change of state is in progress.

How to work it: The pupil is wrong and the heater is working normally. Temperature measures the average energy of motion of the particles. While ice is melting, the supplied energy is not speeding the particles up — it is being used to overcome the attractions holding the particles in the fixed arrangement of the solid, which is latent heat. So energy is going in continuously with no temperature change to show for it. The thermometer will only begin to rise once every last piece of ice has melted and further energy can go into making the water particles move faster.

5

Connected ideas

Changes of state move a substance between the states of matter, and the whole explanation rests on the particle model. The driving agent is heat, and the reason the thermometer misleads is the difference between heat and temperature. Evaporation and boiling are the two routes from liquid to gas and deserve separate attention. And because nothing new is made, every change of state is a physical change.
