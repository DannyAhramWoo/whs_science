Concept

Colour

Colour is what we see when light of different wavelengths reaches our eyes, and an object's colour is decided by which of those wavelengths it reflects and which it absorbs.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

White light is not simple. It is a mixture of every colour, all travelling together. A prism can pull that mixture apart into a band of colours — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet — called a spectrum. The colours do not really come in seven neat stripes; they blend continuously into one another, and the seven names are a convention rather than a measurement.

What makes one colour different from another is wavelength. Light travels as a wave, and the distance between one crest and the next is the wavelength. Red light has the longest wavelength of the colours we can see, violet the shortest. Your eye contains cells that respond differently to different wavelengths, and your brain reads the pattern of their responses as colour.

Now the important part. A red apple is not red in the dark. It has no colour of its own to give. What it has is a surface that absorbs most wavelengths and reflects the long, red ones back at you. Colour is not a property that objects carry around; it is a description of what a surface does to the light that lands on it.

2

How it works

Start with why a prism separates colours at all. It is refraction, but with one extra fact: different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds inside glass. Violet is slowed most, so it bends most. Red is slowed least, so it bends least. Every colour bends by a slightly different angle, and after passing through the prism they have fanned out into a spectrum. Air affects the colours almost equally, which is why you do not see this happening constantly.

A rainbow is the same process with raindrops instead of a prism. Sunlight enters a droplet, refracts and fans out, reflects off the inside of the back of the drop, then refracts again on the way out. Each droplet sends one colour towards your eye and the rest elsewhere, so the arc you see is built out of millions of droplets each contributing a different part of it. This also explains something people rarely notice: a rainbow has no fixed position. It depends on where you are standing, because it depends on which drops happen to be sending light to your eyes. Two people side by side see two different rainbows.

For coloured objects, the rule is that a surface reflects the colour it appears and absorbs the others. Grass looks green because it absorbs red and blue light and reflects green. A white object reflects nearly all wavelengths; a black object absorbs nearly all of them, which is also why black clothes get hot in the sun — the absorbed light becomes heat.

Now combine the two ideas and something useful falls out. Shine pure red light on a blue jumper. The jumper's surface absorbs red. There is no blue light present for it to reflect. So it reflects almost nothing and looks black. This is not a trick of the eye; the light simply is not there. Under a red lamp, a red object looks bright red, a white object looks red, and a blue or green object looks black or very dark. Photographers' darkrooms used red safelights for exactly this kind of reason.

One caution worth keeping straight: mixing coloured lights and mixing paints follow different rules. Red, green and blue lights together make white, because you are adding wavelengths. Mixing paints removes wavelengths instead — each pigment absorbs more of the spectrum — so mixing many paints tends towards muddy brown or black.

The heart of it

An object has no colour of its own; it looks the colour of whatever light it reflects, and it can only reflect wavelengths that were shining on it in the first place.

3

Where the idea came from

Before Isaac Newton, the usual belief was that white light was pure and simple, and that a prism added colour to it — that the glass somehow stained or corrupted the light, with the thicker parts of the prism doing more damage. This was a reasonable guess. Colour did seem to appear where there had been none.

In 1666 Newton did the prism experiments that settled it. Cambridge had closed because of plague, and he was at his family home at Woolsthorpe. He darkened a room, let a narrow beam of sunlight in through a hole in the shutter, and passed it through a prism to cast a spectrum on the far wall. Splitting light was not itself new; people had seen prisms make colours for a long time. Newton's decisive step was what he did next. He took the spread-out spectrum and passed it through a second prism arranged to recombine the colours — and got white light back.

That killed the staining theory. If the prism were adding colour, a second prism should have added more, not undone it. He also isolated a single colour from the spectrum and passed it alone through another prism: it stayed the same colour and refused to break down further. So the colours were already in the sunlight, and the prism only sorted them.

It was not accepted quietly. Robert Hooke, an established and formidable figure at the Royal Society, rejected Newton's conclusions and thought his own theory of light explained the results just as well. The dispute was bitter and ran for years. Being right did not spare Newton the argument, and the quarrel was one reason he delayed publishing his full book on optics until 1704.

4

What people get wrong

The deepest confusion is treating colour as something an object owns. Once you think of it that way, you will predict that a blue jumper stays blue under red light, because blue is what it "is". It is not. The correct question is always: which wavelengths are present, and which of those does this surface reflect?

A second slip is assuming a filter turns light into a colour. A red filter does not colour the light red. It absorbs the other wavelengths and lets red through. That is why light gets dimmer every time you add a filter — you are always removing, never adding.

Commonly missed

A green leaf is placed under a lamp giving out only blue light. What colour does the leaf appear, and why?

The trap is answering "green" out of habit, or "blue" by assuming everything under a blue lamp looks blue.

How to work it: Ask what light is available: only blue. Ask what the leaf does with blue light: it absorbs it, since green surfaces absorb blue and red and reflect green. There is no green light shining on it to reflect. So almost no light comes back from the leaf and it appears black or very dark. Note the contrast with a white sheet under the same lamp, which reflects all wavelengths and so looks blue.

5

Connected ideas

Colour sits directly on top of two other ideas: refraction, which is what separates white light into a spectrum because each wavelength bends differently, and reflection, which decides what an object does with the light landing on it. Absorbed light does not vanish — it becomes heat, which is why dark surfaces warm faster, linking colour to radiation and to energy in general.

In one line

White light contains all the colours, a prism separates them because each wavelength bends by a different amount, and an object looks the colour of the light it reflects rather than the light it absorbs.
