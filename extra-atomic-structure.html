Going further

Inside the Atom, and the Table That Orders Everything

Everything you have ever touched is built from a few dozen kinds of tiny particle. Once people worked out how to arrange those kinds in order, the arrangement started telling them about elements nobody had found yet.

Going further

This chapter goes further than the numbered ones. It reaches into what is normally taught a few years later, and everything in it is explained here from the beginning, so nothing is assumed.

It is here because it answers a question the numbered chapters keep circling: what is everything actually made of. And because the periodic table is one of the great achievements of science — a chart so well organised that it predicted elements before anyone had seen them.

1

What everything is made of

Take a piece of aluminium foil and cut it in half. Cut one half in half again. Keep going. You cannot do this forever. Eventually you reach a piece so small that cutting it again would leave you with something that is no longer aluminium. That smallest piece is an atom.

Our chapters talk about matter being made of particles. That word is deliberately general, because it covers several different things. In a great many cases, the more precise word for those particles is atom — or a small group of atoms joined together, which is called a molecule. So "particle" is not wrong. It is simply the safe word used before you learn the exact one.

How small is an atom

Small is the wrong word. Atoms are far smaller than anything the word small normally describes. Here is a comparison that helps a little.

Pick up one grain of sand. Now think of every grain of sand on a long beach — all of it, along the whole length, down to the water and deep under your feet. There are more atoms inside that one single grain than there are grains of sand on the beach. And a big beach holds a number of grains that is already impossible to imagine.

You will never see an atom with an ordinary microscope. Light itself is too coarse a tool. Special instruments can now produce images of atoms, but for most of history people believed in atoms purely because the idea explained what chemicals did, not because anyone had looked.

Elements, compounds and mixtures

An element is a substance made of one kind of atom and nothing else. Copper is an element. Oxygen is an element. Gold is an element. There are about 118 known elements, and roughly 90 of them occur naturally on Earth.

A compound is formed when different kinds of atom join chemically. Water is hydrogen joined to oxygen. Ordinary table salt is sodium joined to chlorine. A compound has its own properties, and they can be startlingly unlike the properties of what went in. Sodium is a metal that catches fire in water. Chlorine is a poisonous green gas. Joined, they are the harmless white stuff you put on chips.

A mixture is different again. The substances are jumbled together but not joined, so they keep their own properties and can be pulled apart again by physical means such as filtering, evaporating or using a magnet. Air is a mixture. It is about 78 per cent nitrogen and 21 per cent oxygen, with the last one per cent made up mostly of argon plus small amounts of carbon dioxide and other gases. Nobody has to react anything to get the oxygen out of air, because it was never joined to the nitrogen in the first place.

The test to remember

Ask: can I separate this without a chemical reaction. If yes, it is a mixture. If you would need a reaction to break it apart, it is a compound. If it cannot be broken down into anything simpler at all, it is an element.

2

Inside an atom

For a long time people thought the atom was the end of the line — the word itself comes from a Greek word meaning "uncuttable". It turned out not to be. An atom has parts.

At the centre sits the nucleus. It contains two kinds of particle:

  • pProtons — these carry a positive electric charge.
  • nNeutrons — these carry no charge at all. Neutral, hence the name.

Around the nucleus are electrons, which carry a negative charge. Electrons are extremely light compared with protons and neutrons — the nucleus holds almost all of an atom's mass in a very tiny space.

Charge is the same idea you meet in electricity and, loosely, in magnetism: opposite charges pull towards each other, like charges push apart. The positive nucleus pulls on the negative electrons, and that pull is what holds the atom together.

The atom is almost entirely empty

This is the part that surprises people. An atom is mostly nothing.

Imagine the nucleus is a marble sitting on the centre spot of a football pitch. On that scale, the electrons would be flitting about somewhere out near the stands. Everything between the marble and the stands — the whole pitch — is empty space.

So the desk you are leaning on is almost entirely empty, and so are you. The reason your hand does not pass through the desk has nothing to do with the space being filled. It is that the electrons on the surface of your hand and the electrons on the surface of the desk are both negative, and they push each other away. Solidity is a force, not a fullness.

What makes one element different from another

Here is the neatest rule in chemistry. The number of protons decides which element an atom is. That number is called the atomic number.

An atom with 1 proton is hydrogen. Always. With 6 protons it is carbon. With 8 it is oxygen. With 79 it is gold. Change the proton count and you have changed the element into a different one entirely. Nothing else about the atom has this power — you can vary the neutrons and it is still the same element, just a slightly heavier version.

In an ordinary atom the number of electrons matches the number of protons, so the positives and negatives cancel out and the atom overall has no charge.

Why the alchemists failed

For centuries people tried to turn lead into gold by heating it, mixing it and reciting things over it. They could never have succeeded. Lead has 82 protons and gold has 79, and no amount of chemistry touches the nucleus. Chemistry only ever rearranges electrons.

3

Counting the parts, and writing them down

Chemists got tired of writing "sodium" and "chlorine" out in full, so every element has a short symbol: one capital letter, or a capital followed by a small letter. Never two capitals.

  • 1O is oxygen, C is carbon, H is hydrogen, N is nitrogen.
  • 2Ca is calcium, Mg is magnesium, Al is aluminium, Zn is zinc.
  • 3Some come from Latin names, which is why they look wrong. Na is sodium, from natrium. Fe is iron, from ferrum. Au is gold, from aurum. Cu is copper, from cuprum. K is potassium, from kalium.

Put symbols together and you have a formula, which says exactly what a substance is built from. A small number after a symbol tells you how many of that atom there are, and it applies only to the symbol immediately in front of it.

So H2O means two hydrogen atoms joined to one oxygen atom — water. O2 means two oxygen atoms joined into one molecule, which is how oxygen actually exists in the air. NaCl means sodium and chlorine in equal numbers — table salt. CO2 is one carbon with two oxygens. CH4 is one carbon with four hydrogens, which is methane.

This is the language chemists actually use, and learning to read it is a little like learning to read music. It looks like code until, quite suddenly, it does not — and then it is faster and clearer than words.

The two numbers on the table

Every box in the periodic table carries the element's symbol and two numbers. They are easy to mix up, so it is worth pinning them down.

  • ZThe atomic number is the number of protons. It is the smaller number, and it is the one that decides which element you have.
  • AThe mass number is the number of protons plus neutrons — everything in the nucleus. It is the larger number. Electrons are so light that they are ignored here.

Because the mass number counts protons and neutrons together, and the atomic number counts the protons alone, subtracting one from the other leaves the neutrons. That gives a rule you can use on any element.

Worked example 1 — finding the neutrons

Question: a sodium atom has atomic number 11 and mass number 23. How many protons, neutrons and electrons does it have?

Step 1 — protons. The atomic number is the proton count. So there are 11 protons.

Step 2 — neutrons. Take the protons away from the mass number:
neutrons = mass number − atomic number
neutrons = 23 − 11 = 12 neutrons

Step 3 — electrons. The atom is neutral overall, so electrons must balance protons exactly. So there are 11 electrons.

Check: 11 protons + 12 neutrons = 23, which is the mass number we started with. The answer is consistent.

Try the same three steps on chlorine, which has atomic number 17 and mass number 35. Protons: 17. Neutrons: 35 − 17 = 18. Electrons: 17. The method does not change from element to element.

Relative atomic mass

Atoms are far too light to weigh in grams in any useful way, so chemists compare them with each other instead. One carbon atom of mass number 12 is used as the standard and given a mass of exactly 12. Everything else is measured against it. That comparison figure is the relative atomic mass, and because it is a comparison it has no units.

On this scale hydrogen is about 1, carbon is 12, oxygen is 16, sodium is about 23, and iron is about 56. So an oxygen atom is about sixteen times the mass of a hydrogen atom.

Worked example 2 — comparing two elements

Question: how many times heavier is one iron atom than one carbon atom?

Step 1 — look up both. Relative atomic mass of iron is about 56. Relative atomic mass of carbon is 12.

Step 2 — divide the larger by the smaller.
56 ÷ 12 = 4.67 (to two decimal places)

Answer: an iron atom is roughly 4.7 times heavier than a carbon atom.

Notice that no grams appear anywhere. That is the point of a relative scale — you are only ever comparing atoms with one another, so the units cancel out and vanish.

You may notice that relative atomic masses on a real table are often not whole numbers. Chlorine is usually given as about 35.5, which looks odd, since no atom can contain half a neutron. The explanation is the next idea.

Isotopes

The proton count is fixed for an element. The neutron count is not. Atoms of the same element can carry different numbers of neutrons, and these versions are called isotopes.

They are still the same element, because the protons decide that, and they behave the same way chemically, because reactions involve electrons. They simply have different masses.

Most carbon in the world is carbon-12: 6 protons and 6 neutrons. A small amount is carbon-14: 6 protons and 8 neutrons. The number after the name is simply the mass number.

Carbon-14 has a useful peculiarity. It is unstable and breaks down very slowly at a known rate. Living things take in carbon while alive and stop when they die, so measuring how much carbon-14 is left in old wood or bone tells you roughly how long ago it died. That is carbon dating, and it comes straight out of two extra neutrons.

Isotopes also explain chlorine's odd 35.5. Chlorine comes as a mixture of chlorine-35 and chlorine-37, with roughly three atoms of the lighter kind for every one of the heavier. The relative atomic mass is the average across that mixture, and an average of whole numbers need not be a whole number itself. No single chlorine atom weighs 35.5 — the figure describes the population, not the individual.

4

Why atoms react

Electrons are not scattered randomly. They sit at particular distances from the nucleus, in layers usually called shells. The shell nearest the nucleus fills first, then the next one out, and so on.

For the first twenty elements a simple pattern works well:

  • 1The first shell holds up to 2 electrons.
  • 2The second shell holds up to 8.
  • 3The third shell, for these light elements, also takes 8 before the next one starts.

So the pattern is 2, 8, 8. Chemists write this as a short list separated by commas, called the electron configuration. Sodium has 11 electrons, so sodium is 2,8,1 — 2 in the first shell, 8 in the second, and 1 left over in the third. Reading it is simple: each number is one shell, counting outwards, and the last number is the one that matters, because it is the outer shell.

A few more, so the notation becomes familiar. Helium is 2. Carbon, with 6 electrons, is 2,4. Oxygen, with 8, is 2,6. Neon, with 10, is 2,8 and is full. Chlorine, with 17, is 2,8,7. Calcium, with 20, is 2,8,8,2. Add the numbers up and you always get back the total electron count, which is a quick way to check you have not slipped.

Where the pattern comes from, and why it breaks

Be honest about the 2, 8, 8 rule. It is a simplified picture. It works nicely for the first twenty elements and then gets steadily more complicated, especially once you reach the metals in the middle of the table. Now that you can read a configuration, it is possible to show why it breaks, which is more satisfying than being told to take it on trust.

Shells are not single undivided layers. Each shell is made of smaller compartments called subshells, and they come in types with letter names. Two of them matter here:

  • sAn s subshell holds up to 2 electrons.
  • pA p subshell holds up to 6 electrons.

The notation looks alarming and is not. In 1s2, the 1 is which shell, the s is which type of subshell, and the raised 2 is how many electrons are sitting in it. That is all three pieces of information in three characters.

So the first shell contains only an s subshell: 2 electrons, which is where the first 2 comes from. The second shell contains an s and a p: 2 + 6 = 8, which is where the first 8 comes from. The famous numbers were never arbitrary.

Written out in full, hydrogen is 1s1. Helium is 1s2, and that first shell is now full. Carbon is 1s2 2s2 2p2. Neon is 1s2 2s2 2p6, completely full, which is exactly why neon is a noble gas that refuses to react. Sodium is 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s1 — and that lonely 3s1 at the end is the single outer electron that makes sodium so violently reactive.

Here is the crack in the simple rule. The third shell also has a d subshell, which holds up to 10. If shells simply filled in order, the third shell would take 2 + 6 + 10 = 18 before the fourth began. But the 3d subshell sits at a slightly higher energy than the 4s one, so the fourth shell starts filling before the third has finished. That overlap is what produces the block of transition metals in the middle of the table, and it is the reason the tidy 2, 8, 8 story stops working at exactly that point.

What to take from this

You do not need to memorise subshells at your level. What is worth having is the shape of the idea: the 2 and the 8 are not magic numbers somebody chose, they are what you get when you add up the compartments — and the point where the pattern breaks is not a flaw in the rule, it is a real feature of atoms that produces a whole region of the periodic table.

The outer shell is what matters

Only the electrons in the outermost shell take part in reactions. The inner ones are held too tightly to be involved.

An atom whose outer shell is completely full is contented. It has no reason to swap electrons with anybody, so it takes part in almost no reactions. The elements built this way are the noble gases, and their unreactiveness is the point of them.

An atom with an outer shell that is nearly empty — one electron rattling around out there, like sodium — can reach a full arrangement by getting rid of it. An atom with a nearly full outer shell, like chlorine with one gap, can reach a full arrangement by taking one. Both are highly reactive, for opposite reasons.

One idea explains an enormous amount

Nearly everything in the rest of this chapter follows from a single fact: atoms behave according to how close their outer shell is to being full. Why some elements explode in water and others sit in the ground unchanged for millions of years comes down to that.

5

How atoms join: the two kinds of bond

Atoms want full outer shells. There are exactly two ways to arrange that with a neighbour: give and take, or share. Those two routes are the two kinds of chemical bond, and between them they account for almost every substance there is.

Drawing electrons: dots and crosses

To keep track of whose electrons are whose, chemists draw the outer electrons of one atom as dots and the outer electrons of the other as crosses. A dot and a cross are both just electrons — identical in reality — but marking them differently lets you follow where each one went. That is a dot-and-cross diagram.

Giving and taking: the ionic bond

Go back to sodium and chlorine. Sodium is 2,8,1 with one spare outer electron. Chlorine is 2,8,7 with one gap. The obvious solution is a transfer.

Sodium hands its outer electron to chlorine. Now look at what each has become. Sodium has lost its third shell entirely, so its full second shell is now the outer one: it is 2,8, which is full. Chlorine has gained an electron and become 2,8,8, which is also full. Both are satisfied.

But the charges no longer balance. Sodium still has 11 protons and now only 10 electrons, so it has one more positive than negative and carries a charge of 1+. We write it Na+. Chlorine has 17 protons and now 18 electrons, giving it a charge of 1−, written Cl−.

A charged atom like this is called an ion. And here is what holds the compound together: Na+ is positive, Cl− is negative, and opposite charges attract. That electrical pull between oppositely charged ions is the ionic bond. The product is NaCl, sodium chloride, ordinary salt.

Ionic bonding happens between a metal and a non-metal, because metals have few outer electrons to give away and non-metals have nearly full shells wanting to be topped up. Millions of these ions stack into a regular three-dimensional grid, which is why salt forms hard crystals with flat faces and a high melting point — pulling that grid apart takes a great deal of heat.

Sharing: the covalent bond

Two non-metals cannot do this. Both want to gain electrons, and neither will give any up, so a transfer is impossible. They compromise: they share.

Take two hydrogen atoms. Each has a single electron, and each needs 2 to fill its first shell. Neither can reach 2 alone. So they overlap and hold one pair of electrons between them, and each atom counts both electrons of that pair as its own. Both reach 2. Nothing was given away, so no ions form and nothing is charged. The shared pair is a covalent bond, and the result is H2.

Water works the same way. Oxygen is 2,6 and needs 2 more to reach 8, so it forms one shared pair with each of two hydrogen atoms — which is precisely why the formula is H2O rather than HO or H3O. The formula is not an arbitrary label. It is a direct consequence of how many electrons each atom needs.

Oxygen in the air is O2, where two oxygen atoms share two pairs, a double bond. In CO2, carbon is 2,4 and needs 4 more, so it forms two double bonds, one to each oxygen.

Telling the two apart

Metal + non-metal usually means electrons are transferred, ions form, and the bond is ionic. These compounds tend to be hard crystals with high melting points, and many dissolve in water and conduct electricity when they do, because the ions are free to move.

Non-metal + non-metal means electrons are shared, no ions form, and the bond is covalent. These are often gases or liquids at room temperature, like O2, CO2 and H2O, and they generally do not conduct electricity.

Both routes are the same goal reached two different ways: every atom ending up with a full outer shell.

6

The table that ordered everything

By the middle of the 1800s chemists knew of about sixty elements. They had a growing pile of facts and no good way to organise them. Several people noticed that if you listed the elements in order of weight, similar ones kept turning up at regular intervals — and several people tried to build something from that observation.

Johann Döbereiner spotted triads: groups of three similar elements where the middle one's weight sat roughly halfway between the other two. John Newlands, in England, proposed his law of octaves around 1865, pointing out that every eighth element seemed to resemble the first. He was laughed at. One critic asked whether he had tried arranging the elements alphabetically instead. Newlands was closer to the truth than his audience was.

In 1869, the Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev published the arrangement that worked. He was not alone in this — the German chemist Julius Lothar Meyer was working independently and produced a very similar table at around the same time. Both deserve credit. But Mendeleev did one thing that Meyer did not, and that one thing is why we know his name.

He left gaps

Mendeleev laid the elements out in order of increasing atomic weight and started new rows whenever the properties began repeating. In several places, the next element in weight order simply did not fit the pattern — it belonged further along.

Most people would have shuffled things until the list looked tidy. Mendeleev instead left an empty square and said, in effect: there is an element here that nobody has found yet. Then he went further. Using the neighbours above, below and either side, he predicted what the missing element would be like — its weight, its density, its colour, how it would behave.

Then the missing elements turned up.

  • 1Gallium, found by Lecoq de Boisbaudran in 1875, matched the gap Mendeleev had labelled beneath aluminium. Its density even had to be re-measured after Mendeleev insisted the first figure looked wrong — and Mendeleev turned out to be right.
  • 2Scandium, found by Lars Fredrik Nilson in 1879.
  • 3Germanium, found by Clemens Winkler in 1886, matched its predicted properties strikingly closely.

That is what convinced everyone. A table that merely tidies up known facts is convenient. A table that describes something nobody has ever seen, and is then proved correct, is something else. Newlands, incidentally, was eventually given a medal for the idea he had been mocked for.

How to read the table

The layout carries meaning, so it is worth knowing the two words.

  • ↔A period is a row, read left to right. Going along a row, each element has one more proton than the last, and the outer shell fills up step by step.
  • ↕A group is a column. Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell, and that is why they behave alike. A group is a family.

Modern tables are ordered by atomic number rather than by weight, which fixed the few places where Mendeleev's weight ordering put elements slightly out of position. He had put them in the right places anyway, going by their properties, before anyone knew why he was allowed to.

7

Families of elements

Each column is a family whose members resemble one another. Four families are worth meeting.

Group 1 — the alkali metals

Lithium, sodium, potassium and the ones below them. Each has a single electron in its outer shell, desperate to be got rid of, and that makes them the most reactive metals there are.

They do not behave the way you expect metals to behave. They are soft enough to cut with a knife, like firm cheese, showing a bright shiny surface that dulls within seconds as it reacts with the air. Dropped into water they fizz, skate across the surface and produce hydrogen gas. Lithium fizzes. Sodium may melt into a ball and catch fire. Potassium ignites with a lilac flame almost at once. They are stored under oil to keep air and water away from them.

Going down the group, reactivity increases. Potassium reacts more fiercely than sodium, which reacts more fiercely than lithium. The reason is distance: further down the group the outer electron sits further from the nucleus and is held less tightly, so it is given up more readily.

A trend that does not behave

Not every property changes tidily down a group, and it is worth knowing one case where the obvious guess is wrong. Density does not increase smoothly down Group 1. Sodium is about 0.97 g/cm³ but potassium is about 0.86 — less dense than the element above it. Lithium, sodium and potassium are all less dense than water, which is why they float on it while reacting.

Reactivity has a clean trend. Density does not. When you meet a group trend, check whether it actually holds rather than assuming everything must run the same way.

Group 17 — the halogens

Fluorine, chlorine, bromine and iodine. Each has seven electrons in its outer shell — one short of full — so each is hunting for a single electron. They are the mirror image of Group 1, and they are just as reactive.

They are unusual among elements for being strongly coloured: chlorine a pale green gas, bromine a dark red-brown liquid, iodine a shiny dark solid that gives off a violet vapour. They are all poisonous, and dangerous to handle. Very dilute chlorine is used to disinfect swimming pools and drinking water precisely because it is so reactive that it kills microorganisms.

Going down the group, reactivity decreases. This is the opposite direction from Group 1, and for a matching reason. A halogen is trying to attract an incoming electron rather than give one away, and the nearer the outer shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the pull. So the most reactive halogen is the one at the top: fluorine, which is one of the most aggressively reactive substances known.

Group 18 — the noble gases

Helium, neon, argon, krypton and xenon. Their outer shells are already full, so they have nothing to gain by reacting and they very nearly never do. For a long time chemists thought they could not react at all; a handful of compounds of the heavier ones are now known, but they take real effort to make.

Their whole usefulness is this refusal. Argon fills light bulbs and shields welding, because it will not attack hot metal the way oxygen would. Helium fills balloons and airships, because it is light and — unlike hydrogen — will not burn. Neon glows in tubes without being consumed. When you want something that will not react with anything, you buy an element that cannot be bothered.

The transition metals

The wide block in the middle: iron, copper, zinc, nickel, silver, gold, chromium and many more. These are the metals of ordinary life and of industry.

They are typically hard, strong, dense, and they melt at high temperatures — iron melts at over 1500 °C. They form coloured compounds, which is where a great deal of the colour in minerals, pottery glazes and gemstones comes from: copper compounds are often blue or green, iron compounds brown, orange or yellow. Many are excellent catalysts, meaning they speed up a chemical reaction while remaining unchanged themselves.

Careful with "metals are hard"

Textbooks often list "hard" as a property of metals. Treat that as a rough generalisation, not a rule. Most transition metals really are hard. But sodium and potassium are metals too, and you can cut them with a table knife. Mercury is a metal that is liquid at room temperature. Lead is soft enough to mark paper.

Good general properties of metals — shiny when freshly cut, good conductors of heat and electricity, able to be bent and drawn out rather than shattering — hold up far better than "hard" does. When a rule has famous exceptions, learn the exceptions with it.

8

Which metals win

Metals differ enormously in how eagerly they react. Potassium attacks water on contact. Gold can sit in a river bed for a million years and come out gleaming. Put every metal in order of eagerness and you have the reactivity series.

Displacement

The series does more than rank things. It predicts reactions.

A more reactive metal will push a less reactive metal out of its compound. This is called displacement, and the picture is like a queue where somebody with more right to a place takes it.

Drop an iron nail into blue copper sulphate solution and the nail comes out coated with a pinkish-brown layer of copper, while the solution slowly fades. Iron is above copper in the series, so iron takes the place in the compound and the copper is turned out as pure metal. Try it the other way — a strip of copper in an iron solution — and nothing happens at all. Copper is below iron and cannot displace it.

Once you know the order, you can predict which pairs will react and which will do nothing. That is a genuinely useful thing for a list to be able to do.

Why gold is found as gold

Now the reason the series matters outside a laboratory.

Reactive metals do not survive as metals. Left in the ground with air, water and other chemicals around them, they react and stay reacted, locked up in compounds called ores. Iron is found as iron oxide. Aluminium is found in bauxite. To get the metal out you have to force the reaction backwards, which takes energy — and the more reactive the metal, the more energy it takes.

Gold sits at the bottom of the series and reacts with almost nothing. So gold in the ground stays gold. You can pick it up out of a stream and it is already the finished metal. Silver and copper are nearly as unwilling, which is why they are also sometimes found pure.

This shaped human history. Gold, silver and copper were used thousands of years ago, because they were lying about ready to use. Iron came much later — it needed a furnace hot enough to prise the oxygen off. Aluminium is one of the commonest elements in the Earth's crust, yet it was not produced cheaply until the late 1800s, and for a while it was more valuable than gold. The order of the reactivity series is very nearly the order in which people learned to use metals.

Rusting and tarnishing

Rusting is iron reacting with oxygen and water to form hydrated iron oxide. Iron is reactive enough for this to happen slowly at ordinary temperatures, and rust flakes off rather than sealing the surface, so fresh iron underneath is exposed and the process continues until the object is eaten through.

Gold does not rust because gold does not react with oxygen. Gold jewellery from ancient tombs comes out looking as it did the day it was buried. Silver does tarnish, but not with oxygen — the dark film is silver reacting with tiny traces of sulphur compounds in the air, and it can be polished off.

Aluminium looks like an exception and is not. It is fairly high in the series and reacts with air quickly — but the oxide it forms is tough and sticks tightly to the surface as a thin invisible layer, sealing the metal underneath. Aluminium protects itself. Iron cannot, because rust does not stick.

One list, many answers

Why does the ship rust and the ring not. Why is aluminium foil safe for food. Why did the Bronze Age come before the Iron Age. Why does a nail turn copper-coloured in blue solution. All four are answered by the same vertical list — which is a good sign that the list is describing something real.

9

Questions to think about

These have no marks attached and no single correct wording. Try answering out loud, in full sentences, before opening anything.

1

Sodium is a metal that catches fire in water. Chlorine is a poisonous green gas. Joined together they make ordinary table salt, which you eat. How can that possibly be?

Show answer

Because a compound is a genuinely new substance, not a blend of two old ones. Sodium's dangerous behaviour comes from its single loose outer electron, which it will give to almost anything. Chlorine's comes from its one missing electron, which it will take from almost anything.

When they meet, the trade happens. Sodium hands its electron over. Now both have full outer shells, both are settled, and neither has any reason to attack anything else. The properties that made them dangerous were exactly the properties they used up in reacting with each other.

The general lesson is that you cannot predict a compound's properties by looking at its ingredients. That is why a compound is different in kind from a mixture, where the ingredients keep behaving as themselves.

2

Reactivity increases going DOWN Group 1 but increases going UP Group 17. Why do the two families run in opposite directions?

Show answer

Both trends come from the same fact — the outer electrons are further from the nucleus lower down a group, so the nucleus holds them less strongly — but the two families want opposite things.

A Group 1 metal is trying to lose an electron. A weaker grip makes losing it easier, so the lower ones react more. Potassium beats sodium beats lithium.

A halogen is trying to gain an electron. Now a weak grip is a disadvantage, because the atom has to pull the newcomer in. The higher ones grip better and so react more. Fluorine beats chlorine beats bromine beats iodine.

Same cause, opposite direction, because the two groups have opposite goals.

3

Mendeleev left blank squares in his table instead of closing up the gaps. Why did that turn out to be more impressive than filling the table neatly would have been?

Show answer

Because it made a claim that could have been proved wrong. A tidy table with no gaps only describes what is already known, and nothing can ever contradict it. Mendeleev's gaps said something specific about the world: an element exists, nobody has found it, and here is roughly what it will be like.

That is a risk. If gallium had been found and looked nothing like the prediction, the arrangement would have been in serious trouble. Instead gallium in 1875 and germanium in 1886 came out close to what he had described.

An idea earns trust by surviving a test it could have failed. This is also why Newlands was mocked and Mendeleev was believed — Mendeleev's version put itself in danger and came through.

4

Reactivity increases going down Group 1, but density does not follow a simple pattern down the group. What should you do when a trend you have been taught turns out to have exceptions?

Show answer

First, get the fact right. Sodium is about 0.97 g/cm³ and potassium about 0.86, so potassium is less dense than the element above it. Anyone who assumes every property must rise steadily down a group will get this one wrong.

Second, notice why the two behave differently. Reactivity in Group 1 depends on one thing — how tightly the outer electron is held — and that changes steadily. Density depends on both how heavy the atoms are and how closely they pack, and those two do not have to change in step. A property with one cause behaves tidily. A property with several causes need not.

The habit worth building: treat a trend as a summary of measurements, not as a law the elements are obliged to obey. When someone states a trend, it is fair to ask whether it holds all the way.

5

Aluminium is high in the reactivity series and reacts with air readily, yet aluminium foil sits in a kitchen drawer for years and aluminium window frames last for decades. Explain.

Show answer

It does react. It reacts almost immediately, which is the point. Aluminium meeting air forms a thin layer of aluminium oxide, and that layer is hard, sticks tightly to the metal, and completely covers it. Air and water can no longer reach the aluminium underneath, so the reaction stops.

Iron cannot do this. Rust is flaky and loose, so it falls away and leaves fresh iron exposed, and the rusting continues inwards until the object is destroyed.

So the difference is not how reactive the two metals are. It is what their oxide is like. A protective oxide layer is why the reactivity series alone does not always tell you what will survive in the real world.

Why this is worth knowing

Chapter 14 tells you that matter is made of particles, and that is true and useful. This chapter tells you what those particles are made of and why they behave as they do. The whole of the rest of chemistry — why salt dissolves, why iron rusts, why a candle burns, why one gas is safe in a balloon and another is not — comes out of a small number of facts about protons and outer electrons. Very few ideas explain so much from so little.

The other half of the chapter is about the periodic table, and that is a story about what a good idea can do. Mendeleev was not the only person arranging elements in 1869, and he was not the first to notice the repeating pattern. What he did was trust the pattern more than he trusted the list. Where the elements he knew about did not fit, he left holes and said something was missing. It took six years for gallium to prove him right, and seventeen for germanium to do it again.

That is worth carrying with you. The best scientific ideas do not just tidy up what is known. They stick their necks out and say what has not been found yet — and then somebody goes and looks.

None of this is examined at your level. It is here so that when you do meet it properly, you meet it a second time.
