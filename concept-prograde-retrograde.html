Concept

Prograde and Retrograde Motion

Prograde means moving the usual way for the Solar System — the direction almost everything orbits and spins — and retrograde means going the opposite way, whether that is a real backwards motion or only an appearance from Earth.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Imagine standing high above the Sun's north pole, looking down on the whole Solar System like a plate on a table. From that viewpoint, all eight planets travel round the Sun anticlockwise. Most of them also spin on their own axes anticlockwise. This shared direction is called prograde, sometimes written direct motion.

Retrograde means the reverse: clockwise from that same viewpoint. The word comes from Latin roots meaning to step backwards. Both words need a reference direction to mean anything, which is why we fix that overhead viewpoint first.

Here is the important complication, and the reason this page exists. The word retrograde is used for two quite different things. One is real backwards motion — an object genuinely travelling or turning the opposite way. The other is apparent retrograde motion, where a planet only seems to move backwards across our night sky while doing nothing unusual at all. Papers set traps by blurring the two, so keep them firmly apart.

2

How it works

The shared prograde direction is not a coincidence. The Solar System formed from a huge cloud of gas and dust that was slowly turning. As gravity pulled the cloud inwards it collapsed into a flat, spinning disc, rather as a lump of pizza dough spreads into a flat circle when spun. The Sun formed at the centre and the planets formed from the disc, inheriting the turning direction the cloud already had. That is why the orbits are prograde, why they nearly all lie in the same flat plane, and why most spins are prograde too.

Two planets break the spin rule, and both breaks are real. Venus rotates retrograde: it spins backwards compared with its orbit, so on Venus the Sun would rise in the west. It also turns extremely slowly, once in about 243 Earth days. Uranus is tipped over roughly 98 degrees, so its axis lies nearly in the plane of its orbit and the planet effectively rolls along on its side. Because that tilt is past 90 degrees, its rotation counts as retrograde too. The likeliest explanation for both is a giant collision early in the Solar System's history, though we cannot be certain.

Notice what has not broken. Venus and Uranus spin the wrong way, but they still orbit prograde, like every other planet. Among the planets, retrograde applies to spin only.

Orbits do go retrograde elsewhere. Some comets — lumps of ice and dust on long stretched orbits — travel round the Sun the opposite way to the planets. Halley's Comet is the famous example: it orbits retrograde. Comets can do this because many of them come from far out, where the flat, orderly disc never imposed a single direction, and they arrive on steeply tilted paths pointing any which way. Some moons are retrograde too. Neptune's large moon Triton orbits backwards relative to Neptune's spin, which strongly suggests Triton was captured by Neptune's gravity rather than forming alongside it. Past competition papers, IMSO among them, have leaned on exactly this point: all planets orbit prograde, but some comets do not.

Now the second meaning. Apparent retrograde motion is what we see from Earth when Mars, over several weeks, appears to slow, stop, loop backwards against the fixed stars, stop again and resume. Mars does nothing strange. The loop is caused by us overtaking it. Earth is closer to the Sun than Mars, so by Kepler's third law Earth completes its orbit faster — one year against nearly two. As Earth catches Mars up and sweeps past on the inside, Mars appears to slide backwards, just as a car you overtake on a motorway seems to drift rearwards against the hills behind it, though it is still driving forwards. Once you are well past, it appears to move forwards again. Every outer planet does this whenever Earth passes it, which is why the loop is completely predictable.

The heart of it

Prograde is the shared anticlockwise direction the Solar System inherited from the spinning cloud it formed from; retrograde is the opposite, and it may be a real reversal, as with Venus's spin or a comet's orbit, or only an appearance caused by Earth overtaking an outer planet.

3

Where the idea came from

Apparent retrograde motion was noticed long before anyone could explain it. Babylonian astronomers were recording the backwards loops of the planets more than two thousand years ago, and the puzzle drove much of ancient astronomy. If the Earth stands still at the centre and everything circles it steadily, why should Mars occasionally reverse?

Around the second century, working in Alexandria, Claudius Ptolemy produced the answer that held for over a thousand years. He proposed that each planet moves on a small circle, called an epicycle, whose centre itself moves round a larger circle about the Earth. Loop the small circle at the right moment and the planet swings backwards for a while. This was wrong about how the Solar System is built, but it was serious science: it made predictions, and the predictions largely worked.

In 1543 Nicolaus Copernicus showed that if the Sun is at the centre and Earth is simply another planet, the loops need no epicycles at all. They fall out automatically from the faster inner planet overtaking the slower outer one. That the awkward feature vanished on its own was one of the strongest arguments for the new picture. Kepler, publishing in 1609 and 1619, then replaced Copernicus's circles with ellipses and made the predictions genuinely accurate.

The real retrograde spins came much later, since they need close measurement. Venus's backwards rotation was only pinned down in the 1960s, using radar bounced off the planet through its permanent cloud cover, which no telescope can see through.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is treating apparent retrograde motion as real. When Mars loops backwards in our sky, Mars is not reversing. Nothing about its orbit changes. The whole effect comes from Earth's own movement past it. If you could hover above the Sun and watch, you would see two planets both going steadily anticlockwise and no loop at all.

The second is thinking a retrograde spin means a retrograde orbit. Venus and Uranus spin retrograde; both orbit prograde. Always check which motion the question is about.

The third is a distractor about planet types: the idea that rocky planets go one way and gas giants the other. There is no such split. All eight orbit prograde, and the two odd spins are one rocky planet and one gas giant, which is precisely the wrong pairing for that story.

Commonly missed

Prograde means orbiting in the same direction as most Solar System bodies. Which statement is true? (a) The rocky planets orbit prograde and the gas giants retrograde. (b) All the planets orbit prograde, but some comets orbit retrograde. (c) No object in the Solar System orbits retrograde.

The trap is the neat rocky-versus-giant division in (a). Splitting the planets into two groups is a habit from other topics, so the pattern feels familiar and correct.

How to work it: Take the planets first. Every one of the eight orbits prograde, inherited from the spinning disc they formed in, so (a) is out. Then ask whether anything at all goes the other way. Halley's Comet does, and so does Neptune's moon Triton, so (c) is out. That leaves (b), which is exactly right: the planets are uniform, the smaller bodies are not.
