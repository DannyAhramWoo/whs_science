Science Stories

Matter and Life

Every fact in your notes was once an argument. Somebody had to notice something odd, guess at a reason, and then find out whether the guess survived. These are the stories behind eleven chapters — including the parts where the scientists were confidently, usefully wrong.

Chapter 10

The man who measured the Earth with two sticks

Around 240 BC, Eratosthenes was in charge of the great library at Alexandria in Egypt. He read a report about a well in the town of Syene, several hundred kilometres to the south. At noon on the longest day of the year, the Sun shone straight down the well shaft and lit the water at the bottom. A vertical stick there cast no shadow at all.

In Alexandria on that same day, a vertical stick did cast a shadow. That was the whole clue. If the Earth were flat, the Sun's rays would strike both towns at the same angle and both sticks would behave the same way. A shadow in one place and no shadow in the other meant the ground was curved.

Eratosthenes measured the Alexandria shadow and worked out that the Sun was about 7 degrees off vertical there. Seven degrees is roughly one fiftieth of a full circle. So the distance from Alexandria to Syene had to be about one fiftieth of the way round the Earth. He had that distance — it was a known trade route, paced out by professional surveyors — and he multiplied it by fifty.

His answer for the circumference of the Earth was within a few per cent of the modern figure, though nobody is quite sure how close, because historians still argue about the exact length of the unit he used. His equipment was a stick, a shadow, and geometry. No telescope, no satellite, no travel beyond a library.

What this tells us

A shadow is evidence about the shape of the Earth and the direction of sunlight, which is why shadow questions turn up so often in this topic.

Chapter 11

The wrong answer almost everybody gives

In 1987 a film crew stopped graduates leaving a university ceremony in the United States and asked them a simple question: why is it hotter in summer? Most of them, in gowns, holding new degrees, said the same thing. The Earth must be closer to the Sun in summer.

It is a reasonable guess. Move nearer a fire and you feel warmer. The trouble is that it makes a prediction, and the prediction fails immediately. If distance were the cause, the whole planet would be warm at once. Instead, when it is July in London it is winter in Sydney and Buenos Aires. The two hemispheres have opposite seasons at the same moment, and no distance explanation can produce that.

There is a second problem. The Earth's orbit is very slightly oval, and it is actually nearest the Sun in early January — the middle of the northern winter. The distance changes by only about three per cent, far too little to matter.

The real answer is tilt. The Earth's axis leans over by about 23.5 degrees and keeps pointing the same way all year. For half the year the northern half leans towards the Sun, so sunlight arrives steeply, concentrated on a smaller patch of ground, and the days are longer. Six months later the same half leans away, the light comes in at a slant and spreads thin, and the days are short.

The tilt explanation was understood by astronomers centuries ago. It still has not fully reached the rest of us, which is worth remembering: an idea being correct does not mean it has won.

What this tells us

Seasons come from tilt, not distance, and the fastest way to test any seasons explanation is to ask what it predicts for the other hemisphere.

Chapter 12

Reading the inside of the Earth from a wiggly line

The deepest hole ever drilled reached about 12 kilometres. The centre of the Earth is over 6,000 kilometres down. Nobody has been anywhere near it, and nobody ever will. So how does anyone claim to know it is there?

Earthquakes do the digging for us. A large earthquake sends waves right through the planet, and those waves change speed and bend when they pass from one material into another — the same way light bends going into water. Some kinds of wave stop dead at liquid. Seismographs all over the world record what arrives and when, and from thousands of those records the layers can be mapped.

In 1936 the Danish seismologist Inge Lehmann noticed something that did not fit. There was supposed to be a shadow zone on the far side of the Earth from a large quake, a region where certain waves should not arrive at all because the liquid outer core had swallowed them. But faint signals were turning up inside that zone. Weak, early, and definitely there.

Lehmann worked out that the waves were bouncing off something further in — a hard boundary inside the liquid core. She proposed that the centre of the Earth holds a solid inner core, a ball of iron kept solid by immense pressure despite being extremely hot. Her paper had the memorably short title P'. Other seismologists tested the idea against their own records over the following years, and it held.

She was reading a room she could never enter, from echoes.

What this tells us

You can find out what something is made of without opening it, by studying how waves travelling through it are bent, slowed or reflected.

Chapter 13

The hole the computer deleted

Joe Farman spent years running a small British research station at Halley Bay in Antarctica, pointing an instrument at the sky to measure ozone. It was unglamorous work and his funding was repeatedly at risk. In the early 1980s his readings started dropping. By 1984 the springtime ozone above the station had fallen by about a third.

Farman assumed his ageing instrument was broken. He had a new one shipped down. It gave the same answer. In 1985 his team published the result, and it was almost too strange to believe.

The strangest part came next. A satellite had been watching the whole atmosphere for years and should have spotted this long before three people with a ground instrument. It had. But the processing software had been written with a rule that threw out any reading below a certain value, on the sensible assumption that such a number could only be a fault. The satellite had been recording the ozone hole and quietly binning it. When the team went back to the raw data, the hole was there in the archive.

The cause was traced to CFCs, chemicals used in fridges, aerosols and foam. In 1987 nations signed the Montreal Protocol and began phasing them out — remarkably fast for an international agreement. The ozone layer is now slowly recovering, and current estimates suggest the Antarctic hole may largely close around the middle of this century. It is one of the few environmental problems humans have genuinely started to fix.

What this tells us

Human activity can damage a natural cycle on a global scale, and deciding in advance which measurements count as impossible can hide the very thing you are looking for.

Chapter 14

The jiggle that proved atoms are real

In 1827 the Scottish botanist Robert Brown put grains from pollen into water and looked at them under his microscope. Tiny particles released by the grains were twitching. Not drifting in a current, not settling — jittering about in random directions, never stopping, changing course constantly.

Brown's first thought was that he was watching life. Pollen comes from living plants, and this looked like something swimming. So he tested it properly. He tried particles ground from rock, from glass, from a fragment of the Sphinx. Everything sufficiently small did the same dance. Whatever caused it had nothing to do with being alive. Brown reported the effect honestly and admitted he could not explain it, and for nearly eighty years nobody could.

At that time many respected scientists did not believe atoms existed. They were treated as a useful bookkeeping device for chemistry rather than as real objects.

In 1905 Albert Einstein, then a clerk in a patent office, published a mathematical account of the motion. If water were made of countless molecules in constant motion, a particle small enough would be struck from all sides — and by chance the blows would not balance at every instant. The particle would be shoved this way, then that. Einstein predicted exactly how far such a particle should wander in a given time. Jean Perrin measured it a few years later and found the numbers matched.

The argument was effectively over. Matter is made of particles, and they never stop moving.

What this tells us

The particles that make up matter are always in motion, and that motion is visible in its effects long before anyone can see the particles themselves.

Chapter 15

Weigh everything, including the air

Before the 1770s, burning was explained by phlogiston. The idea was that flammable things contained an invisible substance which escaped as they burned. Wood was rich in phlogiston; ash was wood with the phlogiston gone. It fitted the obvious fact that burnt things weigh less than they started.

It did not fit metals. Heat tin or lead in air and the powdery residue weighs more than the metal did. Defenders of phlogiston suggested that phlogiston might have negative weight, which should have been a warning sign.

Antoine Lavoisier, working in Paris, made one methodical change: he sealed his experiments in closed glass vessels and weighed the entire vessel, contents and air included, before and after. In one famous run he heated tin in a sealed flask. The tin gained weight, the sealed flask as a whole did not change at all. When he opened it, air rushed in. The metal had taken something out of the air inside — a gas he went on to name oxygen.

Nothing had been lost. Nothing had been created. The atoms had only been rearranged into different substances, and the total mass stayed the same. Careful weighing, not clever theorising, was the move that settled it.

Lavoisier was executed during the French Revolution in 1794, at the age of fifty, for reasons that had nothing to do with chemistry.

What this tells us

In any chemical change the total mass is conserved — if something seems to vanish, you have not yet weighed all of it.

Chapter 16

Two beautiful experiments and two wrong conclusions

Some time in the early 1600s, Jan Baptist van Helmont in Brussels planted a small willow sapling in a pot. He weighed the tree, weighed the dried soil, and then did nothing for about five years except add rainwater and keep dust out with a cover.

When he weighed it again, the tree had gained roughly 74 kilograms. The soil had lost only a few grams. He concluded that the entire tree had been made from water. Given his evidence it was a fair conclusion, and it was wrong. He had no way to know that the invisible air around the pot was contributing the carbon, which is most of the tree's dry mass. His experiment was excellent; his interpretation was limited by what could be weighed.

About 150 years later Joseph Priestley sealed a burning candle in a glass jar and watched it go out. The air inside was "injured" — a mouse placed in it died. Then he put a sprig of mint in a similar jar and left it for several days. Afterwards a candle burned in that air again, and a mouse could survive in it. Something living had repaired the air.

Priestley did not know about oxygen as we describe it, and his experiments were frustratingly hard to repeat. Sometimes the mint did not work, most likely because he had left it in the dark. It was Jan Ingenhousz who later showed that the effect needs sunlight and comes from the green parts of the plant.

Between them, two partly mistaken men established that plants take something from the air and give something back.

What this tells us

Plants build themselves largely from a gas in the air, and photosynthesis and respiration are opposite exchanges, which is why a plant can restore air that a flame has used up.

Chapter 17

The sum that showed blood goes round

For roughly 1,400 years, European doctors followed Galen. Blood, he taught, was manufactured continuously in the liver from food, travelled outwards through the vessels, and was consumed by the body at the far end. It was a one-way delivery, made fresh and used up.

William Harvey, physician to the king in London, did something Galen's followers had not. He estimated. He worked out roughly how much blood the heart pushes out with each beat, multiplied by the number of beats in an hour, and looked at the total. It came to far more than the entire body contains — hundreds of times a person's weight in blood every day.

No liver could make that. The only way the numbers work is if the same blood comes back and is used again. Blood must travel in a loop.

He backed the arithmetic with experiments anyone could repeat. Tie a bandage round an upper arm at the right tightness and the veins below swell up. Press a finger along a swollen vein and try to push the blood the wrong way: it will not go, because the valves inside veins only open one way. All the valves point back towards the heart. Harvey published in 1628 in a thin book called De Motu Cordis.

One gap remained that he could not close. He could not see how blood crossed from arteries to veins, only insist that it must. Marcello Malpighi found the capillaries with a microscope in 1661, four years after Harvey died.

What this tells us

Blood circulates in a closed loop driven by the heart, and an estimate of the right quantity can overturn an idea that has stood for centuries.

Chapter 18

Giving every living thing two names

In the early 1700s a naturalist wanting to write about the common tomato had to call it Solanum caule inermi herbaceo, foliis pinnatis incisis, racemis simplicibus. That is not a name. It is a short description in Latin, and it had to grow longer every time somebody discovered a similar plant that the old description no longer distinguished. Names in one country did not match names in another. Two naturalists could describe the same beetle and never realise it.

Carl Linnaeus, a Swedish botanist with an enormous appetite for order, cut the problem in half. Give each species exactly two words: the genus it belongs to, then one word for the species itself. The tomato became Solanum lycopersicum. Humans became Homo sapiens. Short, fixed, and the same everywhere.

He set this out fully in the tenth edition of Systema Naturae in 1758, and he applied it relentlessly, naming thousands of plants and animals and sending his students around the world to collect more. Several of them died on those journeys.

Plenty of his groupings were wrong. He sorted plants mainly by counting stamens, which sometimes lumped together species that are barely related, and his writings on human races reflected the prejudices of his time and were used to justify worse ones. Modern classification, built on evolutionary relationships and DNA, has rearranged much of his work.

The naming system survived all of it. A biologist in Singapore and a biologist in Peru can write Homo sapiens and mean precisely the same thing.

What this tells us

Classification exists so that everyone is talking about the same organism, and a shared naming system is a tool for communication as much as a scientific claim.

Chapter 19

Wolves, willows and a river that moved

The last wolves in Yellowstone National Park were killed in the 1920s, when predators were treated as vermin. Over the following seventy years the elk population grew, and grazed. Young willows and aspens along the streams were eaten before they could grow tall. The banks lost their cover.

In 1995 and 1996, wolves from Canada were released back into the park. Biologists watched what followed. Elk numbers fell, and elk behaviour changed — they stopped lingering in open valley bottoms where escape is difficult. In places, willows and aspens began growing tall again. Beavers, which need willow, increased from a single colony to many. Beaver dams held back water and created ponds. Songbirds returned to the taller trees. Some researchers reported that stretches of riverbank became more stable and the channels narrowed.

This became famous as a "trophic cascade" — one change at the top rippling all the way down to the shape of a river. It makes a wonderful story, and versions of it circulate online with the wolves given rather more credit than the evidence supports.

Ecologists still argue about it, and the argument is the interesting part. Those same decades brought several years of drought, a warming climate, a growing bison population that also eats willow, changes in beaver numbers arriving from elsewhere, and human hunting outside the park boundary. Some studies find willow recovery only where beaver dams already kept the ground wet. Untangling one cause from a dozen tangled ones, in a real ecosystem nobody can run twice, is genuinely hard.

What this tells us

Removing or returning one species can change an entire ecosystem, but in the real world causes overlap, so a neat story deserves a careful check.

Chapter 20

The map that beat the theory

In August 1854, cholera broke out in Soho in central London. Within about ten days more than 500 people in a few streets were dead. The accepted explanation was miasma: disease came from bad air rising out of filth, which is why it struck poor and crowded districts. It was a respectable theory that fitted much of the evidence.

John Snow, a physician, thought cholera was carried in water and swallowed. To test it he walked the neighbourhood and marked every death on a street map as a small bar. The bars piled up around one spot: the public water pump on Broad Street. He also chased the exceptions, which is the part that matters most. Workers at a nearby brewery, who drank beer rather than pump water, were largely unaffected. A widow in Hampstead, miles away, died — and it turned out she liked the taste of Broad Street water and had it delivered by cart.

On 8 September he persuaded the parish authorities to remove the pump handle. Cases fell away afterwards.

It would be tidier to say the handle stopped the outbreak. Snow himself did not claim that. Deaths were already declining, partly because terrified residents had fled the district. The pump was almost certainly contaminated by a leaking cesspit next to it, but nobody could yet see the bacterium responsible, and officials remained unconvinced for years. Snow died in 1858 without seeing his idea accepted. It won slowly, on the strength of the evidence rather than a single dramatic moment.

What this tells us

Good scientific work means gathering evidence, testing the exceptions, and being honest that a result which fits your idea is not the same as proof of it.

Why these stories matter

None of the people here started with the answer. Van Helmont grew a tree for five years and drew the wrong conclusion. Brown thought his jiggling particles might be alive. A generation of chemists defended a substance with negative weight. Snow had a correct theory and could not persuade anyone with it. Science is not a list of facts handed down by people who simply knew; it is what is left after a great many careful, argumentative, frequently mistaken people checked each other's work.

What separates them from being merely wrong is that they were wrong in a way that could be tested. Eratosthenes' shadow, Lavoisier's sealed flask, Harvey's arithmetic and Farman's second instrument all had the same quality: they could have come out the other way. Being carefully wrong leaves something behind for the next person to correct. That is how the right answer eventually gets found, and it is why nobody should feel embarrassed about a hypothesis that fails.
