Concept

Life Cycles and Parasites

A parasite is an organism that lives on or inside another living thing and harms it, and many parasites need to pass through two or more different species to complete their life cycle.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Most feeding relationships are quick. A bird eats a caterpillar and the caterpillar is gone. A parasite feeds differently: it settles on or inside another organism and takes from it slowly, over weeks or years, usually without killing it outright.

The organism lived on is the host. A parasite living on the outside, such as a flea, tick or leech, is an external parasite. One living inside, such as a tapeworm in the gut, is an internal parasite.

The definition has two halves and both matter. The parasite must benefit and the host must be harmed. Harm need not mean death; a slow drain of food, energy or blood counts. Compare two neighbouring relationships. In mutualism both partners gain, as when bacteria in a cow's stomach digest grass the cow cannot manage alone. In commensalism one gains and the other is unaffected. Only parasitism has a loser.

A parasite that kills its host quickly usually does badly, losing its home and food before it has reproduced. So many are restrained — not from kindness, but because the less damaging ones left more offspring.

Two more words are needed for what follows. A life cycle is the full sequence of stages an organism passes through from being produced to producing offspring of its own. And a vector is an animal that carries a parasite from one host to another, whether or not it is harmed itself.

2

How it works

The idea examiners return to is the multi-host life cycle: a parasite that cannot finish growing up inside one species alone. Malaria is the clearest worked example.

Malaria is caused by a single-celled organism called Plasmodium, a protist rather than a bacterium or virus. A female mosquito of the genus Anopheles bites a person and injects saliva containing the parasite. The parasites travel to the liver, multiply, then move into red blood cells, multiply again and burst out in waves, which produces the recurring fevers. Some become a sexual stage circulating in the blood. If a second mosquito bites that person it takes up those stages, and inside its gut the parasite reproduces sexually, then moves to the salivary glands ready for the next person bitten.

Notice what that means. Part of the development happens only in the human and part only in the mosquito, so both species are required. The species in which the parasite reproduces sexually is the definitive host — the mosquito. A host used for an earlier stage is an intermediate host, here the human. The mosquito is also the vector, carrying the parasite between people. Only females bite, needing blood to make eggs; males feed on nectar.

The practical consequence is the whole point. A chain running through two species can be broken at either link. Removing standing water where larvae develop, nets over beds and screened windows all cut bites without treating a single patient, which is why mosquito control has been central to malaria work for over a century. Malaria is treatable and largely preventable, and uncommon in most of the world, though serious where mosquitoes are widespread. Traps use the same biology, releasing carbon dioxide, because mosquitoes find warm-blooded animals partly by detecting it in exhaled breath. A simpler cycle makes the pattern easier to hold. A tapeworm lives as an adult in the gut of a person or dog, gripping with hooks and suckers and absorbing digested food through its skin, having no gut of its own. Its eggs leave in the host's droppings. If a cow or pig swallows them with grass, the young worms burrow into muscle and form cysts, making the cow the intermediate host. If that meat is eaten raw or undercooked, the cyst becomes an adult worm and the cycle closes. Thorough cooking, or clean disposal of waste, breaks it. Similar logic gives some diseases a reservoir host: a species in which the organism survives quietly between outbreaks in people.

Life cycles matter for non-parasites too. In complete metamorphosis there are four stages: egg, larva, pupa, adult. A butterfly is the standard case — egg, caterpillar, chrysalis, butterfly — and beetles, flies, bees and mosquitoes do the same. The larva looks nothing like the adult, and inside the pupa the body is rebuilt. In incomplete metamorphosis there are three stages: egg, nymph, adult, with no pupa. The nymph looks like a small adult without working wings and grows by moulting. Grasshoppers, cockroaches and dragonflies develop this way.

There is a reason complete metamorphosis is so common. Larva and adult have different bodies, so they live in different places and eat different food. A caterpillar chews leaves; the butterfly drinks nectar. So the young do not compete with their own parents for the same meal, and far more survive on the same patch of ground.

The heart of it

A parasite that needs two hosts has a chain that can be broken at either link, which is why controlling mosquitoes controls malaria without touching the parasite directly.

3

Where the idea came from

The name "malaria" records the wrong answer. It comes from Italian words meaning bad air, because the disease was common near marshes and the marsh smell was blamed. That belief lasted well over a thousand years, and it was not stupid: the marsh really was the source, and avoiding marshes really did help. The connection was correct and the cause was not.

In 1880 a French army doctor in Algeria, Alphonse Laveran, examined the blood of malaria patients under a microscope and saw parasites moving inside the red blood cells. Many colleagues disbelieved him at first, since the accepted view was that a bacterium would be found. He had shown what the disease was, but not how it travelled.

Patrick Manson, a Scottish doctor who had worked in China, had shown in 1877 that mosquitoes could carry the worm causing elephantiasis. He argued that mosquitoes might carry malaria too. Ronald Ross, a British doctor in India, took up the problem and dissected mosquitoes for years, at first the wrong kinds, which is why he found nothing for so long. In August 1897 he found the parasite in the stomach wall of a mosquito that had fed on a malaria patient, and the next year traced the full cycle in birds. Italian researchers, chiefly Giovanni Battista Grassi, worked out the human cycle at about the same time, and there was a bitter argument over credit. Ross received the Nobel Prize in 1902.

Insect metamorphosis has no single discoverer, but Jan Swammerdam in the Netherlands, dissecting insects in the 1660s and 1670s, showed that the caterpillar, pupa and butterfly are one animal changing, not three separate creatures or a death and rebirth as had been widely believed.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is saying that mosquitoes cause malaria. They do not. Plasmodium causes malaria; the mosquito is the vector that carries it. The distinction matters, because a mosquito that has never fed on an infected person carries nothing.

The second is assuming that a parasite always kills its host, or that it is simply a predator that is bad at its job. A predator kills and eats; a parasite feeds slowly and usually keeps its host alive, because a living host is a working home.

The third is muddling the two metamorphosis patterns, usually by putting a pupa where there is none. The test is a single question: is there a resting stage in which the body is rebuilt? Butterfly, beetle, fly, bee and mosquito, yes, so complete. Grasshopper, cockroach, dragonfly, no, so incomplete, and the young stage is a nymph rather than a larva.

Commonly missed

A diagram shows a parasite passing between a mosquito and a human. Which one statement about it is false?

The trap is that every option sounds plausible alone, and a reader who has grasped the general shape of the cycle will nod along to all of them. Sound is not the test; the arrows are.

How to work it: Do not judge the statements first. Trace the diagram once, naming each arrow: mosquito bites human, parasite enters blood, develops in human, second mosquito takes it up, develops in mosquito, mosquito bites again. Then match each statement to an arrow. The usual false ones say the parasite passes directly from person to person, that the mosquito carries it unchanged, or that only one species is needed. All three contradict the arrows, which show development in both hosts and no route between people without a mosquito. Answer against the diagram, not your memory.

5

Connected ideas

Parasitism is one of the ways species interact in an ecosystem, sitting alongside predation and competition, and a parasite population rises and falls with its host's. Many parasites are microorganisms, and the long-running struggle between parasite and host is one of the strongest engines of natural selection.
