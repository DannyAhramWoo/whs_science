Concept

Elements, Compounds and Mixtures

An element is one kind of atom, a compound is different atoms chemically joined in fixed proportions, and a mixture is substances sitting together without joining at all.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Think of a box of building bricks. A box holding nothing but identical red bricks is like an element. A red brick and a blue brick clicked together into a shape that stays clicked is like a compound. Loose red and blue bricks tipped in together, not joined, are like a mixture. What differs is whether they are joined and whether they are all the same kind.

An atom is the smallest particle of a substance that still counts as that substance. An element is a substance made of only one kind of atom, and there is no chemical way to break it down into anything simpler. Iron, oxygen, carbon, gold, chlorine and hydrogen are all elements. There are just over one hundred of them, and they are the list from which everything else is built.

A compound is two or more different kinds of atom chemically joined together in a fixed ratio. Joined is the important word. Water, H2O, is two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom, always in that ratio, never two-and-a-bit. Because the atoms have genuinely combined, a compound has new properties, not the average of its parts. Sodium is a soft metal that reacts violently with water; chlorine is a choking green gas. Joined, as sodium chloride, NaCl, they are the harmless white crystals you eat on chips.

A mixture is two or more substances present together but not chemically joined. Nothing has combined, so each part keeps its own properties, the proportions can be anything you like, and the parts can be separated again by physical means such as filtering, evaporating or using a magnet.

2

How it works

Questions on this topic are nearly always sorting tasks: here is a list of substances, put each into the right column. The question looks like recall and is actually reasoning, so it pays to have one reliable test.

The discriminator is this: can it be separated without a chemical reaction? If yes, it is a mixture. If no, it is a single substance, and then you ask a second question — is it made of one kind of atom, or more than one? One kind means element; more than one means compound.

Now work through the cases that catch people out.

Chlorine gas, Cl2, and hydrogen gas, H2, are elements, even though each of their particles contains two atoms. This is the case that decides marks. The rule was never one atom per particle; it was one kind of atom. Both atoms in Cl2 are chlorine atoms, so chlorine is one kind of atom and chlorine is an element. Compare it to carbon dioxide, CO2, which also has three atoms in a particle, but they are two different kinds — carbon and oxygen — so CO2 is a compound.

Water, H2O, is a compound. Hydrogen and oxygen are joined, the ratio is fixed at two to one, and you cannot boil, filter or magnet them apart. Splitting water needs electricity to force a chemical reaction, which is exactly the sign of a compound.

Ink is a mixture. It is dyes dissolved in a solvent, in whatever proportion the manufacturer chose, and chromatography pulls the dyes apart on a strip of paper with no chemical reaction at all. A soft drink is a mixture too: water, sugar, colouring, flavouring and dissolved carbon dioxide, in no fixed ratio, and you can drive the gas out by warming it and get the sugar back by evaporating the water.

Air is a mixture, roughly 78 per cent nitrogen, 21 per cent oxygen and the rest argon, carbon dioxide and water vapour. Two clues prove it. The proportions vary — air over a city differs from air over a forest — and air can be separated by cooling it until it liquefies and then boiling the parts off at their different boiling points, which is a physical process. Salt water is a mixture: you choose how salty to make it, and evaporation gives the salt back unchanged. Brass is a mixture as well, a solid one, made of copper and zinc melted together in a proportion the metalworker picks to suit the job. A mixture of metals has its own name, an alloy, but it is still a mixture.

One warning about the word pure. In everyday speech pure orange juice means nothing added, but orange juice is a mixture of dozens of substances. In science, pure means a single substance — one element or one compound — with nothing else in it. Distilled water is pure in the scientific sense; bottled mineral water is not, because minerals are dissolved in it. Past papers have used tick-table sorting of exactly this sort, for example in IMSO 2009 theory papers.

The heart of it

Ask whether it can be separated without a chemical reaction: if yes it is a mixture, and if no it is an element when all its atoms are one kind and a compound when they are not.

3

Where the idea came from

For two thousand years the going theory was wrong. Greek thinkers, following Empedocles and then Aristotle, held that everything was made from four elements: earth, water, air and fire. It was a reasonable guess from ordinary observation — burn wood and you see flame, smoke and moisture come out — but not one of the four is an element in the modern sense.

The modern meaning arrived with Antoine Lavoisier in France. In 1789 he published a textbook listing substances he could not break down any further, and defined an element as exactly that: a substance which experiment has so far failed to decompose. It was an honest definition, because it admitted the list might change with better methods. Some entries are still elements today; others were not, and he included light and caloric, a supposed fluid of heat, both later dropped. The definition was the breakthrough, because it made elements a question for the laboratory rather than for philosophy.

The fixed-ratio part of a compound came from Joseph Proust, working in Spain around 1799. Weighing his products carefully, he found that a given compound always contained the same elements in the same proportions by mass, no matter how it had been made or where the sample came from. This is called the law of definite proportions. Another French chemist, Claude Berthollet, disagreed for years, arguing proportions could vary, and much of his evidence came from substances we would now call mixtures or solutions. The argument was settled in Proust's favour, and that fixed ratio is precisely what separates a compound from a mixture.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is counting atoms instead of kinds of atom, which turns chlorine and hydrogen and oxygen gas into compounds. Count the different kinds only.

The second is judging by appearance. A mixture does not have to look mixed. Salt water is completely clear and uniform, brass is a single solid lump, and air is invisible, yet all three are mixtures. Uniform appearance tells you the parts are evenly spread, not that they are joined.

The third is assuming anything with a chemical formula is a compound. A formula such as O2 or Cl2 simply says how many atoms travel together in one particle, and if those atoms are all the same kind the substance is still an element.

Commonly missed

Sort these into element, compound or mixture: chlorine gas Cl2, carbon dioxide CO2, ink, and sea water.

It is a trap because Cl2 has two atoms in its particle and looks like a compound, while ink looks like a single liquid substance and looks like it should not be a mixture.

How to work it: Take them one at a time with the separation test. Chlorine, Cl2 — both atoms are chlorine, one kind only, and no reaction can break it into anything simpler, so it is an element. Carbon dioxide, CO2 — two different kinds of atom, joined in a fixed one-to-two ratio, and separating them needs a chemical reaction, so it is a compound. Ink — dyes in a solvent, no fixed proportions, and chromatography separates them physically, so it is a mixture. Sea water — water with salts dissolved in it, saltiness varies from sea to sea, and evaporation recovers the salt unchanged, so it is a mixture.

5

Connected ideas

This idea is the foundation under every separation method, since only mixtures can be pulled apart physically, and chromatography is the clearest demonstration that an apparently single substance such as ink is really several. The particle model explains what joined actually means at the level of atoms, solutions are the most common kind of mixture, and the line between physical and chemical change is the same line drawn a different way.
