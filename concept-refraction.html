Concept

Refraction

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one transparent material into another, caused by light travelling at different speeds in different materials.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

Put a straw in a glass of water and look at it from the side. It appears to snap sideways at the water line, as though it were broken. The straw is fine. What has bent is the light coming from the underwater part of the straw, on its way out of the water and into the air towards your eye. Your brain, as always, assumes light travelled in a straight line, so it places the underwater part of the straw somewhere it is not.

The same effect makes a swimming pool look shallower than it is. Light from the bottom of the pool bends away as it leaves the water, and your brain traces it back to a point higher up. This is not a small effect and it matters: people have misjudged the depth of water they were about to step into.

Refraction means this change of direction at the boundary between two materials. It only happens at the boundary. Once the light is inside the water it goes straight again, at least until it reaches the next boundary. And it only happens if the light hits the boundary at an angle. Light arriving straight on, along the normal, slows down but does not change direction, because there is no sideways direction for it to turn towards.

2

How it works

The whole explanation rests on one fact: light travels at different speeds in different materials. It is fastest in a vacuum, very slightly slower in air, noticeably slower in water, and slower still in glass. In water light travels at about three quarters of its vacuum speed; in ordinary glass, about two thirds.

Now imagine the light as a wide beam rather than a thin ray, and picture the front edge of that beam arriving at the water surface at an angle. One side of the beam reaches the water before the other side does. The moment it enters, that side slows down while the other side is still moving quickly through air. The beam pivots. It is the same reason a trolley with one wheel on grass and one on tarmac swings round: unequal speeds across the width force a turn.

This tells you which way the bend goes, and the rule follows directly. Going from a fast material into a slow one — air into water — the light bends towards the normal. Going from slow into fast — water into air — it bends away from the normal. You never need to memorise this if you remember the trolley: the side that slows down first is the side the beam swings towards.

The relationship between the two angles is not a simple proportion, which is the part that took people a very long time to sort out. Doubling the angle of incidence does not double the angle of refraction. The correct rule involves a trigonometric function of the angles, which you will meet later, but you can see the consequence now: as the angle of incidence in the water gets larger, the refracted ray in the air bends further and further over until it is skimming along the surface. Push past that point and the light cannot escape at all — it reflects back into the water instead. This is total internal reflection, and it is why the underside of a water surface can look like a silver mirror when you are swimming underneath it, and how light is carried along a glass fibre for kilometres.

The heart of it

Light bends at a boundary because it changes speed, and it bends towards the normal when it slows down and away from the normal when it speeds up.

3

Where the idea came from

Refraction has an unusually long and frustrating history, and it is a good illustration of how hard it is to get from "roughly right" to "actually right". Around AD 150 the astronomer Ptolemy, working in Alexandria, did something genuinely modern: he set up an apparatus, passed light between air, water and glass at a series of angles, and wrote down the measurements in a table. From those numbers he produced a rule. The rule fitted his data reasonably well over the range he tested. It was also wrong — it failed badly at large angles, and it was not the true relationship at all.

The correct relationship was found by Ibn Sahl, a mathematician working in Baghdad around AD 984, and set out in a manuscript on burning mirrors and lenses. He had it right roughly six hundred years before anyone in Europe did. His work was then largely overlooked in the European tradition, and the same law was found again independently by Willebrord Snell in the Netherlands in 1621, and published by René Descartes a few years later. In English-speaking countries it is still usually called Snell's law.

Two things are worth taking from this. The first is that a rule which fits your measurements is not automatically the right rule — Ptolemy's table was real data honestly recorded, and it still led him astray, because he tested too narrow a range. The second is that scientific knowledge is not a tidy relay race. Ibn Sahl's result existed, in writing, and was simply not picked up by the people who later needed it. Ideas can be found, lost and found again.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is thinking light bends because it hits something, as if the surface were a wall. It bends because of a change in speed. That is why light entering glass along the normal does not bend at all, even though it definitely hits the glass and definitely slows down. No sideways difference, no turn.

The second mistake is remembering "towards the normal" as a fact about water rather than a fact about slowing down. Light leaving water for air bends the other way, and if the reader has memorised a single direction they will get every such question wrong.

Commonly missed

A ray of light travels through a rectangular glass block, entering one face at an angle and leaving through the opposite, parallel face. How does the direction of the ray leaving compare with the direction of the ray that went in?

The trap is expecting the two bends to add up. People often say the ray comes out bent even further over.

How to work it: On entering, the light slows and bends towards the normal. On leaving, it speeds up and bends away from the normal by the same amount, because the two faces are parallel so the two normals are parallel too. The bends are equal and opposite. The emerging ray is travelling in exactly the same direction as the original — but it has been shifted sideways, so it is displaced, not deflected.

5

Connected ideas

Refraction is the partner of reflection: at most boundaries some light does each. It is also the mechanism behind colour separation in a prism, because different colours slow by slightly different amounts and so bend by slightly different angles. And the whole idea depends on light being something that travels at a definite speed, which links it to how energy moves as radiation.

In one line

Light changes direction when it crosses into a new material because it changes speed there, bending towards the normal when it slows and away from the normal when it speeds up.
