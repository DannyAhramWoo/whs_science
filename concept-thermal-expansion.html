Concept

Thermal Expansion

Almost everything gets slightly bigger when heated, because its particles move faster and end up spaced further apart.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

A jam jar with a stuck metal lid will usually open if you run the lid under hot water for half a minute. Telephone wires droop lower on a hot afternoon than on a cold morning. A railway line laid without gaps will buckle in summer. These look like three unrelated nuisances, and they are all the same thing.

Thermal expansion means an object increasing in size when its temperature rises, and shrinking again when it cools. The change is small — a steel bar 1 metre long grows by about 0.012 mm for each degree Celsius — but across a long bridge or a hot day it adds up to centimetres, and centimetres of steel pushing against concrete will break the concrete.

Now the reason, and this is the part that must be right. Matter is made of tiny particles, always moving. Heating something means giving those particles more energy, so they vibrate faster and more widely about their positions. Vibrating more widely, each particle needs more room, so the average gap between neighbouring particles grows. Millions of slightly larger gaps add up to a slightly larger object.

Say the second half of that out loud, because it is the whole point: the particles do not get bigger. A particle of iron in a hot bar is exactly the same size as a particle of iron in a cold one. What changes is the space between them.

2

How it works

Different materials expand by different amounts for the same temperature rise, because their particles are held by bonds of different strength, and weak bonds let particles wander further. Most metals expand more than glass. Gases expand far more than liquids, and liquids more than solids, because their particles are less tightly held.

That difference is what unsticks the jam jar. The metal lid and the glass jar both get hot, so both expand — but the metal expands more than the glass for the same temperature rise. The lid therefore grows away from the glass, the grip loosens, and the lid turns. Notice that the wrong answer, "the metal expands and the glass does not", still gets the lid off in your imagination but is simply untrue, and comparison is what earns the mark.

Engineers design around expansion rather than fighting it. Bridges sit on rollers and carry toothed expansion joints in the road surface, so the deck can lengthen in summer without pushing its supports apart. Concrete pavements are laid in slabs with gaps between; without them the slabs press on each other on a hot day and crack or lift. Overhead cables are hung deliberately slack in summer so they do not snap taut in winter.

The effect is also useful. A bimetallic strip is two different metals, often brass and iron, bonded together along their length. Heat it and the brass expands more than the iron, but they cannot slide apart, so the strip is forced to curl with the brass on the outside of the curve. That curling can push a switch open. This is how a simple thermostat and an older fire alarm work: the strip bends at a set temperature and makes or breaks a circuit, so a mere change of length becomes a decision.

A liquid-in-glass thermometer is expansion turned into a measurement. A large bulb of liquid feeds a very narrow tube, so a tiny increase in the liquid's volume forces a long, easily read movement up the tube. Because the expansion is steady and proportional over the working range, the scale can be evenly divided, and a reading between two marks can be estimated in proportion. Past IMSO papers have used exactly that proportional reasoning.

Water breaks the rule below 4 degrees C. Cool water from 10 degrees C and it contracts as expected, but only down to about 4 degrees C. Below that it expands as it cools further, and it expands again on freezing. So ice is less dense than the water beneath it and floats, and a pond freezes from the top down, leaving liquid water below where fish survive the winter. It is also why a forgotten bottle bursts in the freezer.

The heart of it

Heating makes particles vibrate more and spread further apart, so the object grows — the particles themselves never change size.

3

Where the idea came from

Expansion was put to work long before anyone could explain it. Around 1592 Galileo Galilei in Padua built a thermoscope: a glass bulb with a long open tube standing in a dish of liquid. Warming the bulb made the air inside expand and push the liquid down the tube. It showed changes in warmth, but it had no fixed scale and was open to the air, so changes in atmospheric pressure moved the liquid too. It could not give a reading anyone else could reproduce.

Sealed liquid-in-glass instruments came next, from Ferdinand II of Tuscany's workshops in the 1650s, removing the pressure problem. What remained missing was an agreed scale. Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit, in Amsterdam, solved much of this by switching to mercury in 1714 — mercury expands very evenly and does not cling to glass — and by fixing his scale to reproducible points. Anders Celsius proposed his own scale in 1742 using the freezing and boiling points of water, though he originally set it upside down with 0 for boiling, and it was reversed after his death.

The particle explanation arrived much later. For most of the 1700s heat was thought to be an invisible weightless fluid called caloric that flowed into a body and swelled it. That picture accounted for expansion and for the flow of heat from hot to cold, but it was wrong, and only through the 1800s did heat come to be understood as the energy of particle motion.

4

What people get wrong

The dominant error, and the one examiners return to, is writing that the particles themselves expand. They do not. Heating changes how far and how fast particles vibrate, and therefore the spacing between them. Train yourself to write "the particles vibrate more and move further apart" every single time.

The second is forgetting that expansion is a comparison. Two materials in contact both expand; what matters is which expands more. Answers about jar lids, bimetallic strips and dental fillings all turn on that comparison.

The third is assuming a hole in a heated plate gets smaller as the metal closes in around it. It does not. The whole plate scales up, hole included, so the hole gets larger.

Commonly missed

A material used to fill a hole in a tooth must expand and contract by almost exactly the same amount as the tooth itself when the patient drinks something hot or cold. Explain why, in terms of particles.

The trap is that expansion is usually presented as a problem to leave room for, so pupils suggest leaving a gap around the filling — which in a tooth would be useless.

How to work it: Start with the mechanism. Heating either material makes its particles vibrate more widely, so the spacing between particles increases and the material grows; cooling reverses this. Now compare. If the filling expanded more than the tooth, a hot drink would make it press outwards on the surrounding tooth and could crack it. If it expanded less, it would shrink away from the walls, opening a gap where food and bacteria collect and where the filling can work loose. A tooth is heated and cooled many times a day, so any mismatch is repeated thousands of times. Matching the two rates keeps the filling sealed against the tooth at every temperature. The general rule: when two materials are bonded and cannot slide, unequal expansion creates stress.

5

Connected ideas

Thermal expansion is the particle model applied to heating, so it depends on knowing that heat is energy and temperature is a measure of particle motion. It differs between the states of matter because the particles are held with different strength, and it is closely related to change of state, where the spacing changes drastically rather than slightly. Because the spacing changes while the amount of matter does not, expansion also changes density.
