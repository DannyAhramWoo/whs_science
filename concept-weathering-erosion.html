Concept

Weathering and Erosion

Weathering is the breaking down of rock where it stands; erosion is the carrying away of the pieces.

Introduced in Chapter 12: The Planet Earth

1

What it means

These two words are constantly used as though they meant the same thing, and they do not. The difference is movement.

Weathering is the breakdown of rock in place. The rock is cracked, crumbled or chemically altered, but nothing is transported anywhere. A gravestone whose lettering has gone blurry has been weathered; the stone is still where it was put.

Erosion is the removal and transport of those loosened pieces by a moving agent — running water, wind, ice or gravity. The word comes from the Latin for gnawing away.

A worn old statue in a churchyard shows weathering. A cliff retreating as the sea carries its debris off along the coast shows both: weathering loosens, erosion takes away. Weathering usually has to come first, because most rock is too strong to be shifted until something has weakened it. A third word completes the set: deposition, which is what happens when the moving agent slows down and drops what it was carrying, forming beaches, sandbanks and river deltas.

2

How it works

Weathering comes in two main kinds. Physical weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces without changing what it is made of. The best-known example is freeze-thaw. Water seeps into a crack, and if the temperature falls below freezing it turns to ice. Water is unusual in expanding when it freezes — by about 9 per cent — because its molecules lock into an open crystal structure with gaps in it. That expansion inside a confined crack exerts a considerable force, widening it. Thaw, refill, refreeze, and eventually the rock splits. This is why mountain regions with temperatures crossing zero repeatedly have so much loose, angular rubble. Plant roots do similar work more slowly, and in deserts the daily heating and cooling of rock makes its surface layers flake off.

Chemical weathering changes the rock's actual composition. Rainwater is naturally slightly acidic, because carbon dioxide from the air dissolves in it to form weak carbonic acid. Limestone is made of calcium carbonate, which reacts with acid, so limestone slowly dissolves. Given long enough this hollows out caves, sinkholes and the deep grooves seen in limestone pavements. Iron-bearing minerals react with oxygen and water and rust, weakening the rock and turning it reddish. Chemical weathering works fastest where it is warm and wet, which is why tropical rock decays deeply while polar rock is mostly attacked physically.

Erosion then removes the debris. Rivers carry the most material overall, rolling large stones along the bed and holding fine silt in suspension; the faster the water flows, the larger the particle it can move, which is why a river in flood can shift boulders it would normally leave alone. Glaciers are the most powerful agent for their size — a river of ice hundreds of metres thick, dragging embedded rock across the bedrock like a giant file, which is what carves the U-shaped valleys of glaciated regions. Wind carries only fine particles but can travel enormous distances with them; dust from the Sahara regularly reaches the Caribbean. Waves attack coasts, and gravity alone brings down landslides.

Notice that weathering and erosion help each other. Breaking a rock into pieces greatly increases its total surface area, and chemical attack only happens on exposed surfaces — so the smaller the fragments, the faster the chemistry works. Meanwhile erosion strips away the loose debris and exposes fresh rock beneath to be weathered in turn.

The heart of it

Weathering breaks rock where it sits; erosion is what carries the broken pieces away.

3

Where the idea came from

The realisation that mountains are being worn away is not obvious. Landscape appears fixed within a human lifetime, and for a long time the dominant explanation for valleys, gorges and scattered boulders was catastrophe — a great flood or a series of them, acting suddenly.

The alternative view came from the same Scottish thinker who worked out the rock cycle. James Hutton, in the late 1700s, argued that ordinary everyday processes were sufficient. He watched soil washing off his own farmland into streams and reasoned that if this continued indefinitely, the continents would eventually be levelled — so there must be some process building land back up, and there must be a great deal more time available than anyone supposed. Hutton was also a farmer, and it is likely that watching his fields lose soil each year shaped how he thought.

Charles Lyell developed this into a general principle in Principles of Geology from 1830: that the same slow processes we can observe today, given sufficient time, account for the whole geological record. This view became known as uniformitarianism, as opposed to catastrophism. Lyell rather overstated it — we now know that genuine catastrophes, such as major asteroid impacts and vast glacial outburst floods, have also shaped the Earth. The modern picture is a mixture: mostly slow processes over immense time, punctuated by rare violent events.

Measurement came much later. Only in the twentieth century did geologists gauge erosion rates properly, by weighing the sediment carried by rivers and later by measuring isotopes produced in surface rock by cosmic rays. Typical rates for a mountain range are a few tenths of a millimetre a year — imperceptible, and yet enough to remove a kilometre of rock in a few million years.

4

What people get wrong

The main error is simply swapping the two words, or using "erosion" for the whole process. Fix it with the movement test: if nothing has been carried away, it is weathering. If material has moved, erosion is involved.

A subtler error is assuming freeze-thaw works because ice is heavy or hard. It works because water expands as it freezes, which is unusual — most substances contract on freezing. If water behaved like most liquids, freeze-thaw weathering would barely exist, and neither would the fact that ice floats.

Commonly missed

A stone wall in a cold mountain village crumbles over many winters, and the fallen fragments stay in a heap at its base. A pupil says this is erosion. Is that correct?

The trap is that something has visibly broken and moved, so "erosion" feels right. But you have to ask what moved it, and how far.

How to work it: Identify the process that broke the rock. Water entered cracks in the stone, froze, expanded by about 9 per cent and widened the cracks; repeated over many winters this split the stone. That is freeze-thaw, which is physical weathering. Then ask whether any agent has transported the pieces away — a river, wind, ice or waves. Nothing has: the fragments are lying at the foot of the wall where they fell, moved only by gravity over a metre or so. So the answer is that this is weathering, not erosion. Erosion would require the fragments to be carried off somewhere else.

5

Connected ideas

Weathering and erosion supply the sediment that becomes sedimentary rock, so they form the surface half of the rock cycle. Freeze-thaw depends on water's change of state, chemical weathering on substances dissolving, and the transport of debris on the moving water of the water cycle.
