Concept

Convection

Convection is the transfer of heat by the bulk movement of a heated fluid, which rises because warming makes it less dense.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Hold your hand beside a candle flame and you feel modest warmth. Hold it a little way above and it is uncomfortably hot. Something is going upwards, and it is not just heat spreading equally in all directions — it is warmed air physically rising and carrying its energy with it. That is convection.

The word fluid here means anything that can flow, so both liquids and gases. Convection cannot happen in a solid, because the particles in a solid are locked in position and cannot travel anywhere. This makes it the exact opposite case to conduction: in conduction the material stays put and only energy moves, while in convection the material itself moves and carries the energy along as cargo.

Convection also cannot happen in a vacuum, for the plain reason that there is no fluid there to move. Of the three ways heat travels, only radiation crosses empty space.

2

How it works

The chain of reasoning has four steps, and it is worth being able to recite all four rather than jumping to "hot air rises".

First, part of the fluid is heated, so its particles move faster. Second, faster particles push each other further apart, so that portion of fluid expands and occupies more space. Third, since it now has the same mass spread through a larger volume, its density has fallen — density being mass divided by volume. Fourth, a less dense region surrounded by denser fluid is pushed upwards by the greater upthrust from the fluid around it, in exactly the way a cork is pushed up through water.

That fourth step is the one usually skipped, and skipping it hides the real cause. Warm fluid does not rise because heat has some natural tendency to go up. It rises because the colder, denser fluid around it sinks under gravity and displaces it. Convection is driven by gravity acting on a density difference, which has a startling consequence: in the weightlessness of an orbiting spacecraft there is no convection at all. A candle there burns as a dim sphere and can smother itself in its own exhaust gases, because no fresh air rises to replace what it has used.

Once the warm fluid rises it moves away from the heat source, cools, becomes denser again, and sinks. Meanwhile more cold fluid arrives at the bottom to be heated. This circular flow is a convection current, and it keeps going as long as the heat source does.

Convection currents run at every scale. In a saucepan they stir the water so it heats evenly, which is why a kettle's element is at the bottom — put it at the top and the hot water would simply stay there. Sea breezes are convection: land warms faster than sea during the day, air above the land rises, and cooler air flows in from the sea to replace it. Winds and ocean currents are convection on a planetary scale. So are the slow currents in the Earth's mantle that shift continents over millions of years, and the churning outer layers of the Sun.

The heart of it

Convection moves heat by moving the fluid itself, and the fluid moves because heating lowers its density.

3

Where the idea came from

Like conduction, convection has no single discoverer. The behaviour was familiar long before anyone named it — smoke rising, chimneys drawing, warm air gathering at the top of a room. What the 1800s added was the recognition that this was a distinct mechanism of heat transfer rather than an incidental habit of hot things.

The word itself is comparatively young. It was coined by William Prout, an English physician and chemist, in the 1830s, in a treatise on chemistry and meteorology. Prout took it from the Latin convehere, meaning to carry together, and the etymology captures the physics precisely: the fluid carries the heat with it. Prout is better remembered for a hypothesis he got interestingly wrong — he proposed in 1815 that all atoms were built from hydrogen atoms, which was rejected as measured atomic weights failed to come out as whole numbers, and which turned out to be nearly right once protons and isotopes were understood a century later.

The three-way division of heat transfer into conduction, convection and radiation was assembled piece by piece across the century, with contributions from many people rather than a single decisive experiment. It appears as a settled framework in physics teaching only in the later 1800s. This is worth noticing: the neat three-part list in a textbook looks like a discovery, but it is really a classification that took decades to agree on, and the boundaries are not always as sharp in practice as the list suggests.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest slip is the phrase "heat rises". Heat does not rise. Heat travels from hotter to colder in whatever direction that lies — downwards, sideways, in every direction at once. What rises is warmed fluid, and only because it is less dense than its surroundings. Say "warm air rises" and the reasoning stays honest.

The second error is expecting convection where it cannot occur. There is none in a solid brick wall, none in a vacuum, and none in the middle of a well-sealed double-glazed window, where the whole point of the narrow gap is to leave the air too confined to circulate.

Commonly missed

Two identical rooms each have a single heater. In room A the heater is on the floor; in room B it is mounted near the ceiling. Both heaters have the same power. Which room warms up more evenly, and why?

The trap is thinking the ceiling heater must be better placed because heat "goes up" and would therefore fill the room from the top down.

How to work it: Trace the convection current in each case. In room A the heater warms the air at floor level, that air becomes less dense and rises, cool air at the top sinks to take its place and is warmed in turn — a full circulation forms and the whole room is stirred. In room B the warmed air is already at the top, and being less dense it has no reason to come down. It stays there, no current forms, and the room heats only slowly from the ceiling downward by conduction and radiation. Room A warms far more evenly, which is precisely why household radiators are fitted low on the wall and air conditioners high.

5

Connected ideas

Convection is one of the three routes for heat, sitting between conduction, which needs contact but no movement, and radiation, which needs nothing at all. It depends entirely on density differences, and it is the engine behind wind and much of our weather.
