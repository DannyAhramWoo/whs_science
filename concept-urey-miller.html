Concept

The Urey-Miller Experiment

The Urey-Miller experiment, run in 1953, sealed a guess at the early Earth's air and water into glass flasks, sparked it like lightning, and produced the building blocks of proteins in a week.

Introduced in The Origin of Life

1

What it means

Before 1953, whether life could begin from ordinary chemistry was something people argued about in words. Nobody had put the question in a flask. The experiment carried out by Stanley Miller, a graduate student at the University of Chicago, under his supervisor Harold Urey, was the first attempt to test it — famous not because it answered the question but because it showed the question could be asked in a laboratory at all.

Miller built a closed loop of glass. In the lower flask he put water and heated it, so steam rose into an upper flask. Into that upper flask he had sealed the gases he believed the early atmosphere contained: methane (the gas in natural gas), ammonia (the sharp-smelling gas in some cleaning fluids) and hydrogen. Two electrodes threw a continuous electric spark through the mixture, standing in for lightning. Beyond it a condenser cooled the gases so any dissolved products fell as liquid, ran into a trap and returned to the boiling flask. It was, in effect, a small ocean, a small sky, a small storm and a small rain cycle, running without stop.

Note what was deliberately left out: oxygen. Oxygen is highly reactive and would have destroyed delicate organic molecules as fast as they formed. The geological evidence indicates the early atmosphere had essentially none, and that free oxygen accumulated only much later, once photosynthesising life had been producing it for a very long time.

2

How it works

The spark does one job: supplying enough energy to break the very stable bonds in methane, ammonia, hydrogen and water, which are otherwise unreactive at ordinary temperatures. A spark tears them into fragments that will bond to almost anything they meet, including each other. Cool the mixture and the new, larger combinations survive, because they are more stable than the fragments were.

Miller ran it and watched. Within a day the water had turned pink; after a week it was deep red and cloudy, with a dark tarry film on the glass. When he analysed it, the water contained several amino acids — the molecules from which all proteins are built. Glycine, the simplest, was the most abundant, and he also identified alanine and others. Alongside them he found formaldehyde, hydrogen cyanide and urea. Those three are worth noticing: formaldehyde and hydrogen cyanide are the starting points from which chemists build sugars and the components of nucleic acids, so their presence hints at routes to more than proteins.

The reason this caused such a stir is the contrast between what went in and what came out: four dull gases, water and electricity produced, without any guidance, a substantial fraction of the molecules life is made of. The result was published in the journal Science in 1953, when Miller was twenty-three.

Now the honest limits, which matter more than the headline. The experiment did not create life. It created building blocks. The distance between a flask of amino acids and a living cell is enormous: the amino acids must be joined into long chains in a specific order, the chains must fold into working shapes, something must store the order and copy it, and the whole must be enclosed in a membrane. None of that happened.

There is a second limit. Scientists now think Miller's starting mixture was probably wrong. The evidence has shifted towards an early atmosphere dominated by carbon dioxide and nitrogen released by volcanoes, with much less methane and hydrogen than Urey had assumed. When the experiment is repeated with a carbon dioxide and nitrogen mixture, amino acids still form, but in far smaller quantities. So the specific conditions Miller modelled were probably not the conditions on the early Earth — which does not mean amino acids were not forming, only that they may have formed elsewhere or by other routes, perhaps near volcanoes, in vent systems, or on meteorites, where amino acids have since been found.

The heart of it

Miller showed that the building blocks of life form by themselves from simple gases and energy — which is a real and surprising result, and a long way short of making life.

3

Where the idea came from

The idea did not begin with Miller. Alexander Oparin and J. B. S. Haldane had proposed in the 1920s that an oxygen-free early atmosphere could have accumulated organic molecules in the sea. Harold Urey, who had won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1934 for discovering deuterium, had reasons from planetary science for thinking the early atmosphere was rich in hydrogen, and mentioned the possibility of testing it in a lecture. Miller, in the audience looking for a research project, asked to try.

Urey was reluctant, thinking it a risky use of a young researcher's time, and suggested a safer project first. Miller persisted, and Urey gave him a year. It worked in weeks. There is a well-documented detail worth knowing: when the paper was written, Urey asked for his own name to be left off it, so that a famous professor's name would not overshadow the student's. The paper was published under Miller's name alone, though the experiment is still usually called the Urey-Miller experiment.

There is a satisfying epilogue. Miller kept his sealed sample vials for the rest of his life. After his death in 2007, former students re-examined them in 2008 using instruments far more sensitive than anything available in 1953. They found over twenty amino acids, considerably more than the handful Miller had been able to detect. Samples from a variant apparatus he had built, which injected steam in a jet to imitate a volcanic eruption, were richer still. Miller's result had been correct and, if anything, understated.

4

What people get wrong

Two opposite mistakes are made about this experiment. The first is to say it showed how life began; it did not, it showed that some ingredients form readily. The second is to say that because the atmosphere assumption turned out to be wrong, the experiment was worthless. Its lasting importance is method rather than result: it converted a question people had only argued about into one that could be investigated with apparatus, and the research field that followed exists because of it.

Commonly missed

A student concludes: "Miller made amino acids from simple gases, so he proved life can arise from non-living chemicals." Evaluate this conclusion.

The trap is treating a building block as the thing built — like saying that making bricks proves you can build a cathedral.

How to work it: Separate what was observed from what was concluded. Observed: amino acids and other organic molecules formed in a sparked flask of methane, ammonia, hydrogen and water. Concluded: life can arise from non-living chemicals. The gap is large. Amino acids are components of proteins, but a living cell also needs those components joined in specific orders, a molecule that stores and copies those orders, and a membrane enclosing everything — none of which appeared. A second weakness: the result depends on a gas mixture that later evidence suggests was probably not the real early atmosphere. The defensible conclusion is narrower — that some building blocks of life can form from simple chemicals given energy, with no living thing involved.

5

Connected ideas

The experiment is one attempt at the wider origin-of-life problem, and it tackles only the building-block stage; the RNA world hypothesis addresses the copying stage and the cell membrane the container stage. It also depends entirely on an assumption about the early atmosphere, and it is a good illustration of how a hypothesis is tested and then revised.
