Concept

Predator and Prey

A predator hunts and eats other animals, and the prey is what it eats — but the interesting part is that their numbers control one another.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

A fox eating a rabbit is a predator eating its prey — so far, a fact about lunch. What makes it a scientific idea is that the relationship runs in both directions.

Obviously the fox affects the rabbits: it eats them, so there are fewer. Less obviously, and more importantly, the rabbits control the foxes. A fox with nothing to eat does not breed or survive the winter, so the rabbits set how many foxes the wood can hold. Each population limits the other.

A predator is not any meat-eater. A scavenger eats animals already dead; a parasite lives on or in another living thing, taking from it slowly without normally killing it. A predator kills what it eats. This matters, because a scavenger does not control its food's numbers the way a predator does.

2

How it works

The two populations do not settle at fixed numbers. They cycle, and the cycle comes out of a delay. Follow one full turn.

Start with plenty of rabbits and few foxes. Food is easy, so the foxes eat well, survive the winter and raise large litters — but their rise lags the rabbits', since a fox cannot turn a meal into extra foxes instantly, having to survive, breed and rear cubs first. That delay drives the cycle.

Now there are many foxes, eating rabbits faster than the rabbits can breed, so rabbit numbers fall, and keep falling because the foxes are already there and still hungry.

Then the surprising part. With rabbits scarce the foxes starve and their numbers fall — but after the rabbits did, because foxes take time to exhaust stored fat and to fail at breeding. With few foxes left, the surviving rabbits breed in safety and rise again, and it begins once more.

On a graph this gives two wavy lines with the same rhythm, the predator shifted right of the prey. That is the test when you are unsure which is which: the line peaking second is the predator, normally the lower one, because many rabbits keep one fox alive.

Real cycles are messier. Foxes eat other things when rabbits are scarce, softening the crash, and weather, disease and people interfere. A real graph is ragged, and we cannot be sure a single dip came from predators rather than a bad winter.

The heart of it

Predator and prey numbers cycle because neither responds instantly to the other, and the predator peak always trails the prey peak.

3

Where the idea came from

The lag was worked out on paper before anyone watched it properly in the wild. In the 1920s two people arrived at the same mathematics independently: Alfred Lotka and Vito Volterra. Each wrote equations for two species, one eating the other, and each found they never settled to steady numbers but produced cycles, lagged between the predator and prey peaks.

That was striking because it was not what most expected. The natural guess is that predator and prey would find a balance and stay there. The equations said no: the delay built into breeding makes the system overshoot in both directions for ever, like a swing that keeps going.

Volterra came from an odd direction, asked about a puzzle in Adriatic fish catches during the First World War. Fishing had been reduced during the fighting, and afterwards the proportions of predatory fish and their prey were not what anyone predicted. His equations explained how less fishing on both could shift the balance between them.

It is worth being honest about how much the equations assume: that the predator eats only that prey, that the prey has unlimited food, that the environment never changes, that nothing else interferes. None holds in a real wood, so they describe no particular forest. They show what the interaction alone would produce, which is what makes them useful — when a real population differs, another cause is at work.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is reading the graph the wrong way round, calling the first peak the predator. Think about cause: predators can only increase after there is more food, so their peak must come second. Cause precedes effect, and the extra prey is the cause.

The second mistake is believing the predator drives its prey to extinction. Usually it cannot: as prey become scarce they are harder to find, so predators catch fewer and starve before the prey run out, and the predator's decline is what saves them. Extinctions happen, but normally when something else is also wrong, such as habitat loss or an introduced predator with other food to fall back on.

Commonly missed

A graph shows two populations in a wood over 30 years. Line A peaks in years 4, 14 and 24. Line B peaks in years 6, 16 and 26, and is always smaller. Which is the predator, and what would happen to line A if line B were removed?

The trap is the second half. Many answer that line A would increase for ever, which ignores everything else in the wood.

How to work it: Line B is the predator on two agreeing grounds: it peaks two years after line A, and its numbers are lower, fitting the energy loss between levels. Remove line B and line A would rise sharply, because nothing is eating it, but not for ever. It would rise until it hit another limiting factor — food, space or disease — probably overshooting what the wood can feed and crashing, and it might overgraze the plants, lowering what the wood supports afterwards.

5

Connected ideas

Predator and prey are the top links of a food chain, and predators are fewer because of the energy lost at each step. Their rise and fall is a population question, and the arms race between them — better hunting against better escaping — is a strong engine of adaptation.
