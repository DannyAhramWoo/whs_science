Concept

Predator and Prey

A predator is an animal that hunts and eats other animals, and the prey is what it eats — but the interesting part is that their numbers control each other.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

A fox eating a rabbit is a predator eating its prey. So far this is just a fact about lunch. What makes it a proper scientific idea is that the relationship runs in both directions.

It is obvious that the fox affects the rabbits: it eats them, so there are fewer. It is less obvious, and more important, that the rabbits control the foxes. A fox with nothing to eat does not breed and does not survive the winter. The number of rabbits sets how many foxes the wood can hold. Each population is a limiting factor on the other.

A predator is not the same as any meat-eater. A scavenger eats animals that are already dead and does no hunting. A parasite lives on or in another living thing and takes from it slowly without normally killing it. A predator kills what it eats, and it does the killing. Keeping those apart matters, because a scavenger does not control its food's numbers the way a predator does.

2

How it works

The two populations do not settle at fixed numbers. They cycle — rising and falling over and over — and the cycle comes out of a delay. Follow it round one full turn.

Start with plenty of rabbits and few foxes. Food is easy, so the foxes eat well, survive the winter, and raise large litters. Fox numbers rise. But they do not rise at the same moment the rabbits do. A fox cannot turn a good meal into extra foxes instantly; it has to survive, breed, and rear cubs. So the fox rise comes after the rabbit rise. That delay is the whole engine of the cycle.

Now there are many foxes. They eat rabbits faster than the rabbits can breed, so rabbit numbers fall. Again there is a delay: the rabbits keep falling for a while because the foxes are already there and still hungry.

Next comes the part people find surprising. With rabbits scarce, the foxes begin to starve, and fox numbers fall — but they fall after the rabbits did, because the foxes take time to run out of stored fat and to fail at breeding. Finally, with few foxes left, the surviving rabbits breed in safety and their numbers rise again, and the whole thing begins once more.

So on a graph you get two wavy lines with the same rhythm, but the predator line shifted to the right of the prey line. The predator peak always comes after the prey peak. If you are ever unsure which line is which, that is the test: the line that peaks second is the predator, and the predator line is normally the lower one, because it takes many rabbits to keep one fox alive.

Real cycles are messier than this. Foxes eat other things when rabbits are scarce, which softens the crash. Weather, disease and human activity interfere. So a real graph is ragged rather than smooth, and we cannot be sure that any single dip was caused by predators rather than by a bad winter.

The heart of it

Predator and prey numbers cycle because neither can respond instantly to the other, and the predator peak always trails behind the prey peak.

3

Where the idea came from

The lag was worked out on paper before anyone had watched it properly in the wild. In the 1920s two people arrived at the same mathematics independently: Alfred Lotka and Vito Volterra. Each wrote equations for two species, one eating the other, and each found that the equations did not settle down to steady numbers. They produced cycles, with a lag between the predator peak and the prey peak.

That was the striking result, because it was not what most people expected. The natural guess is that a predator and its prey would find a balance and stay there. The equations said no: the delay built into breeding makes the system overshoot in both directions for ever, like a swing that keeps going.

Volterra came to the problem from an odd direction. He was asked about a puzzle in fish catches in the Adriatic Sea during the First World War. Fishing had been reduced during the fighting, and afterwards the proportions of predatory fish and their prey in the catches were not what anyone had predicted. His equations offered an explanation of how reducing the fishing on both kinds could shift the balance between them.

It is worth being honest about how much the equations assume. They imagine that the predator eats only that one prey, that the prey has unlimited food, that the environment never changes and that nothing else interferes. None of those is true of a real wood. So the equations should not be read as a description of any particular forest. They are better understood as showing what the interaction on its own would produce, which is exactly what makes them useful: when a real population does something different, you know some other cause is at work.

4

What people get wrong

The most common mistake is reading the graph the wrong way round and calling the first peak the predator. Think about the cause. The predators can only increase after there is more food, so their peak has to come second. Cause comes before effect, and here the extra prey is the cause.

The second mistake is believing the predator drives its prey to extinction. Usually it cannot. As prey become scarce they are harder to find, so the predators catch fewer and start to starve before the prey run out entirely. The predator's own decline is what saves the prey. Extinctions do happen, but normally when something else is also going wrong, such as loss of habitat or a predator brought in from elsewhere that has other food to fall back on.

Commonly missed

A graph shows two populations in a wood over 30 years. Line A peaks in year 4, year 14 and year 24. Line B peaks in year 6, year 16 and year 26. Line B is always the smaller number. Which is the predator, and what would happen to line A if line B were removed completely?

The trap is the second half. Many people answer that line A would simply increase for ever, which ignores everything else in the wood.

How to work it: Line B is the predator, on two grounds that agree: it peaks two years after line A, and its numbers are lower, which fits the energy loss between levels. If line B were removed, line A would first rise sharply, because nothing is eating it. But it would not rise for ever. It would keep rising until it hit a different limiting factor — food, space or disease — and it would probably overshoot what the wood can feed and then crash. It might also damage the plants it eats, lowering what the wood can support afterwards.

5

Connected ideas

Predator and prey are the top links of a food chain, and the reason predators are always the smaller number is the energy lost at each step of that chain. Their rise and fall is a population question, and the long arms race between them — better hunting against better escaping — is one of the strongest engines of adaptation. Reading these graphs is also a lesson in telling what you observed from what you concluded.
