Chapter 2

Mass and Weight

In everyday speech these two words mean the same thing. In science they do not — and questions are written specifically to catch people who treat them as interchangeable.

2.1

Two different questions

Mass answers the question: how much stuff is in this object? How many atoms, how much material. Mass is measured in grams and kilograms.

Weight answers a different question: how hard is gravity pulling on this object? Weight is a force, so it is measured in newtons — the same unit as any other force.

Here is why this matters. Take an object to the Moon and nothing is removed from it. Same atoms, same material, same mass. But the Moon's gravity is weaker, so it pulls less hard — and the weight drops.

The one-line summary

Mass never changes. Take an object anywhere in the universe and its mass is the same. Weight changes with location, because gravity is different in different places.

Isamount of matterthe pull of gravity

2.2

Turning kilograms into newtons

On Earth, gravity pulls with about 10 newtons for every kilogram. So to find weight, multiply the mass in kilograms by 10.

A 2 kg book weighs about 20 N. A 50 kg child weighs about 500 N. A 500 g cup — that is 0.5 kg — weighs about 5 N.

Watch the grams

If a question gives you grams, convert to kilograms first. 500 g is 0.5 kg, so the weight is 5 N — not 5000 N. Forgetting this step turns a right answer into a wrong one.

Commonly missed

What is the smallest force needed to lift a cup of tea of mass 500 g?

Step 1. Convert: 500 g = 0.5 kg.

Step 2. Weight = 0.5 × 10 = 5 N.

Step 3. To lift it, your upward force must at least match its weight. So the smallest force is 5 N.

The trap is answering 0.5 (forgetting to multiply) or 5000 (forgetting to convert). Both wrong numbers appear as options on purpose.

2.3

What the scale in your bathroom really does

A bathroom scale is marked in kilograms, so it looks like it measures mass. It does not. It measures the force you press down with — your weight — and then converts that to kilograms assuming you are standing on Earth.

Take the same scale to the Moon and it would show a much smaller number, even though your mass has not changed at all. The scale is not lying; it is just built for Earth.

If a question asks what a scale shows on the Moon

A scale marked in kilograms will show a smaller number, because it senses force, not matter. But the person's actual mass is unchanged. Read carefully which of the two the question is asking about.

2.4

Gravity: a pull between everything

Gravity is a force of attraction between any two objects that have mass. It is a non-contact force — it reaches across empty space with nothing in between.

Two things decide how strong the pull is:

How much mass. More massive objects pull harder. The Earth is enormous, so its pull is strong. The Moon has far less mass, so its pull is weaker.

How far apart. The further apart two objects are, the weaker the pull between them.

A common misconception

Astronauts float in the space station not because there is no gravity there. Gravity is still pulling them — that is exactly what keeps the station circling the Earth. They float because they and the station are falling together, so nothing pushes back on them.

2.5

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

2.6

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

An astronaut takes a 3 kg toolbox to the Moon. What happens to its mass, and what happens to its weight? Explain both.

Show answer

Its mass stays 3 kg. Mass is the amount of matter in the toolbox, and nothing was added or removed during the journey.

Its weight becomes about six times smaller. On Earth it weighs about 30 N; on the Moon about 5 N. Weight is the pull of gravity, and the Moon's gravity is much weaker than Earth's.

2

A bag of rice has a mass of 2 kg. Work out its weight on Earth, showing your reasoning.

Show answer

On Earth, gravity pulls with about 10 N for every kilogram of mass.

Weight = 2 kg × 10 = 20 N.

Note the units: the mass is in kilograms, the weight is in newtons. Writing "20 kg" for the weight would be wrong.

3

Why is it wrong to say "my weight is 45 kilograms"?

Show answer

Kilograms measure mass, not weight. Saying "my weight is 45 kilograms" mixes the two up.

The correct version is either "my mass is 45 kilograms" or "my weight is about 450 newtons".

In everyday conversation nobody minds. In a science answer it costs marks.

4

Two children stand at the North Pole and at the equator. An astronaut orbits far above the Earth. Who has the smallest weight, and why?

Show answer

The astronaut, because weight depends on distance from the Earth as well as on mass. The astronaut is much further from the centre of the Earth, so the gravitational pull on them is weaker.

The two children are both on the surface, so their weights are very nearly the same — their masses are what would decide between them.

2.7

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 2.5. Judge statement 1 first.

1

An object is taken from Earth to the Moon. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Its mass stays the same.
  • 2Its weight becomes smaller.
  • 3Both its mass and its weight become smaller.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Mass does not depend on location. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. It contradicts statement 1: mass cannot both stay the same and get smaller. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. The Moon's gravity is weaker, so the pull on the object is smaller. Answer is B.

Useful habit: when two statements contradict each other, at most one can be true. Spotting that pair often decides the question by itself.

2

Which statements about weight are correct?

  • 1Weight is measured in kilograms.
  • 2Weight is a force.
  • 3Weight depends on the strength of gravity.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Weight is measured in newtons; kilograms measure mass. And once statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking: statement 2 is correct (weight is the force of gravity) and statement 3 is correct (weaker gravity means less weight). Consistent with C.

3

A 4 kg object rests on a table on Earth. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Its weight is about 40 N.
  • 2The table pushes up on it with about 40 N.
  • 3The forces on the object are balanced.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. 4 kg × 10 = 40 N. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. The object is not moving, so the forces on it must cancel. That points to A.

Statement 2 — correct as well. For the object to stay still, the table's upward push must exactly match the 40 N pulling down. All three are right, so the answer is A.
