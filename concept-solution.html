Concept

Solutions and Solubility

A solution forms when the particles of one substance separate and spread evenly among the particles of a liquid, and solubility is the measure of how much will do so before no more can dissolve.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Stir sugar into tea and it disappears. It has not been destroyed — the tea tastes sweet, so the sugar is still there. Weigh everything before and after and the mass is unchanged. What has happened is that the sugar has broken up into individual particles which have spread out among the water particles, too small and too scattered to see.

Three words are needed. The substance that dissolves is the solute — here, sugar. The liquid it dissolves in is the solvent — here, water. The result is a solution. Notice how a solution differs from muddy water: mud settles out if you leave it, and you can see the particles, but a solution stays evenly mixed indefinitely and is transparent, though it may be coloured.

Solubility means how much of a solute will dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a given temperature. It is a number, not a yes-or-no. Both the amount of solvent and the temperature have to be stated for the figure to mean anything.

A substance that will not dissolve is called insoluble — sand in water, for example. Strictly, almost everything dissolves to some tiny extent, so "insoluble" usually means the amount is too small to matter.

2

How it works

Dissolving is a competition between two attractions. The solute's particles are held to one another, which is what makes it a solid. Whether it dissolves depends on whether the attraction between solute particles and solvent particles is strong enough to pull those solute particles away and surround them. If it is, the solid breaks apart and the particles disperse; if it is not, the solid stays intact and sits at the bottom.

This explains why solubility depends on the pair of substances and not on either one alone. Salt dissolves readily in water and hardly at all in cooking oil. Wax does the reverse. There is no such thing as a substance that is soluble in general.

Now keep adding solute. At first each spoonful vanishes. Eventually a point comes where the solvent can hold no more and further solute sits undissolved at the bottom. The solution is then saturated. This is not the liquid running out of room in a simple sense; it is a balance being reached, in which solute particles are leaving the solid and rejoining it at equal rates.

Temperature usually raises the solubility of a solid, and often steeply. Around 36 g of salt dissolves in 100 g of water at room temperature, and that figure barely changes with heating. Sugar is very different: roughly 200 g dissolves in 100 g of water at room temperature, and around 480 g near boiling. This is why sweets and jam are made by dissolving a great deal of sugar in hot water — and why crystals appear as such a syrup cools, since the cooling liquid can no longer hold what it held when hot.

Gases behave the opposite way: they become less soluble as temperature rises. Warm a glass of cold tap water and bubbles form on the sides, which is dissolved air coming out. This has consequences beyond the kitchen — warm river water holds less dissolved oxygen, which is why hot weather can be dangerous for fish.

Two practical points often confused with solubility. Stirring does not change how much will dissolve; it only makes it happen sooner, by moving saturated liquid away from the solid surface. Grinding the solid into powder also only changes the speed, by exposing more surface for the solvent to attack. Neither changes the final amount by a single gram. Only temperature and the choice of substances do that.

The heart of it

Dissolving spreads solute particles among solvent particles without destroying anything, and solubility — the limit on how much will dissolve — depends on the pair of substances and the temperature, never on stirring.

3

Where the idea came from

There is no single discoverer here, and no dramatic moment. Dissolving substances has been done for as long as anyone has salted food or brewed a drink. What is genuinely interesting is where the careful methods came from.

For many centuries, the people doing the most systematic work on dissolving, filtering, evaporating and crystallising substances were alchemists, working in the Islamic world from around the eighth century and later in Europe. Their goals were largely mistaken: they hoped to turn base metals into gold, and to find a substance that would cure all illness and extend life. That transformation is not possible by chemical means, and they were pursuing it on the basis of a theory of matter that was wrong in its foundations.

But their practical work was another matter. To have any hope of succeeding they had to obtain pure substances, so they developed and refined the techniques of distillation, filtration, crystallisation and controlled heating, and built the glassware to do it. Jabir ibn Hayyan, working in the eighth century, is associated with much systematic laboratory practice, though the writings attributed to him are tangled and some are certainly by later authors, so we should be careful about crediting particular results to him personally.

The lesson is a real one about how science develops. The alchemists' theory was discarded entirely. Their apparatus and procedures were not — they passed almost directly into modern chemistry, and a school laboratory still uses recognisable descendants of them. Good technique can outlive the bad ideas that motivated it.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking dissolved material has gone. It has not. The mass of a solution equals the mass of solvent plus the mass of solute, exactly. If 10 g of salt is stirred into 100 g of water, the solution has a mass of 110 g. Nothing was lost and nothing was created.

The second is confusing dissolving with melting. Melting is a change of state driven by heat, and it involves one substance only — ice melts into water. Dissolving involves two substances and needs no heating at all. Salt does not melt in water; it dissolves in it, and salt's melting point is above 800 °C.

The third is treating "soluble" as a fixed label. It always depends on the solvent and the temperature.

Commonly missed

Two identical beakers each contain 100 g of water at 20 °C. Sugar is added to both. The first is stirred vigorously and the second is left undisturbed. In which beaker will more sugar dissolve in total?

The trap is that stirring visibly helps, so it feels as though it must increase the amount.

How to work it: The maximum amount that can dissolve — the solubility — is fixed by the two substances and the temperature, both of which are identical here. Stirring only moves the liquid around so that solvent which is not yet saturated keeps meeting the sugar surface, which makes the dissolving happen faster. Given enough time, the unstirred beaker reaches exactly the same saturated state. So the same total mass of sugar dissolves in both; the stirred one just gets there sooner.

5

Connected ideas

A solution is a kind of mixture — the components keep their own identities and can be separated again, which is why evaporating a salt solution gives the salt back. Dissolving only makes sense on the particle model, and it is diffusion that spreads the solute evenly once it has broken up. Separating a solute back out relies on evaporation, and the whole business happens without any chemical change taking place.

In one line
