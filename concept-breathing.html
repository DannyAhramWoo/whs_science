Concept

Breathing and Gas Exchange

Breathing is the muscular movement that pulls air into the lungs and pushes it out again; gas exchange is the swapping of oxygen and carbon dioxide between that air and the blood.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Breathing is so automatic that it is easy to treat it as one single thing. It is really two things, and keeping them apart is the key to the whole topic. The first is the physical business of moving air: ribs and a sheet of muscle beneath the lungs change the size of your chest, and air travels in or out as a result. The second is what that air is for, which is a chemical swap deep inside the lungs where oxygen leaves the air and enters the blood while carbon dioxide goes the other way.

There is a third thing, easily muddled with these, that happens nowhere near the lungs. Respiration is the release of energy from food inside every cell of your body, using oxygen and producing carbon dioxide. Breathing supplies respiration and clears its waste; it is not the same process. A person can be breathing while their cells barely respire, and cells respire hardest when you are running, which is precisely why you then breathe harder.

Air is not pure oxygen. It is roughly four-fifths nitrogen and about one-fifth oxygen, with a very small amount of carbon dioxide. The nitrogen goes in and comes straight out again unchanged, because your body has no use for it. What changes between the air you take in and the air you let out is that some of the oxygen has gone, there is much more carbon dioxide, and the air is warmer and wetter than it was.

2

How it works

Start with the movement, because the reason air enters is often stated backwards. You do not suck air in. When you breathe in, the diaphragm — the dome of muscle under your lungs — tightens and flattens downwards, while muscles between the ribs pull the ribcage up and out. Both make the space inside your chest bigger. The same amount of air now has more room, so the air inside is at lower pressure than the air outside, and the outside air pushes its way in to even things out. Air is pushed in from outside rather than pulled in from inside. Breathing out is mostly the reverse and mostly needs no effort: the muscles relax, the chest gets smaller, the pressure inside rises, and air is squeezed out.

Now the exchange. Air travels down the windpipe, which divides into two tubes, one for each lung, and these divide again and again into ever narrower branches, rather like an upside-down tree. At the ends of the smallest branches sit the alveoli: tiny air sacs, clustered like bunches of grapes, and there are hundreds of millions of them. This is where the swap happens, and nowhere else in the lungs.

Why go to all that branching trouble? Because gases cross from air to blood by diffusion, which is simply substances spreading from where they are crowded to where they are sparse. Diffusion needs no energy but it is only quick over very short distances and across large areas. So the alveoli are built for exactly those two conditions. The wall of an alveolus is a single cell thick, and pressed against it is a capillary whose wall is also one cell thick, so oxygen has almost no distance to travel. And splitting the lungs into hundreds of millions of tiny sacs creates an enormous total surface — far, far more than two simple bags of the same volume could offer. A great deal of surface, packed into a small chest.

The direction of each gas follows automatically from where it is crowded. Blood arriving at the lungs is low in oxygen and high in carbon dioxide; the freshly breathed-in air in the alveoli is the opposite. Oxygen therefore diffuses from air into blood, and carbon dioxide from blood into air. Nothing chooses this. Breathing out and in again keeps the alveolar air fresh, which keeps the difference large, which keeps diffusion fast. The alveoli are kept moist too, because gases must dissolve before they can cross a living surface — which is part of why the air you breathe out is damp.

The heart of it

Air is pushed into your chest because you made the space bigger, and the swap of gases happens only in the alveoli, where a huge moist surface lies one cell away from the blood.

3

Where the idea came from

Two separate puzzles had to be solved: what the lungs are made of, and what breathing actually does to the air. They were solved by different people using different tools.

The structure had to wait for the instrument. Alveoli are far too small to see with the naked eye, so anyone dissecting a lung sees spongy tissue and cannot tell what the sponge is made of. It was only once microscopes improved enough that the lung could be seen as a vast collection of tiny air sacs wrapped in fine blood vessels. This is worth noticing as a pattern: for a long time the answer was not hidden because nobody was clever enough, but because nobody could see. Better lenses did what better arguments could not.

The chemistry came from a different direction, in the late 1700s. Joseph Priestley worked with airs, as gases were then called, and found that a candle burning in a sealed container eventually went out and a mouse in such a container eventually could not survive — but that a plant kept in the container could restore the air so that a candle would burn again. Something in ordinary air was being used up by both burning and breathing, and plants could put it back. Priestley had isolated the gas we call oxygen, but he interpreted it within an older theory and did not describe it as we now would.

Antoine Lavoisier, working in France, gave the modern account. He identified the active gas, named it oxygen, and argued that breathing is a kind of slow burning: the same gas is consumed in both, carbon dioxide is produced in both, and both release heat. That reframed breathing as a chemical process rather than merely a cooling or a stirring of the blood, which had been suggested before. Lavoisier did not have the full picture of where in the body it happens — the understanding that it occurs inside individual cells came considerably later.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest slip is treating breathing and respiration as the same word for the same thing. Breathing is a movement of air, done by muscles, happening in the chest. Respiration is a chemical reaction releasing energy, happening inside every cell. Questions often rely on this distinction being clear.

The second is saying we breathe in oxygen and breathe out carbon dioxide, as though it were a clean swap. Exhaled air still contains a great deal of oxygen — enough that breathing into someone else's lungs can keep them alive. Only some of the oxygen is taken; the rest, and all the nitrogen, comes back out.

Commonly missed

The lungs contain hundreds of millions of alveoli rather than a smaller number of larger air sacs. Explain why this matters for gas exchange.

The trap is answering that it lets the lungs hold more air. Volume is not the point — the same chest space could hold the same air in a few big sacs.

How to work it: Gases move between air and blood by diffusion, and the rate of diffusion depends on the area available to cross. Dividing the same volume of air into many tiny sacs creates a vastly greater total surface than a few large ones, because every extra sac adds new wall. Each alveolus is also thin-walled, moist and wrapped in capillaries, so the crossing distance is tiny. Many small sacs therefore give a large area and a short distance, and both make the exchange fast enough to keep up with the body's demand.

5

Connected ideas

Breathing exists to serve respiration, and it hands its oxygen straight to circulation, which carries it to the cells that need it. The crossing itself is diffusion, the same spreading that carries a smell across a room. Lavoisier's insight that breathing resembles burning links it to combustion, since both consume oxygen and release energy. And because plants take in the carbon dioxide you breathe out and release oxygen during photosynthesis, your breathing is one strand of the carbon cycle.
