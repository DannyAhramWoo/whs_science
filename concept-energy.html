Concept

Energy

Energy is the quantity that lets something do work, and its strange and useful property is that the total never changes, however many forms it passes through.

Introduced in Chapter 8: Energy and Its Changes

1

What it means

Everyone uses the word energy every day. A battery has it, food has it, the wind has it, and you run out of it by bedtime. Those everyday uses are all pointing at something real, but they do not say what the thing actually is.

Here is the honest answer. Energy is not a substance. There is no invisible fluid inside a battery that drains out. Energy is a number you can calculate for a system — from how fast things are moving, how high they are, how hot they are, what chemicals they contain — and the remarkable discovery is that if you add up all those numbers, the total stays the same no matter what happens inside. That constancy is the whole reason the idea is worth having.

So when the chapter says energy is the ability to do work, it is giving you the practical handle rather than the definition. Something with a large store can push, lift, heat or light things. Something with a small store cannot do much before the store is gone.

Energy is measured in joules, written J. A joule is small: lifting an apple from the floor to a table takes roughly one joule. A slice of bread holds about 300,000 J of chemical energy.

2

How it works

The chapter listed forms of energy — kinetic, potential, chemical, electrical, light, sound, heat. Why bother with a list, since energy is one quantity? Because the forms are the different places you must look when hunting for the total.

Think of counting money. A person's wealth might be partly cash, partly in a bank, partly in things they own. Those are not three kinds of wealth, they are three places wealth sits, and moving money between them leaves the total untouched. The forms of energy are the pockets.

This makes many forms less mysterious than they sound. Chemical energy in food or fuel is really energy stored in the arrangement of atoms, released when they rearrange into a more settled arrangement. Heat is really kinetic energy of a hidden kind: the random jiggling of individual particles, far too small to see. Rub your hands and they warm up, because you are converting the organised movement of your palms into disorganised movement of particles in your skin.

That distinction explains something the chapter states but does not justify: why heat is always the wasted form. If all the particles in a block of metal happened to jiggle the same way at the same moment, the block would leap off the table. They never do, because there are far too many of them for that to happen by chance. Organised energy scatters into disorganised energy easily, and almost never gathers itself back up. Energy is conserved, but its usefulness is not.

Notice also that you can never measure the energy something has, only what it gives up or takes in. A book on a shelf has potential energy relative to the floor, but also relative to the lower ground outside. Which is the true value? Neither. Only the change matters.

The heart of it

Energy is not a substance stored inside things; it is a bookkeeping number whose total never changes, which is precisely what makes it useful for working out what must happen next.

3

Where the idea came from

Energy in its modern scientific sense is surprisingly young. The word existed for centuries, but only as a loose term for vigour or force of expression. It was Thomas Young, an English physician and scholar, who gave it a technical meaning, in lectures delivered to the Royal Society in 1802 and published in 1807 as A Course of Lectures on Natural Philosophy. There he wrote that the product of a body's mass and the square of its velocity "may properly be termed its energy".

What is striking is how long people managed without the word. For more than a century before Young, natural philosophers had argued about something they called vis viva, Latin for "living force", roughly what we now call kinetic energy. The arguments were fierce and largely inconclusive.

The real obstacle was not the missing word but a missing realisation. Nobody had noticed that heat, motion, food and fuel were all the same currency. Heat was widely thought to be a weightless fluid called caloric, poured from hot things into cold ones; motion was studied by one group, chemistry by another, and steam engines by engineers who mostly ignored both. There was no single word for energy because nobody yet believed there was a single thing to name.

Once the connection was made in the 1840s, the word Young had coined was waiting, and it spread quickly. In science, naming something usually comes after understanding it, not before.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating energy as a fuel-like substance that gets consumed. People write that a car "uses up" energy, that a battery "runs out" of it, that a bouncing ball "loses" it. In each case the energy is entirely still there. It has moved into forms that are spread out and hard to collect: warm air, warm road, warm brake discs, sound.

The cure is a habit of language. Never write that energy was lost or used up. Write what it turned into and where it went. If you cannot name the destination, you have not finished the question. Heat is the answer surprisingly often, because heat is where organised energy ends up.

Commonly missed

A torch is switched on and left until the battery no longer lights the bulb. Has the energy in the battery been destroyed?

The trap is the phrase "the battery is dead", which sounds like an ending. It invites the answer that the energy has been used up and is gone.

How to work it: track the chain instead. Chemical energy in the battery became electrical energy in the wires, which became light and heat at the bulb. The light crossed the room and was absorbed by the walls, warming them very slightly. The heat spread into the air. All of the energy is still present, scattered thinly through the room as warmth. Nothing was destroyed; it was converted into a form too spread out to light anything.

5

Connected ideas

Kinetic and potential energy are the two forms you will meet most, and conservation of energy is the rule that ties all forms together. Work is how energy is transferred from one thing to another by a force. Heat is the form energy usually ends up in, which is why friction turns up in almost every energy question. Fossil fuels and renewable sources are simply different stores we draw on.

In one line

Energy is a quantity that can move between forms but never changes in total, so the right question is never whether it was lost but where it went.
