Concept

Stromatolites

A stromatolite is a layered rock built up by mats of bacteria trapping sediment, and stromatolite fossils are among the oldest evidence of life on Earth.

Introduced in The Origin of Life

1

What it means

Most fossils are the remains of a creature — a bone, a shell, an imprint of a leaf. A stromatolite is not that. It is a structure that living things built, in the way a termite mound or a coral reef is built. Cut one open and it shows fine layers stacked like the pages of a closed book, often curved into low domes or columns, each layer a record of a period of growth. The name comes from Greek: stroma, a layer, and lithos, stone.

The builders are cyanobacteria, sometimes loosely called blue-green algae, though they are bacteria and not algae. They are microscopic single-celled organisms, and crucially they photosynthesise — using sunlight to build sugars from carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen, exactly as a plant does. They live crowded together in a slimy sheet called a microbial mat, a few millimetres thick, coating the floor of shallow water.

2

How it works

The building process is repetitive and simple. The cyanobacteria secrete a sticky slime. Fine grains of sand and mud drifting in the water settle onto that surface and are held. Given enough grains the mat is smothered, and the bacteria, needing light, grow upwards through the sediment and form a fresh mat on top. Sediment settles again. Each repetition leaves one dark organic layer and one pale mineral layer, and over years the structure creeps upwards a fraction of a millimetre at a time, with calcium carbonate deposited in the mat cementing it into hard rock.

Modern stromatolites grow at roughly half a millimetre a year, so a dome half a metre high represents something like a thousand years of continuous growth. The living ones at Shark Bay in Western Australia, the best-known example anywhere, are estimated to be two to three thousand years old. If cyanobacteria are so ancient and successful, why are living stromatolites now rare? Because they get eaten. Snails, sea urchins and grazing fish crop microbial mats efficiently, and burrowing animals churn the layers apart, so once grazing animals evolved stromatolites almost disappeared. They survive only where conditions are too harsh for grazers — the sheltered water at Shark Bay is roughly twice as salty as normal seawater, so the mats are left alone. Their rarity is not a sign of failure but a sign that the rest of the living world caught up with them.

Now the part that changed the planet. Cyanobacteria release oxygen, and for a long stretch of Earth's history they did so on an enormous scale while nothing used it up. At first it was absorbed: it dissolved in the sea and reacted with iron, which rusted out of solution and settled on the sea floor. Those deposits survive as banded iron formations, striped rocks that are today a major source of the world's iron ore and direct physical evidence of oxygen being produced faster than it could be consumed. Once the available iron was used up, oxygen began to accumulate in the air. This is the Great Oxidation Event, roughly 2.4 billion years ago.

It is easy to call this good news, but for the life that existed then it was a disaster. Almost everything alive was anaerobic, adapted to live without oxygen, and oxygen is chemically aggressive and damages the molecules such organisms depend on. Vast numbers of species are thought to have died out, and the survivors retreated into places oxygen could not reach: deep mud, the ocean floor, and eventually the guts of animals, where their descendants live still. It may have been the largest extinction in the planet's history, caused by organisms poisoning the world with their own waste product.

Two enormous consequences followed. Respiration using oxygen releases far more energy from the same food than the anaerobic alternatives, and large, complex organisms could not exist without that supply. And high in the atmosphere, oxygen was converted by sunlight into ozone, forming a layer that absorbs most incoming ultraviolet light; before it existed the land surface was sterilised and life could persist only under water. Every plant, animal and person living out of the sea is there because cyanobacteria spent a billion years releasing oxygen.

The heart of it

Stromatolites are layered rock built by photosynthesising bacteria — the oldest widespread evidence of life, and the record of the organisms that put oxygen into the air.

3

Where the idea came from

Layered dome-shaped rocks were described in the nineteenth century, but their origin was disputed for decades, some geologists arguing they were purely mineral formations deposited the way a stalactite is, with no life involved. The argument could not be settled while the only examples were fossils hundreds of millions of years old. It was settled in the field: in 1956 living stromatolites were found growing in Hamelin Pool at Shark Bay, in water too salty for grazing animals. Here were the same domes and the same internal layering being made in the present day by visible mats of cyanobacteria, giving the fossils a living counterpart to be compared against.

Attention then turned to how far back they go. Fossil stromatolites about 3.5 billion years old have been described from the Pilbara region of Western Australia, and comparably old ones from Greenland and southern Africa, making them among the oldest claimed evidence of life anywhere. But here an honest caution is required, and it is a live scientific argument rather than a settled matter. The problem is that layered domes can also form without life, through purely chemical deposition of minerals, and after three and a half billion years of heat and pressure the fine details that would distinguish the two are usually destroyed. Several famous specimens have been challenged in print by researchers arguing for a non-biological origin, and some claims have been withdrawn or revised after re-examination. The general view is that some of the very old stromatolites are genuinely biological, but for any individual specimen the case has to be argued carefully, and for the oldest of all it remains disputed.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is imagining a stromatolite as a fossilised organism, and looking for the creature in it. There is no creature. It is a structure, and the builders were microscopic and are long gone; what survives is what they trapped and cemented.

The second is treating the arrival of oxygen as simply an improvement. It was a mass extinction for the life of the time. Whether a change is good or bad depends entirely on what is being asked to survive it, and this is one of the clearest examples in the planet's history.

Commonly missed

A geologist finds a layered dome-shaped rock and claims it proves life existed when the rock formed. Give one reason the claim might be wrong, and one piece of evidence that would strengthen it.

The trap is accepting a shape as proof of a cause. The same shape can be produced in more than one way, so shape alone cannot settle the question.

How to work it: First identify the alternative explanation. Layered domes can be built by minerals coming out of solution and settling in bands, with no organism involved — this happens in hot springs and caves. So the layering is consistent with life but does not require it, and the claim is an inference rather than an observation. Then ask what would distinguish the two cases. The strongest evidence would be traces of the organisms themselves: microscopic fossil filaments of the right size and shape within the layers, or organic carbon carrying a chemical signature that living things produce. Supporting evidence would come from the surroundings — whether the rock formed in shallow water where sunlight could reach, which photosynthesising bacteria need. It is because these tests are so difficult on very old and heavily altered rock that the oldest claims remain genuinely disputed.

5

Connected ideas

Stromatolites are the physical evidence that the origin of life had already succeeded, and their builders' photosynthesis, powered by chlorophyll, rewrote the atmosphere and created the ozone layer. They also show how a population's own waste can transform the environment it lives in.
