Concept

Fossil Fuels

Fossil fuels are coal, oil and natural gas: the buried remains of living things, holding energy that came from sunlight millions of years ago.

Introduced in Chapter 9: Where Energy Comes From

1

What it means

When a car burns petrol or a power station burns coal, something is being used up. That something is a fossil fuel. The word "fossil" is the clue: a fossil is the preserved remains of something that was once alive. So a fossil fuel is a fuel made from the remains of living things — not from rock that happened to be flammable, but from plants and tiny sea creatures that died a very long time ago.

There are three main kinds. Coal formed mostly from land plants — trees, ferns and mosses growing in swamps. Oil (also called crude oil or petroleum) and natural gas formed mostly from plankton, the microscopic living things that drift in the sea. All three are made largely of carbon and hydrogen joined together, which is why chemists call them hydrocarbons.

The important word attached to fossil fuels is non-renewable. That does not mean the Earth has stopped making them. It means the Earth makes them so slowly — over tens or hundreds of millions of years — that on any human timescale the supply is fixed. We are spending a store that took longer to fill than our species has existed.

2

How it works

Start with the question that matters most: where did the energy come from? It came from the Sun. A fern growing in a swamp 320 million years ago used sunlight to make sugars out of carbon dioxide and water, in the process called photosynthesis. That sugar held chemical energy, and the energy in it had arrived as sunlight.

Normally when a plant dies, decomposers — bacteria and fungi — break it apart and release that energy again, which is why a fallen log slowly disappears. Fossil fuels formed in the rare places where this did not happen. In a waterlogged swamp there is very little oxygen in the mud, and most decomposers need oxygen. So the dead plants piled up instead of rotting. Layer upon layer built up, and the weight of everything above squeezed the water out and pressed the material together. Heat from deep in the Earth cooked it. Over enormous time this turned peat into lignite, lignite into coal, and where the raw material was sea plankton, into oil and gas trapped in the pores of rock.

So burning coal is running photosynthesis backwards. The plant took carbon dioxide out of the air and locked carbon away; burning puts that carbon back into the air as carbon dioxide, and releases the stored energy as heat and light. This is why the neatest description of a lump of coal is ancient sunlight.

The numbers are worth holding on to. A kilogram of coal releases roughly 25 to 30 million joules of energy; a kilogram of petrol closer to 45 million. That is a lot of energy in a small, easily carried package, and it explains why fossil fuels were so transformative. No renewable source is as concentrated or as convenient to store. That convenience is exactly why they are hard to give up, and why the carbon released is now a serious problem.

The heart of it

A fossil fuel is sunlight that a living thing captured long ago and that was buried before it could rot away.

3

Where the idea came from

People burned coal for centuries without knowing what it was. Various guesses were made — that it was a kind of stone, or bitumen, or something the Earth grew on its own. What settled the question was fossils: miners kept finding the imprints of ferns and tree bark pressed into coal seams, in such detail that the leaves could be identified. That was hard to argue with. By the early 1800s geologists accepted that coal was fossilised plant matter, and in 1822 the geologists William Conybeare and William Phillips named the Carboniferous period — literally "coal-bearing" — for the stretch of time, roughly 359 to 299 million years ago, when most of the great coal beds were laid down.

Knowing coal was fossilised plants is not the same as knowing where its energy came from. That step was taken in the 1840s by a German doctor, Julius Robert Mayer, who was working out the idea that energy can change form but is never destroyed. He reasoned that if plants grow using sunlight, then the fuel we burn must be sunlight in another form. He was right, and he was largely ignored. Mayer was not a professional physicist, he wrote unclearly, and other scientists were slow to take him seriously. Recognition came late and after considerable personal suffering.

The idea that these fuels might run out came later still, and was resisted. In 1865 the economist William Stanley Jevons argued in The Coal Question that Britain's coal was finite and that using it more efficiently would not save it, because cheaper energy simply gets used more. That last point still holds today.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is thinking coal and oil are made from dinosaurs. They are not, and the timing shows why: most coal formed in the Carboniferous, which ended about 40 million years before the first dinosaurs appeared. Coal is plants. Oil is mostly plankton. Dinosaur remains contributed essentially nothing.

The second error is treating "non-renewable" as though it were about quantity — as if we simply have a big pile. The point is the rate. Fossil fuels are still forming somewhere right now, unimaginably slowly. We burn in a year what took roughly a million years to make.

Commonly missed

Petrol is burned in a car engine. Where did the energy in the petrol originally come from?

It is tempting to answer "from the oil" or "from the ground", but that only names where the fuel was found, not where the energy came from. The question asks for the original source.

How to work it: Trace the chain backwards. Petrol comes from crude oil. Crude oil formed from dead sea plankton. Those plankton were living things that made their food by photosynthesis. Photosynthesis is powered by sunlight. So the original source is the Sun. Almost every energy chain on Earth ends at the Sun when you trace it back far enough — the exceptions are nuclear, geothermal and tidal energy.

Connected ideas

Fossil fuels store chemical energy that photosynthesis put there, and combustion is the reaction that releases it. Because that release returns ancient carbon to the air, fossil fuels sit at the centre of the carbon cycle and of the strengthening greenhouse effect. Renewable energy sources are the alternatives that do not draw on a buried store.

In one line
