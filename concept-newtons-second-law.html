Concept

Newton's Second Law

A resultant force makes a mass speed up, and the acceleration you get is the force divided by the mass.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

Push an empty shopping trolley and it leaps forward. Push the same trolley loaded with bags of rice, using exactly the same effort, and it creeps. Push the empty trolley twice as hard and it picks up speed twice as quickly. Everyone has felt this. Newton's Second Law is that feeling written down as a rule.

Two words first. Acceleration means how fast the speed is changing, not how fast the object is going. A car that goes from 0 to 10 metres per second in 2 seconds has gained 5 metres per second of speed every second, so its acceleration is 5 m/s2. The unit "m/s2" is read as "metres per second per second" — speed added each second.

The second word is resultant force. Usually several forces act on an object at once. The resultant force is what you get when you add them all up, taking direction into account. If you pull a box forwards with 30 N while friction drags backwards with 10 N, the two partly cancel and the resultant force is 20 N forwards. If instead friction were also 30 N, the forces would cancel completely and the resultant force would be zero. Forces that cancel like that are called balanced forces.

The law says: resultant force = mass times acceleration, or force = mass x acceleration. Force is in newtons (N), mass in kilograms (kg), acceleration in m/s2. Rearranged, acceleration = force / mass. That single division carries both halves of the trolley experience — more force gives more acceleration, more mass gives less.

2

How it works

Work it forwards first. A trolley of mass 4 kg is pushed with a resultant force of 12 N. Acceleration = force / mass = 12 / 4 = 3 m/s2. Every second it gains 3 metres per second of speed. After 4 seconds, starting from rest, it is moving at 12 m/s.

Now double the force to 24 N on the same 4 kg trolley. Acceleration = 24 / 4 = 6 m/s2, exactly twice as much. Instead, keep the force at 12 N but double the mass to 8 kg. Acceleration = 12 / 8 = 1.5 m/s2, exactly half. Force and acceleration rise together; mass and acceleration move opposite ways.

Work it backwards too, because questions often hand you the acceleration and want the force. A 1200 kg car needs to accelerate at 2 m/s2. Force = mass x acceleration = 1200 x 2 = 2400 N. That is the resultant force the engine must supply on top of whatever it is already using to beat friction and air resistance. Or: a 0.5 kg ball is struck and accelerates at 400 m/s2. Force = 0.5 x 400 = 200 N.

Here is the harder use, and it is the one that separates people who have understood the law from people who have memorised it. Suppose the same force is applied to two blocks. Block 2 accelerates twice as fast as block 1. What does that tell you? Since acceleration = force / mass, and the force is the same, twice the acceleration means half the mass. So block 2 has mass m and block 1 has mass 2m.

Now glue the two blocks together and apply the same force again. The total mass is 2m + m = 3m. The acceleration is force / 3m. Compare it with block 1 on its own, which had acceleration force / 2m. The new acceleration is (2/3) of block 1's acceleration, because 3m is one and a half times 2m. No numbers were needed at all, only the rule that mass and acceleration trade off. Past IMSO papers, including a 2014 one, have set up exactly this sort of glued-masses ratio.

The heart of it

Acceleration equals resultant force divided by mass, so pushing harder speeds things up more and loading them down speeds them up less.

3

Where the idea came from

For nearly two thousand years the accepted view came from Aristotle in ancient Greece: a force was needed to keep something moving, and heavier objects fell faster. Both parts are wrong, but both match careless everyday observation. A pushed cart does stop when you stop pushing — not because motion needs force, but because friction is a hidden backward force.

Galileo Galilei, working in Italy in the early 1600s, chipped away at this. He rolled balls down and up smooth ramps and noticed that the smoother he made them, the further a ball ran along the flat before stopping. He reasoned that with friction removed entirely a ball would simply keep going. That reasoning turned motion, not force, into the thing that needs no explanation. What needs explaining is any change of motion.

Isaac Newton assembled the full account in his book Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica, published in 1687 in London. In it he set out three laws of motion. The second is the one on this page. Newton did not actually write "F = ma" in that neat form; he stated it in terms of change of motion over time, and the tidy equation we use came later from others working from his ideas. The physics is his; the notation is not.

The story of an apple falling and prompting Newton's thinking about gravity comes from Newton himself in old age, retold by friends. Something like it may have happened, but the tale has certainly been polished in retelling, and nobody sensible claims an apple hit his head.

4

What people get wrong

The big one is believing that steady motion requires a steady resultant force. It does not. Constant speed in a straight line means zero resultant force. A car cruising at 60 km/h on a flat road has its engine's driving force exactly cancelled by friction and air resistance. The forces are balanced, the resultant is zero, the acceleration is zero — and zero acceleration means the speed is not changing, which is precisely what cruising is. Most people find this counter-intuitive because they think of the engine "keeping it going". The engine is really only cancelling out the drag.

The second mistake is using a single force in the equation when several are acting. F = ma only ever takes the resultant. Add up the forces first, minding directions, then divide.

A third confusion involves falling objects. Two balls thrown from a cliff with equal speed, one straight up and one straight down, hit the ground at the same speed. The one thrown up rises, stops, and comes back down past the launch point moving downwards at the same speed it left with, so from that moment the two journeys are identical. Gravity gives both the same acceleration regardless of which way they were thrown.

Commonly missed

A lift is moving upwards at a steady 3 m/s. The lift and passengers together have a mass of 500 kg. What is the resultant force on the lift?

The trap is the word "upwards". It invites you to answer that there must be a large upward force, and to reach for the weight, 5000 N. But the question says steady speed.

How to work it: steady speed in a straight line means the acceleration is zero. Force = mass x acceleration = 500 x 0 = 0 N. The resultant force is zero. The cable does pull up with about 5000 N, and gravity does pull down with about 5000 N, but those two cancel. Moving is not the same as accelerating. Ask yourself only one question: is the speed changing? If not, the resultant force is zero, whatever direction the object happens to be travelling.

5

Connected ideas

This law sits on top of the idea of force itself, and only makes sense once you can find a resultant from balanced and unbalanced forces. The reluctance of a large mass to accelerate is inertia, and mass is the measure of it. Weight is simply gravity's force on a mass, which is why all objects fall with the same acceleration. Friction is usually the hidden force that makes the resultant smaller than you expect, and terminal velocity is what happens when drag grows until the resultant reaches zero. Once a force acts over a distance it also does work and transfers energy.
