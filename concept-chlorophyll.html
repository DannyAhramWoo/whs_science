Concept

Chlorophyll and Chloroplasts

Chlorophyll is the green substance that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis, and chloroplasts are the small structures inside plant cells holding it.

Introduced in Chapter 16: Photosynthesis and Respiration

1

What it means

Plants are green, and it is easy to accept that as simply how plants are. But green is a clue, not a decoration: it tells you something specific about how a leaf handles light.

An object looks a particular colour because of the light it fails to absorb. A leaf absorbs red and blue light strongly and uses that energy to build sugar. Green it absorbs poorly, so much of it bounces off or passes through and reaches your eye. A plant's greenness is, in a sense, the light it did not want.

The substance doing the absorbing is chlorophyll. It is not spread evenly through the cell like dye in water but packed inside small green parcels called chloroplasts, and one leaf cell may hold dozens. Both words come from the Greek chloros, meaning pale green.

2

How it works

Chlorophyll's job is to catch light energy and pass it into a chemical reaction. Light travels in packets of energy; when one strikes a chlorophyll molecule it is absorbed, briefly raising the energy of one of its electrons. That extra energy is handed on and used to build sugar from carbon dioxide and water. Chlorophyll is the doorway through which the Sun's energy enters the living world.

Why keep it inside chloroplasts rather than loose in the cell? Because photosynthesis is not one reaction but a sequence, and a sequence works far better when its parts sit close together in the right order. A chloroplast contains stacks of flat membranes with chlorophyll embedded in them, giving an enormous light-catching surface in a small volume while keeping the reacting substances in the right neighbourhood. Chloroplasts cluster in the upper layers of a leaf where light arrives first, which is why a leaf's top is usually a deeper green than its underside.

Chlorophyll is not the only pigment in a leaf. Yellow and orange ones exist too, but in a healthy leaf chlorophyll is abundant enough to mask them. In autumn, deciduous trees break down their chlorophyll before dropping their leaves, salvaging useful materials, and the yellows there all along are revealed. A leaf kept in the dark turns pale for a related reason: chlorophyll is constantly broken down and must be rebuilt, and rebuilding requires light.

A variegated leaf, one with white or cream patches, is a useful natural experiment, because the white regions have no chlorophyll. Test it for starch and only the green parts give a positive result, showing directly that chlorophyll is necessary rather than a coincidence of colour.

The heart of it

Chlorophyll captures light energy and looks green because green is the light it absorbs least, and chloroplasts are the compartments holding it where the work happens.

3

Where the idea came from

The green substance was separated out and named in 1817 by two French chemists, Pierre-Joseph Pelletier and Joseph Bienaimé Caventou. Naming it mattered more than it may sound. Before, greenness was a property of leaves; afterwards a particular substance that could be extracted, studied and compared. Their approach — get the pure material out, then investigate it — was becoming chemistry's standard method.

Chloroplasts came into view later, once microscopes could show structures inside cells, and what was found raised an odd question. They have their own membranes, divide by splitting in two rather than being built afresh by the cell, and carry a small amount of their own genetic material, separate from the cell's main supply. That is peculiar for a mere part of a cell. It is how an independent organism behaves.

The explanation now accepted is that chloroplasts descend from free-living bacteria taken inside another cell long ago and never released, the two becoming so dependent neither survives alone. The bacterium gained shelter; the host gained the ability to make food from light.

This idea was argued forcefully by the biologist Lynn Margulis from the 1960s, and was rejected for years — her early work was turned down by many journals and established biologists found the notion far-fetched. Evidence from genetic material accumulated over the following decades until it became standard. It is a reminder that being rejected by experts is not proof of being right, and that what settled the question was evidence rather than persistence alone. Details of exactly how and when it happened are still debated, and on some of them we cannot be sure.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking chlorophyll absorbs green light because it is green. The reverse: it looks green because green is the light it absorbs least and so reflects. This is why grow lamps are often red and blue.

The second is treating chlorophyll and chloroplast as one word. Chlorophyll is a substance; a chloroplast is a structure containing it.

Commonly missed

A plant is kept in the dark for two days, then a variegated leaf is partly covered with black paper and the plant is left in bright light for six hours. The leaf is then tested for starch. Which parts turn positive, and why was the plant destarched first?

It is a trap because two conditions are varied at once and it is easy to track only one.

How to work it: Starch appears only where both conditions were met: chlorophyll present and light received. So only the green, uncovered parts test positive. Green covered parts had chlorophyll but no light; white uncovered parts light but no chlorophyll; white covered parts neither. The plant was destarched first so any stored starch was used up, otherwise a positive result might come from old starch rather than photosynthesis during the experiment.

Connected ideas

Chlorophyll exists to serve photosynthesis, so that is the idea to read alongside this one. Understanding why it looks green depends on how colour works, which depends on reflection and absorption. The energy chlorophyll captures becomes the stored energy feeding every food chain, and the process it drives is one arm of the carbon cycle.
