Chapter 1

Forces and Friction

A force is a push or a pull. That sounds simple — but almost every mark lost on this topic comes from one of three traps hidden inside that sentence.

1.1

What a force actually does

A force is a push or a pull. You cannot see a force itself. You only see what it does.

A force can do four things, and a question will usually be testing one of them:

The first trap

A moving object does not need a force to keep moving. It only needs a force to change what it is doing. A ball rolling across the floor slows down because friction pushes back on it — not because it "runs out of force".

1.2

Contact and non-contact forces

Some forces only work when two things are touching. Push a door, pull a rope, feel friction under your shoe — all of these need contact.

Three important forces work across a gap, with nothing touching at all:

Memorise these three

The non-contact forces are magnetic, gravitational and electrostatic. Only three. Anything not on that list is a contact force.

Commonly missed

Which force cannot exist without a point of contact?

The wording flips the question round, and that alone catches people. It is asking which force needs touching — so the answer is whichever option is not magnetic, gravitational or electrostatic.

Friction is the usual answer. Friction only exists where two surfaces rub together.

1.3

Friction: useful and unhelpful at once

Friction is the force that resists sliding. It appears whenever two surfaces touch and one tries to move across the other. Friction always pushes against the direction of motion.

Exam questions love friction because it is both a help and a nuisance, often in the same situation.

What friction does

Three effects come up again and again:

It slows things down. A rolling ball stops. A sliding book stops. Friction is what took the motion away.

It produces heat. Rub your palms together. That warmth is friction turning movement into heat.

It wears surfaces away. Shoe soles thin out. Tyres go bald. Brake pads need replacing.

A question you should be able to answer

What would happen if friction disappeared? You could not walk — your foot would slide backwards. You could not hold a pencil. A car could not start moving or stop. Nails would slip out of wood. Almost nothing would work.

Commonly missed

Which activity could still succeed in the complete absence of friction?

Work through each option and ask: does this need grip? Walking, writing, braking, climbing, striking a match — all need friction and all fail.

The answer is usually something that needs no grip at all — a magnet attracting a pin, or an object simply falling. Falling works fine without friction. In fact it works better.

1.4

Balanced and unbalanced forces

Forces rarely act alone. What matters is not each force by itself but what is left over after they combine.

When forces are balanced, they cancel out. Nothing left over. The object keeps doing exactly what it was already doing — staying still if it was still, or continuing at a steady speed if it was moving.

When forces are unbalanced, something is left over. That leftover force changes the motion: it speeds the object up, slows it down, or turns it.

The second trap

Balanced forces do not mean "not moving". They mean "not changing". A car travelling at a steady 60 km/h has balanced forces on it — the engine's push exactly equals the drag holding it back. Steady speed and standing still both count as balanced.

Commonly missed

A box is at rest. Two equally strong forces then act on it, and the box starts to move. Which statement is correct?

If the box started moving, the forces cannot have cancelled — so they were not acting in opposite directions. Two equal forces pointing the same way add together, and two equal forces at an angle also leave something over.

The reasoning: movement began, therefore the forces were unbalanced, therefore they were not opposite and equal. Read what the box did and work backwards.

1.5

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

1.6

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A book slides across a table and gradually stops. Explain what happened, without using the phrase "it ran out of force".

Show answer

Friction acts between the book and the table surface, pushing backwards against the direction the book is sliding.

This backward force is unbalanced — nothing is pushing the book forwards any more — so it slows the book down until it stops. The book's movement is turned into a small amount of heat at the two surfaces.

2

Give one situation where friction is helpful and one where it is a nuisance. Explain both.

Show answer

Helpful: friction between shoe soles and the ground gives grip, so your foot does not slide backwards when you walk. Brakes work the same way — friction on the wheel is what stops a bicycle.

A nuisance: friction between moving machine parts wastes energy as heat and slowly wears the parts away, which is why machines need oil.

3

A lorry is driving along a straight road at a steady speed. Are the forces on it balanced or unbalanced? Explain.

Show answer

Balanced.

The lorry's speed and direction are not changing, and that is what balanced forces mean. The forward push from the engine is exactly matched by air resistance and friction pushing backwards, so nothing is left over.

Balanced does not mean stationary. It means nothing about the motion is changing.

4

A magnet held above a steel paperclip lifts it into the air, with a gap between them. Name the forces acting on the paperclip and say which is larger.

Show answer

Two forces act on the paperclip: magnetic force pulling it upwards, and weight (gravity) pulling it downwards. Both are non-contact forces.

Since the paperclip rose, the magnetic force must be larger than its weight. If the two were equal the clip would hang still; if weight were larger it would stay on the table.

1.7

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 1.5. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which of the following are non-contact forces?

  • 1Frictional force
  • 2Magnetic force
  • 3Gravitational force
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Friction only exists where two surfaces touch, so it is a contact force. And the moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: magnetic and gravitational forces both act across a gap, so statements 2 and 3 are correct. Consistent with C.

2

A parachutist falls at a constant speed. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The forces acting on the parachutist are balanced.
  • 2Air resistance is equal to the parachutist's weight.
  • 3The parachutist is still accelerating downwards.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Constant speed means the motion is not changing, which is exactly what balanced forces mean. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Accelerating means the speed is changing, but the question says the speed is constant. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. The two forces on the parachutist are weight downwards and air resistance upwards. For them to balance, they must be equal. So the answer is B.

Watch for this pair: "constant speed" and "accelerating" cannot both be true. Whenever a statement contradicts the question stem, it is wrong — no physics needed.

3

Which statements about friction are correct?

  • 1Friction always acts against the direction of motion.
  • 2Friction can produce heat.
  • 3Friction is always unhelpful and should be removed.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Friction opposes sliding, so it always points against the motion. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Friction lets you walk, grip and brake. Words like "always" and "should be removed" are usually a warning sign. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Rubbing produces heat. So the answer is B.
