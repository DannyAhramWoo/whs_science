Chapter 6

Heat and Temperature

Heat and temperature are not the same thing, and almost every mark lost on this topic comes from treating them as one word. This chapter separates them, then follows heat as it travels by conduction, convection and radiation.

6.1

Heat is not temperature

Heat is energy. It is the energy that flows from one place to another because one place is hotter. Heat is measured in joules, like every other kind of energy.

Temperature is not energy at all. Temperature is a number that tells you how hot something is. It is measured in degrees Celsius, and you read it off a thermometer.

Here is the difference that questions are built on. A bathtub of warm water at 40 °C contains far more heat energy than a cup of boiling water at 100 °C. The cup is hotter, but there is so much more water in the bath that the total energy stored in it is much greater. Temperature does not count how much stuff there is. Heat energy does.

Two different questions

Temperature answers how hot? Heat answers how much energy? A question that mentions the amount of water, or the size of the object, is almost always testing this difference.

Commonly missed

A cup of water at 100 °C and a bucket of water at 60 °C are both left in a cold room. Which contains more heat energy at the start?

The trap is that 100 is a bigger number than 60, so the cup looks like the obvious answer. But the question asks about heat energy, not temperature.

How to work it: heat energy depends on two things — the temperature and the amount of material. A bucket holds perhaps fifty times as much water as a cup. Even at a lower temperature, that much water stores far more energy in total. The bucket. Whenever the amounts are clearly different, the larger amount usually wins.

6.2

Heat only flows one way

Heat always flows from the hotter object to the colder object. Never the other way round. This rule has no exceptions, and it is worth saying out loud until it feels obvious.

That means "cold" never travels. When you hold an ice cube, cold does not flow into your hand — heat flows out of your hand into the ice. Your hand loses energy, which is why it feels cold. Getting this backwards is the reason a lot of explanation questions lose marks.

Heat keeps flowing for as long as there is a temperature difference. When the two objects reach the same temperature, the flow stops. The objects are then in thermal equilibrium. The final temperature always lies somewhere between the two starting temperatures — never above the hotter one, never below the colder one.

Say it this way

Never write "the cold moved into the drink". Write "heat moved out of the drink into the ice". Only heat travels, and it only ever travels downhill from hot to cold.

Commonly missed

A metal spoon at 90 °C is put into a mug of water at 25 °C and left. What is the final temperature of the spoon?

The word "spoon" makes people hunt for a separate answer for the spoon, as though the two objects end up different. They do not.

How to work it: once heat stops flowing, the two objects are at the same temperature — that is what thermal equilibrium means. So the spoon and the water share one final answer, and it must be between 25 °C and 90 °C. Since the mug holds much more water than the spoon holds metal, the final value sits close to 25 °C, perhaps 28 °C. The answer is never 90 °C and never 25 °C.

6.3

Conduction: heat travelling through solids

Conduction is heat moving through a material by particles passing energy along to their neighbours. Heat the end of a metal rod and the particles there vibrate faster. They knock into the particles next to them, which speed up too, and the energy travels along the rod. Nothing moves from one end to the other — only the energy does.

Materials that let heat pass through quickly are good conductors. Metals are the best: copper, aluminium, steel, iron. That is why saucepans and radiators are metal.

Materials that let heat through only slowly are poor conductors, also called insulators. Wood, plastic, rubber, cloth, water and air are all poor conductors. Air is one of the worst of all, which is exactly why it is useful. A woollen jumper, a duvet and a double-glazed window all work by trapping air so that heat cannot escape.

Commonly missed

A metal spoon and a wooden spoon have both been sitting in the same room all night. Why does the metal one feel colder when you pick it up?

The trap is the word "colder". It sounds like a question about temperature, so people answer "because metal is colder". But both spoons have been in the same room all night, so both are at exactly the same temperature — room temperature. Neither is colder than the other.

How to work it: "feels cold" is not a measurement of the object. It is a measurement of how fast your hand is losing heat. Metal is a good conductor, so it carries heat away from your skin quickly, and your nerves report that rapid loss as cold. Wood is a poor conductor, so it takes heat from your hand only slowly and feels warmer. Same temperature, different rate of heat loss. The same reasoning explains why a metal bench feels hot in the sun and why tiles feel colder underfoot than carpet.

6.4

Convection and radiation

Convection happens in liquids and gases, where the particles are free to move about. When part of a fluid is heated it expands, so the same mass now takes up more space. That makes it less dense, and less dense fluid floats upwards. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to take its place, gets heated in turn, and rises. This endless loop is a convection current.

Convection currents explain a great deal of everyday life. A room heater warms the whole room even though it only touches the air beside it. Hot air balloons rise. Sea breezes blow. Smoke goes up a chimney. Convection cannot happen in a solid, because the particles are locked in place and cannot travel.

Radiation is different from both. It travels as waves and needs no material at all. This is the only way heat can cross empty space, and it is how energy reaches us from the Sun across millions of kilometres of vacuum. You feel it the instant you step out of a shadow.

Surfaces matter for radiation. Dark, dull surfaces absorb radiation best, and they also emit it best. Shiny, light-coloured surfaces reflect it. That is why a black car gets unbearable in the sun, why people wear light clothes in hot weather, and why the inside of a vacuum flask is silvered.

How to tell them apart

Ask what is between the hot thing and the cold thing. A solid → conduction. A liquid or gas that can circulate → convection. Nothing, or a gap you cannot cross → radiation. A vacuum flask blocks all three: a vacuum layer stops conduction and convection, and silvered walls reflect radiation.

6.5

Expansion and contraction

When something is heated its particles vibrate more strongly and push each other slightly further apart. The object therefore gets bigger. This is expansion. When it cools the particles settle closer together again and the object shrinks — contraction.

The particles themselves do not swell. Only the gaps between them change. Nothing is added and nothing is removed, so the mass stays exactly the same while the volume grows. That is why hot material is less dense, and it is the reason convection works at all.

Gases expand most, liquids next, solids least. But even the tiny expansion of a solid matters when the solid is long. Railway lines are laid with small gaps, and bridges rest on rollers or have zigzag joints, so that a hot afternoon does not buckle them. A thermometer is expansion put to work: the liquid in the narrow tube expands as it warms and creeps further up the scale, so you can read a temperature by reading a length.

Mass stays, volume changes

Heating an object increases its volume but not its mass. Because density is mass divided by volume, the density goes down. This single sentence explains convection currents, hot air balloons and why warm water floats on cold.

6.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

6.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A swimming pool at 28 °C and a mug of tea at 70 °C. Which has the higher temperature, and which contains more heat energy? Explain both answers.

Show answer

The tea has the higher temperature, because 70 °C is a bigger reading than 28 °C. Temperature only tells you how hot a thing is.

The pool contains far more heat energy. Heat energy depends on how much material there is as well as how hot it is, and a pool holds many thousands of times more water than a mug. All that water at 28 °C adds up to a huge amount of stored energy.

This is why the two words cannot be swapped. Hotter does not mean more energy.

2

Explain why you cannot cook food by convection on the Moon, but the Sun can still warm the Moon's surface.

Show answer

The Moon has no atmosphere. Convection needs a liquid or a gas that can circulate, carrying warm material upwards and letting cool material sink. With no air, there is nothing to circulate, so no convection current can form.

Radiation is different. It travels as waves and needs no material at all, so it crosses the empty space between the Sun and the Moon without difficulty. That is why the Moon's surface still heats up in sunlight.

3

Two houses in a hot country are identical, except one is painted white and one is painted black. Which stays cooler inside, and why?

Show answer

The white house stays cooler. Light, shiny surfaces reflect most of the radiation that lands on them, so less energy is absorbed into the walls and passed on to the air inside.

The black house has dark, dull walls, which are the best absorbers of radiation. It takes in far more of the Sun's energy, and that energy then conducts through the walls and warms the rooms.

The same reasoning explains why loose light-coloured clothing is more comfortable outdoors than a black T-shirt.

4

A vacuum flask keeps soup hot for hours. Explain how it blocks each of the three ways heat travels.

Show answer

The flask has a double wall with the air pumped out from between the two layers. That vacuum has no particles, so conduction cannot happen — there is no chain of particles to pass energy along. For the same reason no convection current can form, because there is no fluid to circulate.

Radiation would still cross the vacuum, so the facing walls are made shiny and silvered. Shiny surfaces reflect radiation back towards the soup instead of absorbing it.

A stopper on top also stops hot air escaping and carrying energy away with it.

6.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 6.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

A metal chair and a wooden chair have stood in the same shaded room since yesterday. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The metal chair is at a lower temperature than the wooden chair.
  • 2The metal chair feels colder to touch than the wooden chair.
  • 3Metal conducts heat away from the hand faster than wood does.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Both chairs have been in the same room since yesterday, so both have reached the same temperature as the air around them. Neither is colder. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is correct — metal really does feel colder. Statement 3 gives the reason, and it is also correct. Consistent with C.

Watch for this pattern: "feels colder" and "is colder" are two different claims. Questions on this topic separate them on purpose.

2

A hot metal block at 90 °C is placed against a cool metal block at 30 °C, and the pair is left on an insulated bench. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Heat flows from the hot block to the cool block.
  • 2The two blocks end up at the same temperature.
  • 3The final temperature of the pair is above 90 °C.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Heat always flows from hotter to colder, never the reverse. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. No energy is being added from outside, so nothing can end up hotter than the hottest starting object. The final temperature must sit between 30 °C and 90 °C. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Heat keeps flowing while there is a temperature difference and stops when there is none, which means both blocks finish at the same temperature. So the answer is B.

3

A pan of water is heated from below. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The water near the base expands and becomes less dense.
  • 2The warmed water rises and cooler water sinks to replace it.
  • 3Heat reaches the metal base of the pan by conduction.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Heated water expands, so the same mass takes up more space and its density falls. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. The base is a solid metal in contact with the flame, and heat travelling through a solid is conduction. That means the answer is A.

Statement 2 — correct. Less dense water floats upwards and denser cool water sinks, forming a convection current. All three stand, so A.

Note: all three ways of travelling can appear in one situation. Conduction through the pan base, convection in the water, and radiation from the flame to the outside of the pan.
