Concept

Variables and Fair Tests

A variable is anything in an experiment that could change, and a fair test is one where you deliberately change only a single variable so that any difference in the result can be blamed on that one thing.

Introduced in Chapter 20: How Scientists Work

1

What it means

Suppose you want to know whether a particular fertiliser helps plants grow. You feed it to one plant, and that plant ends up taller than another plant in the room. Has the fertiliser worked? You cannot say, because if the fed plant also sat nearer the window, or was watered more often, or started off bigger, any of those could explain the height on its own.

A fair test is the arrangement that removes those alternatives. You change exactly one thing and hold everything else steady, so that when a difference appears there is only one candidate left to blame.

Scientists give the different roles names. The independent variable is the one thing you deliberately change — the fertiliser. The dependent variable is what you measure to see the effect — the height after four weeks. It is called dependent because its value depends on what you did. The controlled variables are everything you keep the same on purpose: same species, same pot size, same soil, same water, same light, same starting height.

There is one more piece, and it is the piece most often left out. A control is a run of the experiment with no treatment at all — a plant given no fertiliser but otherwise identical. Without it you have nothing to compare against, and a plant simply growing is not evidence that anything you did caused it.

2

How it works

The logic underneath is simpler than the vocabulary. If two situations differ in twenty ways and give different results, you have learned nothing about which of the twenty mattered. If they differ in one way and give different results, you have learned a great deal. Controlling variables is not fussiness — it is the only thing that makes the conclusion follow.

The hard part in practice is that variables hide. In the plant experiment the obvious ones are water, light and soil. The less obvious ones include the temperature at different heights in the room, whether one pot drains better than the other, and whether you unconsciously water the fertilised plant a little more because you want it to succeed. That last one is why medical trials are often run so that neither the patient nor the doctor knows who got the real treatment: the experimenter's own hopes are a variable, and one that cannot be controlled by good intentions alone.

Repeats matter for a related reason. Even with everything controlled, plants vary — one seed is simply more vigorous than another. If you use a single plant on each side, a difference of a few centimetres could easily be that natural variation rather than the fertiliser. Ten plants on each side, and you compare the averages, and the natural variation largely cancels out. A single measurement can never distinguish a real effect from ordinary randomness.

It is also worth knowing that some questions cannot be answered by a fair test at all, and that this is not a failure of the scientist. You cannot control the variables affecting a star, or run a second Earth without an atmosphere for comparison. In those cases scientists look for natural comparisons — two similar regions that happen to differ in one respect — and must then argue much more carefully, because they did not get to choose what was held constant.

One warning about the phrase "only change one thing". Sometimes changing one thing forces another to change with it. If you want to test whether a bigger sail makes a model boat faster, a bigger sail is also a heavier sail. You cannot separate them by wishing. Recognising when your independent variable drags a second variable along with it is a genuinely advanced skill, and saying so in an answer is worth more than pretending the problem is not there.

The heart of it

If more than one thing differs between your two setups, the result cannot tell you which one caused the difference.

3

Where the idea came from

One of the clearest early examples belongs to James Lind, a Scottish naval surgeon. In 1747, aboard HMS Salisbury, Lind was faced with sailors dying of scurvy, a disease that on long voyages killed more men than enemy action. Nobody knew the cause; the leading suspicions included bad air, damp and idleness.

Lind took twelve sailors who were all suffering from scurvy at a similar stage, housed them in the same part of the ship, and gave them all the same basic diet. He then split them into six pairs and gave each pair a different supposed remedy: cider, dilute sulphuric acid, vinegar, seawater, a paste of garlic and other ingredients, and — the sixth pair — two oranges and a lemon a day. The pair on citrus recovered so quickly that one was fit for duty within about a week.

What makes this worth remembering is not the answer but the design. By holding the diet, the accommodation and the stage of illness the same, Lind had made the treatment the only thing that differed between his groups. That is a fair test carried out well before anyone had written down the rules for one.

The disappointing part of the story is what happened next. The Royal Navy did not adopt lemon juice fleet-wide until around the 1790s, roughly four decades later. Lind himself did not fully trust his own result; he continued to believe scurvy had several causes, and he damaged the effect by boiling citrus juice into a concentrate for storage, which destroys the vitamin C that made it work — though nobody would identify vitamin C until the 1930s. A good experiment does not automatically produce a good conclusion, and a good conclusion does not automatically get acted upon.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest fault is listing controlled variables that do not matter while missing the one that does. Writing "we used the same table and the same colour of pot" is padding. Ask instead which variables could plausibly change the thing you are measuring, and control those.

The second fault is forgetting the control group entirely, then concluding from a treated plant growing that the treatment worked. Plants grow anyway. Without an untreated comparison there is no baseline, and the result is not evidence of anything.

The third is mixing up dependent and independent variables. The rule is mechanical: whichever one you decide the value of before you start is independent; whichever one the experiment hands back to you is dependent.

Commonly missed

To find out whether ice melts faster in warm water, a student puts a small ice cube into a cup of warm water and a large ice cube into a cup of cold water, and times them. She concludes that warm water melts ice faster. What is wrong with her test, and what should she change?

The trap is that her conclusion happens to be true. Warm water really does melt ice faster, so the answer looks right and it is tempting to accept the experiment along with it. But her experiment did not show it.

How to work it: list what differs between the two cups. The water temperature differs, which is intended. But the ice cube size also differs, and a small cube melts faster than a large one regardless of temperature. Two things changed, so the result cannot be pinned on either. To fix it she must use ice cubes of the same mass and shape, the same volume of water in identical cups, both cups in the same place, and start both timings at once. Only then does the temperature stand alone as the independent variable. Better still, she should repeat the whole thing several times, because a single pair of cubes could differ by chance in ways she did not notice.

5

Connected ideas

Controlling variables is how a hypothesis stops being an opinion and becomes something testable, and the conclusion you draw at the end is an inference from what the measurements showed. Accuracy and precision decide whether the difference between your two setups is large enough to be believed at all. Fair tests turn up throughout the rest of the book: comparing insulating materials in heat, comparing rates of dissolving, or testing what a plant needs for photosynthesis all depend on the same one-thing-at-a-time discipline.
