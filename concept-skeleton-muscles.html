Concept

Skeleton and Muscles

The skeleton is the rigid frame that supports and protects the body, and muscles are the tissues that move that frame by pulling on it.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

A body made only of soft tissue would be a puddle. Bones give you a shape to keep and something firm for everything else to hang from. The skeleton also protects: the skull is a hard box round the brain, the ribs a cage round the heart and lungs, the spine a sheath round the nerves of your back. And it provides anchorage, the job most people forget, because a muscle is only useful with something solid to pull against.

Muscles are the movers. A muscle does one thing when switched on: it shortens and pulls. It cannot push and cannot lengthen itself. It can only contract, then relax and be stretched back by something else.

Two words are needed. A tendon joins muscle to bone, so that when the muscle shortens the bone is dragged with it. A ligament joins bone to bone across a joint, holding it together while allowing movement. To keep them straight: tendons transmit pull, ligaments hold things in place.

2

How it works

Since a muscle can only pull, a single muscle at a joint could move it one way only, leaving the limb stuck. The solution is muscles in pairs on opposite sides, each undoing the other. Such a pair is an antagonistic pair, and it is why a limb can go back and forth.

The arm is the clearest case. The biceps sits on the front of your upper arm, the triceps behind. To bend your elbow, the biceps contracts and pulls the forearm up while the triceps is stretched; to straighten it, the triceps contracts and pulls the forearm down while the biceps is stretched. Neither pushes: straightening your arm is a pull too, from the other side.

The joint turns a pull into a particular movement, and joints differ by shape. A hinge joint, at the elbow or knee, bends one way only. A ball and socket joint, at the shoulder or hip, has a rounded end in a cup, so the limb can swing almost any way and rotate. The shoulder moves most freely of any joint and dislocates most easily, because the shallow socket that allows the movement holds it less securely.

Three kinds of muscle

The biceps and triceps are one kind of muscle, but the body has three, and they differ by what controls them.

Skeletal muscle is what attaches to bone and moves the skeleton. It is voluntary, meaning you decide when it contracts. It is also the strongest and fastest of the three, and it tires: hold a heavy book out in front of you and your arm will begin to shake and then ache, because skeletal muscle cannot sustain a hard contraction indefinitely.

Cardiac muscle is found in the wall of the heart and nowhere else. It is involuntary, so you cannot stop your heart by deciding to, and it has a property no other muscle has: it contracts rhythmically on its own, without needing a signal from the brain to start each beat. That matters, because a heart that needed conscious instruction would stop the moment you fell asleep. Cardiac muscle also does not tire, having beaten without a rest since before you were born.

Smooth muscle lines the hollow organs — the gut, the bladder, the blood vessels. It is also involuntary, and it is the slow one, contracting steadily rather than sharply. It is what pushes food along your gut, which is why digestion continues whether you attend to it or not.

The dividing line examiners test is voluntary against involuntary, so tie each type to a job. If the question describes someone choosing to lift, hold or move something, the answer is skeletal. If it describes the heart, it is cardiac. If it describes an internal organ working without you noticing, it is smooth. More detail is on Muscle Types.

The heart of it

Muscles can only pull, never push, so they must work in opposing pairs across a joint, with each one stretching the other back.

3

Where the idea came from

For well over a thousand years, European medicine took its anatomy from Galen, a Greek physician working in the Roman Empire in the second century AD. Galen was a careful investigator who reasoned from real dissection. The difficulty was what he dissected: human dissection was largely unavailable to him, so he worked on animals — apes, pigs and others — and transferred what he found to the human body.

Much of it carried across, because animals do share much structure with us. But not all did, and the errors were copied from book to book for centuries. What made this last so long was not stupidity but authority. Galen's writings were treated as settled, and where a body on the dissecting table disagreed with the book, it was often assumed the body was unusual rather than the book wrong. That is the exact reversal of how science is supposed to work, and it is the most useful lesson here.

In 1543 Andreas Vesalius, working in Padua, published a detailed study of human anatomy based on his own dissections, illustrated with unusually careful drawings. He set out where the accepted descriptions did not match what was in front of him, showing that a number of Galen's fitted animal rather than human anatomy. He did this not by being cleverer than Galen, but by insisting that the body itself, not the text, was to be believed.

The reaction was not universally warm, and some scholars defended the old descriptions rather than accept the correction. Vesalius was not right about everything either, and later anatomists corrected him in turn — which is the point, since the method he argued for expects it.

4

What people get wrong

The dominant error is describing a muscle as pushing. People say the triceps pushes the arm straight. It does not; it pulls the forearm from behind. When explaining a movement, work out which muscle is shortening and which way that shortening drags the bone.

The second is thinking the relaxing muscle in a pair does nothing. It is being stretched, and that stretching is what readies it to pull next time, since a muscle cannot lengthen itself.

Commonly missed

Describe what happens to the biceps and the triceps when a person straightens their arm at the elbow, and explain why two muscles are needed.

The trap is to say the biceps pushes the arm straight, or to name one muscle only. Bending is the movement people rehearse, so the reverse gets described sloppily.

How to work it: To straighten the arm, the triceps contracts and pulls the forearm bone from behind the elbow, while the biceps relaxes and is stretched. Two muscles are needed because a muscle can only shorten and pull, never push or lengthen itself. One alone could move the joint one way with no means of returning it, so a second on the opposite side both produces the reverse movement and stretches the first ready to contract again.

5

Connected ideas

Muscles pulling on bones apply forces directly: a tendon transmits force, a joint is a pivot, a bone a lever. Every contraction is paid for by energy released from food during respiration, which is why hard exercise makes you breathe faster and why muscles need a rich blood supply. An internal skeleton also serves in classification, separating vertebrates from invertebrates, and its shape is usually an adaptation to how the animal moves.

In one line

Bones give the body a frame to be pulled on, and because a muscle can only shorten and pull, muscles work in opposing pairs across a joint, each moving the limb one way and stretching its partner ready to move it back.
