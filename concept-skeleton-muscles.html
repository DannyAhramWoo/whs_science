Concept

Skeleton and Muscles

The skeleton is the rigid frame that supports and protects the body, and muscles are the tissues that move that frame by pulling on it.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

A body made only of soft tissue would be a puddle. Bones give you a shape to keep and something firm for everything else to hang from. The skeleton also protects: the skull is a hard box round the brain, the ribs a cage round the heart and lungs, the spine a sheath round the nerves running down your back. And it provides anchorage, the job most people forget, because a muscle can only do something useful if it has something solid to pull against.

Muscles are the movers. A muscle does exactly one thing when it is switched on: it gets shorter and pulls. It cannot push and it cannot lengthen itself. It can only contract, and then relax and be stretched back by something else.

Two words are needed. A tendon joins a muscle to a bone, so that when the muscle shortens the bone is dragged with it. A ligament joins bone to bone across a joint, holding the joint together while still allowing it to move. To keep them straight: tendons transmit pull, while ligaments hold things in place.

2

How it works

Since a muscle can only pull, a single muscle at a joint could move that joint one way only, and the limb would be stuck. The solution is muscles arranged in pairs on opposite sides of the joint, so that each undoes the other. Such a pair is an antagonistic pair, and it is why a limb can go back and forth.

The arm is the clearest case. The biceps sits on the front of your upper arm, the triceps on the back. To bend your elbow, the biceps contracts and pulls the forearm up while the triceps relaxes and is stretched. To straighten it, the triceps contracts and pulls the forearm down while the biceps is stretched in turn. Neither muscle ever pushes: straightening your arm is a pull too, applied on the other side.

The joint turns a pull into a particular kind of movement, and different joints allow different movements because of their shape. A hinge joint, at the elbow or knee, can only bend one way, like a door. A ball and socket joint, at the shoulder or hip, has a rounded end sitting in a cup, so the limb can swing in almost any direction and rotate. Freedom and stability trade against each other: the shoulder moves most freely of any joint and is also the most easily dislocated, because a shallow socket is what makes that freedom possible.

The heart of it

Muscles can only pull, never push, so they must work in opposing pairs across a joint, with each one stretching the other back.

3

Where the idea came from

For well over a thousand years, European medicine took its anatomy from Galen, a Greek physician working in the Roman Empire in the second century AD. Galen was a careful investigator who reasoned from real dissection. The difficulty was what he dissected: human dissection was largely unavailable to him, so he worked on animals — apes, pigs and others — and transferred what he found to the human body.

Much of it carried across, because animals do share a great deal of structure with us. But not all of it did, and the errors were copied from book to book for centuries. What made this last so long was not stupidity but authority. Galen's writings were treated as settled, and where a body on the dissecting table disagreed with the book, it was often assumed the body was unusual rather than that the book was wrong. That is the exact reversal of how science is supposed to work, and it is the most useful lesson here.

In 1543 Andreas Vesalius, working in Padua, published a detailed study of human anatomy based on his own dissections of human bodies, illustrated with unusually careful drawings. He set out where the accepted descriptions did not match what was in front of him, and showed that a number of Galen's descriptions fitted animal rather than human anatomy. He did this not by being cleverer than Galen, but by insisting that the body itself, not the text, was to be believed.

The reaction was not universally warm, and some scholars defended the old descriptions rather than accept the correction. Vesalius was not right about everything either, and later anatomists corrected him in turn — which is the point, since the method he argued for expects to be corrected.

4

What people get wrong

The dominant error is describing a muscle as pushing. People say the triceps pushes the arm straight. It does not; it pulls the forearm from the other side. When explaining a movement, work out which muscle is shortening and in which direction that shortening drags the bone.

The second is thinking the relaxing muscle in a pair is doing nothing. It is being stretched, and that stretching is what makes it ready to pull next time. A muscle cannot lengthen itself, so without a partner to stretch it, it could act once and then be useless.

Commonly missed

Describe what happens to the biceps and the triceps when a person straightens their arm at the elbow, and explain why two muscles are needed.

The trap is to say the biceps pushes the arm straight, or to name only one muscle. Bending is the movement people rehearse, so the reverse often gets described sloppily.

How to work it: To straighten the arm, the triceps contracts and pulls the forearm bone from behind the elbow, while the biceps relaxes and is stretched. Two muscles are needed because a muscle can only shorten and pull, never push or lengthen itself. One alone could move the joint one way and then have no means of returning it, so a second on the opposite side both produces the opposite movement and stretches the first ready for its next contraction.

5

Connected ideas

Muscles pulling on bones apply forces directly: a tendon transmits a force, a joint acts as a pivot, a bone acts as a lever. Every contraction is paid for by energy released from food during respiration, which is why hard exercise makes you breathe faster and why muscles are richly supplied by circulation. An internal skeleton is also used in classification, since it separates vertebrates from invertebrates, and a skeleton's shape is usually an adaptation to how the animal must move.
