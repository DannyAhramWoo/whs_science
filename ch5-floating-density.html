Chapter 5

Floating, Sinking and Density

Whether something floats has almost nothing to do with how heavy it is. This chapter replaces "heavy things sink" with the rule that actually works — and shows why a steel ship stays on top of the sea while a small pebble goes straight to the bottom.

5.1

What density means

Density is how much mass is packed into a given amount of space. It is mass per unit volume.

Imagine two boxes of exactly the same size. One is filled with feathers, the other with sand. The sand box has far more mass squeezed into the same volume, so the sand is denser. Nothing about the size changed — only how much matter was crammed inside.

You work density out by dividing: density = mass ÷ volume. If a block has a mass of 60 g and takes up 20 cm3 of space, its density is 60 ÷ 20 = 3 g/cm3. That number means every single cubic centimetre of the block holds 3 g of matter.

Mass, volume, density

Mass is how much matter there is. Volume is how much space it takes up. Density is the two compared: mass ÷ volume. You cannot judge density from mass alone, and you cannot judge it from size alone. You always need both.

5.2

Measuring volume by displacement

Finding the mass of an object is easy — put it on a balance. Finding the volume is easy too if the object is a neat cube or box, because you can multiply length × width × height.

But most things are not neat. A stone, a key, a lump of clay: there is no formula for those shapes. So we use displacement. When you lower an object into water, it pushes water out of the way. The amount of water pushed aside is exactly equal to the object's own volume, because two things cannot be in the same place at once.

Read the water level before, lower the object in, read the level again, and subtract. If the level rises from 50 cm3 to 74 cm3, the object's volume is 24 cm3. Now divide its mass by 24 and you have its density.

Commonly missed

A cork is pushed right under the water with a thin wire. The level rises by 12 cm3. If the cork is then released and allowed to float, what happens to the level?

The trap is assuming the reading stays at 12 cm3. It does not. A floating cork sits mostly above the surface, so only part of it is in the water pushing water aside.

How to work it: displacement measures the volume of the object that is actually below the surface. Fully submerged, the whole 12 cm3 counts. Floating, only the sunken part counts, so the level falls. That is why you must hold an object completely under the water when you are measuring its volume.

5.3

The rule that decides floating

Here is the rule the whole chapter turns on. An object floats if it is less dense than the liquid it is placed in, and sinks if it is more dense. Nothing else matters.

Water has a density of 1 g/cm3. That is a useful number to memorise, because it turns the rule into a simple test. Below 1 g/cm3 means it floats in water. Above 1 g/cm3 means it sinks. Wood is about 0.6, so it floats. Iron is about 7.9, so it sinks.

Notice what is not in that rule: mass. A cargo ship has a mass of tens of thousands of tonnes and floats. A pebble weighing 20 g sinks. Being heavy is not what makes something sink — being densely packed is.

Commonly missed

A steel ship with a mass of 40 000 tonnes floats. A steel nail with a mass of 5 g sinks. Both are made of steel. Explain why they behave differently.

This is the heavy-versus-dense trap, and it is the single most common error in this topic. The instinct is "the ship is heavier, so it should sink harder". Weight is not the test. Density is.

How to work it: ask what the object is made of as a whole, not what its outer material is. The nail is solid steel all the way through, so its density is steel's density, about 7.9 g/cm3 — far above 1, so it sinks. The ship is a thin steel shell wrapped around a very large volume of air. Add the small mass of steel to the huge volume of the hull and the ship's overall density comes out below 1 g/cm3. So it floats.

Test yourself with the same nail: hammer it flat into a wide thin dish shape and it will float, even though not one atom has been added or removed. Same mass, bigger volume, lower density.

Say this to yourself

Not "is it heavy?" but "is it denser than the liquid?" Heavy things float all the time. Light things sink all the time. Only the density comparison predicts correctly.

5.4

Upthrust: why water holds things up

Something has to be pushing up on a floating object, or gravity would pull it straight down. That upward push is called upthrust, or buoyant force. Any liquid pushes upwards on anything placed in it.

When an object floats, it has settled at the depth where upthrust exactly equals weight. Two forces, equal and opposite, so they are balanced — which is why a floating boat neither rises nor sinks. If the object is denser than the liquid, the most upthrust the liquid can supply is still less than the object's weight, so the forces are unbalanced downwards and it sinks.

Upthrust also explains why you feel lighter in a swimming pool, and why a heavy rock is easy to lift underwater but suddenly feels heavy the moment it breaks the surface. Your weight has not changed. The water was pushing up on you, so less of your weight had to be carried by your legs or your arms. Take the water away and the upthrust goes with it.

Floating is balanced forces

A floating object is not "weightless". Its weight still pulls down with exactly the same force as on land. It is simply that the upthrust pushing up is equal in size, so the two cancel and nothing moves.

5.5

Layers of liquid, and the oddity of ice

The floating rule applies to liquids too. Pour honey, water and cooking oil into the same glass and they settle into three separate bands: honey at the bottom, water in the middle, oil on top. The densest liquid sinks below the others, exactly as a dense solid would.

That gives you a free way to read densities off a picture. Whatever is at the top of the jar is the least dense thing in it, and whatever is at the bottom is the most dense. A solid resting between two liquid layers is denser than the liquid above it and less dense than the liquid below.

Water itself does something strange, and questions like to test it. Nearly every substance shrinks when it freezes, because the particles pack closer together. Water expands when it freezes. The particles lock into an open framework with gaps in it, so the same mass of water takes up more space as ice. More space for the same mass means lower density — about 0.92 g/cm3 — which is why ice cubes float and why icebergs sit on the sea rather than under it. It is also why a full bottle of water cracks if you leave it in the freezer.

Commonly missed

The table gives four objects and three liquids. In which liquid or liquids will object Q float?

The trap is looking at the mass column. Q has the largest mass of the three objects, so it looks like the one most likely to sink. Mass is irrelevant here — it is only there to mislead you.

How to work it: turn every row into a single density number first, then compare. Q's density is 120 ÷ 150 = 0.8 g/cm3. Now check it against each liquid one at a time. Against oil: 0.8 is less than 0.9, so Q floats. Against water: 0.8 is less than 1.0, so Q floats. Against syrup: 0.8 is less than 1.4, so Q floats. Q floats in all three.

Run the same method on R, density 1.6, and it sinks in all three, because 1.6 is bigger than every liquid in the table. And notice R is the lightest object on the list. Heaviest floats everywhere, lightest sinks everywhere — proof that mass was never the test.

5.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

5.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A ball of modelling clay sinks in water. The same ball is then shaped into a bowl and it floats. Nothing was added or taken away. Explain.

Show answer

The mass of the clay is unchanged, but the volume the object occupies has changed. The bowl shape encloses a large pocket of air inside its walls, so the object as a whole takes up much more space than the tight ball did.

Density is mass ÷ volume. The mass on top stayed the same while the volume underneath got much bigger, so the overall density dropped below 1 g/cm3. Once the object is less dense than water, it floats.

This is exactly how a steel ship works, on a smaller scale.

2

A large rock lying on the bottom of a pond is easy to lift while it is underwater, but feels much heavier the moment it comes out. Has its weight changed? Explain what is happening.

Show answer

Its weight has not changed. Gravity pulls on the rock with the same force underwater as in the air.

While the rock is submerged, the water pushes upwards on it with an upthrust. That upward push carries part of the rock's weight, so your arms only have to supply the rest. The rock feels lighter because you are lifting less of it.

As soon as the rock leaves the water there is no upthrust any more, so your arms must carry the whole weight themselves.

3

A block has a mass of 45 g. When lowered fully into a measuring cylinder, the water level rises from 60 cm3 to 90 cm3. Will the block float in water? Show your reasoning.

Show answer

First find the volume by displacement: 90 − 60 = 30 cm3. That rise is the space the block pushed the water out of, so it is the block's own volume.

Now find the density: 45 ÷ 30 = 1.5 g/cm3.

Water is 1.0 g/cm3. The block is 1.5, which is greater, so the block is denser than water and it will sink.

4

Why do ice cubes float in a drink, when almost every other solid sinks in its own liquid?

Show answer

Most substances pack their particles more tightly when they freeze, so the solid is denser than the liquid and sinks in it.

Water is unusual. When it freezes, the particles lock into an open framework with spaces inside it, so the same mass of water spreads out into a larger volume. More volume for the same mass means a lower density — ice is about 0.92 g/cm3 against water's 1.0.

Because ice is less dense than the liquid water around it, it floats. The same expansion is why a sealed bottle of water can split open in the freezer.

5.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 5.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about floating and sinking are correct?

  • 1An object sinks if it has a large mass.
  • 2An object floats if it is less dense than the liquid it is placed in.
  • 3A liquid pushes upwards on any object placed in it.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Mass alone decides nothing. A 40 000 tonne ship floats and a 5 g nail sinks. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is the correct rule, and statement 3 is true because upthrust acts on everything in a liquid, floating or sinking. Consistent with C.

2

A wooden block is floating on water. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The upthrust on the block is equal to its weight.
  • 2The block is less dense than water.
  • 3There is no gravitational force acting on the block.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. The block is resting, neither rising nor sinking, so the forces on it are balanced. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Gravity never switches off. The block still has weight — that is precisely the force the upthrust is balancing. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Wood floats in water, and floating means the object is less dense than the liquid. So the answer is B.

Watch for this: statements containing "no force at all" are almost always wrong. Balanced forces means the forces cancel, not that they are absent.

3

A stone of mass 80 g raises the water level in a measuring cylinder from 40 cm3 to 80 cm3. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The volume of the stone is 40 cm³.
  • 2The density of the stone is 2 g/cm³.
  • 3The stone will sink in water.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. The volume is the rise in level: 80 − 40 = 40 cm3. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Once you know the density is 2 g/cm3, compare it with water at 1.0. The stone is denser, so it sinks. Since 3 is correct, the answer is A.

Statement 2 — correct. Density is mass ÷ volume = 80 ÷ 40 = 2 g/cm3. All three are correct, confirming A.
