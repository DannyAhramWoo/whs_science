Chapter 8

Energy and Its Changes

Energy never disappears. It only changes form. Nearly every mark lost on this topic comes from saying energy was "lost" or "used up" — when the real answer is where it went.

8.1

What energy is

Energy is the ability to do work. Something with energy can push, lift, heat, light or move something else. Energy is measured in joules, written J.

You cannot see energy. You only see what it does — the same problem you had with forces. A stretched rubber band looks no different from a limp one, but let it go and it fires across the room. That is the energy showing itself.

Energy comes in several forms, and a question will nearly always be asking you to spot which form is which.

The unit

Energy is measured in joules (J) — the same unit used for work. That is not a coincidence. Doing 100 J of work on something gives it 100 J of energy.

8.2

Kinetic and potential energy

Kinetic energy is the energy of movement. Anything that is moving has it — a rolling ball, running water, a bird, a bullet, air in the wind.

Two things make kinetic energy larger. More mass: a lorry at 30 km/h has far more kinetic energy than a bicycle at 30 km/h, which is why the lorry does far more damage in a crash. More speed: the same bicycle going twice as fast has much more kinetic energy than before.

Gravitational potential energy is energy stored because of height. Lift a book onto a shelf and you have done work against gravity. That work is now stored in the book. Let it fall and you get the energy back as movement. The higher you lift something, and the heavier it is, the more potential energy it stores.

Potential means stored

Potential energy is not "energy waiting to happen by luck". It is energy you put in by lifting, and it stays there until something lets the object fall.

8.3

Conversion: the swing in detail

Energy is never created and never destroyed. It only changes from one form to another. This rule is called the conservation of energy, and it is the single most useful idea in this chapter.

A swing shows it perfectly. At the very top of the swing, the child is momentarily still — not moving at all. So there is no kinetic energy there. But the child is at the greatest height, so potential energy is at its maximum.

As the swing falls, height drops and speed rises. Potential energy turns into kinetic energy, joule for joule. At the lowest point the child is moving fastest and is closest to the ground: kinetic energy is at its maximum, potential energy at its minimum. Then the swing rises on the other side and the whole trade runs backwards.

The trade is exact

Ignoring friction, potential lost equals kinetic gained. The swing does not "make" energy at the bottom. It only swapped what it already had at the top.

The same trade happens in a ball thrown straight up. On the way up it slows as kinetic energy turns into potential. At the very top it is still, holding maximum potential. On the way down the swap reverses. A roller coaster works the same way — the first hill is the tallest because every hill after it must be paid for out of that first store of potential energy.

8.4

Why the bouncing ball gets lower

Drop a ball from one metre and it never comes back to one metre. Each bounce is lower than the last, until it stops. If energy cannot be destroyed, where has it gone?

Listen. Each bounce makes a sound, and sound is a form of energy. Feel the ball after many bounces — it is slightly warmer, and so is the floor. Squashing and un-squashing the rubber heats it up. Air resistance heats the air a little too.

So the energy has not vanished. Some of it has been converted to heat and sound instead of going back into height. Heat and sound spread out into the room and cannot easily be gathered up again, which is why we sometimes call it wasted energy. Wasted is not the same as destroyed.

Never say "lost"

Whenever a question asks why something slows, stops or falls short, do not write "the energy was lost". Write what it turned into — almost always heat, and often sound as well.

The same reasoning explains a real swing eventually stopping, a spinning top toppling, and a car needing fuel even on a flat road. Friction and air resistance are quietly converting movement into heat the whole time.

8.5

Conversion chains, and work done

Most real devices do not make one conversion. They make a chain of them, and questions often ask you to write the chain out in order.

SituationEnergy chain
Torchchemical (battery) → electrical → light + heat
Photosynthesislight → chemical (stored in the plant)
Carchemical (fuel) → kinetic + heat + sound
Eating and runningchemical (food) → kinetic + heat
Radioelectrical → sound + heat

Notice that heat appears in almost every chain. No device converts energy perfectly. If a question offers you a chain that ends in only one useful form and no heat, be suspicious.

Work done

Work is done when a force moves something in the direction of the force. It is calculated as:

work done = force × distance moved in the direction of the force

Work is also measured in joules. Push a trolley with a force of 20 N for 3 m and you have done 20 × 3 = 60 J of work, and the trolley has gained 60 J of energy.

The words "moves" and "in the direction of the force" are both doing real work in that sentence. If either one fails, no work is done at all — however tired you feel.

Commonly missed

In which of these situations is no work done? (i) A boy pushes hard against a brick wall. (ii) A girl carries a heavy bag horizontally along a level corridor. (iii) A man lifts a box onto a table.

The trap is that (i) and (ii) both feel exhausting. Your muscles are burning chemical energy and producing heat the whole time, so it feels obvious that work is being done. In science, work has a narrower meaning, and effort does not count.

How to work it: ask two questions of each case, in this order. Is there a force? Did the object move in the direction of that force? Both must be yes.

(i) There is a force, but the wall moves 0 m. Distance is zero, so work is 0 J. No work.

(ii) The lifting force on the bag points upwards. The bag travels sideways. The movement is not in the direction of the force, so no work is done against gravity.

(iii) There is an upward force and the box moves upwards. Same direction, so work is done. This is the only one of the three.

So the answer is (i) and (ii). Remember: tired arms are not evidence of work.

8.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

8.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A child on a swing is momentarily still at the highest point. Does she have any energy at that moment? Explain.

Show answer

Yes. She has no kinetic energy, because kinetic energy is the energy of movement and she is not moving at that instant.

But she is at her greatest height, so her gravitational potential energy is at its maximum. All the energy of the swing is stored in that form at the top.

That store is what makes her speed up as she falls back down. Being still and having no energy are not the same thing.

2

A rubber ball dropped from 1 m only bounces back to 60 cm. Explain what happened to the missing energy.

Show answer

No energy went missing. Energy cannot be destroyed, so all of it is still there — just in different forms.

When the ball hit the floor it squashed and sprang back, and that squashing produced heat in the rubber and in the floor. The bounce also made a sound, and sound carries energy away. A small amount was also lost to air resistance as heat.

Because some of the energy left as heat and sound, less was available to lift the ball, so it reached only 60 cm.

3

A man pushes a heavy crate with all his strength for two minutes. The crate does not move. How much work has he done on the crate? Explain.

Show answer

Zero joules. Work done = force × distance moved in the direction of the force. The distance is 0 m, so whatever the force was, the work is 0 J.

He has certainly used energy — his muscles converted chemical energy from food into heat, which is why he is hot and tired. But none of that energy went into the crate.

In science, "work" means a force that actually moves something. Effort alone does not count.

4

Write out the energy conversion chain for a battery-powered torch, and say which part is useful and which is wasted.

Show answer

Chemical energy in the battery → electrical energy in the wires → light energy and heat energy at the bulb.

The light is the useful output, since that is what a torch is for. The heat is wasted, which is why a bulb left on for a while becomes warm to touch.

The heat is not destroyed and not created out of nothing. It spreads into the surrounding air, where it is no longer useful to us.

8.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 8.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

A ball is thrown straight upwards. Which statements about the ball at its highest point are correct?

  • 1Its kinetic energy is at its maximum.
  • 2Its gravitational potential energy is at its maximum.
  • 3Kinetic energy has been converted into gravitational potential energy.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. At the highest point the ball has stopped rising and is momentarily still, so its kinetic energy is at its minimum, not maximum. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is correct, because the ball is at its greatest height. Statement 3 is correct, because the ball slowed down as it rose — the kinetic energy it started with became potential energy. Consistent with C.

2

A rubber ball is dropped and bounces several times, each bounce lower than the last. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Some energy is converted into heat and sound at each bounce.
  • 2The total amount of energy in the ball and its surroundings stays the same.
  • 3Energy is destroyed each time the ball hits the ground.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Squashing the rubber warms it, and each impact makes a noise. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Energy is never destroyed. It is converted into forms that spread out and are no longer useful. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. That is exactly what conservation of energy means. So the answer is B.

Watch for this pair: statements 2 and 3 contradict each other, so they cannot both be right. Spotting a contradiction is often faster than the physics.

3

Which statements about work done are correct?

  • 1Work is done only when a force makes an object move in the direction of that force.
  • 2Work done is calculated as force multiplied by distance.
  • 3Work done is measured in joules.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Both conditions are needed: a force, and movement in the direction of that force. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Work and energy share the same unit, the joule. That points to A.

Statement 2 — correct. Work done = force × distance moved in the direction of the force. So the answer is A.

All three being correct is uncommon, so check carefully before choosing A — but do not avoid it out of suspicion.
