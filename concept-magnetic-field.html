Concept

Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where its force can be felt.

Introduced in Chapter 4: Magnetism

1

What it means

Slide a paper clip slowly towards a magnet across a table. For most of the journey nothing happens. Then, at some particular distance, the clip suddenly jumps. You have just found the edge of the region where the magnet's influence is strong enough to matter. That region is the magnetic field.

The word field is doing real work here. It means the magnet changes the space around itself, whether or not anything is there to be pushed. The field exists in empty air; the paper clip merely reveals it. This is a genuinely strange idea, and it was resisted for a long time, because it means a magnet acts on a place rather than directly on a distant object.

A field has both a strength and a direction at every point, which is why we draw it with arrows rather than shading. Scatter iron filings on paper over a bar magnet and each filing becomes a tiny compass, turning to line up with the field where it lies. Together they trace out curved lines running from one pole to the other. Those are field lines, and by convention we draw them leaving the north pole and entering the south.

2

How it works

Field lines are a drawing device, not physical threads. Nothing is actually looped through the air. But the drawing carries two pieces of real information, and reading them properly is most of the skill.

First, the direction of a line at any point is the direction a compass needle's north pole would turn if placed there. Second, how closely packed the lines are tells you the strength. Lines crowd together near the poles, which is exactly where a magnet picks up the most paper clips, and they spread far apart at the sides, where the pull is feeble. Anywhere you see lines bunching, the field is strong.

Field lines never cross. The reason is simple once you see it: a crossing point would be a place where a compass needle was told to point in two directions at once, which is impossible. If a diagram shows crossing lines, the diagram is wrong.

When two magnets are brought together their fields combine, and the resulting pattern shows you the force. Put a north pole facing a south pole and the lines run straight across the gap from one to the other, as though pulling them together. Put two north poles facing and the lines from each refuse to meet, curving sharply away and leaving a bare spot between the magnets where the fields cancel exactly. That spot is called a neutral point, and a compass placed there would sit undecided.

The Earth has a field of the same shape, produced by molten iron circulating in its outer core, and it stretches tens of thousands of kilometres into space. It is weak — far weaker than a fridge magnet at close range — but it is everywhere, which is enough to turn a freely suspended needle. It also does something more important: it deflects much of the stream of charged particles arriving from the Sun, and where those particles are funnelled down near the poles they make the aurora glow.

The heart of it

A field is the magnet's influence spread through the space around it, and the field lines we draw record its direction by where they point and its strength by how tightly they crowd.

3

Where the idea came from

The idea belongs mainly to Michael Faraday, working at the Royal Institution in London through the 1830s and 1840s. Faraday had left school early and been apprenticed to a bookbinder; he had almost no formal mathematics, and this turned out to shape his thinking in a productive way. Unable to reason in equations, he reasoned in pictures.

The pictures came from experiment. Faraday scattered iron filings around magnets and watched the curved patterns form, and he came to believe the patterns were showing him something real in the space itself. He called them lines of force, and argued that a magnet does not reach across a gap to grab a distant object but instead alters the space between, which then acts locally on whatever is there.

Most physicists of the day found this unconvincing. The prevailing view, inherited from Newton's treatment of gravity, was that forces simply act at a distance across empty space, and Faraday's lines struck many as an untidy picture drawn by a man who could not do the mathematics.

He was vindicated by James Clerk Maxwell, who in the 1860s translated Faraday's picture into a set of equations and showed it worked — and showed further that changing electric and magnetic fields travel as waves at the speed of light, which is what light is. Maxwell was explicit that he had begun from Faraday's ideas. The field concept then spread far beyond magnetism and is now among the most important ideas in all of physics. It is worth remembering that it started with a man watching iron filings arrange themselves on a sheet of paper.

4

What people get wrong

The most common misunderstanding is treating field lines as real objects — invisible strings or tubes that a paper clip slides along. There are no lines in the space around a magnet. The field is smooth and continuous everywhere; the lines are marks we draw to record it, and we could equally well have drawn twice as many.

The second is thinking the field stops at the last line drawn. Diagrams have to end somewhere, but the real field weakens gradually with distance and never reaches a hard boundary. It simply becomes too weak to notice.

Commonly missed

A diagram shows the field around a bar magnet. At point A the lines are close together; at point B, further out to the side, they are widely spaced. A pupil says the force is stronger at B because the lines have more room to spread. Is she right?

The trap is reading the spacing backwards, and the wording is designed to encourage it by suggesting that spread-out lines mean an expanded influence.

How to work it: Recall what spacing encodes. The lines are packed most tightly at the poles, and the poles are where a magnet demonstrably picks up the most paper clips — that is the check that fixes the rule. Closely spaced lines therefore mean a strong field. So the field is stronger at A, not B. When unsure, go back to the physical test rather than the picture.

5

Connected ideas

A magnetic field is what a magnet produces, so the two ideas cannot be separated. An electric current produces a field of the same kind, which is what makes an electromagnet possible, and a field is one way of describing how a force acts without contact — gravity is described with fields in exactly the same way.
