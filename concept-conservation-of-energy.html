Concept

Conservation of Energy

Energy can move from place to place and change from one form to another, but the total amount in a closed system never gets larger or smaller.

Introduced in Chapter 8: Energy and Its Changes

1

What it means

"Conserved" here does not mean saved or used carefully, as it does when people talk about conserving water. In science, a conserved quantity is one whose total cannot change, whatever happens. You could not use it up if you tried.

The rule says nothing about energy staying in the same form, or the same place, or remaining useful. All of those change constantly. It says only that if you draw a boundary around a region and let nothing cross it, then add up every scrap of energy inside — motion, height, heat, chemicals, light, sound — the sum you get today is the sum you will get tomorrow.

That boundary matters, which is why the rule is stated for a closed system: something nothing can enter or leave. Count only the ball and forget the floor and air, and energy will appear to vanish. It simply crossed your boundary, because you drew it too small.

2

How it works

The chapter uses this rule to explain a swing and a bouncing ball. What it does not say is why the rule is so much more powerful than it sounds, and the reason is worth knowing: conservation lets you answer questions without knowing what happened in between.

Take a roller coaster. Working out its speed at the bottom of a drop by tracking forces would be extremely hard, because the track curves and the angle changes at every instant. But if you know the height it fell from, you can say immediately how much potential energy became kinetic, and therefore how fast it is going. The messy middle cancels out; only the start and finish matter. This is why conservation laws are treasured in physics: they are shortcuts that hold even when the details are impossible.

The rule also lets you argue backwards, which is where most marks are. If a swing ends up lower than it started, you know with certainty that energy left the swing, and you can go looking for where. Rubbing at the pivot warmed the metal. Air resistance warmed the air. The rope creaked, making sound. Conservation turns "some energy disappeared" from an answer into a question you are obliged to answer.

There is a limit worth stating honestly. Conservation says energy is never destroyed, but not that it stays useful. A joule of heat spread through a warm room is still there, but you cannot get it back out to lift anything. As energy passes through conversions it tends to spread into heat, and spread-out heat will not gather itself up again. So the world will never run out of energy, but it can certainly run out of concentrated energy.

The heart of it

Because the total cannot change, "where did the energy go" is always a fair question with a real answer, and you are never allowed to answer it with "it was lost".

3

Where the idea came from

Two people arrived at this independently in the 1840s, by completely different routes, and both were dismissed at first.

Julius Robert Mayer was a German ship's doctor, not a physicist. On a voyage to Java in 1840 he was bleeding feverish sailors, a standard treatment then, and noticed their venous blood was unusually bright red. He reasoned from there: venous blood is dark when the body has taken a lot of oxygen out of it. In the tropics the body needs less heat, so it burns less oxygen, so more is left in the blood and it looks redder. That told him body heat comes from burning food, which meant heat and work were interchangeable. He published the mechanical equivalent of heat in 1842.

James Prescott Joule, an English brewer's son, reached the same place by the opposite method: years of painstaking experiment, measuring tiny temperature rises in water stirred by falling weights. His figure was published in 1847, and the unit of energy now carries his name.

Both were initially ignored, Mayer especially. He was an outsider with no position in physics, and his reasoning struck professional physicists as philosophy rather than science. That reception is part of the story: a correct idea does not announce itself, and who says something affects whether it is heard.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is thinking the rule has exceptions. A ball stops bouncing, a swing stops swinging, a toy car runs down — surely energy went missing. It did not. In each case it left the object as heat and sound, and had you drawn the boundary around the whole room instead of the object, the total would have held exactly.

A second error is the opposite: applying the rule where the system is not closed. Saying the energy of a rolling ball must stay constant is wrong, because the floor and air constantly take energy from it. The rule applies to the total, not to whichever object you happen to be watching.

Commonly missed

A pendulum is released from a height of 20 cm. On the far side it rises to only 18 cm. Statement: energy has been destroyed. Is this correct?

The trap is that the numbers are given, and 2 cm of height really has gone. It looks like evidence, and a plain reading of the data seems to say energy has been lost.

How to work it: the statement is wrong, and the reason is the boundary. Energy has left the pendulum, which is why it rises less high, but it has not left the room. Friction warmed the pivot. Air resistance warmed the air, and made a faint sound as well. Add pendulum, pivot, air and sound together and the total is unchanged. Always name what the energy became and where it went.

5

Connected ideas

This rule governs every energy conversion, so it sits behind kinetic and potential energy swapping on a swing, and behind work as a transfer of energy. Friction is the usual reason energy leaves the system you were watching, and heat is nearly always where it goes.

In one line

Energy is never created or destroyed, only moved and converted, so whenever something appears to lose energy the real task is to name the form it turned into and the place it went.
