Concept

The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is the warming of the Earth caused by gases in the atmosphere that let sunlight in but slow the escape of heat radiated back out.

Introduced in Chapter 13: Cycles and the Environment

1

What it means

The first thing to be clear about is that the greenhouse effect is not a fault. It is why the Earth is habitable. Without it the average surface temperature would be about minus 18 degrees Celsius, and the oceans would be frozen. With it, the average is about plus 15. The natural greenhouse effect is worth roughly 33 degrees, and life depends on it entirely.

What has gone wrong is not the effect but its strength. By adding extra greenhouse gases to the atmosphere — chiefly carbon dioxide from burning fossil fuels, and methane — humans have made the effect stronger than it was. This is called the enhanced greenhouse effect, and it is the cause of global warming. Keeping these two apart matters: one is natural and essential, the other is an intensification of it that we have caused.

The gases responsible are called greenhouse gases. Carbon dioxide and methane are the ones most discussed, but water vapour is actually the most abundant, and there are others including nitrous oxide and ozone. What they share is a particular ability to absorb the kind of radiation the Earth gives off.

2

How it works

The mechanism turns on a single fact: hot objects and cool objects emit different kinds of radiation. The Sun's surface is around 5,500 degrees Celsius, and it radiates mostly as visible light and shorter wavelengths. The Earth's surface is around 15 degrees Celsius, and it radiates too — everything above absolute zero does — but at a much longer wavelength, in the infrared, which we feel as heat rather than see as light.

Now follow the energy. Sunlight passes down through the atmosphere. Greenhouse gases are largely transparent to visible light, so most of it gets through and is absorbed by the ground and oceans, warming them. The warmed surface then radiates energy back upwards as infrared. And here the gases behave differently: carbon dioxide, methane and water vapour absorb infrared strongly. The molecules absorb it, gain energy, and shortly re-emit it in a random direction — some upwards, some sideways, and some back down towards the surface. That returned energy warms the ground further.

So the atmosphere behaves rather like a one-way valve for energy: easy to enter as light, harder to leave as heat. Energy still escapes eventually — it must, or the Earth would heat without limit — but it is delayed, and the surface settles at a higher temperature than it otherwise would.

Why do some gases absorb infrared while others do not? It comes down to molecular structure. Infrared radiation makes molecules vibrate — bonds stretching and bending — but a molecule can only absorb it if that vibration changes how its electric charge is distributed. Nitrogen and oxygen are made of two identical atoms, perfectly symmetrical, so their vibrations do not produce that change and they are transparent to infrared. Carbon dioxide, with three atoms, can bend in ways that break its symmetry, and methane and water vapour are asymmetric too. This is why 99 per cent of the atmosphere does almost nothing, while trace gases do the entire job.

Small quantities matter here. Carbon dioxide is about 0.04 per cent of the air, but the concentration has risen from roughly 280 parts per million before industrialisation to over 420 now, an increase of about half again, and global average temperature has risen by around 1.2 degrees Celsius since the late 1800s.

The heart of it

Sunlight comes in as visible light and leaves as infrared, and greenhouse gases are transparent to the first but not to the second.

3

Where the idea came from

Joseph Fourier in France reasoned in the 1820s that the Earth ought to be much colder than it is, given its distance from the Sun, and suggested the atmosphere retains some heat. He had the outline of the question, though not the mechanism.

The first known experimental demonstration was made by Eunice Newton Foote, an American scientist and campaigner for women's rights. In 1856 she filled glass cylinders with different gases, placed them in sunlight and compared how much they warmed. The cylinder of carbon dioxide became hottest and stayed warm longest. She concluded that an atmosphere richer in that gas would give the Earth a higher temperature — an inference that reads remarkably well today. Her paper was presented at the American Association for the Advancement of Science meeting that year, but read aloud by a male colleague, Joseph Henry, since women did not present. Her work was then almost entirely forgotten for over a century, and was only widely recognised again after 2010.

Three years later, in 1859, the Irish physicist John Tyndall carried out far more precise measurements in London using a ratio spectrophotometer of his own design, measuring how strongly different gases absorbed infrared radiation specifically. Tyndall's work was more rigorous and identified the mechanism more exactly, and it is his name that became attached to the discovery. There is no evidence he knew of Foote's experiments. Both deserve credit, for different things.

In 1896 the Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius did the first quantitative calculation, working out by hand over roughly a year how much the Earth would warm if the carbon dioxide in the atmosphere were doubled by burning coal. His answer, around 5 to 6 degrees Celsius, is higher than modern estimates of about 3, but the calculation was of the right kind and the right order. Interestingly, Arrhenius expected the warming would take thousands of years and thought it might be beneficial to a cold country like Sweden. He was wrong about the timescale, chiefly because coal consumption grew far faster than he assumed.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is treating the greenhouse effect as inherently harmful. Without it the planet would be frozen. What is harmful is strengthening it.

The second is confusing it with the hole in the ozone layer. These are different problems with different causes, different gases and different consequences. Ozone depletion is about ultraviolet radiation getting in; the greenhouse effect is about infrared not getting out.

The third is the name itself. A real greenhouse stays warm mainly because the glass physically stops warm air escaping by convection — it traps the air. The atmosphere has no such barrier; it works by absorbing and re-emitting radiation. The analogy is useful but the physics is not the same, and it is worth knowing the name is a little misleading.

Commonly missed

Carbon dioxide makes up only about 0.04 per cent of the atmosphere, while nitrogen makes up 78 per cent. Explain how such a small proportion of gas can have such a large effect on the Earth's temperature.

The trap is assuming that influence must be proportional to quantity. In chemistry and physics this is often false — what matters is what a substance does, not how much of it there is.

How to work it: Ask what job needs doing and which molecules can do it. The job is absorbing the infrared radiation the warm Earth emits. Nitrogen and oxygen molecules are made of two identical atoms and are symmetrical, so their vibrations do not alter the distribution of electric charge within them, and they cannot absorb infrared. They are effectively transparent to it, so 99 per cent of the atmosphere contributes almost nothing however abundant it is. Carbon dioxide has three atoms and can bend and stretch in ways that do change its charge distribution, so it absorbs infrared strongly and re-emits some of it back towards the surface. A tiny amount of a gas that can do the job outweighs a vast amount of gas that cannot.

5

Connected ideas

The effect works through radiation, the transfer of heat by infrared waves through the atmosphere, and is strengthened by carbon released from fossil fuels into the carbon cycle. It sets the temperature that the world's climate settles around, and is often confused with the quite separate ozone problem.
