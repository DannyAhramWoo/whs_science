Concept

Biotic and Abiotic Factors

Biotic factors are the living or once-living parts of an ecosystem; abiotic factors are the physical conditions that were never alive.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

An ecosystem is made of living things and their non-living surroundings, and the two halves have names. Anything to do with life is a biotic factor; anything physical that was never alive is an abiotic factor. The words come from Greek: bios means life, and the a in front means "not".

Biotic factors include the plants and animals, the fungi and bacteria breaking down dead matter, and the relationships between them. Being hunted is a biotic factor, and so is competing for light, catching a disease, or depending on a bee for pollination.

The word is broader than "alive right now": biotic covers anything living or once living. Dead leaves, a fallen log, peat and the humus in soil are all biotic. They came from organisms, which matters because they still hold the carbon and nitrogen that living things built into them.

Books disagree here. Some treat dead leaves and rotting wood as biotic; others put dead organic matter in a separate box called detritus. Use the once-living rule unless told otherwise, and where there is room, saying why you sorted it that way is stronger than the label alone.

Abiotic factors are the physical conditions: light, temperature, water, oxygen and carbon dioxide, wind, soil pH, salinity, and the mineral part of soil such as sand and clay. None was ever alive. They set the stage on which living things act.

2

How it works

The split is a sorting tool, but the two halves keep acting on each other, so you can rarely explain anything using only one. Biotic changes abiotic. Trees are biotic, and their leaves block sunlight, so the light reaching a woodland floor may be a small fraction of that in the field next door — an abiotic change caused by something living. The shade keeps the ground cooler by day and warmer by night, and the canopy slows the wind and holds moisture in. Cut the trees down and all those conditions change within a season.

Abiotic decides biotic. Soil pH is a good example, pH being the measure of how acidic or alkaline something is. Some plants take up the minerals they need only within a narrow pH range, so acidic soil supports heather while chalky soil supports quite different plants a few miles away. Change nothing but the soil chemistry and you change the whole list of species, and with it the insects and birds that feed on them.

That leads to the most useful idea here: limiting factors. Whatever an organism needs most and has least of sets the limit, and no amount of everything else makes up for it. A plant on a woodland floor may have ample water, warmth and rich soil, and still grow slowly because light is short. Water it more and nothing happens; open a gap in the canopy and it grows at once. The limiting factor also shifts: a crop may be limited by water on a dry afternoon and by temperature in early spring. Fixing one limit only reveals the next.

The heart of it

Whatever is in shortest supply usually sets the limit, however plentiful everything else may be, and biotic and abiotic factors constantly reshape one another.

3

Where the idea came from

These two terms have no single inventor. They were built from Greek roots by nineteenth-century biologists and came into use gradually, so there is no discovery story to tell.

What does have a history is the insistence that non-living conditions belong in the same account as the organisms. Arthur Tansley coined ecosystem in 1935 for that reason, arguing against Frederic Clements, who had described a plant community as a kind of superorganism growing towards a mature form. Tansley objected that this made the vegetation the whole story and pushed soil, water and climate into the background when they were half of it.

The limiting factor idea has a firmer origin, from agriculture. Work on plant nutrition in the mid nineteenth century, associated with the German chemist Justus von Liebig, established that adding more of a nutrient a crop already has plenty of does nothing, since growth is held back by whichever is scarcest. It is often illustrated by a barrel of staves of different heights, which fills only to the shortest, though that picture was attached to Liebig's name later and we cannot be sure he used it.

4

What people get wrong

The classic trap is sorting dead things into the wrong column. Dead leaves, a fallen log and the humus in soil are all biotic, because biotic means living or once living. People file them under abiotic because they are not moving or growing. The question is not "is it alive now" but "did it ever come from something alive". A log did. A stone did not. A second error is naming factors from one column and stopping, when most explanations cross the line: shade from trees is a biotic cause with an abiotic effect.

Commonly missed

Sort these into biotic and abiotic: a fallen log, rainfall, soil pH, a fox, dead leaves on the ground, wind, a fungus, sand grains.

The trap is the fallen log and the dead leaves. They are motionless and clearly not alive, so they look physical, and they lie on the ground beside the sand where the real abiotic material is.

How to work it: Ask of each item whether it came from a living thing. Fallen log — from a tree, so biotic. Dead leaves — the same. Fox and fungus — alive, biotic. Rainfall, soil pH, wind and sand grains — never alive, so abiotic. The rule to carry away is that "biotic" asks about origin, not about being alive now. It matters because the log and leaves still hold carbon and nitrogen that decomposers will release into the soil, which a sand grain never will.

5

Connected ideas

Biotic and abiotic factors together make up an ecosystem, and those acting on one organism make up its environment. Abiotic conditions set the problems adaptations solve. Biotic factors include the feeding relationships of food chains and predator and prey pairs, and the decomposers returning once-living matter to the raw materials tracked by the carbon and water cycles.
