Concept

Rusting

Rusting is a chemical change in which iron reacts with both oxygen and water to form a new, crumbly orange-brown substance.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Leave a nail outside for a few weeks and it turns orange-brown and flaky. Leave the same nail in a dry cupboard indoors and it stays bright for years. That difference is the whole of rusting in one observation, and it tells you that something in the outside air is doing the damage.

Two things are doing it, and this is the part worth memorising: rusting needs both oxygen and water. Neither one alone is enough. Iron sitting in perfectly dry air does not rust however long you wait. Iron sitting in water with all the dissolved air removed does not rust either. Only when both are present does the reaction go, which is why damp air is so much worse for iron than either dry air or clean sealed water.

Rust itself is not iron with a coat of dirt on it. It is a genuinely new substance, formed by the iron combining with oxygen and water. Its everyday name is iron oxide. It is a different colour from iron, it is soft and crumbly instead of hard and strong, it flakes away instead of holding together, and it does not conduct electricity as iron does. Every one of those is a different property, which is how you know a new substance is there.

2

How it works

The standard experiment settles the question cleanly, and it is worth being able to describe from memory. Three test tubes, each with an identical iron nail. In the first, the nail sits in dry air with a drying agent in the bottom of the tube to remove all moisture, and the tube is stoppered. In the second, the nail sits in water that has been boiled to drive out dissolved air, with a layer of oil poured on top to stop air getting back in. In the third, the nail sits half in ordinary water and half in ordinary air, with the tube open.

After a week, only the third nail has rusted. The first had oxygen but no water. The second had water but no oxygen. The third had both. Because everything else about the three tubes was kept the same, the difference in result can only be caused by the difference in what was present, and that is what makes the experiment convincing rather than merely suggestive.

Rusting is a chemical change, and it is worth listing the reasons rather than just asserting it. A new substance with different properties has appeared. The change is not easily reversed — you cannot cool rust or squeeze it back into iron the way you can refreeze melted ice. And the rusted object gains mass, because the oxygen and water that joined the iron came from outside and are now part of the rust. Compare that with a physical change such as melting or bending, where the substance stays the same, the change reverses easily, and the mass does not budge.

Only iron and steel rust, since steel is mostly iron. Other metals do react with air and moisture over time, but that is called corrosion, not rusting. Copper slowly turns a green colour, which is why old copper roofs and statues look green. Aluminium reacts almost at once with air to form a thin, dull, tough layer that then seals the surface and protects the metal underneath, which is why aluminium window frames last so well without any treatment. Calling any of these rusting is a common slip.

Because you now know rusting needs oxygen and water, every method of prevention becomes predictable rather than something to memorise. Painting, oiling or greasing, and coating in plastic all put a physical barrier between the iron and the air, so neither oxygen nor water can reach the surface. Keeping tools dry removes the water. Galvanising means coating the iron with a layer of zinc, which both acts as a barrier and is itself attacked in preference to the iron underneath. Every method works by blocking oxygen, blocking water, or both — so if a barrier is scratched, rusting begins at the scratch.

One more useful fact: salt speeds rusting up considerably. It does not cause rust and it is not needed for it, but salty water makes the reaction go faster. That is why cars and railings near the sea rust so much sooner than the same objects inland, and why cars in countries that salt icy roads in winter suffer badly underneath.

The heart of it

Rusting needs oxygen and water together, so every way of preventing it is a way of keeping one or both away from the iron.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of the 1700s, scientists explained both burning and rusting with the phlogiston theory. The idea was that flammable and corrodible things contained a substance called phlogiston, and that burning or rusting was the process of releasing it. It was a serious theory that explained a good deal, and it is worth appreciating before laughing at it.

The observation that broke it was weighing. A metal that burns or corrodes ends up heavier than it started, not lighter. If rusting released something, the rust should weigh less than the iron. Supporters of phlogiston were reduced to suggesting it might have negative weight, which is the kind of repair that tells you a theory is in trouble.

In the 1770s and 1780s the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier did the careful weighing that settled it, working with sealed vessels and an accurate balance. He showed that the extra mass came from the air: the metal had combined with a gas, which he named oxygen, rather than lost anything at all. He was not the first to isolate that gas — Joseph Priestley and Carl Wilhelm Scheele have strong claims there — but he was the one who saw what it meant. Rusting and burning turned out to be relatives, both being iron or fuel joining with oxygen, one fast and hot, the other slow and cold.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is thinking that water alone causes rust, since rust obviously appears on wet things. Water alone will not do it, and the boiled-water test tube with its oil layer is the proof. The mirror-image error is thinking air alone causes it, which the drying-agent tube rules out just as firmly.

The second error is calling every corroded metal rusted. Green copper and dull aluminium are corroded, not rusted, because rusting is specifically what happens to iron and steel.

The third is expecting rust to weigh less than the iron it came from, because it looks so ruined and flaky. It weighs more, since the oxygen and water joined the iron rather than leaving it.

Commonly missed

Three identical iron nails are set up. Tube A holds a nail in dry air over a drying agent, stoppered. Tube B holds a nail in boiled water with a layer of oil on top. Tube C holds a nail in ordinary water, open to the air. After one week, which nail has rusted, and explain the result for each tube.

It is a trap because tube B looks the wettest and tube A looks the most exposed, so both attract an answer if you are matching pictures rather than reasoning.

How to work it: Start from the requirement: rusting needs oxygen and water. Tube A has oxygen from the air but the drying agent has removed the water, so no rusting. Tube B has plenty of water, but boiling drove out the dissolved air and the oil layer stops more getting in, so there is no oxygen and no rusting. Tube C has water below and air above, so both are present and the nail rusts, most obviously near the surface of the water where it meets both at once. Only C rusts, and because the three tubes are identical in every other way, the experiment shows both conditions are needed.

5

Connected ideas

Rusting is the clearest slow example of a chemical change, sitting alongside combustion as a fast one — both are substances combining with oxygen. Recognising rust as a new substance depends on comparing properties, and understanding that rust cannot simply be filtered or strained off the iron depends on knowing the difference between a mixture and a compound.
