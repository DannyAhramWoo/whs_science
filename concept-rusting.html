Concept

Rusting

Rusting is a chemical change in which iron reacts with both oxygen and water to form a new, crumbly orange-brown substance.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Leave a nail outside for a few weeks and it turns orange-brown and flaky. Leave the same nail in a dry cupboard and it stays bright for years. That is the whole of rusting in one observation.

Rusting needs both oxygen and water, and this is the part worth memorising. Neither alone is enough. Iron in perfectly dry air does not rust however long you wait, and iron in water with the dissolved air removed does not rust either. Only when both are present does the reaction go, which is why damp air is so much worse for iron.

Rust is not iron with a coat of dirt on it. It is a genuinely new substance, formed by the iron combining with oxygen and water, and its everyday name is iron oxide. It is a different colour, crumbly instead of strong, and it flakes away — all different properties, which is how you know a new substance is there.

2

How it works

The standard experiment settles the question. Three test tubes, each with an identical iron nail. In the first, the nail sits in dry air with a drying agent to remove all moisture, stoppered. In the second, it sits in water boiled to drive out dissolved air, with a layer of oil on top to stop air getting back in. In the third, it sits half in ordinary water and half in ordinary air, tube open.

After a week, only the third nail has rusted. The first had oxygen but no water; the second had water but no oxygen; the third had both. Because everything else was kept the same, the difference in result can only come from the difference in what was present.

Rusting is a chemical change. A new substance has appeared, the change is not easily reversed — you cannot cool rust back into iron as you can refreeze melted ice — and the object gains mass, because the oxygen and water came from outside and are now part of the rust. Compare melting, a physical change, where the substance stays the same and the mass does not budge.

Only iron and steel rust, since steel is mostly iron. Other metals react with air and moisture over time, but that is corrosion: copper slowly turns green, and aluminium forms a thin dull layer that seals and protects the metal underneath. Calling either rusting is a common slip.

Because rusting needs oxygen and water, prevention is predictable rather than something to memorise. Painting, oiling and coating in plastic put a barrier between the iron and the air; keeping tools dry removes the water; galvanising coats the iron with zinc, which is itself attacked in preference to the iron beneath. Every method blocks oxygen, water, or both, so a scratched barrier starts rusting at the scratch. Salt does not cause rust but speeds it up, which is why cars near the sea rust sooner.

The heart of it

Rusting needs oxygen and water together, so every way of preventing it is a way of keeping one or both away from the iron.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of the 1700s, scientists explained both burning and rusting with the phlogiston theory: the idea that flammable and corrodible things contained a substance called phlogiston, and that burning or rusting released it. It was a serious theory that explained a good deal.

The observation that broke it was weighing. A metal that burns or corrodes ends up heavier, not lighter. If rusting released something, the rust should weigh less. Supporters were reduced to suggesting phlogiston might have negative weight, the kind of repair that tells you a theory is in trouble.

In the 1770s and 1780s the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier did the weighing that settled it. He showed the extra mass came from the air: the metal had combined with a gas, which he named oxygen, rather than lost anything. He was not the first to isolate that gas — Joseph Priestley and Carl Wilhelm Scheele have strong claims — but he saw what it meant. Rusting and burning are relatives, one slow and cold, the other fast and hot.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is thinking water alone causes rust, since rust appears on wet things. It will not, and the boiled-water tube with its oil layer is the proof. The mirror-image error is thinking air alone causes it, which the drying-agent tube rules out just as firmly.

Two others are worth naming. Calling every corroded metal rusted is wrong, since rusting is specifically what happens to iron and steel. And rust is often expected to weigh less than the iron, because it looks so ruined; it weighs more, since the oxygen and water joined the iron.

Commonly missed

Tube A holds a nail in dry air over a drying agent, stoppered. Tube B holds a nail in boiled water with a layer of oil on top. Tube C holds a nail in ordinary water, open to the air. After one week, which nail has rusted, and why?

It is a trap because tube B looks the wettest and tube A the most exposed, so both attract an answer if you match pictures rather than reason.

How to work it: Rusting needs oxygen and water. Tube A has oxygen but the drying agent removed the water, so no rusting. Tube B has plenty of water, but boiling drove out the dissolved air and the oil stops more getting in, so no oxygen and no rusting. Tube C has water below and air above, so both are present and the nail rusts. Only C rusts, and because the tubes are identical otherwise, both conditions are shown to be needed.

5

Connected ideas
