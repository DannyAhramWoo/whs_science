Concept

Radiation

Radiation is the transfer of heat by invisible waves that travel through empty space and need no material at all.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Step out of the shade on a clear day and you feel the Sun's warmth on your face immediately. Now consider what lies between your face and the Sun: about 150 million kilometres, almost all of it empty space with no air, no particles, nothing to conduct through and no fluid to circulate. Conduction and convection are both impossible across that gap, yet the energy arrives. It arrives by radiation.

Radiation is energy travelling as infrared waves — waves of the same family as visible light, but with a slightly longer wavelength that our eyes cannot detect. Being a form of light, they travel at the speed of light and need no medium whatever. This is the only one of the three heat transfers that works in a vacuum, and it is the reason the Earth is warm at all.

Everything emits radiation, all the time. You are emitting infrared as you read this. The hotter an object is, the more it emits, and heat it enough and the radiation shifts into wavelengths the eye can see — which is why an iron bar in a forge glows first dull red, then orange, then white.

2

How it works

Radiation is produced by the movement of charged particles inside matter. Since every object above absolute zero has particles in motion, every object radiates, and it does so whether or not anything else is nearby. There is no need for contact and no need for a fluid.

An object also absorbs the radiation falling on it from everywhere else. What matters is the balance. A hot object emits more than it absorbs and so cools; a cold object absorbs more than it emits and so warms. When the two rates match, the object holds steady. Heat still flows in both directions constantly — it just cancels out.

Surfaces matter enormously, and here is the rule worth remembering: dull black surfaces are the best emitters and the best absorbers; shiny silver surfaces are the worst at both. The same property governs both directions, which sounds paradoxical until you see the logic — a surface that easily lets radiation pass into it will just as easily let radiation out. A good absorber is necessarily a good emitter.

This explains a long list of everyday choices. Solar panels are black to absorb as much as possible. The cooling fins on the back of a fridge are black to emit heat efficiently. Marathon runners are wrapped in shiny foil blankets, which reflect their body radiation back rather than letting it escape. Houses in hot countries are painted white to reflect sunlight. Wearing a black shirt in strong sun genuinely makes you hotter.

A vacuum flask is a fine piece of design because it defeats all three transfers at once. Its double wall with the air pumped out between stops conduction and convection, since neither works without particles. But radiation would still cross that vacuum, so the facing surfaces are silvered to reflect it back. The stopper blocks convection out of the top. Every feature is aimed at one specific mechanism, which is why a flask is such a good test of whether the three routes are properly understood.

The heart of it

Radiation needs no matter to travel through, and a surface that absorbs it well emits it just as well.

3

Where the idea came from

Infrared was found by accident during a measurement of something else. In 1800 the astronomer William Herschel, working in England, was looking for a way to observe the Sun safely and wondered whether different colours of sunlight carried different amounts of heat. He split sunlight with a prism into a spectrum on a table and placed thermometers in each colour band, with one set aside as a control.

The temperature rose as he moved from violet towards red. Then he checked the thermometer he had put just beyond the red end, in what looked like plain dark tabletop where no light fell — and found it read higher than any of the colours. Something was arriving there that could not be seen. Herschel called them calorific rays; we call them infrared, meaning below red. It was the first evidence that light extends beyond what human eyes can register, and it opened the way to the discovery of ultraviolet the following year and eventually to the whole electromagnetic spectrum.

Understanding the surface rules took longer. John Leslie, a Scottish scientist, published experiments in 1804 using a metal cube with faces finished differently — one blackened, one polished — filled with hot water, and showed with a sensitive detector that the black face emitted far more strongly than the shiny one. The Leslie cube is still used in school laboratories today, essentially unchanged.

The three mechanisms were only separated gradually through the 1800s, and radiation was the hardest of the three to accept, because heat crossing a vacuum seemed to require action at a distance with nothing in between. Its full explanation had to wait for Maxwell's demonstration in the 1860s that light is an electromagnetic wave, and then for work on how hot bodies radiate that led, in Max Planck's hands in 1900, to the beginnings of quantum physics.

4

What people get wrong

The first difficulty is the word itself. In everyday news "radiation" usually means the dangerous kind emitted by radioactive materials. Thermal radiation is not that. It is infrared light, the same family as the light from a lamp, and it is entirely harmless at ordinary intensities. The shared word is an accident of language, not a shared danger.

The second error is thinking only hot things radiate. Every object above absolute zero radiates, including ice. An ice cube emits infrared continuously; it simply absorbs more from the warm room than it gives out, so on balance it gains energy and melts.

Commonly missed

A shiny silver teapot and a dull black teapot are identical in every other way and are filled with equally hot tea. Which cools faster, and would your answer change if both were instead left empty in strong sunshine?

The trap is in the second half. Many people correctly say the black pot cools faster, then reverse themselves in the sunshine case, thinking that a good emitter must be a poor absorber.

How to work it: Apply the single rule that good emitters are also good absorbers. Full of hot tea, the black pot loses heat by radiation faster, so the black one cools faster. Empty in sunshine, the same black surface now absorbs incoming radiation better, so the black one heats up faster. Both answers are the black pot, and they do not contradict each other — the surface is better at radiation in whichever direction the heat happens to be flowing.

5

Connected ideas

Radiation is the third route for heat and the only one that works across empty space, unlike conduction and convection which both need matter. Being a form of light, it obeys the same rules of reflection that visible light does, and radiation trapped by gases in the atmosphere is the mechanism of the greenhouse effect.

In one line
