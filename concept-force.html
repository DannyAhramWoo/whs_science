Concept

Force

A force is a push or a pull that one object exerts on another, and its job is to change how something is moving or what shape it is.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

You lean on a door and it swings open. You pull a drawer and it slides towards you. Both times you acted on an object and it responded. That is a force: an action of one object on another that can change its motion or its shape.

The word "exerts" matters. A force is never a thing an object owns, like a colour or a mass; it is something happening between two objects. A rolling ball has speed and mass, but no force stored inside it. Force exists only while something acts on it — the floor rubbing underneath, the air pushing on the front. So "it ran out of force" is wrong: there was never any force inside the ball to run out of.

Force is measured in newtons, written N, after Isaac Newton. One newton is roughly the pull of gravity on a small apple. A force has both a size and a direction: ten newtons east and ten newtons west are not the same force, and the direction is usually where the marks are.

2

How it works

A force can start something moving, stop it, change its direction, or change its shape. The first three are one thing said three ways, since starting, stopping and turning are all changes in motion. So the shorter version is: a force changes motion, or changes shape, or both.

Why "changes"? Because an object left alone carries on doing exactly what it was already doing. If it was still, it stays still. If it was gliding at a steady speed in a straight line, it keeps gliding. Nothing is needed to maintain that; something is needed to interrupt it. Force is the name for that interruption.

How much interruption you get depends on how big the force is and how much matter you are shifting. Push an empty shopping trolley and it picks up speed quickly. Load it with sacks of rice, push just as hard, and it speeds up far more slowly, because the same push is spread across more mass. Heavier means harder to change. This is Newton's second law: force equals mass times acceleration.

One more feature catches almost everyone: forces come in pairs. Push a wall while standing on a skateboard and you roll backwards, because the wall pushes back on you just as hard. The two forces do not cancel, because they act on different objects: yours acts on the wall, the wall's acts on you.

Reading motion off a graph

Questions often hand you a graph instead of a sentence, and the whole answer sits in two features: the gradient (how steep the line is) and the area underneath it. Which one you want depends on what is up the side.

On a distance-time graph, the gradient is the speed, because gradient means distance gained divided by time taken, and that is what speed is. A steeper line means faster. A horizontal line means the distance is not changing, so the object is stationary — not "moving slowly", but stopped. A curve that steepens means it is speeding up.

On a speed-time graph, the same steepness now means something else. Gradient is speed gained divided by time taken, which is acceleration. So here a horizontal line means steady speed, not stopped. And on this graph the area under the line gives the distance travelled, because speed multiplied by time is distance.

Work one through. A cyclist speeds up from rest to 8 m/s in 4 s, holds 8 m/s for 10 s, then brakes to rest in 2 s. The acceleration at the start is the gradient: 8 m/s gained over 4 s, so 8 ÷ 4 = 2 m/s2. For the total distance, split the area into shapes. The first stage is a triangle, area = 1/2 × 4 × 8 = 16 m. The middle stage is a rectangle, 10 × 8 = 80 m. The braking stage is a triangle, 1/2 × 2 × 8 = 8 m. Adding them gives 104 m. Notice the braking section still has a positive area — the cyclist keeps moving forwards while slowing, so that distance counts. Only the gradient goes negative there.

One counter-intuitive force is worth adding here, because it is asked directly. When you walk, the force that pushes you forwards is friction from the ground. Your foot pushes backwards against the floor, and by the pairing rule the floor pushes forwards on you. That is why walking on ice is so hard: with almost no friction there is nothing to push you along.

The heart of it

A force is not something an object contains; it is something one object does to another, and its only effect is to change motion or change shape.

3

Where the idea came from

For nearly two thousand years, educated people in Europe followed the Greek philosopher Aristotle, who taught that a moving object needs a continuous push to keep moving. Take the push away, he said, and the motion stops.

It is easy to laugh at that now, but Aristotle was not being careless. He was doing what a good scientist does: generalising from what he could see. And everything he could see agreed with him. Carts stop when the ox stops pulling. A rolling ball comes to rest. In a world absolutely full of friction, "motion needs a cause" is the obvious conclusion. That is why it lasted so long — it worked.

The escape came from reducing friction rather than reasoning harder. Around 1600 Galileo Galilei rolled balls down one smooth slope and up another, and saw a ball rise to almost the height it started from — closer to that height the smoother he made the surfaces. Flatten the second slope towards horizontal and the ball had to roll further and further to reach that height. Galileo saw where this was heading: on a perfectly smooth level surface, the ball would never stop at all.

Isaac Newton built that insight into a system in Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica, published in 1687. There, force stopped being the cause of motion and became the cause of change in motion. That shift is the single most important correction in the history of this topic.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is Aristotle's, reinvented by nearly every learner: believing a moving object must have a forward force on it, or that force gets "used up" as it slows. A ball you have let go of has no forward force on it. It keeps going because nothing has stopped it yet, and it stops because friction and air resistance push it backwards.

The cure is a habit, not a fact. Do not ask "what is making it move". Ask "what is touching it, and what is pulling on it from a distance". Only things that touch the object, plus gravity and magnetism, belong on your list. "The force from the throw" does not, because your hand stopped touching the object the moment you let go.

Commonly missed

A ball has been kicked and is rolling across level ground, slowing down. Which forces are acting on it?

The trap is the word "kicked". It invites you to include a forward force from the kick, which feels right because the ball is still moving forwards. But the foot is long gone, and a force needs an object doing the pushing.

How to work it: list what is in contact with the ball right now — the ground and the air. The ground provides friction, acting backwards. The air provides air resistance, also backwards. Add the non-contact force always present, its weight pulling down, matched by the ground pushing up. So the only sideways force is backwards, which is exactly why the ball is slowing rather than speeding up.

5

Connected ideas

Force is the root of this whole chapter. Friction is one particular force, the one that resists sliding, and balanced and unbalanced forces are about what happens when several act at once. Inertia is the reluctance a force must overcome, mass measures how much of that reluctance an object has, and weight is the force gravity exerts on a mass.
