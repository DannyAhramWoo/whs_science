Concept

Force

A force is a push or a pull that one object exerts on another, and its job is to change how something is moving or what shape it is.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

You already know what a push feels like. You lean on a door and it swings open. You pull a drawer and it slides towards you. In both cases you did something to an object and the object responded. That is a force: an action of one object on another that can change the object's motion or its shape.

The word "exerts" matters more than it looks. A force is never a thing an object owns, like a colour or a mass. It is something happening between two objects. You cannot point at a ball rolling along the floor and say "that ball has 5 N of force in it". The ball has speed. It has mass. It does not have force stored inside it. Force only exists while something is acting on it — the floor rubbing underneath, the air pushing on the front, your hand at the moment you released it.

This is why the everyday phrase "it ran out of force" is wrong in physics even though it sounds sensible. There was never any force inside the ball to run out of.

Force is measured in newtons, written N, named after Isaac Newton. One newton is roughly the pull of gravity on a small apple — about 100 grams of it. A useful thing to hold on to: a force has both a size and a direction. Ten newtons east and ten newtons west are not the same force, and getting the direction right is usually where the marks are.

2

How it works

The chapter listed four things a force can do: start something moving, stop it, change its direction, or change its shape. It is worth noticing that the first three are really one thing said three ways. Starting, stopping and turning are all changes in motion. So the honest version of the list is shorter: a force changes motion, or a force changes shape, or both at once.

Why "changes"? Because an object that is left completely alone carries on doing exactly what it was already doing. If it was still, it stays still. If it was gliding along at a steady speed in a straight line, it keeps gliding. Nothing needs to be done to maintain that. Something needs to be done to interrupt it. Force is the name for that interruption.

How much interruption you get depends on two things: how big the force is, and how much matter you are trying to shift. Push a shopping trolley with a certain force and it picks up speed at a certain rate. Load the trolley with sacks of rice and push with the same force, and it picks up speed far more slowly. The same push spread across more mass produces less change. This is the reasoning behind Newton's second law, usually written as force equals mass times acceleration.

Try it with numbers. A 2 kg toy car pushed with a steady force of 6 N speeds up at 3 metres per second every second. Keep the 6 N but make the car 6 kg, and it speeds up at only 1 metre per second every second — three times the mass, one third of the effect. You will not be asked to do this arithmetic at this level, but knowing that the relationship is a fair division, not a mystery, makes the qualitative questions much easier: heavier means harder to change, always.

One more feature of forces catches almost everyone. Forces come in pairs. If you push on a wall, the wall pushes back on you with exactly the same size of force in the opposite direction. You can feel this if you push a wall while standing on a skateboard — you move backwards, and the only thing that could have pushed you is the wall. The two forces do not cancel each other, because they act on different objects: yours acts on the wall, the wall's acts on you.

The heart of it

A force is not something an object contains; it is something one object does to another, and its only effect is to change motion or change shape.

3

Where the idea came from

For nearly two thousand years, educated people in Europe followed the Greek philosopher Aristotle, who taught that a moving object needs a continuous push to keep moving. Take the push away, he said, and the motion stops. His view went essentially unchallenged for centuries.

It is easy to laugh at that now, but Aristotle was not being careless. He was doing what a good scientist does: generalising from what he could see. And everything he could see agreed with him. Carts stop when the ox stops pulling. A thrown stone lands. A rolling ball on a stone floor comes to rest. In a world absolutely full of friction, "motion needs a cause" is not a stupid conclusion. It is the obvious one. That is precisely why it lasted so long — it worked.

The escape came from reducing friction rather than reasoning harder. Around the turn of the seventeenth century Galileo Galilei rolled balls down and up smooth inclined planes and noticed something suggestive: a ball rolling down one slope and up another rose to almost the same height it started from, and the smoother he made the surfaces, the closer to "the same height" it got. Flatten the second slope towards horizontal and the ball had to keep rolling further and further to reach that height. Galileo saw where this was heading: on a perfectly smooth horizontal surface, the ball would never stop at all.

Isaac Newton took that insight and built it into a system in his book Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica, published in 1687. There, force stopped being the cause of motion and became the cause of change in motion. That shift — from motion to change of motion — is the single most important correction in the history of this topic.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is Aristotle's, arrived at independently by nearly every learner: believing that a moving object must have a forward force on it, or that a force gets "used up" as the object slows. Neither is true. A ball you have already let go of has no forward force on it whatsoever. It keeps going because nothing has stopped it yet, and it eventually stops because friction and air resistance push it backwards.

The cure is a habit, not a fact. Whenever you are asked about forces, do not ask "what is making it move". Ask "what is touching it, and what is pulling on it from a distance". Only things that touch the object, plus gravity, magnetism and electrostatic attraction, are allowed on your list. "Its own force" and "the force from the throw" are not on the list, because your hand stopped touching the object the moment you let go.

Commonly missed

A ball has been kicked and is rolling across level ground, slowing down. Which forces are acting on it?

The trap is the word "kicked". It invites you to include a forward force from the kick, which feels right because the ball is still moving forwards. But the foot is long gone, and a force needs an object doing the pushing.

How to work it: list what is in contact with the ball right now — the ground and the air. The ground provides friction, which acts backwards. The air provides air resistance, also backwards. Add the one non-contact force always present, its weight pulling it down, matched by the ground pushing up. So the only sideways force is backwards, which is exactly why the ball is slowing rather than speeding up.

5

Connected ideas

Force is the root of this whole chapter. Friction is one particular force, the one that resists sliding. Balanced and unbalanced forces are about what happens when several forces act at once. Inertia is the reason a force is needed at all — it is the reluctance a force has to overcome. Mass measures how much of that reluctance an object has, and weight is simply the name of the force gravity exerts on a mass. Work is what you call it when a force moves something through a distance.
