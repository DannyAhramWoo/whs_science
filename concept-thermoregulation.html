Concept

Keeping Body Temperature Steady

Your body holds itself near 37 degrees Celsius by detecting any drift away from that value and then acting to undo it — losing heat when too hot, making and saving heat when too cold.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Stand outside on a cold morning and the air might be 5 degrees Celsius. Sit in a hot room and the air might be 35 degrees. Your body, in both cases, stays close to 37 degrees. The air swings by thirty degrees; you swing by less than one. Something is clearly working hard.

That work is called thermoregulation — keeping the inside of the body at a steady temperature no matter what the outside is doing. It is one example of a bigger idea called homeostasis, which means holding the conditions inside the body steady: temperature, water, sugar, salt and more.

Animals that generate their own heat are called endotherms — mammals and birds. Animals that do not, such as lizards, fish and insects, are ectotherms; their body temperature drifts with the surroundings, which is why a lizard is sluggish on a cold morning and quick once the sun has warmed it. Plot body temperature against air temperature and an endotherm gives a near-flat line, an ectotherm a rising diagonal one. That flat line is the whole achievement.

The reason it matters is chemical. Your body runs on enzymes — proteins that speed up reactions, from digesting a sandwich to releasing energy inside a cell. Every enzyme has a particular shape, and that shape is what lets it grip the substance it works on. Too cold and molecules move sluggishly, so reactions crawl. Too hot and the enzyme's shape is damaged permanently; we say it is denatured, like the clear part of an egg turning white and never turning back. Around 37 degrees the body's enzymes work fast without being wrecked. The body defends 37 degrees because its chemistry only works there.

2

How it works

The control centre is a small region at the base of the brain called the hypothalamus. It measures the temperature of the blood flowing through it, and it also receives messages from temperature sensors in the skin. It compares what it finds against a target value — the set point, normally about 37 degrees. If the reading is above the set point, it triggers cooling. If below, it triggers warming. That pattern, where the body detects a change and then acts to reverse it, is called negative feedback. "Negative" here does not mean bad; it means the response pushes in the opposite direction to the change.

Cooling down. Three things happen when you are too hot. First, sweating: glands in the skin release watery sweat, and as that water evaporates it takes heat energy from the skin with it, which is why a breeze on damp skin feels cold and why sweating is far less effective on a humid day when water evaporates poorly. Second, vasodilation: the small blood vessels supplying the skin surface widen, so more warm blood flows close to the surface and loses its heat to the air. That extra blood near the surface is exactly why a person turns red when hot. Third, behaviour: you move into the shade, take off a jumper, drink something cold. Behaviour is not a lesser mechanism — it is often the fastest one available, and it is the only one an ectotherm has much of.

Warming up. The reverse set. Shivering is rapid involuntary contraction of skeletal muscles; muscle contraction is inefficient, and the energy that does not become movement becomes heat, so shivering is really a heater made of muscle. Vasoconstriction narrows the skin vessels, so less warm blood reaches the surface and less heat escapes — which is why cold hands look pale. Tiny muscles pull body hairs upright, trapping a layer of still air against the skin; air is a poor conductor of heat, so that trapped layer insulates. In humans the hairs are short and the effect is small, leaving us with goose bumps and not much else, but in a cat or a bird the same mechanism visibly doubles the animal's apparent size. The body also raises its metabolic rate — the overall speed of its chemical reactions — so more heat is produced throughout. And again, behaviour: curling up reduces the surface area losing heat, and putting on a coat adds insulation.

Fever. This is the part worth slowing down for, because it works in a way most people never guess. When bacteria or viruses invade, white blood cells release chemicals that travel to the hypothalamus and change the set point — raising it from 37 to, say, 39 degrees. The thermostat has been moved. The hypothalamus now compares your actual 37 degrees against a target of 39, finds you too cold, and triggers every warming response it has: shivering, vasoconstriction, a higher metabolic rate. So at the start of a fever you shiver and pile on blankets while your temperature climbs. Then, when the infection is beaten, the set point drops back to 37. Suddenly your actual 39 degrees is too hot, and the cooling responses fire — you sweat heavily and throw the blankets off. The sweating at the end of a fever is not the fever being fought; it is the fever being over.

Notice what this means. During the rising phase of a fever, sweating is the one response that is not happening, because sweating loses heat and the body is trying to gain it. That single observation answers a whole family of questions.

A moderate fever probably helps: many bacteria reproduce more slowly at higher temperatures, and parts of the immune response work faster. But the margin is thin. Above roughly 40 to 41 degrees the body's own enzymes begin to be damaged, which is why a very high fever is dangerous rather than merely uncomfortable.

The heart of it

The body defends about 37 degrees because its enzymes only work well there, correcting any drift by negative feedback — and in a fever it does not lose control of that thermostat, it deliberately moves the setting upwards.

3

Where the idea came from

For a long time it was not obvious there was anything to explain. Body heat was treated as a property a living thing simply had, sometimes described as a vital flame in the chest. Careful thermometers only became common in medicine in the nineteenth century, and until you can measure a thing reliably you cannot notice that it stays constant.

The decisive idea came from the French physiologist Claude Bernard, who proposed around 1865 that the cells of a complex animal do not live in the outside world at all. They live bathed in the body's own internal fluid, which he called the milieu interieur — the internal environment. His claim was that the freedom of an animal to roam a changing world depends entirely on keeping that internal fluid constant. Temperature was one instance of a much larger principle.

Bernard did not have the mechanism. The nerve pathways, the role of the hypothalamus and the chemical signals were worked out over the following decades by many researchers rather than any one person. In 1926 the American physiologist Walter Cannon gave Bernard's principle the name we still use, homeostasis, from Greek words meaning roughly "staying similar". Cannon chose "similar" rather than "same" on purpose: the internal conditions do wobble, and the point is that they are corrected, not that they never move.

Fever took even longer to understand, and the wrong idea lasted centuries. Fever was widely treated as the disease itself, something to be driven out of the body — which is part of why bloodletting stayed in use so long. The modern view, that fever is the body's own regulated response and often a useful one, only settled in the twentieth century once the chemical signals from immune cells were identified.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is thinking a fever means the body's temperature control has broken down. It has not. The controller is working precisely; it has simply been given a new target. A broken thermostat and a thermostat turned up look similar from outside and are completely different underneath.

The second is treating sweating as a general response to being ill. Sweating only ever loses heat, so it can never be part of raising temperature. It appears at the end of a fever, not the start.

A third is muddling the two vas- words. Vasodilation is vessels dilating, meaning widening, which loses heat and makes skin red. Vasoconstriction is vessels constricting, meaning narrowing, which saves heat and makes skin pale. If you can remember which way the redness goes, you can rebuild both.

One more worth naming: the hairs and the vessels are different systems. Hairs standing up trap insulating air. Vessels narrowing keeps blood away from the surface. Both warm you; neither causes the other.

Commonly missed

While a person's temperature is climbing during a fever, which one of these is NOT taking place: shivering, vasoconstriction, sweating, or a raised metabolic rate?

The trap is that sweating is strongly associated with being feverish, because people do sweat when a fever breaks. Anyone answering from memory of what an ill person looks like will keep sweating in and reject something else.

How to work it: Ignore what illness looks like and ask a single question of each option — does this gain heat or lose it? Shivering: muscles contracting produce heat, so it gains. Vasoconstriction: less blood at the surface, so less heat escapes, so it saves. Raised metabolic rate: faster chemical reactions release more heat, so it gains. Sweating: evaporation removes heat, so it loses. The temperature is rising, so the body wants heat gained and saved. Three options do that and one does the opposite. Sweating is the odd one out.

This angle — picking the response that does not belong to raising temperature, and reading a graph of body temperature against surrounding temperature to sort endotherms from ectotherms — has appeared in IMSO papers, including 2019.

Connected ideas

The whole reason for defending 37 degrees is that enzymes work well only in a narrow band, so the enzymes page explains the "why" behind this one. Heat and temperature supply the physics: evaporation carrying energy away, and air trapped in fur working as an insulator. Circulation is the machinery of vasodilation and vasoconstriction, since it is blood flow that is being redirected. Skeletal muscle is what shivers, and the kidneys handle the other great homeostatic job, water and salt. Microorganisms are what fevers are usually a response to.

In one line

The hypothalamus compares body temperature against a set point of about 37 degrees and reverses any drift — sweating and vasodilation to cool, shivering and vasoconstriction to warm — and a fever is that set point being deliberately raised, which is why you shiver while getting hotter.
