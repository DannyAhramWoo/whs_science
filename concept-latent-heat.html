Concept

Latent Heat and the Temperature Plateau

While a substance is melting or boiling its temperature does not rise, because the heat going in is being used to pull its particles apart rather than to speed them up.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Put a thermometer in a pan of water and heat it. The reading climbs steadily — 40, 60, 80, 100 degrees C — and then stops. The flame is still on, the water is boiling hard, and the thermometer sits at 100 and will not move until the last of the water has boiled away.

That refusal to rise is the thing to explain. Heat is going in and temperature is not going up, which sounds as though energy is disappearing. It is not. It is doing a different job.

Temperature measures how fast the particles of a substance are moving, on average. Heat is energy transferred because of a temperature difference. Usually adding heat speeds the particles up, and the thermometer reading rises. But particles in a solid or a liquid are held to one another by forces of attraction, and to change state those forces must be overcome. Pulling particles apart against an attraction takes energy, and while that is happening the energy goes into the separating and not into the speeding up.

Heat absorbed or released during a change of state, without any change of temperature, is called latent heat. Latent means hidden, and the name was chosen precisely because the energy is hidden from the thermometer.

2

How it works

Draw a heating curve: temperature up the side, time along the bottom, as ice is heated steadily from below zero all the way to steam. The graph has five parts and each one has a reason.

It rises from about −20 degrees C to 0 degrees C, as the ice warms and its particles vibrate faster. Then it goes flat at 0 degrees C while the ice melts — the first plateau. Then it rises again from 0 to 100 degrees C as the liquid water warms. Then it goes flat at 100 degrees C while the water boils — the second plateau, and a longer one. Then it rises again as the steam gets hotter.

The sloping parts are heating that changes temperature. The flat parts are heating that changes state. During a plateau, ice and water exist together, or water and steam exist together, and the temperature of the mixture stays fixed until the change is complete.

Now the crucial point, and it is the one examiners build questions around. The energy absorbed at the boiling plateau overcomes the forces of attraction between water molecules, so that molecules which were clinging together in the liquid can move apart into a gas. It does not break the bonds inside a water molecule. If those bonds broke, the hydrogen and oxygen would come apart and you would no longer have water — but steam is still H2O, exactly the same molecule as liquid water. Boiling is a physical change, not a chemical one. The wrong answer, that boiling breaks the bonds between hydrogen and oxygen, is the standard distractor and it is worth being able to reject it immediately. IMSO papers have set this in 2018 and 2009.

The second plateau is longer than the first, and that tells you something. Melting only loosens particles enough to let them slide past one another; they stay in contact. Boiling has to separate them completely, so that each one flies free with nothing holding it. Complete separation needs far more energy than mere loosening — for water, roughly seven times as much per gram — which is why boiling a pan dry takes so much longer than melting the ice that filled it.

Three consequences follow, and each is a standard question.

Steam scalds worse than boiling water. Both are at 100 degrees C, so temperature alone cannot explain the difference. When steam touches your skin it first condenses back to liquid, and condensing releases all the latent heat that was absorbed during boiling — that large quantity is dumped into your skin in an instant, on top of the heat the resulting hot water then gives up as it cools. Boiling water delivers only the second part.

A drink with ice in it stays at 0 degrees C. Heat leaking in from the room goes into melting the ice rather than warming the liquid, so the temperature holds steady. Only once the last piece of ice has melted does the drink begin to warm up. That is why ice keeps things cold so effectively, and why a drink cooled with ice behaves quite differently from one simply put in a cold place.

Sweating cools you. To evaporate, the water on your skin needs latent heat, and it takes that energy from the warmest thing in contact with it, which is you. Losing that energy is what lowers your skin temperature. This also explains why a breeze feels cooler — it carries evaporated water away so more can evaporate — and why stepping out of a swimming pool feels cold even on a hot day.

The heart of it

During melting or boiling the heat going in overcomes the attractions holding particles together instead of speeding them up, so the thermometer stays still until the change of state is finished.

3

Where the idea came from

Joseph Black, a professor at Glasgow and later Edinburgh, identified latent heat around 1761. His starting point was a puzzle about snow. If heat simply raised temperature, a thaw ought to be sudden: the moment the air rose above freezing, all the snow should turn to water at once and cause catastrophic floods. That plainly does not happen; snow melts gradually, over hours or days.

Black concluded that melting must absorb a large quantity of heat without any rise in temperature, and he tested it by measuring. He compared how long it took to melt a quantity of ice with how long it took to warm the resulting water by a measured number of degrees, and found the melting consumed far more heat. He called it latent, meaning hidden, because no thermometer could detect it.

Black's own explanation of what heat is was wrong. Like most of his contemporaries he thought heat was a weightless fluid, called caloric, that flowed from hot bodies into cold ones. That theory was abandoned in the nineteenth century in favour of heat as energy of particle motion, yet Black's measurements survived intact — careful numbers outlast the story told about them.

James Watt worked as an instrument maker at Glasgow while Black taught there, and the two knew one another. Watt was trying to understand why the Newcomen steam engine wasted so much fuel, and latent heat gave him the answer: the engine cooled its whole cylinder to condense the steam and reheated it every stroke, and the latent heat involved was enormous. His solution, patented in 1769, was a separate condenser, so the cylinder could stay hot while condensation happened elsewhere. How much Black taught Watt directly is debated and we cannot be sure, but the connection between the idea and the machine is real.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is assuming that continued heating must mean rising temperature, so that a flat section of graph means the heating stopped. It does not. The heat is still arriving and is fully accounted for; it is doing the work of separating particles.

The second, and the one that costs most marks, is saying that boiling breaks the bonds within the water molecule. It breaks the attractions between molecules. Steam is still H2O.

The third is forgetting that the process runs both ways. Freezing and condensing release latent heat rather than absorbing it, which is why steam scalds and why a bowl of water placed in a greenhouse on a frosty night gives out heat as it freezes and helps protect the plants.

Commonly missed

Water is heated steadily and boils at 100 degrees C. The heating continues for another five minutes while the water boils, and the thermometer stays at 100 degrees C the whole time. What happens to the heat energy supplied during those five minutes?

It is a trap in two ways. Some answer that the energy is lost or wasted, which breaks the conservation of energy. Others answer that it breaks the bonds holding hydrogen and oxygen together in the water molecule, which sounds impressively chemical and is wrong.

How to work it: Start from what temperature measures — the average speed of the particles. The temperature is constant, so the particles are not getting faster, so the energy is not going into their motion. It must therefore be going into changing their arrangement. In liquid water the molecules are attracted to one another and held close; in steam they are far apart and free. The supplied energy is used to overcome those attractions between molecules and pull them apart. The molecules themselves are unchanged: steam is H2O just as liquid water is, which is why this is a physical change and no chemical bond inside the molecule is broken.

5

Connected ideas

Latent heat is what actually happens during a change of state, and it explains why boiling and evaporation cool the liquid left behind. It depends on the particle model for its picture of attractions between particles, and it contrasts usefully with specific heat capacity, which is the heat needed to change a temperature rather than a state. Because pressure alters the boiling point, it also connects to how gases behave under pressure.

In one line

The temperature stays flat while something melts or boils because the heat is overcoming the attractions between particles instead of speeding them up, and that hidden energy is given back out again when the substance condenses or freezes.
