Concept

Speed-Time Graphs

A speed-time graph shows how fast something is going at every moment, and its shape tells you both the acceleration and the distance travelled.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

Imagine sitting in a car watching the speedometer. It reads 0 at the traffic light, climbs to 20 m/s as the car pulls away, holds steady, then drops back to 0 at the next junction. Write down the reading every second, plot those readings, and you have a speed-time graph.

Time runs along the bottom, in seconds. Speed runs up the side, in metres per second. Speed is distance divided by time — how much ground you cover each second. A car travelling 100 m in 20 s has a speed of 100 divided by 20, which is 5 m/s.

The important thing is what the height of the line means. Here the height is the speed right now. It is not distance. A line sitting flat at 5 m/s does not mean the object has stopped — it means it is moving steadily at 5 m/s.

2

How it works

Two measurements come out of the graph, and almost every question asks for one of them.

The first is gradient, which means steepness — how much the line rises for each second across. Gradient equals acceleration, the rate at which speed changes. Suppose a line climbs from 0 m/s to 20 m/s over 5 s. The rise is 20, the run is 5, so the gradient is 20 divided by 5, which is 4. The object gains 4 m/s of speed every second. A steeper line means a bigger acceleration. A line sloping downwards means slowing down. A flat line means zero acceleration, so a steady speed.

This makes the graph a detective tool. A sky diver's graph climbs steeply, then bends and levels off. If it suddenly drops sharply and flattens at a much lower speed, something has caused a large backwards force — that is the moment the parachute opened. The change in gradient told you, without being told.

The second measurement is area under the line, which equals the distance travelled. This works because speed multiplied by time gives distance, and multiplying height by width is exactly what finding an area does.

Here is a worked case. A car accelerates from 0 m/s to 10 m/s over the first 10 s, then holds 10 m/s for the next 10 s. What distance has it covered in the first 20 s? The shape underneath is a trapezium — a four-sided shape with one pair of parallel sides. Split it in two. The first 10 s form a triangle: half of base times height, so half of 10 times 10, which is 50 m. The second 10 s form a rectangle: 10 s wide by 10 m/s tall, which is 100 m. Total distance is 150 m. Splitting a trapezium this way is faster than recalling a formula.

You can also work backwards and draw a graph from a description. "A cyclist speeds up steadily from rest to 8 m/s in 4 s, rides at 8 m/s for 6 s, then brakes to a stop in 2 s." That is a line from the origin up to 8 at t = 4, a flat line across to t = 10, then a line down to zero at t = 12. Every phrase becomes one straight segment.

The heart of it

On a speed-time graph the gradient gives you the acceleration and the area underneath gives you the distance travelled.

3

Where the idea came from

Drawing motion as a picture is older than most people assume. In the 1300s thinkers at Merton College in Oxford, and separately Nicole Oresme in Paris, worked out that plotting speed against time gives the distance covered as the area underneath — centuries before anyone had a clock accurate enough to test it.

The testing fell to Galileo Galilei in Italy in the early 1600s. He could not time a falling body, as it fell too fast for his water clocks. So he slowed gravity down by rolling balls along a gently sloping groove and measured how far they travelled in equal intervals of time. The distances grew as 1, 3, 5, 7 and so on — exactly what a steadily rising speed-time line predicts.

He did not get there in one leap. For some years he held that a falling object's speed grew in proportion to the distance fallen rather than the time taken. That version is wrong, and he later abandoned it. The tale of him dropping two balls from the Leaning Tower of Pisa may well be embroidered — it comes mainly from a biography written by a pupil long afterwards, and we cannot be sure it happened.

4

What people get wrong

The single biggest error is mixing up a speed-time graph with a distance-time graph. They look similar and mean opposite things. On a distance-time graph the height is how far you have gone, so a flat line means the distance is not changing — the object is at rest. On a speed-time graph a flat line means the speed is not changing — the object is moving at a constant speed. Before reading any graph, check the label on the vertical axis.

The second error is finding the area when the question wanted the gradient. Anchor it to the units. Distance is in metres, and m/s multiplied by s gives metres, which is an area. Acceleration is in metres per second per second, and m/s divided by s gives that, which is a gradient.

Both turn up often in past competition papers. One IMSO paper shows a speed-time graph of a falling sky diver and asks where the parachute opened; another asks for the distance covered in the first 20 s from a trapezium shape.

Commonly missed

A graph has a horizontal line at a height of 6 for 10 s. What has the object done during that time?

The trap is answering "nothing" or "it stopped". A flat line looks like inactivity. But the answer depends entirely on which graph it is, and the question deliberately does not say.

How to work it: check the vertical axis first. If it reads speed, the object has been travelling at a steady 6 m/s, and in 10 s it has covered 6 times 10, which is 60 m. If it reads distance, the object has not moved at all — it has stayed 6 m from the start point for the whole 10 s. Same line, opposite answers.

5

Connected ideas

A graph only bends when a force acts, so every sloping section is the fingerprint of an unbalanced force, and every flat section means the forces have balanced. That is why a falling object's graph levels off at terminal velocity. Friction and air resistance produce the downward slopes, and reading these graphs quickly is a skill in its own right.
