Chapter 11

Seasons, Climate and Wind

Seasons come from the tilt of the Earth's axis, not from how far away the Sun is. Almost every mark lost on this topic comes from believing the distance story, so that is where we begin.

11.1

The tilt, not the distance

The Earth spins on an imaginary line through its centre called its axis. That axis is not upright. It leans over by about 23.5 degrees, and it keeps leaning the same way all year, wherever the Earth is in its orbit.

That fixed lean is what makes seasons. For half the year the northern half of the Earth leans towards the Sun. Six months later the Earth is on the other side of its orbit, so the northern half leans away — even though the axis itself never moved.

Many people believe instead that summer comes when the Earth is nearer the Sun. One fact settles it: the Earth is actually closest to the Sun in early January, in the middle of the northern winter. If distance controlled the seasons, January would be London's hottest month. It is its coldest.

The one sentence to keep

Seasons happen because the Earth's axis is tilted, so different parts of the Earth lean towards the Sun at different times of the year. Distance from the Sun has almost nothing to do with it.

Commonly missed

A student says: "It is summer in Britain in July because the Earth is closer to the Sun then." Explain why this cannot be right.

The trap is that it sounds sensible — nearer a fire means hotter — so students accept it without testing it.

How to work it: ask what the distance idea would predict. Distance from the Sun is the same for the whole planet at any moment, so every country would have summer together. But Australia has winter in July while Britain has summer. One planet cannot be near and far at once, so distance cannot be the cause. The tilt can, because it affects the two hemispheres in opposite ways at the same time.

11.2

Why leaning towards the Sun makes it warmer

Tilting changes two things at once, and both push in the same direction.

First, the angle of the sunlight. When your half of the Earth leans towards the Sun, light arrives from high overhead and strikes the ground almost straight on. All that energy lands on a small patch, so the patch heats quickly. When your half leans away, the same light comes in at a slant and smears across a much wider patch, giving less warmth per square metre.

Try it with a torch. Shine it straight down onto a table and you get a small bright circle. Tip the torch over and the circle stretches into a long dim oval. The torch has not changed — only how thinly its light is spread.

Second, the length of the day. The hemisphere leaning towards the Sun spends more of each spin in the light, so days are longer and there are more hours of heating. In winter the days are short, and the long night lets the ground cool down again.

Say it this way

Summer is not "more sunlight arriving at the planet". It is the same sunlight arriving more concentrated, for more hours.

11.3

Opposite seasons, day length, and why Singapore is different

Because the tilt leans one hemisphere in while leaning the other out, the two halves of the world always have opposite seasons. June is summer in Britain, Japan and Canada, and winter in Australia, Chile and New Zealand. December swaps them over.

Day length changes too, and it changes more the further you go from the equator. In London a June day lasts about 16 hours; a December day about 8. At the poles it becomes extreme: near the North Pole the Sun does not set at all for weeks in June, and does not rise at all in December, because the tilt holds the pole either permanently in the light or permanently in the shadow as the Earth spins.

Now look at the equator, where Singapore sits. The equator is the fattest part of the Earth and faces the Sun at a fairly steep angle all year, and a 23.5 degree tilt barely changes that. So sunlight stays roughly as concentrated in January as in July, and the day stays close to 12 hours every day of the year — sunrise near 7 am, sunset near 7 pm, in December and June alike.

That is why Singapore has no spring, summer, autumn or winter. It is hot all year, and what changes is rainfall rather than temperature.

Commonly missed

A graph shows sunrise and sunset times for a place over one year. The gap between them is widest in December and narrowest in June. Which hemisphere is this place in?

The trap is answering from the month rather than the graph. Students see "December" and think winter, because that is what December means where most textbooks are written.

How to work it: the gap between sunrise and sunset is the length of the day. Widest gap means longest day means summer. So this place has summer in December — and that happens only in the southern hemisphere. One more check: if the gap barely changed all year, the place would be near the equator, and day length alone could not tell you the hemisphere.

11.4

Weather and climate are not the same word

Weather is what the air is doing right now, or over the next few days — the temperature, rain and wind outside your window this afternoon. It changes hour by hour, and nobody can predict it reliably more than about a week ahead.

Climate is the pattern weather follows over many years, usually thirty or more. It answers "what is it normally like here?" rather than "what is it like today?".

So a cool morning in Singapore is weather. The fact that Singapore is hot and humid every month is climate. One rainy week does not make a desert's climate wet, because climate is an average over a very long run of years.

Weather vs climate

Weather is now. Climate is the pattern over decades. Weather tells you what coat to wear today; climate tells you what coats to own.

11.5

Wind: air sliding from high pressure to low

Wind is simply moving air, and air moves for one reason: a difference in air pressure. Air always flows from where the pressure is high to where it is low, the same way water runs downhill.

The pressure differences come from heating. Warmed air expands, so the same amount of air takes up more space and becomes less dense. Less dense air floats upwards, exactly as a bubble rises in water. That rising leaves fewer molecules pressing down below, so the pressure there drops. Elsewhere cooler, denser air sinks and presses down harder, raising the pressure. Air then slides along the ground from the high to the low, and that slide is the wind you feel.

The clearest example is at the coast, because land heats faster than sea by day and cools faster at night. That one fact produces two opposite breezes.

Sea breeze (daytime). The land gets hot, so air above it rises and leaves low pressure behind. The sea stays cooler, so the air above it is denser and the pressure higher. Air flows in from sea to land — a cool afternoon breeze off the water.

Land breeze (night). The land loses heat quickly and becomes colder than the sea. Now the rising air is over the warmer sea, so the low pressure is out there and the breeze blows from land to sea.

The rule that answers every wind question

Warm air rises and leaves low pressure behind. Cooler air sinks and makes high pressure. Wind blows from the high to the low — that is, from the cooler surface towards the warmer one.

Commonly missed

At a beach at 3 pm, in which direction does the breeze blow, and why?

Students often reason "the land is hot, hot air pushes outwards, so the wind blows off the land". That is exactly backwards. Rising air does not push sideways — it leaves a gap.

How to work it: three steps. (1) Which surface is warmer? At 3 pm the land, because land heats faster than water. (2) So air rises over the land, making pressure there low. (3) Wind runs high to low, so it blows from sea to land. The name helps: a sea breeze is named after where it comes from.

11.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

11.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

Explain why it is summer in Australia in December but winter in Japan at the same time.

Show answer

The Earth's axis is tilted by about 23.5 degrees and it keeps pointing the same way all year. In December the Earth is at the point in its orbit where the southern half leans towards the Sun and the northern half leans away.

Australia is in the south, so it receives direct, concentrated sunlight and long days — summer. Japan is in the north, so its sunlight arrives slanted and spread thin, and its days are short — winter.

The two hemispheres must always be opposite, because one cannot lean towards the Sun without the other leaning away.

2

Singapore is close to the equator. Explain why its day length hardly changes through the year, and why it has no summer or winter.

Show answer

The equator runs around the widest part of the Earth. Wherever the Earth is in its orbit, tilting the axis by 23.5 degrees only shifts the equator's position slightly, so it always faces the Sun at a fairly steep angle.

That means sunlight arriving at Singapore is roughly as concentrated in January as in July, so the temperature stays high all year. It also means the equator is cut almost exactly in half by the line between day and night, giving about 12 hours of daylight every day of the year.

With neither the angle nor the day length changing much, there is nothing to produce seasons. Singapore's year varies by rainfall instead.

3

On a hot afternoon at the coast, a cool breeze blows in from the sea. Explain how it forms.

Show answer

Land heats up faster than water, so by the afternoon the land is much warmer than the sea. The air touching the hot land is warmed, expands, becomes less dense and rises.

Rising air leaves fewer molecules pressing down over the land, so the air pressure over the land falls. Over the cooler sea the air is denser and sinks, so the pressure there stays higher.

Air always flows from high pressure to low pressure, so it moves from the sea towards the land. That flow is the sea breeze, and it feels cool because the air came from over the water.

4

A newspaper says "it rained heavily in the desert last week, so the desert's climate is now wet". What is wrong with this?

Show answer

The report has confused weather with climate. One week of rain is weather — what the air happened to do over a few days.

Climate is the average pattern over thirty years or more. A desert is defined by decades of very low rainfall, and a single wet week barely moves that average.

To claim the climate had changed you would need to show that rainfall had stayed much higher over many years, not point to one unusual week.

11.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 11.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about the seasons are correct?

  • 1Seasons happen because the Earth is nearer the Sun in summer.
  • 2The Earth's axis stays tilted the same way throughout its orbit.
  • 3When the northern hemisphere has summer, the southern hemisphere has winter.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Seasons come from the tilt of the axis, not from distance. The Earth is in fact closest to the Sun in early January, during the northern winter. And the moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: the axis does keep the same lean all year, and the two hemispheres always have opposite seasons. Both correct, so C fits.

2

A country lies very close to the equator. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Day and night are each about 12 hours long all year.
  • 2Sunlight reaches the ground at a fairly steep angle in every month.
  • 3The country has four clearly different seasons.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. The line between day and night cuts the equator almost exactly in half all year, so days stay near 12 hours. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Four distinct seasons need big changes in sunlight angle and day length, and the equator has neither. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. The tilt of 23.5 degrees barely changes how the equator faces the Sun, so the light stays fairly direct. So the answer is B.

3

Which statements about wind and breezes are correct?

  • 1Wind blows from a region of high pressure to a region of low pressure.
  • 2Warm air rises because it is less dense than the cooler air around it.
  • 3During the day at the coast, the breeze blows from the sea towards the land.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. A pressure difference is the only thing that makes air move, and it always moves from high to low. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. By day the land is hotter than the sea, so air rises over the land and pressure there is low. The breeze therefore comes off the sea. Since statement 1 and statement 3 are both right, the answer must be A.

Statement 2 — correct. Warming makes air expand, so the same air takes up more space and becomes less dense, and less dense air floats upwards. Consistent with A.
