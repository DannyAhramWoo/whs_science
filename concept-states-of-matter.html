Concept

States of Matter

Solid, liquid and gas are three ways the same particles can be arranged, differing in how closely they are packed and how freely they can move.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

A solid keeps its own shape and its own volume. A brick left alone stays brick-shaped. A liquid keeps its volume but not its shape: pour a litre of water into a tall jug or a flat tray and it is still a litre, but it takes the shape of whatever holds it. A gas keeps neither. Release a gas into a room and it spreads until it fills the whole room.

Those three descriptions are what you can observe. The important point is that the same substance can be all three. Ice, water and steam are not three different materials — they are the same particles in three different arrangements. Nothing is added or removed when ice melts. Only the arrangement changes.

Notice that the definitions are about shape and volume, not about hardness or heaviness. Mercury is a metal and a liquid at room temperature. Butter is soft but solid. Whether something is a solid is a question about whether it holds its own shape, not about whether it is tough.

2

How it works

Everything follows from two things: how close the particles are, and how much energy of motion they have compared with the strength of the attractions holding them together.

In a solid the particles are packed closely, usually in a regular pattern, and the attractions between them are strong enough to hold each particle in place. They still move — they vibrate about fixed positions — but they cannot swap places. This explains all three properties of a solid at once. Fixed positions mean fixed shape. Close packing means it cannot be compressed. And close packing means solids are usually the densest state.

In a liquid the particles are still touching, still about as close as in a solid, but they now have enough energy to break free of individual neighbours and slide past one another. Still touching explains why a liquid cannot be squashed and why its volume is fixed. Free to move explains why it flows and takes the shape of its container. Note carefully: the difference between solid and liquid is about freedom to move, not about spacing. That is why a liquid is only slightly less dense than its solid.

In a gas the particles have enough energy to escape one another's attraction altogether. They fly about independently, with distances between them typically about ten times their own size, meaning the space is mostly empty. This is why a gas can be compressed, why it fills any container, and why it is roughly a thousand times less dense than the liquid it came from.

Numbers make the point. One cubic centimetre of liquid water becomes about 1,700 cm³ of steam at ordinary atmospheric pressure. The particles have not grown at all; they have simply spread out. That expansion is what drove steam engines and, when it happens unintentionally, what makes a sealed container of water dangerous when heated.

Water is worth one extra remark, because it breaks a rule. Almost every substance is denser as a solid than as a liquid, since cooling packs particles closer. Ice is less dense than water, because when water freezes its particles lock into an open structure with gaps in it. That is why ice floats, and why ponds freeze from the top down — which lets fish survive the winter underneath.

The heart of it

Solid, liquid and gas differ in how much energy the particles have compared with the forces holding them together — fixed in place, sliding past each other, or flying free.

3

Where the idea came from

This concept has no single discoverer, and it would be dishonest to hand it to one. That solids, liquids and gases behave differently is obvious to anyone who has seen ice melt, and every culture has noticed it. The ancient Greek scheme of four elements — earth, water, air and fire — is partly a scheme of states, with earth standing for solids, water for liquids and air for gases. As a theory of what things are made of it was wrong, but as an observation that matter comes in these forms it was sound.

What was missing for a very long time was any explanation of why the states differ. The states were simply taken as brute facts about the world. Gases in particular were poorly understood; for centuries all gases were lumped together as varieties of "air", and it was only in the 1700s that chemists such as Joseph Black, Henry Cavendish and Joseph Priestley established that there were distinct gases with distinct properties.

The explanation came with the development of kinetic theory during the 1800s — the idea that heat is particle motion and that gas pressure is caused by particles striking the container walls. James Clerk Maxwell in Britain and Ludwig Boltzmann in Austria worked out the mathematics of how a huge population of moving particles behaves on average. Once that was in place, the three states stopped being three separate mysteries and became one idea: the same particles with different amounts of energy. It is a useful reminder that describing something correctly and explaining it are two very different achievements, and can be separated by thousands of years.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking particles in a solid do not move. They vibrate constantly. If they truly stopped, the solid would be at absolute zero, which nothing ever quite reaches. "Fixed position" means they cannot travel past each other — it does not mean still.

The second is assuming a liquid's particles are much further apart than a solid's. They are barely further apart at all. If liquid particles were widely spaced, liquids would be compressible, and they are not.

The third is judging state by feel. Sand pours and takes the shape of its container, so it can seem liquid — but each individual grain is a solid holding its own shape, and it is only the pile that flows.

Commonly missed

A gas is squeezed into a much smaller volume by a piston. Explain why this is possible, and explain why the same could not be done to the liquid form of the same substance.

The trap is saying the gas particles are squashed smaller. Particles never change size.

How to work it: In a gas, the particles are far apart with mostly empty space between them. Compressing simply pushes the particles closer together into that empty space; the particles themselves are unchanged and none are lost, so the mass stays the same while the volume falls, meaning the density rises. In a liquid the particles are already in contact with one another, so there is essentially no empty space to remove. That is why liquids are treated as incompressible, and it is the reason hydraulic brakes work with liquid rather than air.

5

Connected ideas

The states of matter only make sense through the particle model, which supplies the particles and their motion. Moving between states is the subject of changes of state, and the energy that drives those moves comes from heat. Density differs sharply between the states because the spacing differs, and gas particles spreading out is diffusion.

In one line

Solids hold their shape because their particles are locked in place, liquids flow but keep their volume because their particles touch yet slide, and gases fill any space because their particles have broken free entirely.
