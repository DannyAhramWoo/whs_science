Concept

DNA Fingerprinting

DNA fingerprinting is a way of turning a sample of DNA into a pattern of bands, so that two samples can be compared to see whether they came from the same person or from close relatives.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Press your thumb on a window and you leave a smudge of loops and ridges. No two people have the same set, so police have used thumb prints for over a century. A DNA fingerprint does the same job, but uses the chemical instructions inside your cells rather than the skin on your hands. You leave DNA behind everywhere: in a drop of blood, a hair root, spit on a cup.

DNA is the long molecule inside almost every cell that carries the instructions for building and running a body. It is written in an alphabet of four chemical letters, shortened to A, T, C and G.

Here is the surprise. Almost all of your DNA is the same as almost all of mine. Humans share something like 99.9 per cent of their sequence, because we all need the same working parts. So the method cannot look at the whole thing; it would show no difference. It looks at the small patches where people really do differ.

Those patches are short stretches of letters that repeat over and over, like the word "cat" written again and again: CATCATCATCAT. The order of letters is the same in everyone. What varies is how many times the pattern repeats. One person may have eleven repeats at a particular spot, another nineteen. More repeats means a longer stretch of DNA there. Length, not spelling, is what the test measures.

You inherit one copy of each chromosome from each parent, so you carry two versions of every repeated patch, and they need not match. Check enough patches and the chance of two unrelated people matching at all of them becomes very small. The exception is identical twins, who grew from one fertilised egg that split and whose DNA is the same. Their skin fingerprints still differ, because those form partly by chance in the womb.

2

How it works

The method has four stages, and it is worth holding all four, because questions usually turn on the last one.

First, take a sample and get the DNA out. Blood, saliva, a hair with the root attached, a scrap of skin, or a piece of meat or leaf for non-human work. The cells are broken open with chemicals and the DNA separated out. Only a tiny amount is needed, since laboratories can copy a small sample many times over.

Second, cut the DNA into pieces. Special proteins called restriction enzymes act like chemical scissors. Each kind recognises one short sequence of letters and cuts there, and only there. Because the cutting points sit on either side of the repeated patches, a person with more repeats gets a longer piece from that patch, and a person with fewer gets a shorter one. The pieces are called fragments.

Third, sort the fragments by size. The mixture is loaded into a slot at one end of a slab of jelly and an electric current passed through it. This is gel electrophoresis. DNA carries a slight negative charge, so every fragment creeps towards the positive end. The jelly is a fine mesh, and a short fragment slips through it more easily than a long one. So in a fixed time the small fragments travel far and the large ones barely move.

That one fact is the key to reading any gel: the further down the gel a band sits, the smaller the fragment that made it.

Fourth, make the fragments visible and read the pattern. They are stained or tagged so they show as a ladder of dark stripes, called bands, in a vertical strip called a lane. Each sample gets its own lane, so lanes sit side by side and compare straight across.

Comparing lanes is the examined skill, and it is pure pattern matching. To ask whether two samples came from the same individual, check whether every band in one lane sits at the same height as a band in the other. One band out of place is enough to say no.

Parentage works by a stricter rule. A child inherits half its DNA from each parent, so every band in the child's lane must appear in one parent's lane or the other. No band in the child should be absent from both. A child may lack bands a parent has, since each parent passes on only one of their two copies. So the test is one-way: take the child's bands and ask, for each, where it came from. If one band has no possible source, that pair cannot be the parents. This is how a calf is matched to its mother among several cows, an angle in IMSO papers from around 2013 and 2014, alongside blood-group crosses using the same either-or logic.

The uses follow. In forensics, a sample from a scene is compared with samples from named people. In parentage testing, a child is compared with possible parents. In conservation, the same methods tell close species apart, trace which elephant population a seized tusk came from, and show how inbred a small surviving population has become.

The heart of it

Smaller fragments travel further down the gel, and in a parentage test every band in the child must be matched by a band in one parent or the other.

3

Where the idea came from

DNA fingerprinting has an unusually clear beginning. On a morning in September 1984, Alec Jeffreys, a geneticist at the University of Leicester in England, developed an X-ray film in his laboratory. He had been studying repeated sequences of DNA for another purpose entirely, and expected a messy result. Instead the film showed a pattern of bands that differed from person to person while family members plainly shared bands. He and his colleagues realised within minutes what it could be used for.

The first real case was not a crime. In 1985 a Ghanaian boy living in Britain was refused re-entry because officials doubted the woman claiming to be his mother really was. Jeffreys's test showed the family relationship and the boy stayed. Immigration and parentage disputes, not murder, were the earliest use.

The first murder case came in 1986, near Leicester, and went the opposite way to what everyone expected. A young man had confessed to one of two killings and was widely assumed guilty. The test showed his profile matched neither scene, and he was released. Police then sampled thousands of local men. The guilty man tried to avoid screening by having someone give a sample in his name, was found out, and was convicted in 1988. That the technique first proved somebody innocent, before it convicted anybody, is worth remembering when people speak of DNA evidence as if it only ever accuses.

The technique rested on older work. Restriction enzymes were found in bacteria in the late 1960s and early 1970s, and gel electrophoresis had been in use for years before that. Jeffreys did not invent the tools; he saw what they could reveal when pointed at the repeated regions everybody else had ignored as junk.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is reading the gel upside down, assuming that a band which travelled furthest must be the biggest because it went the greatest distance. It is the reverse. The jelly is an obstacle course, and small fragments squeeze through the mesh fastest. Furthest down means smallest.

The second error is treating a match as absolute proof. It is not. A DNA fingerprint checks a handful of patches, not the whole genome, so a laboratory reports a probability: the chance that an unrelated person would match by accident. That figure can be extremely small, but it is never zero, and it is much larger for close relatives, who share many bands with each other by descent. Samples can also be contaminated, mixed with somebody else's DNA at the scene or in the laboratory, or degraded so that faint bands go missing. A careful answer says the samples are very likely from the same source, not that they certainly are.

The third is expecting a child's lane to be a blend of the parents' lanes, half-way between them. Bands do not blend. Each band is present or absent, inherited whole from one parent or the other.

Commonly missed

A gel shows a calf and three cows. The calf has five bands. Cow A shares four of them, Cow B shares three, Cow C shares two. Which cow is the mother?

The trap is picking Cow A because she shares the most bands. Counting matches is the wrong test, and a well-set question will make the highest scorer wrong.

How to work it: The calf has one mother and one father, so every one of its five bands needs a source. Work band by band down the calf's lane. If Cow A shares four and the fifth appears in no cow, that fifth band came from the bull and Cow A stays possible. If the fifth appears in Cow B, then B could supply what A cannot. So choose the cow whose bands, together with an unknown father, account for the whole of the calf's lane. Count of shared bands never decides it; complete coverage does.

5

Connected ideas

DNA fingerprinting only makes sense once you know what DNA is and how it is passed on, so it sits directly on top of genetics. The same repeated sequences that vary between people are the raw material that natural selection works on, and the separation step is the same sorting-by-size idea used in chromatography and other separation methods. Because the technique can tell close species apart, it now feeds back into classification and naming.
