Concept

Weather and Climate

Weather is what the atmosphere is doing at a particular place and moment; climate is the pattern that weather follows in that place over many years.

Introduced in Chapter 11: Seasons, Climate and Wind

1

What it means

If you look out of the window and see rain, that is weather. If you say that where you live it rains a great deal in November most years, that is climate. The two words describe the same physical thing — the state of the atmosphere — but over completely different stretches of time.

Weather is the short-term condition of the atmosphere: temperature, rainfall, cloud, wind speed and direction, humidity, air pressure. It changes over hours and days and is measured directly with thermometers, rain gauges, barometers and anemometers.

Climate is the long-term average of weather for a place, together with how much it usually varies. By international convention, climate is calculated over at least 30 years — long enough that one unusual year cannot shift the picture. Climate is not something you can go outside and measure today; it is worked out from decades of records.

A useful way to keep them apart: climate is what you expect, weather is what you get. Climate tells you what clothes to own; weather tells you which to wear this morning.

2

How it works

Weather exists because the Sun heats the Earth unevenly. Equatorial regions receive steep, concentrated sunlight; polar regions receive it at a shallow angle, spread thin. Land heats and cools faster than water. Dark surfaces absorb more than light ones. These differences make some patches of air warmer than others, warm air rises because it is less dense, cooler air flows in beneath it, and the atmosphere is set permanently churning. Add water evaporating from oceans, condensing into cloud as it rises and cools, and falling as rain, and you have essentially all of weather.

Climate is what you get when you let that churning run for decades and take the average. Several factors control a place's climate. Latitude matters most: how far you are from the equator sets how directly the Sun ever shines on you. Altitude matters because air cools with height, roughly 6.5 degrees Celsius per kilometre, which is why mountains near the equator can carry snow. Distance from the sea matters because water has a very large heat capacity — it takes far more energy to warm a kilogram of water than a kilogram of rock — so the sea warms and cools slowly and steadies the temperature of nearby land. Coastal places have mild winters and cool summers; places deep inside a continent swing between extremes. Ocean currents matter too: the North Atlantic Drift carries warm water towards north-west Europe and keeps Britain far milder than its latitude alone would suggest.

An example makes the difference concrete. Two cities can share a climate and have very different weather on the same day. And a single hot day in a cold country tells you nothing about its climate — but if the average of every day over 30 years rises, the climate itself has changed.

This is the distinction that matters in discussions of climate change. A cold winter does not disprove long-term warming, any more than one tall child disproves that adults are taller than children. You have to look at the average of very many measurements, over a long time, across the whole planet.

The heart of it

Weather is one measurement; climate is the average of thousands of them over decades.

3

Where the idea came from

People have always described their local climate, and the word itself comes from a Greek term for the slope or inclination of the Earth towards the Sun — which shows the Greeks already connected climate to latitude. But describing is not measuring, and measurement had to wait for instruments.

Those arrived in the 1600s. Galileo and others experimented with early thermometers, though these were unreliable because they were affected by air pressure as well as temperature. The decisive instrument was the barometer, invented by Evangelista Torricelli in Italy in 1643, which measured the pressure of the air. Pressure turned out to be strongly linked to coming weather — falling pressure often means a storm approaching — so for the first time it was possible to record something predictive rather than merely note what the sky looked like. Daniel Fahrenheit's mercury thermometer of 1714 finally made temperature readings comparable between places, which is essential if records from different observers are to be averaged.

Forecasting needed one more thing, and it was not a weather instrument at all. Weather systems travel, typically at a few tens of kilometres per hour. Before the telegraph, news of a storm could travel no faster than the storm itself, so knowing that bad weather had struck a town to your west was useless. The electric telegraph in the 1800s changed that: a warning could now outrun the weather. After a storm sank ships off the British coast in 1859, Robert FitzRoy — who had earlier captained the Beagle on Charles Darwin's voyage — set up a system of telegraphed reports and issued the first public storm warnings. He coined the word "forecast", and was careful to choose a word admitting uncertainty. He was mocked by some scientists for predicting something so unruly.

4

What people get wrong

The main error is treating a single day's weather as evidence about climate. This is a mistake about sample size rather than about science: any one measurement can sit far from the average without changing the average. A useful comparison is a class test. One low mark does not mean a pupil is weak; you would look at a year of marks. Climate is the year of marks; weather is today's.

A related error is thinking climate never changes. It changes, but slowly, and the change shows up in the averages rather than in any particular day.

Commonly missed

Two towns lie at the same latitude and the same altitude. One is on the coast, the other 500 km inland. Their annual average temperatures are almost identical, yet their climates are described as different. Explain how this is possible.

The trap is assuming climate means only average temperature. It also includes how much conditions vary through the year, and the two towns differ sharply in that.

How to work it: Think about what the sea does. Water has a much higher heat capacity than land, so it warms up slowly in summer and cools slowly in winter. The coastal town's temperature is steadied by the sea beside it: cool summers, mild winters, a small annual range. The inland town has no such moderating influence, so it heats strongly in summer and cools strongly in winter, giving a large annual range. Averaging a hot summer with a cold winter can produce the same mean as averaging a mild summer with a mild winter — but the climates are plainly different, because climate includes the spread of conditions, not just the middle value.

5

Connected ideas

Weather is driven by uneven heating that sets air moving, so wind and convection are its engine, and the seasons give it its yearly rhythm. All of it takes place within the atmosphere, and the greenhouse effect is what sets the overall temperature the climate settles around.

In one line
