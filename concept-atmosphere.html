Concept

The Atmosphere

The atmosphere is the layer of gases held around the Earth by gravity, which supplies the air we breathe, shields us from harmful radiation and keeps the planet warm enough to live on.

Introduced in Chapter 12: The Planet Earth

1

What it means

The atmosphere is the air, considered as a whole and as a thing wrapped round the planet. Because it is invisible it is easy to think of it as nothing much, but it has mass — about 5.15 million billion tonnes of it — and it is held in place by gravity exactly as the oceans are.

Dry air is about 78 per cent nitrogen and 21 per cent oxygen. That leaves only 1 per cent for everything else: argon takes most of it, and carbon dioxide is about 0.04 per cent. Water vapour varies from almost nothing over a desert to about 4 per cent in humid tropical air, and is usually left out of the standard figures for that reason. It is worth noticing how small some of these numbers are compared with their importance. Carbon dioxide is a trace gas by quantity, yet without it there would be no photosynthesis and the Earth would be frozen.

The atmosphere has no sharp outer edge; it simply thins until it is indistinguishable from space. But it thins fast. Half of all the air lies below about 5.5 km, and 99 per cent below about 32 km. Compared with the Earth's 6,371 km radius it is a very thin skin indeed.

2

How it works

Air pressure is the key to much of this. Every square metre of ground has a column of air standing on it reaching to the top of the atmosphere, and the weight of that column presses down at about 101,000 newtons per square metre at sea level — roughly the weight of a car pressing on each square metre. We do not feel crushed because the same pressure acts inside our bodies and in all directions at once, so it is balanced.

Pressure falls with height, and the reason is straightforward: climb a mountain and there is simply less air above you to press down. It also falls faster near the ground than higher up, because air is squashable. The air at the bottom is compressed by everything above it and is therefore densest, so most of the mass is packed into the lowest few kilometres. This is why climbers above about 8,000 m need oxygen — the air is still 21 per cent oxygen, but each breath contains only about a third as many molecules.

The atmosphere is layered. The troposphere, from the ground to about 12 km, holds around three quarters of the mass and nearly all the water vapour, so essentially all weather happens here. Temperature falls with height through it. Above it the stratosphere reaches to about 50 km, and here temperature rises with height, because ozone in this layer absorbs ultraviolet radiation from the Sun and warms the air. That reversal makes the stratosphere very stable — warm air on top of cool air does not overturn — which is why airliners cruise at its base, above the weather. Above that come the mesosphere, where most meteors burn up, and the thermosphere, where the aurora glows.

The atmosphere does at least four jobs. It supplies oxygen for respiration and carbon dioxide for photosynthesis. Its ozone absorbs most of the Sun's ultraviolet. Its greenhouse gases trap enough outgoing heat to raise the average surface temperature from about minus 18 degrees Celsius to about plus 15. And it shields the surface from meteors, which burn up through friction with the air.

The heart of it

Air has weight, so its pressure comes from the column of air above you — and that column runs out surprisingly quickly.

3

Where the idea came from

For centuries the accepted explanation of why suction works was that "nature abhors a vacuum" — an idea from Aristotle, holding that empty space simply cannot exist, so matter rushes to fill it. It worked well enough until Italian well-diggers found that suction pumps refused to lift water more than about 10 metres, no matter how well built. Nature's abhorrence of a vacuum apparently had a fixed limit, which made no sense.

Evangelista Torricelli, who had been Galileo's assistant, solved it in 1643. He suggested that the water was not being pulled up at all but pushed up by the weight of the air pressing on the surface of the well — and that the air could only push so hard. To test it he used mercury, about 13.6 times denser than water, so the column would be manageably short. He filled a glass tube about a metre long with mercury, sealed the end, inverted it into a dish, and the mercury fell until it stood about 76 cm high, leaving an empty space at the top. He had made both the first barometer and the first sustained laboratory vacuum. He wrote that we live "submerged at the bottom of an ocean of air", which is as good a description as anyone has managed since.

The decisive test came in France in 1648. Blaise Pascal reasoned that if the mercury is held up by the weight of the air above it, then carrying the barometer up a mountain should shorten the column, because there would be less air above. Pascal was in poor health, so his brother-in-law, Florin Périer, carried the apparatus up the Puy de Dôme in the Auvergne, about 1,460 m high. He left one barometer at the bottom under observation and took another to the summit. The summit column had fallen by around 8 cm, while the one at the bottom had not moved. It was a well-designed experiment, with a control, and it settled the matter: air has weight, and there is less of it above you the higher you go. The SI unit of pressure is named the pascal in his honour.

4

What people get wrong

A very common error is thinking the air is mostly oxygen, since oxygen is the part we need. It is mostly nitrogen, at 78 per cent, and nitrogen in the air is chemically unreactive — which is fortunate, because pure oxygen would make fires almost impossible to control.

The other frequent error is describing suction as pulling. A straw does not pull the drink up. Lowering the pressure in your mouth means the air pressing down on the drink's surface is no longer balanced, so the atmosphere pushes the liquid up the straw. This is why you could not drink through a straw longer than about 10 metres even with a perfect vacuum in your mouth, and why the same limit stopped those Italian pumps.

Commonly missed

A sealed empty plastic bottle is closed at the top of a mountain and carried down to sea level. It is found badly crushed. Explain.

The trap is thinking something has been removed from the bottle, or that the bottle has cooled. Nothing has left it — it is sealed — and cooling is not the main effect.

How to work it: Consider the pressure on each side of the bottle wall. When the bottle was sealed on the mountain, the air trapped inside was at the low pressure found up there, because there was little air above to press down. Carrying it down does not change what is inside: the same amount of air remains sealed in, still at that low pressure. But the outside pressure rises steadily during the descent, because there is now a much taller column of air above pressing on it. The outside pressure is therefore greater than the inside pressure, the forces on the bottle wall are no longer balanced, and the wall is pushed inwards until it crumples. Do it in reverse — seal a bottle at sea level and carry it up — and the bottle bulges instead.

Connected ideas

The atmosphere is held down by gravity and behaves as a fluid, with pressure differences within it producing wind and driving the weather. Its ozone layer blocks ultraviolet radiation, and its greenhouse gases keep the surface warm.
