Concept

Weight

Weight is the force of gravity pulling on an object, measured in newtons — so it changes whenever the gravity around you changes.

Introduced in Chapter 2: Mass and Weight

1

What it means

Hold a bag of shopping at arm's length and your arm aches, because something is pulling that bag downwards and your muscles must fight it. That downward pull is the bag's weight. It is not a property the bag simply has, like its colour; it is something being done to the bag, all the time, by the Earth.

That is why weight is a force. A force is a push or a pull, and forces are measured in newtons, written N. One newton is roughly the pull the Earth exerts on a 100 g apple.

The chapter gave you the working rule: on Earth gravity pulls with about 10 N for every kilogram, so weight in newtons is the mass in kilograms times 10. That 10 is not a fact about the object but about Earth. It is written g, the gravitational field strength, meaning "newtons of pull per kilogram". Change planets and g changes, so the weight of the very same object changes with it.

This is the whole reason weight and mass need separate words. Mass belongs to the object. Weight belongs to the object and its surroundings together.

2

How it works

Work an example properly. A 6 kg rock on Earth has weight 6 × 10 = 60 N. On the Moon g is about 1.6 N per kilogram, so the same rock weighs about 9.6 N. Nothing was removed from the rock; there is simply less pull where it now sits.

Now the part that catches almost everyone: weightlessness. Astronauts on the space station float, and the obvious explanation is that gravity has run out up there. It has not — at that height Earth's gravity is still close to 90 per cent of its strength at the ground. The astronauts float because they and the station are falling together, so there is no floor pressing up on them.

That points at what a bathroom scale is really doing. It never senses gravity directly; it senses how hard something presses on its top surface. Standing still on Earth that push equals your weight, so the scale can convert its reading to kilograms and be right. Stand on it in a lift that starts moving upwards and the reading rises, because the floor must push harder to speed you up — yet your mass did not change.

The heart of it

Weight is not a thing an object owns; it is the size of the gravitational pull acting on it right now, so moving the object to a place with different gravity changes its weight without changing the object at all.

3

Where the idea came from

Weight was measured for thousands of years before anybody defined it, and balances and standard stones are among the oldest scientific instruments we have. But those systems treated weight and quantity of goods as a single idea, for good reason: on the Earth's surface they always vary together. Nothing in daily trading would make a merchant ask whether they were two things.

The separation arrived with Isaac Newton and his Principia of 1687. Newton's mathematics required a quantity describing how much matter an object contained, distinct from the force the Earth applied to it. Once he had drawn that line, weight became a force like any other, and forces have their own unit. Fittingly, that unit is now named the newton, although the name was adopted long after his death.

The kilogram, weight's partner in every calculation, has a history of its own. From 1889 to 2019 it was defined by a physical object: a platinum-iridium cylinder stored near Paris, the International Prototype of the Kilogram. Careful comparisons suggested its mass had drifted very slightly relative to its copies, and there was no way to say which had changed, since the master was the definition. In 2019 the kilogram was redefined using the Planck constant — because a physical object can change, and a constant of nature cannot.

4

What people get wrong

The headline error is giving weight in kilograms. Kilograms measure mass; newtons measure force. Everyday speech does this constantly and nobody minds, but in a science answer "my weight is 45 kg" is wrong twice over — wrong unit, wrong quantity. Say "my mass is 45 kg" or "my weight is about 450 N".

The second error is arithmetical and costs marks quietly. When a question gives a mass in grams, convert to kilograms before multiplying. A 250 g mug is 0.25 kg, so its weight is 2.5 N. Answering 2500 N means the conversion was skipped, and that wrong value is usually offered as an option deliberately.

Commonly missed

An astronaut of mass 70 kg is inside a space station orbiting the Earth. What is the gravitational force on the astronaut?

The trap is the word "weightless". Because astronauts are seen floating, the tempting answer is zero — and zero is always on offer.

How to work it: Ask what "weightless" describes. It describes a sensation: nothing is pressing back on the astronaut. It does not describe the gravitational force, which is still there and still large. At orbital height g is only slightly reduced, roughly 9 N per kilogram, so the pull is about 70 × 9 = 630 N. If it were truly zero, the station would fly off in a straight line. That it keeps circling is the proof gravity is still doing its job.

5

Connected ideas

Weight is the meeting point of two ideas: mass, which the object carries with it, and gravity, which the surroundings supply. Being a force, weight is usually the downward half of a pair of balanced forces — a table pushing up on a book exactly matches the book's weight, which is why the book stays still. In water, weight competes with upthrust, and which wins decides whether an object floats. Weight is also what you lift against, making it the starting point for calculating work.

In one line

Weight is the force of gravity acting on an object, found by multiplying its mass in kilograms by the local gravitational field strength, and measured in newtons — so the same object weighs less on the Moon simply because the Moon pulls more weakly.
