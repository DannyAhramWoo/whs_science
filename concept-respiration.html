Concept

Respiration

Respiration is the chemical process, going on inside every living cell, that releases energy from food using oxygen.

Introduced in Chapter 16: Photosynthesis and Respiration

1

What it means

In everyday speech, respiration means breathing. In science it means something quite different, and confusing the two causes more trouble than almost any other mix-up in biology. Breathing is a chest movement that pulls air in and pushes it out. Respiration is a chemical reaction inside your cells, far too small to see, releasing the energy you need to move, grow, think and stay warm.

The two are connected. Breathing is how oxygen reaches your cells and how waste carbon dioxide gets away. But breathing is only the delivery service; respiration is what the goods are used for. A plant respires without any breathing apparatus, and so does a single-celled organism in a pond.

The reaction is simple to state. Glucose, a sugar, reacts with oxygen to give carbon dioxide and water, releasing energy. Everything you have ever done was paid for by that reaction in your cells.

2

How it works

Compare that with combustion and you should feel a jolt of recognition. Burning is fuel plus oxygen, giving carbon dioxide, water and energy. Respiration is sugar plus oxygen, giving the same three. Inputs and outputs match. Respiration really is a kind of slow, controlled burning — not a loose analogy but the same chemistry.

So why is there no flame inside you? Because of how the energy is released. A fire dumps everything at once in one violent step, which is why it is hot and bright and destroys what it touches. Respiration takes glucose apart in a long chain of small steps, each releasing a little energy and each controlled by a substance in the cell, so the energy can be captured in usable form as it comes out. A cell cannot survive at candle-flame temperatures, so the energy must arrive in manageable pieces.

Not all of it is captured, and the part that escapes is heat. That is why your body is warm. Clothes only slow the loss; the warmth comes from respiration running in every cell every second. Exercise hard and you respire faster, which is why you get hot. It also explains breathing rate: running muscles need energy quickly, so respiration speeds up, so they need oxygen faster and make carbon dioxide faster. Your body detects the rising carbon dioxide and makes you breathe harder. Breathing follows respiration, not the other way round.

If oxygen cannot arrive fast enough, cells fall back on anaerobic respiration, meaning respiration without oxygen. It releases far less energy per glucose molecule and leaves a partly broken-down product, in human muscle lactic acid. It is a short-term emergency measure, which is why a sprint cannot be sustained and why you keep panting after stopping.

The heart of it

Respiration is the same reaction as burning, taken apart into small controlled steps so the energy can be captured instead of wasted.

3

Where the idea came from

For a long time nobody had reason to connect breathing with fire. They looked completely different: one warm and alive, the other violent and destructive. The link was made experimentally in the 1780s by Antoine Lavoisier, working with the mathematician Pierre-Simon Laplace.

They built an apparatus that measured heat by how much ice it melted, and placed a guinea pig inside a chamber. The animal, simply by living, produced heat and gave off carbon dioxide. When they burned charcoal in the same apparatus, it too produced heat and carbon dioxide, in a comparable relationship. The living animal was behaving like a very slow fire. That was a startling conclusion, because it suggested life was powered not by some special force unavailable to non-living matter but by ordinary chemistry of the kind that made a candle burn.

They did not have the full picture. They thought the burning happened in the lungs, because that is where the air went. Much later work established that respiration takes place in every cell, and later still came the discovery of the structures inside cells where most of it occurs. Their measurements were rough by modern standards. What they got right was the crucial link, reached by measuring heat and gas rather than arguing about what life was.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is answering a respiration question by describing breathing. If a question asks what respiration does, the answer is about releasing energy from food, not air moving in and out of lungs.

The second is thinking plants respire only at night, or not at all because they photosynthesise. Every living cell respires continuously; a plant does both at once in daylight.

The third is treating exhaled air as pure carbon dioxide. It still holds a great deal of oxygen — enough that rescue breathing works — plus more carbon dioxide and water vapour than the air that went in.

Commonly missed

Germinating seeds are sealed in a flask with a thermometer. Over two days the temperature inside rises above room temperature. Boiled seeds in an identical flask show no rise. Explain both results.

It is a trap because seeds do not look alive, so people reach for explanations like decay or sunlight rather than respiration.

How to work it: Germinating seeds are living and growing, so they respire rapidly to release energy from their food stores. Some of that energy escapes as heat, and because the flask is sealed it builds up faster than it leaks away, so the temperature rises. The boiled seeds are dead, so no respiration and no heat. That flask is the control, showing the rise came from the seeds being alive rather than from the flask, room or thermometer.

5

Connected ideas

Respiration is the reverse of photosynthesis and the quiet cousin of combustion, sharing its chemistry without its flame. It depends on breathing to bring oxygen in, circulation to carry it to the cells, and digestion to supply the glucose. The heat it produces is why warm-blooded animals stay warm, and the carbon dioxide it releases is one arm of the carbon cycle.
