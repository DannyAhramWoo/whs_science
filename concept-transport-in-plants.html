Concept

Transport in Plants

A plant has two separate systems of pipes: xylem carries water upwards from the roots, and phloem carries dissolved food from the leaves to wherever it is needed.

Introduced in Chapter 16: Photosynthesis and Respiration

1

What it means

Stand a white flower in a glass of water with red food colouring in it. Come back next morning and the petals have red veins. Something has carried that dye from the glass, up the stem, and out into the tips of the petals — against gravity, with no heart and no pump anywhere in the plant.

A plant has a transport problem. It makes its food in the leaves at the top and collects its water at the roots at the bottom, but every living cell needs both. A tall tree may have to lift water more than sixty metres. So a plant needs plumbing, and it has two sets, running side by side in bundles through the roots, stem and leaf veins.

Xylem (say it "ZY-lem") carries water and dissolved minerals. Phloem (say it "FLOH-em") carries dissolved food, meaning the sugar made by photosynthesis. Keeping these two straight is the single most examined thing in this topic. Almost every question is really asking: which substance, and which direction?

2

How it works

Xylem: water, upwards, dead tubes. Xylem is built from cells that die as they mature. The ends of the dead cells dissolve away and their contents empty out, leaving hollow tubes stacked end to end — a continuous open drainpipe running from root to leaf. Their walls are strengthened with a tough substance called lignin, which is what makes wood woody, so the tubes hold their shape and hold the plant up as well. Xylem carries water and the minerals dissolved in it, and it carries them in one direction only: upwards, roots to leaves. It never carries food, and it never runs downwards.

Phloem: food, both ways, living cells. Phloem is different in every respect. Its cells stay alive. They are joined end to end with sieve-like walls between them, holes let the sugary liquid pass through, and companion cells alongside supply the energy the process needs. Phloem carries dissolved sugar from where it is made — usually the leaves — to wherever it is wanted. That might be a growing shoot tip above the leaf, or roots, a tuber, a bulb or a swelling fruit below it. So phloem transport goes both up and down. This movement of dissolved food has its own name, translocation.

Why the water rises. Nothing pushes it. It is pulled, and the pull starts in the leaves. Water evaporates from the moist cell surfaces inside the spongy layer of the leaf and escapes as vapour through the stomata, the small pores on the leaf's underside. This loss of water vapour from a plant is called transpiration. Because water molecules cling to each other, the water in the xylem behaves like a rope rather than a loose stream: pull the top and the whole column comes with it. Every molecule that evaporates at the top tugs the entire chain up one place, all the way to the roots. This is the transpiration stream, and it is why cut flowers stand up in water and wilt without it.

Getting water in at the bottom. Root tips are covered in root hair cells, each one drawn out into a long thin projection into the soil. Their purpose is surface area. A hundred thin hairs touch far more soil, and far more of the water film clinging to soil particles, than a smooth root of the same size could. Water passes in through the hair's surface, then across the root into the xylem. It is the same trick as the folds in your intestines or the branching air sacs in your lungs: when something must be absorbed, make the surface as large as you can.

What changes the rate. Transpiration is evaporation, so anything that speeds up evaporation speeds it up. Heat increases it, because warm water molecules escape more readily. Wind increases it, because moving air sweeps away the damp layer sitting just outside the stomata, so vapour keeps leaving. Humidity — the amount of water vapour already in the air — decreases it, because if the surrounding air is already loaded with vapour there is much less room for more to leave. Bright light increases it too, indirectly, because the stomata open wider in light to let carbon dioxide in for photosynthesis, and open pores lose water. That is the plant's permanent dilemma: it cannot take in the gas it needs without giving up water.

The heart of it

Xylem is dead pipework carrying water one way, upwards; phloem is living pipework carrying dissolved sugar either way, to wherever the plant needs it.

3

Where the idea came from

The English clergyman and naturalist Stephen Hales made the first careful measurements in the 1720s. He attached glass tubes to cut stems and vine stumps and watched how far sap rose, weighed potted plants to find how much water they lost in a day, and worked out that a sunflower could give off many times its own weight of water over a growing season. He published the results in Vegetable Staticks in 1727, establishing that the loss was real, enormous, and tied to the leaves.

What he could not do was explain the force. That took two centuries of argument. Some thought the roots pushed water up like a pump; root pressure is real but nowhere near strong enough for a tall tree. Others thought the living cells of the stem passed water along actively, which was ruled out when experimenters killed a stem with poison or heat and found water still rose through it. Only around 1900, once the pulling power of evaporation was combined with the stickiness of water between its own molecules, did the picture settle into what is now taught.

The separation of the two systems was proved by a neat experiment called ringing. Cut away a ring of bark from all round a tree trunk, and you remove the phloem, which lies in the bark, while leaving the xylem in the wood untouched. The leaves stay green and supplied with water, showing xylem still works — but sugar can no longer travel down past the cut, so a swelling appears above the ring and the roots slowly starve. Two pipes, two cargoes, two fates.

4

What people get wrong

Nearly every error here is a swap between the two tubes. Two anchors will keep you right. First, the letters: phloem carries food, and both sound the same at the start. Second, xylem is the dead one, and something dead can only be a passive pipe, which fits a one-way flow driven by evaporation from outside.

The other trap is the comparison with animals. Papers do ask this directly, and it appeared in an IMSO paper as early as 2008: which part of a plant does the same job as a blood vessel. The honest answer is that both xylem and phloem are transport vessels, so the plant's vascular tissue as a whole is the counterpart to blood vessels. But the analogy has a limit worth remembering — a plant has no heart, and its water moves because it is pulled from the top, not pushed from the middle.

Commonly missed

Sugar made in the leaves of a potato plant ends up stored in tubers underground. Which tissue carries it there, and in which direction does that tissue transport materials?

It is a trap because the word "underground" makes people think of roots, and roots make people think of water, and water makes them answer xylem. The downward direction also feels wrong for the food pipe, since food is usually pictured travelling up from the soil.

How to work it: Ignore where it ends up and look at what is moving. It is sugar, which is dissolved food. Only phloem carries food, so the tissue is phloem — no other answer is possible. Then handle the direction separately. The sugar starts in leaves at the top and finishes in tubers at the bottom, so this journey is downwards, and phloem can do that because it transports in whichever direction the plant needs, up or down. Xylem could not, both because it never carries food and because it only flows upwards. The same reasoning covers sugar moving into a bulb, a fruit or a growing root.

5

Connected ideas

Transport exists to serve photosynthesis: xylem delivers one raw material and phloem removes the product. The two tubes run through the leaf veins you meet in a leaf cross-section, and water evaporating from leaves is a real contributor to the water cycle. The comparison with an animal's circulation is worth making, and so is the contrast — plants manage without a pump.
