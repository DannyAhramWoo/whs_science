Concept

Physical and Chemical Change

A physical change alters only the form or arrangement of a substance and can usually be reversed, while a chemical change produces one or more genuinely new substances and usually cannot.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Tear a sheet of paper in half and you have two pieces of paper. Burn it and you have ash, smoke and gases — and no amount of effort will turn those back into paper. Something fundamentally different has happened in the second case, and the distinction between the two is one of the most useful in chemistry.

In a physical change, the substance is still the same substance afterwards. Melting, freezing, boiling, dissolving, crushing, bending, mixing: in every case the particles are the same particles, merely rearranged, moved apart, or broken into smaller lumps. Ice, water and steam are all water.

In a chemical change, also called a chemical reaction, the particles are taken apart and rebuilt into different arrangements, making substances that were not there before and that have different properties. Iron rusting produces iron oxide, a crumbly orange solid quite unlike the shiny metal. Burning magnesium produces a white powder. Cooking an egg produces something that will never return to being raw.

The everyday word "reversible" is a useful clue but it is not the definition. The definition is whether new substances have been made.

2

How it works

Since you cannot see particles, you need signs to work from. Several point towards a chemical change, though none is conclusive on its own.

A colour change that is not just dilution or mixing — shiny iron turning orange, a green leaf turning brown. Gas produced where there was no gas before, such as the fizzing when vinegar meets bicarbonate of soda. A precipitate, meaning a solid appearing when two clear solutions are mixed. Heat or light given out or taken in, as in burning, or in the cold-pack reactions that draw heat from their surroundings. And a smell appearing that was not present in either starting material.

Each of these has a physical impostor, which is why you must think rather than pattern-match. Dissolving a coloured crystal changes the colour of the water, but is physical. Boiling produces bubbles of gas, but is physical. Condensation appears on a cold glass, but is physical. The reliable question is always: is the substance I have now a different substance from the one I started with, with different properties?

The most useful single test is often reversibility by simple physical means. Salt dissolved in water can be got back by evaporating — physical. Wood burnt to ash cannot be got back by any physical process — chemical. Note "usually": some chemical reactions can be reversed, but only by another chemical reaction, not by heating, cooling or filtering.

Underneath all of this sits a law that holds in both cases: mass is conserved. Nothing is created or destroyed, only rearranged. This can look wrong. Burn a log and the ash weighs far less than the log did — but the gases that went up the chimney have mass too, and if you could collect everything, the total would be unchanged. The reverse case is more striking. Burn a strip of magnesium in air and the white powder produced weighs more than the magnesium did, because oxygen from the air has combined with it and is now part of the product. Weigh the oxygen used and the books balance exactly.

The heart of it

Ask not whether something looks different, but whether the substance itself is now a different substance — and remember that mass is conserved either way, including any gas that escaped or joined in.

3

Where the idea came from

Before the 1770s, burning was explained by the phlogiston theory. The idea, developed by Georg Ernst Stahl around 1700, was that combustible materials contained a substance called phlogiston which was released when they burned. Wood burned well because it was rich in phlogiston; ash would not burn because its phlogiston had gone. A candle went out in a sealed jar because the trapped air had become saturated with phlogiston and could hold no more.

It is worth taking this theory seriously rather than laughing at it, because it explained a great deal correctly and was believed by capable chemists for decades. It had one stubborn problem: when metals were burned, the product weighed more than the metal. If burning released something, the product should weigh less. Some defenders proposed that phlogiston had negative weight, which should have been a warning sign — a theory that has to invent an impossible property to survive is usually in trouble.

Antoine Lavoisier, working in Paris in the 1770s and 1780s, resolved it by insisting on careful weighing, including of the gases involved, and by working in sealed vessels so nothing could escape unmeasured. He showed that a burning substance combines with a component of the air, which he named oxygen, rather than releasing anything. The mass increase was simply the mass of the oxygen that had joined. He also showed that in a sealed vessel the total mass before and after a reaction is identical, establishing the conservation of mass.

The decisive change was as much in method as in conclusion. Lavoisier weighed everything, including gases, which earlier chemists had largely ignored because gases were hard to handle and easy to lose. Once gases were on the balance sheet, phlogiston was unnecessary.

Lavoisier was executed by guillotine in Paris in 1794, during the Terror. This had nothing to do with his science — he had been a tax collector under the old regime, and that is what he was condemned for. It is a bleak reminder that scientific standing offers no protection from politics.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is using "irreversible" as the definition rather than as a clue. Some physical changes are hard to reverse in practice — a smashed vase is still glass, and the change is physical, but nobody is putting it back together.

The second is treating any change in appearance as chemical. Chopping, dissolving, melting and mixing all change appearance dramatically while producing no new substance.

The third is assuming mass is lost when something burns away or gas escapes. Mass is always conserved. If the reading has changed, something has entered or left the container, and finding out what is usually the whole point of the question.

Commonly missed

A piece of steel wool is weighed, then heated strongly in air until it glows and turns dark. It is weighed again and found to be heavier. A pupil says this proves the balance is faulty, because nothing was added. Explain what has happened.

The trap is that we expect burning to consume material and so reduce mass, and it is easy to forget that air has mass and can join in.

How to work it: Heating steel wool in air is a chemical change: the iron reacts with oxygen from the air to form iron oxide, a new substance with different properties, which is why it is dark and brittle rather than shiny and springy. The oxygen atoms that combined with the iron are now part of the solid sitting on the balance, and oxygen has mass. So the product must be heavier than the iron alone by exactly the mass of oxygen absorbed. Nothing is created — the surrounding air has lost precisely that mass. This is the same observation that phlogiston theory could never explain and that Lavoisier used to overturn it.

5

Connected ideas

Changes of state are the clearest examples of physical change, since the substance is unaltered throughout, and dissolving to make a solution is another. Separating a mixture is physical too, which is why it needs no reaction. Burning is the standard example of a chemical change, and photosynthesis and respiration are chemical changes happening inside living things.

In one line

A physical change rearranges a substance without making anything new and can normally be undone, while a chemical change builds genuinely new substances with different properties — and in both cases the total mass, counting any gases, stays exactly the same.
