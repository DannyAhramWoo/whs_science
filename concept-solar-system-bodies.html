Concept

The Solar System

The Solar System is the Sun together with everything held in orbit by its gravity: eight planets, their moons, and vast numbers of asteroids, comets and smaller lumps of rock and ice.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Look up at night and the stars appear fixed while a few bright points wander against them from week to week. Those wanderers are the planets, and the word planet comes from a Greek term meaning wanderer. They move because they are close, circling the same Sun we do.

The Sun is a star: a huge ball of hot gas producing energy in its core. It holds more than 99 per cent of the mass in the Solar System, and its gravity — the pull every mass exerts on every other mass — keeps everything else in orbit around it.

The eight planets, outward from the Sun, are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. The first four are the inner planets, small and made of rock and metal. The last four are the outer planets or gas giants, far larger and made mostly of gas and ice with no solid surface to stand on.

2

How it works

The split between rocky inner planets and gaseous outer ones traces back to how the Solar System formed. Close to the young Sun it was hot, so light substances such as water and methane stayed as gas and were driven outwards, leaving only rock and metal to gather into planets. Farther out it was cold enough for them to freeze, so the outer planets collected far more material and grew massive enough for their gravity to hold hydrogen and helium too.

Density follows from that. Earth is the densest planet in the Solar System — density meaning mass packed into each unit of volume — because it is largely iron and rock, compressed by its own weight. Saturn is so light for its size it would float in a large enough bath.

Distance is not the whole story for temperature. Venus is farther from the Sun than Mercury yet much hotter, around 460 degrees Celsius, because of its atmosphere: a thick blanket of carbon dioxide that lets sunlight in but blocks heat radiating back out. Mercury has almost no atmosphere, so its heat escapes and its night side falls far below freezing. Past competition papers, including IMSO ones, ask for that explanation rather than a list of distances.

Most planets have moons, natural bodies orbiting a planet rather than the Sun directly: Earth has one, Jupiter and Saturn dozens each. Between Mars and Jupiter lies the asteroid belt, a broad ring of millions of rocky bodies, from dust grains up to objects hundreds of kilometres across. They never formed into a planet, most likely because Jupiter's gravity kept stirring them too violently to join. That location, dividing the inner planets from the outer, is the fact worth memorising.

Beyond Neptune is the Kuiper belt, a much wider ring of icy bodies. Comets are lumps of ice and dust, many from that region, on long stretched orbits. When a comet swings near the Sun its ice turns to gas and streams away, giving it a glowing tail that always points away from the Sun, not backwards along its path.

Three words describe one lump of rock at three stages. A meteoroid travels through space. If it enters Earth's atmosphere and burns up, the streak of light is a meteor, a shooting star. Any piece surviving to land is a meteorite. Space, sky, ground. To picture the scale, shrink it: with the Sun a football 22 centimetres across, Earth is a 2-millimetre grain 24 metres off and Neptune three quarters of a kilometre away.

The heart of it

The Sun's gravity holds everything in orbit, the inner planets are rocky and the outer ones gaseous because of how hot it was where each formed, and the asteroid belt marks the boundary between them.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of history the Earth was thought to sit still at the centre with everything circling it, which fits what the eye reports. In 1543 Nicolaus Copernicus put the Sun at the centre instead, though he kept perfect circles for the orbits; Johannes Kepler showed early in the 1600s that they are ellipses.

In 1610 Galileo Galilei turned a telescope on Jupiter and saw four points of light beside it that shifted night after night. They were moons circling Jupiter — proof that not everything went round the Earth. William Herschel found Uranus in 1781 while surveying stars from Bath, at first taking it for a comet. Neptune followed in 1846, its position worked out from mathematics before anyone looked.

Clyde Tombaugh found Pluto in 1930 by comparing photographic plates from different nights and looking for the one dot that had shifted. It was called the ninth planet for 76 years. From the 1990s astronomers found many similar icy bodies in the Kuiper belt, at least one of comparable size, so either the list of planets would grow long or the definition needed tightening. In 2006 the International Astronomical Union set three criteria: a planet must orbit the Sun, be massive enough for its own gravity to pull it round, and have cleared other objects from its orbital path. Pluto meets the first two but shares its region with the Kuiper belt, so it became a dwarf planet. Some astronomers still dispute that third criterion.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is assuming temperature falls steadily with distance from the Sun. It largely does, but atmosphere can outweigh distance, and Venus is the standing exception. Always ask what a planet's air is like before answering a temperature question.

The second is placing the asteroid belt beyond Neptune. The asteroid belt is rocky and lies between Mars and Jupiter; the Kuiper belt is icy and lies beyond Neptune. The third is calling Jupiter the densest planet because it is the biggest. Size is not density: Jupiter is enormous but mostly hydrogen, while Earth is small, iron and rock, and the densest of the eight.

Commonly missed

A rock from space burns up in the atmosphere and a fragment lands in a field. What was it called at each stage?

The trap is that the three words look almost identical, so a reader picks whichever is most familiar and uses it throughout.

How to work it: Track where the object is. In space it is a meteoroid. The bright streak it makes while burning in the atmosphere is a meteor — the meteor is the light, not the rock. The fragment that reaches the ground is a meteorite, the ending -ite being the clue, as in granite.

5

Connected ideas

The Sun's gravity explains the orbits, Earth's spin and orbit explain day and year, and the Moon's own orbit explains phases and eclipses. Venus shows what an atmosphere can do to a planet's heat.

In one line

The Sun's gravity holds eight planets and countless smaller bodies in orbit, with rocky worlds and the asteroid belt on the inside, gas giants and the icy Kuiper belt outside, and Pluto reclassified in 2006 because it has not cleared its own orbital path.
