Concept

Time Zones and the Date Line

Because Earth turns steadily on its axis, noon arrives at different moments in different places, so the world is divided into time zones running roughly 15 degrees of longitude wide — one for each hour of the day.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Telephone a relative on the other side of the world and you may wake them at three in the morning. Their clock disagrees with yours, and neither is wrong. The disagreement exists because the Sun cannot be overhead everywhere at once.

Earth rotates — spins on its own axis — once in 24 hours, turning from west to east. That is why the Sun appears to rise in the east and set in the west: we are turning towards it. At any moment, half the planet faces the Sun and has day while the other half has night, and the boundary between them creeps westwards around the globe.

To describe where a place sits east or west, we use longitude, measured in degrees. Lines of longitude, called meridians, run from the North Pole to the South Pole like the segments of an orange. The meridian counted as zero passes through Greenwich in London and is called the prime meridian. From there longitude runs up to 180 degrees east and 180 degrees west, meeting on the far side of the world in the Pacific Ocean.

A time zone is a region that agrees to share one clock setting. Zones are labelled by how far ahead of or behind the time at Greenwich they are — GMT+8, GMT-5 and so on. GMT stands for Greenwich Mean Time; you will also see UTC, Coordinated Universal Time, which for our purposes means the same thing.

2

How it works

The size of a zone comes from one division. A full turn is 360 degrees and takes 24 hours, so Earth turns 360 divided by 24, which is 15 degrees every hour. That single number is the whole basis of time zones: places 15 degrees of longitude apart see the Sun reach its highest point one hour apart, so their clocks are set one hour apart. It also means Earth's surface turns 1 degree every four minutes.

Which way round? Earth spins west to east, so places to the east turn into the sunlight first. East is later, west is earlier. If you travel east you must put your watch forward; travelling west you put it back. Tokyo, well east of London, is already having its evening when London is having its morning.

Here is a worked example. Two cities lie on the same latitude. City A sits at 105 degrees east and City B at 15 degrees east. How far apart are their clocks? First find the difference in longitude: 105 minus 15 gives 90 degrees. Then divide by 15 degrees per hour: 90 divided by 15 is 6. So the cities are six hours apart. City A is further east, so City A is ahead. If it is 09:00 in City B, it is 15:00 in City A. In zone labels, City A is around GMT+7 and City B around GMT+1, and 7 minus 1 is the same 6 hours. Working from either the longitudes or the zone labels gives the same answer, which is a useful way to check yourself.

A second example, this time working backwards. A flight leaves a city at GMT+7 at 10:00 local time and lasts 11 hours, landing in a city at GMT+1. What is the local landing time? Convert the departure to GMT first: 10:00 minus 7 hours is 03:00 GMT. Add the flight: 03:00 plus 11 hours is 14:00 GMT. Convert to the destination zone: 14:00 plus 1 hour is 15:00 local. Going through GMT is more reliable than trying to add the flight and the zone change in one leap.

There is a neat consequence worth thinking through. Earth's surface at the equator turns eastwards at roughly 1,670 kilometres per hour. An aircraft flying east to west is flying against that rotation, so if it could match that speed it would keep pace with the day and the Sun would appear to hang still in its sky. Concorde came closer to this than any airliner, and passengers could land at a local clock time earlier than the one they took off at. Flying west to east does the opposite: you race towards the sunset and the local clock jumps forward hard, which is why that direction gives worse jet lag.

Zones are not tidy 15-degree stripes on a map. Their boundaries bend round countries, because a country wants one clock for its trains, schools and broadcasts. China spans about 60 degrees of longitude — enough for four zones — but runs the whole country on a single time, so the Sun rises very late by the clock in the far west. India uses one zone offset by half an hour rather than a whole one. Russia, being far too wide for that, uses eleven. Politics and convenience shape the map; physics only sets the underlying 15 degrees per hour.

Finally, the International Date Line. It runs roughly along 180 degrees longitude, on the opposite side of the world from Greenwich, zigzagging to keep island groups and countries whole. It exists out of necessity. Going east you gain an hour per zone; going west you lose one. Travel right round the world and you would gain or lose 24 hours and end up a day out of step with everyone. The Date Line is where that day is put back. Cross it going west and you jump forward a day — Tuesday becomes Wednesday. Cross it going east and you repeat a day — Wednesday becomes Tuesday again. The clock time barely changes; only the date does.

The heart of it

Earth turns 360 degrees in 24 hours, which is 15 degrees per hour, so every 15 degrees of longitude you move east the local clock is one hour later.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of history every town set its clocks by its own Sun, so noon in one town was a few minutes off noon in the next. Nobody minded, because nobody travelled fast enough to notice.

Railways ended that. Trains crossed dozens of local times in a day, and timetables became a menace, with a real risk of collisions when two stations disagreed about the hour. British railway companies began adopting a single standard time from the 1840s. North American railways adopted standard zones in 1883.

The international agreement followed a year later. In October 1884 the International Meridian Conference met in Washington and chose the meridian through the Royal Observatory at Greenwich as the world's prime meridian, largely for practical reasons: a great many of the world's ships already carried charts based on it. The choice was not unanimous, and France continued to use its own Paris meridian for decades afterwards.

Long before any of this, the crew of Magellan's expedition met the problem the hard way. Returning to Spain in 1522 after sailing westwards round the world, they found their carefully kept ship's log was one day behind the calendar on shore. They had lost a day by crossing what we now call the Date Line without knowing it existed.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is reversing the direction. It is easy to reason that the Sun moves east to west across the sky, so east must be behind. It is the other way round: Earth turns towards the east, so eastern places meet the Sun first and are ahead on the clock. Fix one pair you know — Tokyo is ahead of London — and use it as an anchor.

The second is dividing by the wrong number. Fifteen degrees per hour is the rate, not 24 and not 360. If your answer to a time-difference question is larger than 12 hours or has strange decimals, check that division first.

The third is confusing the Date Line with the prime meridian. They are on opposite sides of the world: Greenwich at 0 degrees, the Date Line near 180 degrees.

Commonly missed

In which direction would an aircraft have to fly so that the Sun appears to stay in the same place in the sky throughout the journey?

The trap is answering west to east, because that feels like the "forward" direction and matches the way Earth spins.

How to work it: Earth spins west to east, which is why the Sun appears to travel east to west across the sky. To hold the Sun still, the aircraft must cancel that apparent movement by chasing it — flying east to west, against the planet's rotation. Test the answer with the extreme case: a plane flying east to west at exactly Earth's rotational speed would stay above the same point relative to the Sun, so the Sun would never move.

5

Connected ideas

Time zones exist entirely because Earth rotates, so rotation and revolution is the foundation. The tilt that produces the seasons is a separate effect of the same spinning planet, and Kepler's laws describe the orbital motion that gives us the year rather than the day.

In one line
