Chapter 15

Changes in Matter

Matter changes state, and matter changes into new substances. These are two different things, and most marks lost here come from mixing them up — or from assuming that heating something must always make it hotter.

15.1

The six changes of state

Matter comes in three states: solid, liquid and gas. Moving between them has six names, and you are expected to know all six.

Melting is solid to liquid. Freezing is liquid to solid. Boiling is liquid to gas. Condensation is gas to liquid. Sublimation is solid straight to gas, with no liquid in between — dry ice does this, and so does a snowman slowly shrinking on a freezing day.

The sixth is evaporation, which is also liquid to gas. It gets its own section because it is not the same thing as boiling.

Every change has a direction, and the direction tells you what the heat is doing. Going up the list — solid to liquid to gas — the particles have to be pulled further apart, and pulling them apart takes energy. So melting, boiling, evaporation and sublimation all absorb heat. Going the other way, the particles fall back together and give that energy up, so freezing and condensation release heat.

One rule covers all six

Solid → liquid → gas absorbs heat. Gas → liquid → solid releases heat. You do not have to memorise six facts, only which way the arrow points.

15.2

Melting point, boiling point, and reading the table

A pure substance melts at one exact temperature and boils at one exact temperature. Water melts at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C, every single time. These fixed values are its melting point and boiling point.

Because they are fixed, a table of melting and boiling points lets you work out what state anything is in at any temperature — without ever having seen the substance. This is one of the most common question types you will meet, and it is completely mechanical once you know the method.

Put the temperature you are given on a number line, with the melting point and boiling point marked on it. Then read off which region it lands in.

Try it with this table. What state is each substance in at room temperature, 25 °C?

Oxygen boils at −183 °C, and 25 is well above that, so oxygen is a gas — which fits, because we breathe it. Sulfur melts at 115 °C, and 25 is below that, so sulfur is still solid. No knowledge of sulfur is needed at all; the numbers do the work.

Commonly missed

Substance X has a melting point of −39 °C and a boiling point of 357 °C. What state is X in at −50 °C?

The trap is the minus signs. Many students see −50 and −39 and pick the "bigger number", concluding that −50 is above the melting point and calling X a liquid.

How to work it: on a number line, −50 sits to the left of −39, so −50 is colder than the melting point. Colder than the melting point means it has not melted yet. X is a solid. With negative temperatures, the number that looks larger is the colder one.

15.3

Evaporation is not boiling

Both turn a liquid into a gas, so they are easy to confuse. They are not the same process, and questions test the difference constantly.

Boiling happens only at the boiling point, and it happens throughout the whole liquid at once. That is why bubbles form deep down at the bottom of a pan and rise up. Wet clothes hung out to dry are certainly not at 100 °C, yet they dry, so something else must be going on.

That something else is evaporation. It happens at any temperature, and only at the surface of the liquid. A few of the fastest particles at the top escape into the air. No bubbles, no fixed temperature, no heating required.

Evaporation vs boiling

Evaporation: any temperature, surface only, no bubbles, gradual. Boiling: one fixed temperature, throughout the liquid, bubbles, rapid.

Four things make evaporation faster, and all four make sense once you picture particles escaping from a surface. Raise the temperature and more particles have enough energy to escape. Spread the liquid over a larger surface area and there are more places to escape from — which is why washing dries faster hung out flat than crumpled in a heap. Add wind and the escaped particles are blown away before they can drop back in. And drier air holds more room for water vapour, so escaping is easier. Hot, wide, windy and dry: those are the four.

15.4

Heat going in, temperature standing still

Heat a beaker of ice on a steady flame and take the temperature every minute. You would expect a straight climb. You do not get one.

The temperature rises to 0 °C, then stops and stays there while the ice melts, even though the flame is still burning. Once all the ice has gone, the temperature climbs again — up to 100 °C, where it stops once more until every drop has boiled away.

The heat has not vanished. During melting it is being used to pull the particles apart from their fixed positions, not to make them move faster. Temperature measures how fast the particles are moving, so the temperature does not budge while that pulling-apart is going on.

Commonly missed

A beaker holds ice cubes floating in water. It is heated gently. What happens to the temperature of the mixture?

The instinct is that heating always raises temperature, so students answer that it rises steadily. It does not. While ice and liquid water sit together in the same beaker, the mixture is at exactly 0 °C and it stays there.

How to work it: ask "is there still ice left?" If yes, the mixture is at 0 °C, because all the incoming heat is being spent melting that ice rather than warming anything. Only once the last piece of ice has melted does the temperature start to climb. The same reasoning explains why a pan of boiling water stays at 100 °C no matter how high you turn the flame — turning it up makes it boil away faster, not hotter.

15.5

Physical change, chemical change and burning

A physical change makes no new substance. The material is rearranged but it is still the same stuff, so it can usually be changed back. Melting ice, dissolving salt, cutting paper, mixing sand into water — every one of these is reversible, and the substance you started with is still there.

A chemical change makes a new substance, with different properties from the original. These are usually hard or impossible to reverse. You cannot un-fry an egg.

Five signs suggest a chemical change: a colour change, bubbles of gas appearing, heat or light given out, a solid precipitate forming in a clear liquid, and a new smell.

Rusting is chemical: iron plus oxygen and water makes rust, a flaky orange solid nothing like shiny iron. Cooking an egg is chemical: the clear runny white turns solid and white, and cooling it down does not undo that.

Combustion, or burning, is a chemical change that needs three things at once — fuel, oxygen and heat. Remove any one and the flame goes out. That is exactly how firefighting works: water removes the heat, a fire blanket removes the oxygen, and a firebreak removes the fuel.

When there is plenty of oxygen you get complete combustion: a clean blue flame, producing carbon dioxide and water. When oxygen is short you get incomplete combustion: a yellow smoky flame, producing soot (black carbon) and carbon monoxide, a poisonous gas with no smell. This is why a blocked gas heater is dangerous and why a candle held under a cold plate leaves a black mark.

The deciding question

Ask only this: has a new substance been made? If the original stuff is still there in a different shape or state, it is physical. If it has turned into something with different properties, it is chemical.

15.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

15.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

Wet clothes hung outside dry completely, even though the air is nowhere near 100 °C. Explain how.

Show answer

The water is leaving by evaporation, not boiling. Evaporation happens at any temperature, because at the surface of the water a few particles are always moving fast enough to escape into the air.

Boiling would need 100 °C and would happen throughout the liquid with bubbles. Evaporation needs neither. It is slower, which is why drying takes hours rather than minutes.

Hanging the clothes spread out gives a bigger surface area, and a breeze carries escaped particles away, so both make the drying faster.

2

A substance melts at −7 °C and boils at 59 °C. What state is it in at −20 °C, at 20 °C, and at 80 °C? Explain your method.

Show answer

Compare each temperature with the two fixed points. At −20 °C the substance is colder than its melting point of −7 °C, so it has not melted yet: it is a solid. Watch the minus signs — −20 is colder than −7, not warmer.

At 20 °C it is above the melting point but below the boiling point, so it sits in the middle region: liquid.

At 80 °C it is above the boiling point of 59 °C, so it has boiled away: gas.

3

A pan of water is boiling. The cook turns the flame up as high as it will go. Does the water get hotter? Explain.

Show answer

No. The water stays at 100 °C. While a liquid is boiling, the extra heat is used to pull the particles apart into gas, not to speed them up, and temperature is a measure of how fast the particles move.

Turning up the flame supplies heat faster, so the water boils away faster — the pan empties sooner. But the temperature reading stays at 100 °C until the last of the water has gone.

4

Sugar is stirred into hot tea. Sugar is also heated in a dry pan until it goes black. One is a physical change and one is chemical. Say which is which and how you can tell.

Show answer

Stirring sugar into tea is a physical change. The sugar has dissolved but it is still sugar — the tea tastes sweet, and if the water were evaporated away the sugar would be left behind. No new substance was made.

Heating sugar until it blackens is a chemical change. The black solid is carbon, which is a different substance with different properties, and there is usually a burnt smell as well. Cooling it does not turn it back into sugar.

The test that separates them is always the same: has a new substance been made, and can you get the original back?

15.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 15.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which of the following changes release heat to the surroundings?

  • 1Melting
  • 2Freezing
  • 3Condensation
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Melting takes a solid to a liquid, which needs the particles pulled apart, so it absorbs heat. The moment statement 1 is wrong the answer is C, and you can stop.

Checking anyway: freezing and condensation both go the other way, from looser to tighter, so the particles give energy up. Both release heat. Consistent with C.

2

A beaker contains ice and water together at 0 °C. It is heated steadily. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The temperature stays at 0 °C while ice is still present.
  • 2The heat is being used to melt the ice.
  • 3The temperature rises steadily from the moment heating begins.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. A mixture of ice and water sits at exactly 0 °C for as long as any ice remains. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. It directly contradicts statement 1, and two statements cannot both be true if they disagree. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. The heat has to be going somewhere, and it is going into breaking the ice particles apart rather than raising the temperature. So the answer is B.

3

Which statements about burning a candle are correct?

  • 1Burning the wax is a chemical change.
  • 2The wax melting near the flame is a physical change.
  • 3A yellow smoky flame suggests there is not enough oxygen.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Burning makes new substances, carbon dioxide and water, and gives out heat and light. The wax cannot be recovered. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. A yellow smoky flame is the sign of incomplete combustion, which happens when oxygen is short. That points to A.

Statement 2 — correct. Melted wax is still wax, and it hardens back when it cools, so that part is physical. All three hold, so the answer is A. Note that one candle shows a physical and a chemical change happening at the same time.
