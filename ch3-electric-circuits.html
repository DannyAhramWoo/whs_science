Chapter 3

Electric Circuits

Current, conductors, and why bulbs in parallel stay bright. We start from what a circuit actually is, not from formulas.

3.1

A circuit must be a closed loop

Electric current is the flow of charge. Charge can only flow if there is a complete path — a loop — from one end of the battery, through the components, and back to the other end.

Break the loop anywhere and everything stops. Not "the part after the break" — everything. This is the single most useful idea in the whole chapter.

Key idea

Current is not "used up" by the bulb. The same amount of charge that leaves the battery comes back to it. What the bulb uses is energy, not charge.

3.2

Conductors and insulators

A conductor lets charge move through it freely. Metals — copper, iron, aluminium — are conductors because their electrons are loosely held and can drift from atom to atom.

An insulator holds its electrons tightly. Rubber, plastic, glass and dry wood are insulators. Put an insulator in the loop and you have made a gap, even though the wire looks connected.

Commonly missed

Points 1–8 sit on a board. Some are joined by copper, some by iron, one by rubber. Which connections will light the bulb?

The trap here is that all three look like connections. But copper and iron are metals — current passes. Rubber is an insulator — it is a gap wearing a disguise.

How to work it: trace the loop with your finger. If your finger crosses the rubber link at any point, that path is dead, no matter how neat it looks.

3.3

Series and parallel

Two bulbs can share a battery in two different ways, and the difference matters enormously.

In series, there is only one loop. The charge must pass through the first bulb and then the second. Both bulbs share the push from the battery, so each one gets less — both glow dim. And if one bulb burns out, the loop breaks and both go dark.

In parallel, the loop splits into two branches. Each bulb gets the full push of the battery, so each glows brightly. Remove one bulb and the other branch is still a complete loop — the second bulb keeps shining.

Why your house is wired in parallel

If your home were wired in series, switching off the kitchen light would switch off every light in the house. Parallel wiring is why one blown bulb doesn't darken every room.

3.4

Where the meters go

This is the question you missed, and it catches almost everyone. The rule comes straight from what each meter measures.

An ammeter measures current — the charge flowing through a point. To measure what flows through something, the meter must sit in the loop so the same current passes through it. That means in series.

A voltmeter measures the push across a component — the difference between one side and the other. To compare two sides, it must touch both sides at once, bridging across. That means in parallel.

Remember it this way

Ammeter — Along the loop. Voltmeter — Vaults over the component. If a diagram shows a voltmeter sitting in the loop like a bead on a string, it is wrong.

Commonly missed

Which circuit shows the correct way of connecting an ammeter and a voltmeter?

How to eliminate fast: look only at the V first. Any option where the voltmeter sits inside the main loop is out immediately — you can reject two or three options in about five seconds. Then check that the A is in the loop, not bridging.

3.5

Resistance, without the algebra

Resistance is how much a component slows the flow of charge. A thin wire resists more than a thick one, in the same way a narrow doorway slows a crowd more than a wide one.

You do not need resistance formulas here. But you do need two facts you can reason out:

More resistors in series → more total resistance. Every extra resistor is one more narrow doorway the charge must squeeze through, one after another.

More resistors in parallel → less total resistance. This feels backwards until you picture it: adding a parallel branch is opening a second doorway beside the first. More ways through means an easier journey overall.

Commonly missed

Given three resistors with different values, which arrangement produces the maximum equivalent resistance?

Straight from the rule above: series adds up, parallel cuts down. For the largest total, everything must be in series.

Answer: (B) arrange all resistors in series. You never need to calculate a single number — the reasoning alone gets you there.

3.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

This section is worth 2 marks per question, so it carries far more weight than its length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

3.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A torch stops working. The bulb is fine and the batteries are new. Give two other things that could break the circuit.

Show answer

The switch may be faulty, so the loop never closes. A metal contact inside may be dirty, corroded or bent away from the battery, leaving a gap. A spring or connecting strip may have come loose.

All of these are the same idea in different clothing: the loop is not complete.

2

Two identical bulbs are in series. A third identical bulb is added, still in series. What happens to the brightness of the original two, and why?

Show answer

They get dimmer.

Adding another bulb in series adds more resistance to the single loop, so less current flows. On top of that, the battery's push is now shared between three bulbs instead of two, so each one receives a smaller share of the energy.

3

Why would it be a serious mistake to connect an ammeter across a bulb, the way a voltmeter is connected?

Show answer

An ammeter is built to have almost no resistance, because it must not disturb the current it is measuring. Connecting it across the bulb gives the charge a nearly free path that bypasses the bulb entirely.

The result: the bulb dims or goes out, a very large current flows through the meter, and the ammeter can be damaged.

4

A string of decorative lights is wired in series. One bulb burns out and the whole string goes dark. Explain why, and suggest how the manufacturer could prevent it.

Show answer

In series there is only one loop. A burnt-out bulb is a break in that loop, so current stops everywhere and every bulb goes dark.

The fix is to wire the lights in parallel. Each bulb then sits on its own branch, and a failed bulb only breaks its own branch — the rest stay lit.

3.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 3.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Two identical bulbs are connected in parallel to a battery. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Each bulb receives the full voltage of the battery.
  • 2If one bulb is removed, the other goes out.
  • 3The bulbs are brighter than the same two bulbs in series.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: D

Statement 1 — correct. In parallel, each branch is connected straight across the battery, so each bulb gets the full voltage. Since 1 is right, the answer must be A, B or D.

Statement 2 — wrong. This is the whole point of parallel wiring. Removing one bulb leaves the other branch a complete loop, so it stays lit. That rules out A and B.

Statement 3 — correct, but by now it doesn't matter. With 1 right and 2 wrong, only D remains.

2

A student sets up a circuit to measure the current through a bulb and the voltage across it. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The voltmeter should be connected in series with the bulb.
  • 2The ammeter should be connected in series with the bulb.
  • 3The voltmeter should be connected across the bulb.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. A voltmeter goes across, never in the loop. And the moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C — no other option is possible.

You could stop right there. But checking: statement 2 is correct (ammeters go in series) and statement 3 is correct (voltmeters go across). Both consistent with C.

3

A circuit contains a battery, a switch and a bulb, all in one loop. The switch is opened. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The bulb goes out.
  • 2Current still flows in the part of the circuit before the switch.
  • 3The circuit is now an open circuit.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: D

Statement 1 — correct. Breaking the loop stops the current, so the bulb goes out. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 2 — wrong. This is the classic misunderstanding. Current does not "pile up" before a gap and keep flowing there. A break stops the flow everywhere in the loop at once. That eliminates A and B.

Statement 3 — correct, but D is already forced.
