Chapter 9

Where Energy Comes From

Every energy source we use has a story about where it came from and what it costs. The biggest mistake in this topic is assuming that "renewable" and "clean" mean the same thing. They do not.

9.1

Renewable and non-renewable sources

An energy source is renewable if nature replaces it at least as fast as we use it. Sunlight arrives every morning whether we collect it or not. Wind keeps blowing. Rivers keep flowing. We cannot use these up.

An energy source is non-renewable if it took far longer to form than it takes to burn. Coal, oil and natural gas are called fossil fuels because they formed from the remains of living things — plants and tiny sea creatures — buried and squeezed under rock for millions of years. We burn in one second what took thousands of years to make. That is why they will run out.

Nuclear fuel is the fourth non-renewable source. It is not a fossil fuel — it does not burn and it did not come from living things. It is uranium, a metal dug out of the ground, and there is only a fixed amount of it.

The test to apply

Ask: can nature make more of this within a human lifetime? Trees grow back in decades, so wood is renewable. Coal needs millions of years, so it is not. The question is never about how much is left — it is about how fast it is replaced.

9.2

Renewable does not mean clean

Biomass means fuel made from living or recently living material — wood, straw, animal waste, or crops such as sugar cane and maize. When biomass is processed into a liquid fuel for vehicles it is called biofuel.

Biofuel is renewable. You can plant another field of sugar cane next season, and it will be ready in months. But biofuel still has to be burnt to release its energy, and burning anything that contains carbon produces carbon dioxide. A bus running on biofuel puts carbon dioxide out of its exhaust pipe just as a diesel bus does.

So there are two separate questions, and a question in this format will often ask only one of them. "Will it run out?" is about renewability. "Does it pollute?" is about cleanliness. A source can be renewable and dirty, like biofuel. It can also be non-renewable and produce no carbon dioxide, like nuclear.

Renewable ≠ clean

Biofuel is renewable because we can grow more of it, not because it is clean. Burning it still releases carbon dioxide. The word "renewable" only tells you about supply, never about pollution.

Commonly missed

Which of these renewable energy sources releases carbon dioxide when it is used?

The trap is that the word "renewable" pulls your mind straight to "clean", so you look for a non-renewable option and find none. Read the stem again: it has already told you every option is renewable.

How to work it: ask which one is burnt. Solar, wind, hydroelectric, tidal and geothermal are never set on fire — they capture movement or heat that is already there. Only biomass or biofuel is burnt, so only that one releases carbon dioxide.

9.3

Almost everything traces back to the Sun

Follow any energy source backwards far enough and you usually arrive at the Sun.

Fossil fuels are ancient stored sunlight. Millions of years ago, plants used sunlight to make food by photosynthesis. That energy was locked inside them. The plants died, were buried, and slowly turned into coal and oil. Burning coal today releases sunlight that arrived before the dinosaurs.

Wind is sunlight too. The Sun heats some parts of the Earth more strongly than others. Warm air rises, cooler air moves in underneath, and that moving air is wind. Without uneven heating there would be no wind at all.

Hydroelectric power is sunlight as well. The Sun evaporates water from the sea. It rises, cools, forms clouds and falls as rain on high ground. Gravity then pulls that water downhill through the turbines. The Sun did the lifting.

Three sources do not come from the Sun. Nuclear energy comes from splitting uranium atoms. Geothermal energy comes from heat inside the Earth itself. Tidal energy comes mostly from the Moon's gravitational pull on the oceans.

Commonly missed

Which energy source does not ultimately depend on energy from the Sun?

Coal looks like the obvious answer because it comes out of the ground and has nothing to do with sunshine now. That is the trap. Coal formed from plants, and those plants grew using sunlight, so coal is stored solar energy.

How to work it: tick off everything that traces to the Sun — fossil fuels, wind, waves, hydroelectric, biomass. Whatever is left will be nuclear, geothermal or tidal.

9.4

Solar cells and what changes their output

A solar cell converts light energy directly into electrical energy. There are no moving parts, no burning and no fuel. This makes it different from almost every other power station, where a fuel heats water, the steam spins a turbine, and the turbine turns a generator.

Questions about solar cells nearly always test one thing: what makes the output bigger or smaller? Four factors matter.

Brightness of the light. Brighter light carries more energy each second, so the cell produces more electricity. A cell gives less on a cloudy day and nothing at night.

Angle of the panel. A panel facing the Sun squarely catches the most light. Tilt it away and the same beam is spread over a larger area of panel, so each part of it receives less.

Area of the panel. A larger panel intercepts more light, so it produces more electricity. Two identical panels side by side give roughly twice the output of one.

Shading. A tree, a cloud or even a layer of dust blocks light before it reaches the cell. Output drops immediately.

Direct conversion

A solar cell turns light energy into electrical energy in one step. Do not write that it turns heat into electricity — a solar cell needs light, not warmth. A bright cold day gives more output than a dull warm one.

9.5

Reading a table about energy sources

A very common question gives you a small table and asks you to draw a conclusion. The science needed is usually easy. The marks are lost by reading the table carelessly.

Here is the sort of table you will meet. It compares four ways of generating the same amount of electricity.

SourceCost per unitCarbon dioxide releasedAvailable at night
PLowHighYes
QHighNoneNo
RMediumNoneYes
SMediumMediumYes

Work through it in a fixed order and it becomes simple.

First, read the column headings. Every one of them. Here "carbon dioxide released" and "available at night" pull in different directions, and the question will exploit that.

Second, name the sources if you can. Q releases no carbon dioxide and is unavailable at night — that is solar. P is cheap, dirty and always available — a fossil fuel. R is clean and always available, which fits hydroelectric, geothermal or nuclear.

Third, answer only what was asked. If the question says "best for reducing air pollution", ignore the cost column completely. If it says "cheapest", ignore everything except cost. Most wrong answers come from choosing the source that is best overall rather than best at the one thing asked.

Commonly missed

Using the table, which source is the most suitable for a country that wants a steady electricity supply and low air pollution?

The trap is Q. It releases no carbon dioxide, so it looks like the winner on pollution, and many people stop reading there. But the question also asks for a steady supply, and Q is unavailable at night.

How to work it: when a question gives two conditions, cross out every row that fails either one. Q fails "steady". P and S fail "low pollution". Only R satisfies both, so R is the answer. Two conditions means two passes through the table.

9.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

9.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A friend says "biofuel is renewable, so it does not pollute". Explain what is right and what is wrong in that sentence.

Show answer

Right: biofuel is renewable. It is made from crops that can be grown again within a season, so we cannot use it up the way we can use up oil.

Wrong: renewable says nothing about pollution. Biofuel has to be burnt to release its energy, and burning it produces carbon dioxide, which contributes to global warming.

Renewability is about supply. Cleanliness is about what comes out when the fuel is used. They are two different questions.

2

Explain how the energy in a lump of coal originally came from the Sun.

Show answer

Millions of years ago, plants used sunlight to make food by photosynthesis. That trapped the Sun's energy inside the plants as chemical energy.

When the plants died they were buried under mud and rock. Over millions of years, heat and pressure turned them into coal, and the energy stayed locked inside.

Burning coal today releases that energy as heat and light. It is sunlight that arrived on Earth long before there were any people to use it.

3

Two identical solar panels are set up outside on the same sunny day. One produces much less electricity than the other. Give three possible reasons.

Show answer

Shading. A tree, a wall or a building could be blocking part of the light from reaching one panel.

Angle. One panel may be tilted away from the Sun, so the light spreads across it more thinly and fewer rays land on it.

Dust or dirt on the surface. A dusty panel lets less light through to the cells underneath.

The panels are identical and the day is the same, so brightness and area cannot be the difference. That is why the answer must involve how much light actually reaches each cell.

4

Give one environmental problem caused by burning coal, and explain how it happens.

Show answer

Burning coal releases carbon dioxide. This gas builds up in the atmosphere and traps heat that would otherwise escape into space, which slowly warms the planet. That is global warming.

Coal also contains sulfur. Burning it releases sulfur dioxide, which dissolves in the water droplets in clouds and falls as acid rain. Acid rain damages trees, kills fish in lakes and wears away stone buildings.

Burning coal also produces smoke and tiny particles, which pollute the air people breathe.

9.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 9.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about energy sources are correct?

  • 1Nuclear fuel is a fossil fuel.
  • 2Coal, oil and natural gas will eventually run out.
  • 3Tidal energy does not come from the Sun.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Nuclear fuel is uranium, a metal mined from rock. Fossil fuels formed from the remains of living things, and uranium did not. Nuclear is non-renewable but it is not a fossil fuel. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C.

Checking anyway: coal, oil and gas take millions of years to form, so they will run out — statement 2 is correct. Tides are caused mainly by the Moon's gravitational pull, not the Sun — statement 3 is correct. Consistent with C.

2

A solar cell is connected to a small motor outdoors. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The solar cell converts light energy into electrical energy.
  • 2The motor will run faster if a cloud moves away from the Sun.
  • 3The motor will keep running at night as long as the air stays warm.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. That is exactly what a solar cell does, and it does it in one step with no moving parts. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. A solar cell needs light, not warmth. With no light there is no electricity, however warm the air is. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Removing the cloud makes the light brighter, so the cell produces more electricity and the motor speeds up. So the answer is B.

Watch for this trap: statements that swap "light" for "heat" are a favourite. A solar cell is not a solar heater.

3

Which statements about renewable energy are correct?

  • 1Renewable sources are replaced by nature as fast as we use them.
  • 2Burning biofuel releases carbon dioxide.
  • 3Wind exists because the Sun heats the air unevenly.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. That is the definition. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. The Sun warms some parts of the Earth more than others. Warm air rises and cooler air flows in to take its place, and that moving air is wind. Since 1 and 3 are both correct, the answer must be A.

Statement 2 — correct. Biofuel contains carbon and has to be burnt, so carbon dioxide comes out. It is renewable because more can be grown, not because it is clean. Confirms A.
