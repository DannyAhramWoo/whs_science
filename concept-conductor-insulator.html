Concept

Conductors and Insulators

A conductor is a material that lets electric charge move through it; an insulator is one that does not.

Introduced in Chapter 3: Electric Circuits

1

What it means

Every wire you have ever seen is really two materials doing two opposite jobs. Inside is metal, chosen because charge moves through it easily. Around it is plastic, chosen because charge cannot move through it at all. The metal is a conductor; the plastic is an insulator. The wire works because both are there.

The difference comes down to one question: are there charged particles inside the material that are free to move? In a metal, each atom releases one or two of its outer electrons — the small negatively charged particles that orbit an atom's centre. Those loose electrons wander through the whole block of metal, belonging to no atom in particular. Connect a battery and they all drift the same way. That drift is a current.

In plastic, rubber, dry wood or glass, every electron is tightly bound to its own atom. There is nothing loose to push. You can apply a voltage and almost nothing moves, so no useful current flows.

It helps to know that this is a scale rather than two boxes. Silver and copper conduct superbly, iron less well, tap water weakly, pure water hardly at all, and glass essentially not. "Conductor" and "insulator" are the two ends of a range, and some materials sit awkwardly in the middle.

2

How it works

Think of a metal as a fixed lattice of positive atom-cores sitting in a sea of loose electrons. The cores stay put; the sea can slosh. This picture explains more than just electricity. It also explains why metals conduct heat well, because the same mobile electrons carry energy quickly from a hot end to a cold end. That is why a metal spoon in hot soup heats up along its whole length while a wooden one does not, and why metal feels cold to touch even at room temperature — it is drawing heat out of your hand faster than wood can.

It also explains why metals are shiny and can be bent without shattering. The electron sea holds the atoms together even when they slide past one another, so metal bends rather than cracks. One underlying structure, four everyday properties.

Insulators are not merely useless conductors. They do a job. Without the plastic sheath, two wires touching would give charge a shortcut and the circuit would fail — or worse, the enormous current in a short circuit would heat the wire enough to start a fire. Insulators force charge to go the long way round, through the components you actually want to power.

Some cases need care. Pure water is a poor conductor, but tap water, sweat and seawater conduct reasonably well because dissolved salts break into charged particles called ions that can move through the liquid. That is why wet hands and electricity are a serious danger while distilled water is comparatively tame. Similarly, dry air is an excellent insulator, yet push the voltage high enough and it breaks down and conducts violently — that is lightning. Graphite is another exception worth remembering: it is a non-metal, yet it conducts, which is why pencil lead completes a circuit.

The heart of it

A material conducts if it contains charged particles free to move, and insulates if all its charges are locked in place.

3

Where the idea came from

The split was first made in England around 1729 by Stephen Gray, a dyer by trade who did science in his spare time and was living as a pensioner at the Charterhouse in London. Static electricity had been known for centuries, but everyone assumed it stayed where it was made. Gray discovered that it could travel.

He rubbed a glass tube to charge it, and found the charge could pass along a long damp thread to a distant object and attract feathers there. Then came the crucial observation. When he hung his thread from lines of ordinary string, the "electric virtue", as he called it, drained away and never reached the far end. When he hung it from lines of silk instead, it arrived. The silk was not carrying the electricity anywhere — it was refusing to.

Gray had accidentally sorted the world into two classes. His French correspondent Charles Du Fay took the work further, and the words conductor and insulator settled into use over the following decades. Note what made the discovery possible: Gray was paying attention to the part of the apparatus he was not studying. The silk was meant to be scaffolding, not evidence.

The deeper explanation had to wait almost two centuries, until the electron was identified around 1897 and it became clear why some materials had mobile charges and others did not. Gray sorted materials correctly long before anybody could say what he was sorting.

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent slip is treating "metal" and "conductor" as the same word. Most conductors are metals, but graphite is not, and salty water is not a metal either. Going the other way, a few people assume anything non-metallic must be safe around electricity, which is exactly the assumption that makes wet hands dangerous.

A second confusion is between electrical and thermal insulation. They usually go together, because both depend on mobile electrons, but not always. Diamond is an outstanding electrical insulator and yet conducts heat better than most metals, because heat can also travel as vibrations through a stiff, well-ordered structure. So do not assume one from the other without checking.

Commonly missed

A pupil tests a circuit and finds it works when a metal paper clip is used to bridge a gap, but not when a pencil is placed across the gap end to end. She concludes graphite is an insulator. What has she overlooked?

The trap is that she tested the pencil the way you would hold it to write, so the two ends of the gap were touching the wooden casing, not the graphite core.

How to work it: Ask which material the charge would actually have to pass through on the route it is offered. From end to end, the outside of the pencil is wood, an insulator, so no current flows. Sharpen both ends, or clip onto the exposed graphite, and the circuit works — graphite is a genuine non-metal conductor. The lesson is that a negative result tells you about the path you tested, not necessarily about the material you meant to test.

5

Connected ideas

Conductors are the materials that permit an electric current, and how strongly a conductor opposes that flow is its resistance. The same mobile electrons that carry charge also carry heat, which links straight to conduction as a way heat travels. Whether components are in series or parallel decides which conducting paths the charge is offered.

In one line

Conductors have charges free to move and so allow current, insulators hold every charge in place and so block it, and the wire in your hand needs both at once.
