Chapter 20

How Scientists Work

This chapter is not about one topic. It is about the skill hidden inside questions on every topic: telling what you saw from what you concluded, spotting the experiment that changed two things at once, and knowing when a result is strong enough to trust.

20.1

Observation and inference

An observation is what you actually see, hear, smell or measure. It comes in through your senses or off an instrument. If you tried to argue about it, you would settle it by looking again.

An inference is a conclusion you build on top of an observation. It goes beyond what you saw. It might be right, but it is your reasoning, not your eyes.

Put two liquids together and the mixture turns cloudy. "The liquid turned cloudy" is an observation — anyone in the room would report the same thing. "A new substance was formed" is an inference. You did not see a new substance. You saw cloudiness, and you reasoned your way to a cause.

The one-question test

Ask: did I see this, or did I work it out? "The plant grew 4 cm" — saw it. "The plant grew because of the fertiliser" — worked it out. Words like because, therefore, must have and shows that almost always mark an inference.

Commonly missed

A student records: "The wet cloth felt cold." Is this an observation or an inference?

The trap is thinking that anything involving feeling rather than measuring must be an opinion, so it must be an inference. It is not. Touch is a sense, and coldness came straight through it.

How to work it: "The cloth felt cold" is an observation. "The water was evaporating and taking heat from my hand" is the inference. Same event, two different kinds of statement — and the question is always asking which one you have been handed.

20.2

Hypotheses, variables and the fair test

A hypothesis is a testable prediction. It says what you think will happen, and it is worded so that an experiment could prove it wrong. The usual shape is if … then …: "If a plant is given more light, then it will grow taller."

A question is not a hypothesis. "Does light affect plant growth?" predicts nothing. A fact is not a hypothesis either — "plants need light" is something already known, not something you are about to test.

To test a hypothesis you handle three kinds of variable. The independent variable is the one thing you deliberately change. The dependent variable is what you measure to see the effect. The controlled variables are everything else you deliberately keep the same.

Take the plants. You change the hours of light — that is independent. You measure the height after two weeks — that is dependent. You keep the pot size, soil, water, temperature and type of seed identical — those are controlled. Change only one thing and any difference in height must have come from that one thing. That is a fair test.

Spotting the flaw

When a question describes an experiment and asks what is wrong with it, read across the two set-ups and list every difference. If you find two, that is the flaw — and the correct wording is that you cannot tell which of the two caused the result.

Commonly missed

Which of these is a hypothesis? (1) Does sugar dissolve faster in hot water? (2) Sugar dissolves in water. (3) If water is heated, sugar will dissolve faster in it.

Statement 1 looks scientific and mentions exactly the right things — but it is a question, and a question predicts nothing, so nothing can prove it wrong. Statement 2 is a plain fact everyone already accepts. Neither can be tested by an experiment, because neither takes a risk.

How to work it: statement 3. Check three boxes in order — does it predict something, could an experiment show it to be false, and does it link a change to an effect? Only 3 passes all three. A hypothesis must be capable of being wrong.

20.3

Repeating, and why one result proves nothing

Suppose you grow one plant in bright light and one in dim light, and the bright one ends up taller. Have you shown that light makes plants grow? No. That one seed might simply have been a stronger seed.

The fix is repeating. Use ten plants in each group instead of one. Take each measurement three times and calculate the average (add the readings and divide by how many there are). Averaging pulls the odd freak reading back towards the truth, and a large group makes it unlikely that every plant in it happened to be unusual.

Results you can trust after repeating are called reliable. Reliable does not mean the answer is correct — it means that if you did the whole thing again, you would get much the same numbers.

Two different repairs

Repeating fixes flukes. Controlling variables fixes confusion about the cause. A question asking how to improve an experiment usually wants one or the other, so check which problem the experiment actually has before answering.

20.4

Accuracy and precision are not the same thing

Accurate means close to the true value. Precise means the readings agree closely with each other. These sound similar in ordinary English. In science they are separate, and questions are built on the gap between them.

An instrument can be precise and still be wrong. A thermometer that reads 1.2 degrees too high every single time gives beautifully consistent readings — 26.2, 26.2, 26.3 — that are all wrong by the same amount. Precise, not accurate. That steady error has a name: it is a systematic error, and repeating the measurement will never remove it.

A very common question gives you a table of instruments and asks which is most accurate. The method is always the same: work out how far each reading sits from the true value, and pick the smallest gap. Ignore how tidy the numbers look.

ThermometerReadings (°C)Distance from true 25.0 °C
W26.2, 26.2, 26.3about 1.2 too high, every time
X24.9, 25.1, 25.0about 0.1 out
Y23.0, 27.0, 25.1average is close, single readings are not
Z28.4, 28.5, 28.4about 3.4 too high, every time

X is the most accurate — its readings sit closest to 25.0 °C. W and Z are precise, because their readings agree with each other, but both are consistently wrong. Y is neither: it is scattered, and only its average happens to land near the truth.

Commonly missed

In the table above, a student picks W because its three readings are so close together.

This is the trap the whole question is built around. Consistency is easy to see and feels like quality, so the eye goes to the tightest set of numbers. But agreeing with yourself is not the same as agreeing with reality.

How to work it: cover the readings and write down the true value first. Then compare each instrument to that number, not to itself. If the question says "accurate", the true value must appear in your working. If it says "precise", it must not.

20.5

Measuring well, and knowing what your result proves

Choosing apparatus is part of the science. For volume, a measuring cylinder is the everyday choice; a burette lets you release a liquid slowly and read exactly how much came out; a pipette delivers one fixed volume very accurately. For small lengths, a vernier caliper reads far finer than a ruler — the width of a coin, the diameter of a wire.

Then read it properly. Liquid in a narrow tube curves at the top; that curve is the meniscus, and you read the bottom of it with your eye level with the mark. Look from above or below and the reading shifts, because the scale and the liquid line up differently from different angles. That shift is parallax error, and it is a fault in the person, not the instrument.

Once you have your numbers, show them the right way. A bar chart is for separate categories — four different metals, three types of soil, five brands of battery — because there is nothing in between "copper" and "iron". A line graph is for something changing continuously, usually over time or over a steadily increasing measurement, because every point between your readings really exists.

Finally, keep your conclusion inside your evidence. If you tested four metals and all four conducted electricity, you have shown that those four conduct. You have not shown that all metals do — you did not test all metals. The correct wording is careful: "the four metals tested all conducted electricity." A conclusion that reaches wider than the experiment is the single most common way a good set of results gets thrown away.

Match the graph to the data

Ask whether the space between two of your labels means anything. Between 10 seconds and 20 seconds there is 15 seconds, so that is a line graph. Between "copper" and "iron" there is nothing at all, so that is a bar chart.

20.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

20.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A student writes: "The ice cube in the black cup melted in 4 minutes. The black cup absorbed more heat." Separate the observation from the inference, and explain how you decided.

Show answer

The observation is "the ice cube in the black cup melted in 4 minutes". That came off a clock and out of the student's eyes, and anyone else watching would have recorded the same thing.

The inference is "the black cup absorbed more heat". Nobody saw heat being absorbed. The student saw faster melting and reasoned backwards to a cause.

The test is whether you could settle a disagreement by looking again. You could re-run the melting and time it. You could not simply look and see absorption.

2

A student tests how the length of a pendulum affects its swing. She uses a 20 cm string with a heavy metal bob, and a 40 cm string with a light plastic bob. What is wrong, and how would you fix it?

Show answer

Two things changed at once: the length of the string and the bob. That means it is not a fair test.

If the two pendulums swing differently, there is no way to tell whether the length caused it or the bob did. The independent variable was meant to be length alone, so the bob should have been a controlled variable.

The fix: use the same bob on both strings, changing only the length. She should also time several swings and take an average, so that one slightly mistimed swing does not decide the result.

3

A balance reads 2 g when nothing is on it. A student weighs the same stone five times and gets 47 g every time. Are the readings precise? Are they accurate? Explain.

Show answer

They are precise. Five readings agreed exactly, so the balance is highly consistent with itself.

They are not accurate. The balance starts at 2 g instead of 0 g, so every reading is 2 g too high. The stone's real mass is 45 g.

This is a systematic error: it is the same size and in the same direction every time, so repeating the measurement will never reveal it. The only cure is to reset the balance to zero before use, or subtract 2 g from every reading.

4

A student tested four metals. All four conducted electricity. She concludes: "All metals conduct electricity." Is her conclusion valid? Write a better one.

Show answer

Her conclusion is not valid from this experiment. She tested four metals, not all metals, so her evidence does not stretch as far as her claim.

A better conclusion: "All four of the metals tested conducted electricity." That says exactly what the results support and nothing more.

It happens that all metals really do conduct electricity — but she did not show that, she assumed it. In science the strength of a conclusion depends on the evidence behind it, not on whether it turns out to be true.

20.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 20.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

A student investigates how the amount of fertiliser affects plant height. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The height of the plants is the independent variable.
  • 2The amount of fertiliser is the independent variable.
  • 3The amount of water given must be kept the same for every plant.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Height is what she measures, so it is the dependent variable, not the independent one. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: fertiliser is the one thing she deliberately changes, so statement 2 is correct. Water must be controlled, or a taller plant might be explained by extra water rather than fertiliser — so statement 3 is correct too. Consistent with C.

2

A thermometer reads 1.2 °C higher than the true temperature every time it is used. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Its readings are precise.
  • 2Its readings are not accurate.
  • 3Taking more readings and averaging them would remove the error.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. The readings agree closely with each other, which is what precise means. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Averaging only helps when errors go both ways and cancel out. This error is 1.2 °C too high every single time, so the average of the readings is also 1.2 °C too high. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Every reading sits 1.2 °C away from the true value, so it is not accurate. So the answer is B.

3

A student measures the volume of water in a measuring cylinder. Which statements are correct?

  • 1She should read the bottom of the curved liquid surface.
  • 2Her eye should be level with the liquid surface.
  • 3Reading from above the liquid would give a reading that is too low.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. The meniscus curves, and the bottom of that curve is the agreed point to read. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Looking down, your line of sight meets the scale at a mark below the true one, so the reading comes out too low. That means the answer is A.

Statement 2 — correct. Eye level is exactly what prevents parallax error. All three are right, so the answer is A.
