Concept

Food Chains and Webs

A food chain is a way of drawing who eats whom, so that you can follow energy as it passes from one living thing to the next.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Every living thing needs energy, and almost all of it starts in the same place: the Sun. A plant catches sunlight and uses it to build sugar. A caterpillar eats the leaf. A bird eats the caterpillar. A hawk eats the bird. If you write that down in order and put an arrow between each pair, you have a food chain.

The arrows matter more than people expect, and they are the first thing to get right. The arrow points from the eaten to the eater, so you write grass → caterpillar, not the other way round. It is tempting to draw the arrow the way the mouth goes, but the arrow is not showing eating. It is showing which way the energy travels. Energy leaves the grass and ends up in the caterpillar, so the arrow points at the caterpillar.

Each step along the chain has a name. The plant at the start is the producer, because it produces its own food rather than taking it from something else. Everything after it is a consumer. The caterpillar, eating the producer directly, is a primary consumer; the bird eating the caterpillar is a secondary consumer; the hawk is a tertiary consumer. These are just labels for position in the chain, not permanent titles for the animal. The same bird can be a primary consumer when it eats seeds and a secondary consumer ten minutes later when it eats a beetle.

A food web is what you get when you stop pretending each animal eats only one thing. Real habitats are webs, not chains. A food chain is a single thread pulled out of the web so it is easier to look at.

2

How it works

The most useful thing a food chain tells you is why chains are short. Chains almost never run beyond four or five links, and there is a reason rather than a coincidence. At every step, most of the energy is lost.

Think about what a caterpillar does with the leaf it eats. Some of it passes straight through undigested. A great deal of the rest is used up simply staying alive — moving, growing, repairing, and running the chemical work of respiration, which releases energy as heat. Only what is left over gets built into caterpillar body, and only that leftover part is available to the bird that eats it. As a rough guide, biologists often use about a tenth. Roughly a tenth of the energy at one level ends up stored at the next.

Follow the numbers and the shortness explains itself. Suppose the plants in a field capture 10,000 units of energy. The insects eating them hold about 1,000. The small birds hold about 100. A hawk eating those birds gets about 10. A fifth link would be working with about 1 unit, which is not enough to feed an animal large enough to hunt hawks. The chain does not stop because of a rule; it stops because the energy runs out.

The same arithmetic explains why there are always fewer large predators than small prey animals, and why big predators need enormous territories. A hawk is not rare because it is unlucky. It is rare because supporting one hawk quietly requires an entire field of grass underneath it.

Two groups get left off most diagrams and should not be. Decomposers — fungi and bacteria — feed on dead bodies and waste, breaking them down and returning the nutrients to the soil so producers can use them again. And scavengers eat animals that died without being hunted. Without decomposers the whole system would stall, because the raw materials would stay locked inside dead matter instead of being recycled.

The heart of it

Food chains are short because roughly nine tenths of the energy is lost at every step, mostly as heat, so there is nothing left to support a sixth or seventh link.

3

Where the idea came from

People have always known that animals eat each other. Turning that into a tool you can reason with is much more recent. The British ecologist Charles Elton set it out in his book Animal Ecology, published in 1927, which introduced both the food chain and the pyramid of numbers to a wide readership.

Elton's pyramid of numbers was the striking part. He pointed out that if you count the individuals at each level of a habitat, you almost always get a shape that is wide at the bottom and narrow at the top: many plants, fewer plant-eaters, fewer still meat-eaters. Drawing it as a pyramid made a pattern visible that had been in front of everyone for centuries without being named.

The pyramid also carried a problem that later work had to fix. Counting individuals sometimes gives a shape that is not a pyramid at all. A single oak tree can feed thousands of caterpillars, so counting bodies gives you one producer supporting a huge number of consumers — an upside-down pyramid. The pattern was real, but counting heads was the wrong measurement. Ecologists later moved to measuring the mass of living material, and better still the energy, and with those measurements the pyramid shape returns reliably. This is a good example of a scientist being right about the pattern and wrong about how to measure it.

4

What people get wrong

The arrow direction is the single most common error, and it is worth fixing by remembering what the arrow means rather than by memorising a rule. The arrow follows the energy, and energy always moves into the eater.

The second error is assuming that removing an animal only affects the thing directly above or below it. In a web, effects travel both ways and often surprise people. Remove a predator and its prey may increase so much that they strip the plants bare, which then starves them. Remove a plant and a predator three links away can decline, even though it has never touched that plant.

Commonly missed

In a food web, owls eat both mice and small birds, and the small birds also eat insects that feed on grass. If all the owls are removed, what is likely to happen to the grass, and why?

The trap is that the question looks like it is asking about owls and mice, so people answer only the first step and stop.

How to work it: With no owls, both mice and small birds increase, because nothing is eating them. More small birds means more insects eaten, so insect numbers fall — which on its own would help the grass. But more mice means more grass eaten directly. The mice effect is direct and the insect effect is indirect, so the grass most likely decreases overall. The honest answer includes both routes and says which is stronger, and notes that we cannot be sure without knowing how much of the mice's diet is grass.

5

Connected ideas

Food chains begin with photosynthesis, which is how a producer turns sunlight into stored chemical energy in the first place. The energy lost at each step is lost mainly through respiration, which releases it as heat, and the whole picture is one long example of energy being transferred without ever being created or destroyed. The numbers at each level are studied as populations, and the top links of a chain are the predator and prey relationships you can watch most easily.

In one line

A food chain traces energy from the Sun through a producer and up through consumers, with the arrows pointing the way the energy travels, and it stays short because roughly nine tenths of that energy is lost as heat at every single step.
