Concept

Food Chains and Webs

A food chain draws who eats whom, so you can follow energy as it passes from one living thing to the next.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Every living thing needs energy, and almost all of it starts in the same place: the Sun. A plant catches sunlight and builds sugar. A caterpillar eats the leaf, a bird eats the caterpillar, a hawk eats the bird. Write that in order with an arrow between each pair and you have a food chain.

The arrows are the first thing to get right. They point from the eaten to the eater, so you write grass → caterpillar. It is tempting to draw the arrow the way the mouth goes, but it is not showing eating; it shows which way the energy travels. Energy leaves the grass and ends up in the caterpillar, so the arrow points at the caterpillar.

Each step has a name. The plant at the start is the producer, because it makes its own food rather than taking it from something else. Everything after is a consumer: the caterpillar primary, the bird secondary, the hawk tertiary. These label position, not the animal. The same bird is a primary consumer eating seeds and a secondary consumer ten minutes later eating a beetle.

A food web is what you get when you stop pretending each animal eats only one thing. Real habitats are webs; a chain is one thread pulled out to make it easier to look at.

2

How it works

The most useful thing a food chain tells you is why chains are short. They almost never run beyond four or five links, for a reason: at every step, most energy is lost.

Consider what a caterpillar does with the leaf. Some passes through undigested. Much of the rest is used up staying alive — moving, growing, repairing, and running respiration, which releases energy as heat. Only the leftover is built into caterpillar body, and only that reaches the bird that eats it. As a rough guide, about a tenth of the energy at one level ends up stored at the next.

Follow the numbers and the shortness explains itself. If the plants in a field capture 10,000 units of energy, the insects hold about 1,000, the small birds about 100, a hawk about 10. A fifth link would have about 1 unit, not enough to feed an animal large enough to hunt hawks. The chain stops not by rule but because the energy runs out. The same arithmetic explains why large predators are fewer than their prey and need enormous territories: one hawk requires an entire field of grass underneath it.

Two groups get left off most diagrams. Decomposers — fungi and bacteria — feed on dead bodies and waste, returning nutrients to the soil for producers to use again, and scavengers eat animals that died without being hunted. Without decomposers the system would stall, the raw materials locked inside dead matter instead of recycled.

The heart of it

Food chains are short because roughly nine tenths of the energy is lost at every step, mostly as heat, leaving nothing to support a sixth link.

3

Where the idea came from

People have always known that animals eat each other; turning that into a tool you can reason with is recent. The British ecologist Charles Elton set it out in Animal Ecology, published in 1927, which introduced both the food chain and the pyramid of numbers to a wide readership.

The pyramid of numbers was the striking part. Counting individuals at each level almost always gives a shape wide at the bottom and narrow at the top: many plants, fewer plant-eaters, fewer still meat-eaters. Drawing it as a pyramid made visible a pattern that had been in front of everyone for centuries without being named.

It also carried a problem later work had to fix. A single oak can feed thousands of caterpillars, so counting bodies gives one producer supporting a huge number of consumers — an upside-down pyramid. The pattern was real, but counting heads was the wrong measurement. Ecologists later measured the mass of living material, better still the energy, and with those the pyramid returns reliably. Elton was right about the pattern and wrong about how to measure it.

4

What people get wrong

The arrow direction is the commonest error, best fixed by remembering what the arrow means rather than a rule: it follows the energy, and energy always moves into the eater.

The second error is assuming that removing an animal only affects what is directly above or below it. In a web, effects travel both ways. Remove a predator and its prey may increase so much they strip the plants bare and starve. Remove a plant and a predator three links away can decline, though it never touched that plant.

Commonly missed

In a food web, owls eat both mice and small birds, and the small birds also eat insects that feed on grass. If all the owls are removed, what is likely to happen to the grass, and why?

The trap is that the question looks like it is about owls and mice, so people answer the first step and stop.

How to work it: With no owls, both mice and small birds increase. More small birds means more insects eaten, so insect numbers fall, which alone would help the grass. But more mice means more grass eaten directly. That route is direct and the insect one indirect, so the grass most likely decreases overall. The honest answer gives both routes, says which is stronger, and notes we cannot be sure without knowing how much of the mice's diet is grass.

5

Connected ideas

Food chains begin with photosynthesis, how a producer turns sunlight into stored chemical energy. The energy lost at each step goes mainly through respiration, which releases it as heat, and the whole picture is an example of energy transferred without being created or destroyed. The numbers at each level are studied as populations, and the top links are predator and prey relationships.

In one line

A food chain traces energy from the Sun through a producer and up through consumers, the arrows pointing the way the energy travels, and stays short because roughly nine tenths is lost as heat at every step.
