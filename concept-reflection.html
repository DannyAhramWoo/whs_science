Concept

Reflection

Reflection is what happens when light hits a surface and bounces off instead of passing through or being absorbed.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

You already know reflection better than almost any other idea in science, because you have looked in a mirror every day of your life. What you may not have thought about is that you are seeing reflection every single moment you are awake. You can see this page because light from a lamp or a window landed on it and bounced off into your eyes. Almost nothing you look at makes its own light. Nearly everything you see, you see by reflected light.

So why does a mirror look so different from a sheet of paper, if both are reflecting? The difference is not whether the light bounces but how tidily it bounces. A mirror is extremely smooth — smoother than you can feel with a finger. Rays of light arriving side by side leave side by side, keeping their arrangement. That preserved arrangement is what carries the picture, which is why you see an image. Paper, magnified enough, is a tangle of fibres pointing every which way. Rays arriving side by side leave in scattered directions, and the picture is destroyed. You still see the paper, brightly, but you see no image in it.

The tidy kind is called regular reflection and the scattered kind is called diffuse reflection. Both obey exactly the same law. The only difference is the surface.

2

How it works

The law of reflection says that the angle at which light arrives equals the angle at which it leaves. To measure those angles fairly we do not measure them from the surface itself. We draw an imaginary line standing straight up out of the surface at the exact point where the light lands. That line is called the normal, and both angles are measured from it. The angle between the incoming ray and the normal is the angle of incidence; the angle between the outgoing ray and the normal is the angle of reflection. They are always equal.

Why bother with the normal instead of the surface? Because the normal keeps working when the surface is curved. On a curved mirror there is no single flat surface to measure from, but at every individual point there is still a normal sticking out, and the law still holds point by point. This is exactly how a curved shaving mirror magnifies your face and how a torch reflector gathers scattered light into a beam. Nothing new is happening — it is the same rule, applied at a surface whose normals fan out or converge.

Now consider what a plane mirror image actually is. Light from your nose spreads out in all directions; some of it hits the mirror and bounces to your eye. Your brain has no way of knowing the light has been bent. It assumes, as it always does, that light travelled in a straight line. So it traces the rays backwards to where they seem to have come from — a point behind the mirror. That is why the image looks as though it is behind the glass, and why it looks as far behind the mirror as you are in front. If you stand two metres from a mirror, your image is two metres behind it, and you are looking at something four metres away. This is a genuine prediction you can test with a camera that has to focus.

The image is also reversed in a particular way that people describe carelessly. A mirror does not swap left and right. It swaps front and back. Your reflection's nose points towards you instead of away, and everything else follows from that one flip. The reason it feels like a left-right swap is that a human body is roughly symmetrical top to bottom in a way that makes us reinterpret the front-back flip as a sideways one.

Working out where the image is, and how fast it moves

The rule that the image sits as far behind the mirror as the object sits in front is worth using as a calculating tool, not just a fact. Two kinds of question depend on it.

The first is a periscope. A periscope is two plane mirrors, each set at 45° to the tube, one at the top and one at the bottom, facing each other. Light from an object comes in horizontally, strikes the top mirror at 45° to its normal, and is turned through 90° so it travels straight down the tube. At the bottom it strikes the second mirror, also at 45°, and is turned through 90° again so it travels horizontally into the eye. Two turns of 90° in opposite senses means the light ends up going the way it started, which is why a periscope shows you the world the right way up and not reversed. To find where an object must be from a marked image position, trace the path backwards: run the ray out of the eye, up the tube, and out of the top mirror horizontally, and the object lies along that line.

The second is a speed question, and it is asked directly. Suppose you walk towards a plane mirror at 4 m/s. How fast does the gap between you and your image close?

Do it with numbers rather than intuition. Start 10 m from the mirror. Your image is then 10 m behind it, so the two of you are 20 m apart. One second later you have moved 4 m forward and are 6 m from the mirror. Your image is now 6 m behind the mirror, because it always matches your distance — so it has moved 4 m as well, towards the mirror from the other side. The gap between you is now 6 + 6 = 12 m. It was 20 m, and it is now 12 m, so it shrank by 8 m in one second. The image approaches you at 8 m/s, twice your own speed, because you and the image are each closing at 4 m/s from opposite sides. The trap is answering 4 m/s by forgetting that the image is moving too.

The heart of it

Light bounces at the same angle it arrives, measured from the normal — and whether you get a clear image or not depends only on how smooth the surface is.

3

Where the idea came from

Reflection is one of the oldest pieces of physics we have written down. The Greek mathematician Euclid, working in Alexandria around 300 BC, stated the law of reflection in a work on optics. He treated it geometrically, the way he treated triangles: here is a rule about angles, here is a proof. That was a real achievement, but it left a question hanging. Euclid could tell you the angles were equal. He could not tell you why they should be.

Some centuries later — the dating of his life is uncertain, but he is usually placed in the first century AD — Hero of Alexandria offered an answer. He argued that of all the possible paths light could take from one point, off a mirror, to another point, the path where the angles are equal is the shortest one. Light, he suggested, takes the shortest route. That turns a geometric rule into a physical principle: the angles are not equal by coincidence, they are equal because that is what the shortest path looks like.

This is worth noticing because it is a pattern that repeats throughout physics. First someone finds a rule that describes what happens. Much later someone finds a deeper reason why that rule and no other. Hero's idea was later sharpened by others into a more general principle about the path light takes, which also explains refraction. Hero himself did not have the full picture, and he was reasoning partly from the assumption that nature does not waste effort — an assumption which is not obviously true and which he could not prove.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is measuring angles from the surface instead of from the normal. If light comes in at 30° to the mirror, the angle of incidence is 60°, because the normal is perpendicular to the mirror. Questions are often written to catch exactly this, giving you the angle to the surface and expecting you to convert.

The second error is thinking a rough surface breaks the law of reflection. It does not. Every individual ray hitting every individual bump obeys the law perfectly. It is only because the bumps face different directions that the rays end up scattered. A dull wall and a mirror follow the same physics.

Commonly missed

A ray of light strikes a flat mirror, making an angle of 25° with the mirror surface. What is the angle between the incoming ray and the reflected ray?

It is a trap because two conversions are needed and each one can go wrong. Many people answer 25° or 50° by reflex.

How to work it: The normal is at 90° to the mirror, so the angle of incidence is 90° − 25° = 65°. By the law of reflection the angle of reflection is also 65°. The two rays sit on opposite sides of the normal, so the angle between them is 65° + 65° = 130°.

5

Connected ideas

Reflection is one of two things light can do when it meets a new surface; the other is refraction, where light passes into the material and changes direction. Which one dominates depends on the material. Reflection is also how we see colour, because a red jumper is red only because it reflects red light and absorbs the rest. Sound reflects too, which is what an echo is, and it explains why an empty room sounds harsh.
