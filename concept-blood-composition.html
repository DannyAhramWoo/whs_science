Concept

What Blood Is Made Of

Blood has exactly four parts: a straw-coloured liquid called plasma, plus three kinds of solid floating in it — red blood cells, white blood cells and platelets.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Blood looks like a single red liquid. It is not. It is a mixture, and you can separate it. Spin a tube of blood very fast in a machine called a centrifuge and the heavier parts are flung to the bottom, leaving two layers with a thin line between them.

The bottom layer, about 45 out of every 100 parts, is packed red cells. The top layer, about 55 parts in 100, is a clear straw-coloured liquid — that is plasma, and people are often surprised it is yellowish rather than red. The thin whitish line between them, under one part in a hundred, holds the white blood cells and platelets. So blood is a little over half liquid and slightly under half cells. The exact figure varies, running higher in men than women and higher in people living at altitude; doctors call the percentage made of red cells the haematocrit.

Four components. That number is worth fixing in memory, because the commonest error here is not forgetting one — it is accepting a fifth that does not exist.

2

How it works

Plasma. This is the transport liquid, and it is about 90 parts water in every 100. Because so many substances dissolve in water, plasma carries almost everything that needs moving: digested food such as glucose and amino acids, absorbed from the gut; wastes such as urea, on their way to the kidneys; hormones, which are chemical messengers made by one organ to give instructions to another; dissolved salts; most of the carbon dioxide, in the form of hydrogencarbonate ions; and heat. That last one is easy to overlook. Warm blood from the working muscles and the liver spreads heat through the body and carries it to the skin to be lost, which is what makes vasodilation and vasoconstriction work at all. Plasma also carries the clotting factors, proteins that are needed to seal a wound.

Red blood cells. These carry oxygen, and everything about them is shaped by that one job. They are packed with haemoglobin, a red protein containing iron, which grabs oxygen where oxygen is plentiful in the lungs and releases it where oxygen is scarce in a working tissue. They have no nucleus — the nucleus being the control centre that most cells keep — which leaves more room for haemoglobin and gives the cell its dented disc shape, a shape with more surface for its volume and so faster to load and unload. Say mammals when you state this rule, because it is a mammal arrangement rather than a rule for all animals: the red blood cells of birds and reptiles keep their nuclei. Having no nucleus means a red cell cannot repair itself and lives only about 120 days, so your bone marrow must replace them constantly.

White blood cells. These defend the body, and there are two main kinds worth naming. Phagocytes engulf invaders — the cell flows around a bacterium, swallows it whole and digests it, which is roughly how pus forms. Lymphocytes make antibodies, proteins shaped to lock onto one particular invader and mark it for destruction. Unlike red cells, white cells do have nuclei, and there are far fewer of them: roughly one white cell for every 600 red cells. Their number rises sharply during an infection, which is why a blood test can suggest you are fighting something.

Platelets. These are not whole cells but small fragments broken off from much larger cells in the bone marrow. Their job is clotting. When a vessel is cut, platelets gather at the damage, stick together to plug the gap, and release chemicals that set off a chain of reactions among the clotting factors in the plasma. The end of that chain converts a dissolved protein called fibrinogen into threads of solid fibrin, which form a mesh across the wound. Red cells get trapped in the mesh, and the whole thing dries into a scab. A clot stops blood loss and also keeps bacteria out.

Plasma versus serum. This distinction is the one that catches people, so take it slowly. Plasma is the liquid part of blood with the clotting factors still in it. If you let blood clot in a dish, the clotting factors are used up building the fibrin mesh; the pale liquid that is left over afterwards is serum. So serum is plasma minus the clotting factors. Serum is a real substance and a useful one, but it is not a component of blood in the body — it is what remains after clotting has happened. If a list of blood's four parts offers you serum instead of plasma, that list is wrong.

What happens after heavy blood loss. Lose a lot of blood and you have fewer red cells, so less oxygen reaches the tissues. The body responds immediately in two ways at once. The heart beats faster, pushing the remaining blood round more often. Breathing becomes faster and deeper, loading what red cells remain as fully as possible. Pulse rate and breathing rate both rise, and they rise together — neither one instead of the other. Both are attempts to keep oxygen delivery up when the carrier has been reduced.

The heart of it

Blood is plasma, red cells, white cells and platelets — four parts, no more — and serum is not a fifth, it is simply plasma after the clotting factors have been used up.

3

Where the idea came from

For roughly fourteen centuries European medicine followed the Greek physician Galen, who taught that blood is made continuously in the liver from food and then consumed at its destination, seeping between the two sides of the heart through pores in the wall. There is no such flow and there are no such pores, but Galen's account was detailed and confident, and went largely unchallenged.

In 1628 the English physician William Harvey demonstrated that blood circulates — that the same blood goes round a closed loop. His strongest argument was arithmetic rather than dissection. He estimated how much blood the heart pushes out per beat, multiplied by the beats in an hour, and found a quantity far greater than the body's weight. No liver could manufacture that much from food, so the blood must be the same blood returning. Harvey could not see the vessels joining arteries to veins; capillaries were observed by Marcello Malpighi around 1661, after Harvey's death.

The parts of blood came into view with better microscopes. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek described red blood cells in the 1670s. Platelets took far longer, being small and easily mistaken for debris; they were properly recognised in the 1880s, largely through Giulio Bizzozero in Italy.

One discovery changed medicine directly. Early transfusions often killed the patient and nobody knew why. In 1901 the Austrian physician Karl Landsteiner showed that human blood comes in different groups and that mixing incompatible ones makes the red cells clump. Matching donor to recipient turned transfusion from a gamble into routine.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking plasma is red. It is not; it is straw-coloured. All the red is in the haemoglobin inside the red cells, and once you spin those out, what remains looks like weak tea.

The second is accepting invented components. Questions on this topic often list plausible-sounding items — "blood fibres", "red plasma", "haemoglobin cells" — that do not exist. Learn the four names precisely and treat anything else as suspect until proved otherwise.

The third is the plasma-and-serum swap, which is the trap most often used, because serum is a genuine word from real laboratories rather than an obvious invention. Hold on to the one-line difference: plasma still has its clotting factors, serum has spent them.

Commonly missed

Which of these correctly lists what blood is made of: (a) red cells, white cells, platelets and serum; (b) red cells, white cells, platelets and plasma; (c) red cells, white cells, plasma and haemoglobin; (d) red cells, blood fibres, platelets and plasma?

The trap is that every option contains three correct items, so the eye slides over them and the whole thing looks familiar. Option (a) is the dangerous one, because serum sounds authoritative.

How to work it: Do not judge the lists as wholes. Take one item at a time and ask whether it is a genuine component of circulating blood. Option (d) fails on "blood fibres", which is not a thing — fibrin exists, but only after clotting, and it is not a component. Option (c) fails on haemoglobin: haemoglobin is real, but it is a protein inside the red cells, so listing it separately is like listing "car" and "engine" as two vehicles. Option (a) fails on serum: serum is what is left after clotting, so it is a product of blood, not a part of it. Option (b) survives every test and is the answer.

This angle — naming the four components with serum offered as a near-miss, and asking what happens to pulse and breathing rate after heavy blood loss — has appeared in IMSO papers, including 2017 and 2019.

5

Connected ideas

Circulation is the system that moves this mixture, and gas transport explains in detail how the haemoglobin in the red cells does its work. Breathing loads the oxygen in the first place. At high altitude the body responds by making more red cells, which changes the proportions described here. White cells connect to microorganisms, the invaders they exist to fight, and to the raised set point of a fever. The kidneys clean the plasma, and plasma is also what carries heat around the body for temperature control.

In one line

Blood is about 55 per cent plasma and 45 per cent cells, and its four components are plasma for transport, red cells for oxygen, white cells for defence and platelets for clotting — with serum being plasma after the clotting factors have been used up, not a fifth part.
