Concept

What Changes a Wire's Resistance

A wire's resistance rises when it is made longer and falls when it is made thicker, because electrons meet more obstruction along a long path and have more room in a wide one.

Introduced in Chapter 3: Electric Circuits

1

What it means

Think of water pushed down a hosepipe. A short fat hose lets water gush. A long thin hose, fed by the same tap, gives a feeble trickle. Nothing about the tap changed. The pipe itself made the flow harder.

Electric wires behave the same way. Current is the flow of electric charge through a wire, carried by electrons, and it is measured in amperes, usually shortened to amps (A). A current of 2 A means charge is flowing at twice the rate of a current of 1 A. Resistance is how strongly a wire opposes that flow. It is measured in ohms, and the symbol is a Greek letter, omega. A wire with high resistance lets only a small current through for a given push from the battery.

Two things about the shape of a wire change its resistance, and they pull in opposite directions:

A longer wire has more resistance. Double the length and you double the resistance. A thicker wire has less resistance. The measure of thickness that matters is the cross-sectional area — the area of the circular face you would see if you cut the wire straight across. Double that area and the resistance halves.

A third factor is the material itself. Copper resists far less than steel of identical shape, which is why household wiring is copper. And a fourth is temperature: for ordinary metals, a hotter wire resists more.

2

How it works

The reason lies in what electrons actually do inside a metal. A metal is a lattice of atoms packed in a regular pattern, and some electrons are loose and free to drift between them. When a battery is connected, those free electrons drift along the wire. But they do not glide unobstructed. They keep bumping into the atoms of the lattice, losing a little energy at every collision. Those collisions are resistance, and the lost energy appears as heat.

Now the length. Make the wire twice as long and an electron crossing it must run twice as far, so it meets roughly twice as many atoms and suffers roughly twice as many collisions. Twice the obstruction along the journey means twice the resistance. This is why resistance is proportional to length: three times the length gives three times the resistance.

Now the thickness. Here the picture is not about the journey but about how many lanes are available. A thick wire has a wide cross-section, so at every point there is far more room — many more paths side by side for electrons, and many more free electrons in play. A six-lane motorway carries far more traffic than a single-lane road of the same length. Double the cross-sectional area and you double the room, so the resistance halves. This is why resistance is inversely proportional to area: three times the area gives a third of the resistance.

Combine both and comparisons become easy. Take four bars of the same metal and ask which has the greatest resistance. The answer is always the longest and thinnest. The least resistance belongs to the shortest and fattest. An IMSO paper from 2015 gave a table of bar dimensions and asked exactly that.

A worked comparison. Wire A is 2 m long with a cross-sectional area of 1 square millimetre, and it has a resistance of 4 ohms. Wire B is the same metal but 6 m long with an area of 3 square millimetres. Handle the two effects one at a time. Tripling the length from 2 m to 6 m triples the resistance, taking 4 ohms to 12 ohms. Then tripling the area from 1 to 3 square millimetres divides the resistance by three, taking 12 ohms back to 4 ohms. Wire B has 4 ohms — exactly the same as wire A. The two changes cancelled. Take them in either order and you get the same answer.

Temperature works differently. Heating a metal makes its atoms vibrate more strongly about their positions, so they are harder for drifting electrons to slip past and collisions become more frequent. That is why a metal's resistance climbs as it warms. Curiously, some materials do the opposite: the vegetable and fruit samples used in school conductivity experiments conduct better when warmer, and those experiments also show the length rule directly — push the two electrodes further apart in a potato and the current drops, because the current now has a longer path through the vegetable.

The heart of it

Longer means more resistance because electrons meet more obstruction on the way; thicker means less resistance because they have more room to travel side by side.

3

Where the idea came from

Georg Ohm, a German schoolteacher working in Cologne, published his findings in 1827. He had a serious practical problem: steady sources of current barely existed, and the batteries of the day faded as he measured. He solved it with a thermoelectric source, which gave a steadier if small current, and built his own measuring instrument from a magnetic needle. He then patiently tested wires of different lengths and thicknesses.

What he found was the relationship now called Ohm's law — that the current through a metal conductor is proportional to the voltage across it — together with the rules on this page, that resistance grows with length and falls with cross-sectional area. The unit of resistance is named after him.

His work was dismissed at first, and quite harshly. German academic opinion then favoured philosophical reasoning over experiment, and one prominent figure reportedly dismissed the book as a web of naked fancies, though the exact wording comes down through later retellings and should be treated with caution. Ohm resigned his teaching post expecting recognition and did not get it. Acceptance came from abroad: the Royal Society in London awarded him the Copley Medal in 1841, and only then did German institutions come round, giving him a professorship in Munich in 1852, two years before he died.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest slip is inverting the thickness rule — assuming a thicker wire must resist more because there is more metal in the way. Hold on to the hosepipe: a wide pipe is easier for water, not harder. More metal across the path means more room, not more obstruction. Only extra length puts more metal in the way of a given electron's journey.

The second is comparing wires while changing two things at once and not tracking both. Deal with length first, then area, as in the worked example above. They can cancel.

The third is forgetting that these rules only compare wires of the same material. A short thick steel wire may still resist more than a long thin copper one, because copper is a far better conductor to begin with.

This explains a familiar contrast. The filament inside an old-fashioned light bulb is deliberately made extremely long and extremely thin — often a fine wire coiled up tightly to fit a great length into a small space. That gives it high resistance, so a lot of electrical energy is turned into heat in a tiny volume, and it glows white hot. The thick cable feeding the lamp carries exactly the same current, but because it is short and fat its resistance is low, so it converts almost no energy to heat and stays cool. That is also why an overloaded thin extension lead can grow dangerously warm while proper thick house wiring does not.

Commonly missed

Four bars are cut from the same metal. Bar P is 10 cm long with a cross-sectional area of 4 square centimetres. Bar Q is 20 cm long with an area of 4 square centimetres. Bar R is 10 cm long with an area of 1 square centimetre. Bar S is 20 cm long with an area of 1 square centimetre. Which bar has the greatest resistance?

The trap is picking bar Q because it looks biggest, or picking bar P because it is chunkiest. The question rewards handling length and area separately rather than judging by overall size.

How to work it: resistance goes up with length and down with area, so you want the longest and the thinnest at once. Take bar P as one unit of resistance. Bar Q is twice as long with the same area, so 2 units. Bar R is the same length with a quarter of the area, so 4 units. Bar S is twice as long and has a quarter of the area, so 2 x 4 = 8 units. Bar S has the greatest resistance, and bar P the least. Scan the table for the longest, thinnest entry and you have the answer before doing any arithmetic.

5

Connected ideas

This page is the detail behind resistance itself, and it only matters because resistance controls current. Whether a material conducts at all is the conductor and insulator distinction, and how much resistance a circuit ends up with depends on whether components sit in series or parallel. Electrolytes conduct in the same length-dependent way, which is why electrode spacing changes the reading. The energy lost in collisions becomes heat, and how fast that happens is power. Static electricity is the same charge when it is not flowing.

In one line

Resistance rises in proportion to a wire's length and falls in proportion to its cross-sectional area, so the longest thinnest wire always resists most and a thin filament glows while a thick cable stays cool.
