Concept

Heat

Heat is energy moving from a hotter place to a colder one.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Wrap your hands around a mug of tea and they warm up. Something has passed from the mug into your hands. That something is heat, and the definition worth memorising is narrow: heat is energy in transit because of a temperature difference.

The words "in transit" matter. An object does not contain heat, in the same way a cheque does not contain a payment. What an object contains is thermal energy — the total energy of all the jiggling, vibrating particles inside it. Heat is what we call that energy while it is crossing from one object to another. Once it has arrived it is simply thermal energy again, now belonging to the second object.

Heat only ever travels one way on its own: from hotter to colder. Never the reverse. This is not a rule someone imposed but something that follows from how particles behave, and it is one of the deepest rules in physics.

Heat is measured in joules, the same unit used for every other kind of energy — which is itself a clue that heat is not a special substance but ordinary energy wearing a different name.

2

How it works

Everything is made of particles, and those particles are always moving. In a solid they vibrate about fixed positions; in a liquid they slide past one another; in a gas they fly about freely. The hotter a thing is, the faster its particles move on average. That is the whole basis of the subject.

Now put a hot object against a cold one. At the boundary, fast-moving particles collide with slow-moving ones. In each collision the fast particle tends to lose a little energy and the slow one to gain a little — the same way a fast marble slows when it strikes a stationary one and sets it moving. Billions of such collisions transfer energy steadily across the boundary. That flow is heat, and it explains why the direction is one-way: there are simply far more ways for energy to spread out than to gather up.

Heat travels by three routes, and each has its own page. Conduction is the direct collision process just described, needing particles in contact. Convection moves heat by carrying warmed fluid bodily from one place to another. Radiation carries energy as invisible waves and needs no material at all, which is how the Sun's energy crosses empty space to reach us.

An important number. How much an object's temperature rises for a given amount of heat depends on the material, a property called specific heat capacity. Water's is unusually high: it takes about 4,200 joules to warm one kilogram of water by one degree, but only about 900 joules for a kilogram of aluminium. So the same heat raises aluminium's temperature more than four times as much. This is why the sea warms and cools far more slowly than the land beside it, why coastal places have milder weather than inland ones, and why water is used in radiators and cooling systems.

Heat can also flow into a substance without raising its temperature at all. Melting ice sits stubbornly at 0 °C while it absorbs a great deal of energy, because that energy goes into breaking the bonds holding the particles in their solid arrangement rather than into speeding them up. Only once all the ice has melted does the temperature begin to climb again.

The heart of it

Heat is not a substance an object holds but energy on the move, always flowing from hotter to colder.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of the 1700s heat was believed to be a real fluid. The theory, developed most fully by the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier around 1783, held that a weightless, invisible substance called caloric flowed from hot bodies into cold ones. It was a good theory in the proper sense: it explained why heat always flowed one way, why objects expanded when heated as caloric squeezed in between their particles, and why heat seemed to be conserved when hot and cold water were mixed. It made correct predictions for decades.

The evidence against it came from a cannon factory. Count Rumford — born Benjamin Thompson in Massachusetts, later a soldier, spy and administrator in Bavaria — was supervising the boring of cannon barrels in Munich in the 1790s. Boring a barrel with a blunt drill produced heat endlessly, hour after hour, enough to boil water surrounding the apparatus. Under caloric theory the metal held a fixed store of the fluid, so the heat should have run out. It never did. Rumford concluded, in a paper of 1798, that heat must be a form of motion.

Caloric theory survived another forty years anyway, and it is worth being honest about why. Rumford had shown that something was wrong, but he had no number to put in its place — no measurement of how much motion corresponded to how much heat. A theory is rarely abandoned because of a problem; it is abandoned when something better arrives. That came in the 1840s when James Prescott Joule, a brewer's son in Manchester, painstakingly measured how much mechanical work produced how much heating, using a paddle wheel churning water in an insulated barrel. His measurements gave the exchange rate, and caloric finally died. The joule is named after him.

4

What people get wrong

The first and largest error is the one caloric theory made, and it survives in everyday speech: talking as though objects contain heat, or as though cold is a thing that flows in. There is no such substance as cold. When you feel a cold windowpane, heat is leaving your hand; nothing is entering it. A coat does not warm you either — it slows the escape of the heat you are already producing.

The second is confusing heat with temperature. They are separate quantities, and a big cool object can hold far more thermal energy than a small hot one.

Commonly missed

A spark from a sparkler is at about 1,000 °C. A bath of water is at 40 °C. The spark lands on your arm and barely stings. Why is the far cooler bath capable of scalding you and the much hotter spark not?

The trap is treating temperature as if it measured how dangerous something is, which it does not.

How to work it: Separate the two quantities. Temperature tells you how fast the particles move; the heat that can flow into you depends also on how many particles there are. A spark is a speck with almost no mass, so even at 1,000 °C its total thermal energy is tiny, and it is exhausted almost instantly. A bath contains many kilograms of water, so even at a modest temperature it can deliver energy into your skin for as long as you stay in it. The answer is mass: high temperature with negligible mass transfers negligible heat.

5

Connected ideas

Heat and temperature are the pair most often confused, and separating them is the key to the whole chapter. Heat travels by conduction, convection and radiation, and when the flow finally stops the objects have reached thermal equilibrium. Heat is one form of energy among many, and adding it can drive a change of state.

In one line

Heat is energy on the move from hotter to colder, transferred by particle collisions or radiation, and never a substance stored inside an object.
