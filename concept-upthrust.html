Concept

Upthrust

Upthrust is the upward push a fluid exerts on anything placed in it, equal in size to the weight of the fluid that the object pushes out of the way.

Introduced in Chapter 5: Floating, Sinking and Density

1

What it means

Hold a football underwater and you can feel the water shoving it back up. Lift a heavy stone while it is submerged and it feels lighter than it does in air; lift it clear of the surface and it suddenly feels heavy again. In both cases the water is pushing upwards on the object. That upward push is called upthrust, or sometimes buoyancy.

A fluid is anything that flows, so this covers liquids and gases alike. Air gives upthrust too. It is normally too small to notice, but it is exactly why a helium balloon rises — the air pushes up on it harder than gravity pulls it down.

The precise statement is Archimedes' principle: the upthrust on an object equals the weight of the fluid it displaces. "Displaces" means pushes out of the way. If a boat pushes aside 500 kg of water, the water pushes back up on the boat with a force equal to the weight of 500 kg. Notice how strange and useful this is — the upthrust does not depend on what the object is made of or how heavy it is, only on how much fluid it shoves aside.

2

How it works

Where does the push come from? From the fact that pressure in a fluid increases with depth. The deeper you go, the more fluid is stacked above pressing down, so the greater the pressure. Now take a submerged block. Its bottom face is deeper than its top face, so the pressure on the bottom is greater than the pressure on the top. The fluid pushes up on the bottom harder than it pushes down on the top, and the difference is the upthrust. The sideways pushes cancel out because opposite faces are at the same depth.

This immediately explains why upthrust depends on volume rather than mass. A bigger object has a greater difference in depth between its top and bottom faces, and more area for the pressure to act on, so it gets more upthrust. Two objects of identical size and shape get identical upthrust even if one is cork and the other is lead.

Floating and sinking follow from comparing two forces. Weight pulls down; upthrust pushes up. If the fully submerged object displaces fluid weighing more than the object does, upthrust wins and it rises until only part of it is under the surface — at which point it displaces less, and it settles where upthrust exactly balances weight. That is floating: not the absence of gravity, but a balance.

If the fully submerged object displaces fluid weighing less than the object does, weight wins and it sinks. Work through the comparison and you arrive at the density rule: an object less dense than the fluid floats, one denser than the fluid sinks. The density rule is not a separate law — it is Archimedes' principle rewritten.

This is also how a steel ship floats although steel is roughly eight times denser than water. A ship is not a solid lump of steel. It is a shell around a large volume of air, so the average density of the whole ship is less than that of water. It sinks into the water until it has displaced water weighing as much as the entire ship, and then it stops. Load the ship with cargo and it sits lower, because it must displace more water to support the extra weight. Overload it and there is no more hull left to submerge — water comes over the side, and it goes down.

Putting numbers on upthrust

Archimedes' principle is a calculation, not just a description, and the route always runs the same way: volume displaced, then mass of that fluid, then its weight. That last step is the one people drop. Upthrust is a force, so it is measured in newtons, and to turn a mass into a weight you multiply by gravity, taking 10 N/kg.

Take a block of volume 400 cm³ held fully under water. It displaces 400 cm³ of water. Water has a density of 1 g/cm³, so that water has a mass of 400 g, which is 0.4 kg. Its weight is 0.4 × 10 = 4 N. So the upthrust is 4 N. If the block itself weighs 6 N, a spring balance holding it under water will read 6 − 4 = 2 N. That missing 2 N is the "lost weight" you feel when lifting a stone underwater, and it is exactly the upthrust.

Change the liquid and the answer changes, because the mass of the displaced fluid changes. Put the same block in salt water of density 1.03 g/cm³ and it displaces 400 × 1.03 = 412 g, which is 0.412 kg, weighing 4.12 N. The upthrust is slightly larger in salt water, even though the block is identical.

Run that idea backwards and you can find an unknown liquid's density. If displacing 250 cm³ of some liquid gives an upthrust of 2.5 N, then the displaced liquid weighs 2.5 N, so its mass is 2.5 ÷ 10 = 0.25 kg = 250 g, and its density is 250 ÷ 250 = 1 g/cm³. Plot displaced mass against volume for several objects and the gradient of that straight line is the liquid's density.

This also settles the cargo question. A ship floats when it displaces water weighing as much as the ship. Salt water is denser, so a smaller volume of it supplies the same weight — the ship sits higher, and it can take on more cargo in the sea than in a river before reaching the same depth.

The heart of it

Upthrust arises because pressure is greater lower down, and it equals the weight of the fluid pushed out of the way — so it depends on the object's volume, not on what it is made of.

3

Where the idea came from

Archimedes of Syracuse set out this principle in his treatise On Floating Bodies, written around 250 BC. He did not describe it as an experimental discovery. He treated it as mathematics: he set out assumptions about how fluids behave and then proved his results from them, in the style of a geometer proving theorems.

This makes it one of the very oldest pieces of physics still used today, unchanged. Naval architects designing ships now use the same principle in the same form. Very little science from the ancient world survives contact with modern measurement. Almost all of Aristotle's physics, for example, turned out to be wrong. Archimedes' principle simply held.

The reason it survived is worth understanding: it does not rest on any theory about what matter is made of, or why gravity exists, or how pressure works at the level of particles. It only requires that a fluid presses on things and that pressure grows with depth. Later scientists explained why those things are true, but the explanations sat underneath Archimedes' result rather than replacing it. That is unusual, and it is a good example of how a result can outlive every theory that was current when it was found.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is believing there is no upthrust on an object that sinks. There is. A stone on the riverbed is being pushed up by the water the whole time — it is simply that its weight is greater, so it sank anyway. This is why the stone feels lighter underwater, and if there were no upthrust it would not.

A second error is thinking heavy things sink and light things float. Weight alone decides nothing. An oil tanker is enormously heavy and floats; a small steel nail sinks. What matters is the comparison between the object's weight and the weight of the fluid it can displace.

Commonly missed

A block of wood floats in water. A second, identical block is pushed right under the surface and held there. Which block experiences the greater upthrust?

The trap is thinking the floating block must get more upthrust, since it is the one that stays up.

How to work it: Upthrust equals the weight of the fluid displaced. The floating block has only part of its volume under water, so it displaces only that part. The held-under block has its whole volume under water, so it displaces more — and therefore experiences greater upthrust. That is precisely why it will not stay there: the upthrust now exceeds its weight, so it accelerates upwards the moment you let go, and it only stops rising when enough of it is out of the water for the two forces to balance again.

5

Connected ideas

Upthrust and density are two ways of saying the same thing: comparing densities is a shortcut for comparing an object's weight with the weight of fluid it displaces. Because upthrust is one force among several, floating is really a case of balanced forces, with upthrust cancelling weight. And weight itself comes from gravity, so all three ideas meet whenever anything floats.

In one line

A fluid pushes up on anything placed in it with a force equal to the weight of the fluid pushed aside, and an object floats when that upward push can grow large enough to balance its weight.
