Concept

Terminal Velocity

Terminal velocity is the steady speed a falling object settles at once air resistance grows large enough to balance its weight.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

Drop a stone off a bridge and it goes faster and faster until it lands. Drop a feather and it drifts down at a slow, unchanging speed. The feather reached that steady speed in a fraction of a second; the stone never had time to. That steady speed is the terminal velocity.

The word terminal means final, not deadly. The object keeps falling, but the speedometer stops climbing.

Two forces are at work. Weight is the pull of gravity, measured in newtons. Each kilogram is pulled with about 10 N, so a 5 kg object weighs about 50 N. Air resistance is the push-back of the air the object shoves aside. It always opposes the motion, so for a falling object it acts upwards.

2

How it works

The key fact is that air resistance is not fixed: it grows as the object goes faster. Put your hand out of a moving car window and you feel it. The faster you move, the more air molecules you strike each second, so the force builds.

Follow a falling object. At release its speed is zero, so air resistance is zero and the only force is weight. Badly unbalanced, it accelerates hard. As speed builds, air resistance builds with it, and since weight has not changed the difference shrinks. A smaller unbalanced force means a smaller acceleration, so it still speeds up, but less eagerly.

Eventually air resistance exactly equals weight and the two cancel. The forces are balanced, so there is no acceleration and the object falls at a constant speed. It has reached terminal velocity. Notice it does not slow down and it does not stop — balanced forces mean no change in motion, so whatever it was doing continues.

On a speed-time graph this is a curve that starts steep, bends over as the acceleration shrinks, and flattens into a horizontal line at the terminal velocity.

A parachute changes the picture halfway through. A sky diver falling flat might reach around 50 m/s. The open parachute gives an enormous area to push air aside, so air resistance leaps above weight. The forces are now unbalanced upwards and the diver slows sharply. As speed drops, air resistance drops too, until it again equals weight, but at perhaps 5 m/s. That gentler terminal velocity is why the diver lands safely.

Now the surprising part. Take two balls of identical size and shape, one heavy and one light. The heavy one has a larger terminal velocity: the balance point is where air resistance equals weight, and it has more weight to match, so it must travel faster before the air pushes back hard enough. At terminal velocity the heavier ball therefore meets the greater air resistance.

The heart of it

An object stops accelerating not because gravity switches off but because air resistance has grown until it exactly balances weight.

3

Where the idea came from

For a very long time the accepted answer was Aristotle's, from ancient Greece: heavier things fall faster, in proportion to their weight. It matches what you see, since a stone beats a leaf down. What Aristotle missed was that the air, not the weight, made the difference.

Galileo Galilei in Italy, in the early 1600s, argued that without air all objects would fall at the same rate, and that observed differences come from resistance. He reasoned by thought experiment and by rolling balls down slopes. The story of him dropping two balls from the Leaning Tower of Pisa is probably embroidered, surviving mainly through a pupil's account written years later.

The reasoning became law once Isaac Newton published his laws of motion in 1687. His first law says an object with balanced forces keeps a constant speed in a straight line, which is what a raindrop at terminal velocity does. His second says acceleration comes from the unbalanced force, so a shrinking difference produces a shrinking acceleration.

The claim got its clean test in 1971, when the Apollo 15 astronaut David Scott dropped a hammer and a feather together on the Moon, where there is no air. They landed together, filmed for anyone to watch.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is thinking a falling object at terminal velocity has "run out of gravity". Gravity and weight are unchanged. The object is still pulled down as hard as at the start, but is now pushed up equally hard.

The second is assuming a heavier object meets less air resistance because it "cuts through" the air. Air resistance depends on speed, size and shape, not on weight. Of two objects of the same shape, the heavier meets more air resistance, because it falls faster to get there. This reversal is asked in past competition papers, including one IMSO paper comparing two balls of the same size but different mass.

Commonly missed

Two balls are the same size and shape. Ball A has a mass of 2 kg and Ball B has a mass of 6 kg. Both are dropped and both reach terminal velocity. Which experiences the greater air resistance at that moment?

The trap is to answer "the lighter one", on the feeling that heavy things power through the air. That instinct comes from watching feathers, which are light and a different shape.

How to work it: start from the definition. At terminal velocity, air resistance equals weight. Ball A weighs about 20 N, so its air resistance is about 20 N. Ball B weighs about 60 N, so its air resistance is about 60 N. Ball B experiences three times the air resistance, and is falling considerably faster to generate it.

5

Connected ideas

Terminal velocity is balanced forces in a special case, so it rests on knowing that balanced does not mean stationary. Gravity supplies the weight, while air resistance is friction through a fluid. The flattening curve on a speed-time graph is the moment of balance.

In one line
