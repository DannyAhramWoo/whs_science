Concept

The RNA World Hypothesis

The RNA world hypothesis proposes that before DNA and proteins existed, life ran on RNA alone — a molecule that can both store information and act as a catalyst.

Introduced in The Origin of Life

1

What it means

Modern life runs on a division of labour between two kinds of molecule. DNA holds the instructions — a long chain whose sequence of four chemical letters records how to build everything the organism needs. Proteins do the work, above all acting as enzymes, molecules that speed up chemical reactions enormously without being used up.

Put those two facts together and a problem appears. DNA cannot copy itself; copying it requires protein enzymes. But proteins cannot be built without instructions read from DNA. Each needs the other already in place. This is a genuine chicken-and-egg problem, and one of the hardest obstacles in explaining how life began: whichever you propose came first, you seem to need the other one first.

The RNA world hypothesis offers a way out. RNA is a close chemical relative of DNA — the same kind of letters, but single-stranded rather than a double helix, and built on a slightly different sugar. In today's cells RNA is mostly a messenger, carrying a copy of a DNA instruction out to where proteins are assembled. But some RNA molecules fold into shapes that let them act as catalysts, speeding up reactions the way enzymes do. An RNA molecule can therefore be both the instruction and the machine, and if early life used RNA for both jobs the chicken-and-egg problem dissolves — there was only ever one bird.

2

How it works

The reason a single molecule can do both jobs comes down to shape. DNA's double helix is beautifully suited to storage: two strands pairing along their whole length, protected, stable, easy to copy accurately. That regularity is also its limitation, because a uniform helix presents the same surface everywhere, and a catalyst needs a distinctive pocket that fits one particular molecule. RNA, being single-stranded, folds back on itself into loops, hairpins and pockets of many different shapes, which can grip other molecules and hold them in position while they react — exactly what an enzyme does. A catalytic RNA is called a ribozyme.

Here is the picture the hypothesis paints. In some setting on the early Earth, short RNA chains formed. Most did nothing, but among the vast number of possible sequences, some folded into a shape that could join RNA letters together — including onto another RNA chain. Such a molecule could copy RNA, and in principle itself. That is the crucial threshold, because copying is never perfect: a copy with an error is a variant, and a variant that copies faster or more accurately leaves more descendants. That is natural selection, operating on bare molecules with no cell involved.

Proteins and DNA would then have arrived later as improvements. Proteins are better catalysts, being built from twenty different amino acids rather than four RNA letters, so they offer far more chemical variety. DNA is a better store, because its double strand and slightly different sugar make it more stable, so information lasts longer and copies more accurately. RNA, on this account, was gradually demoted to being the intermediary between the two specialists.

The strongest evidence for the hypothesis is that the demotion looks unfinished. Most tellingly, the ribosome — the machine in every cell that assembles proteins from amino acids — was found, when its structure was finally solved, to perform the actual bond-forming step with RNA, not with protein. The single most fundamental machine in biology, present in every organism, has an RNA catalyst at its heart. This is hard to explain unless RNA came first.

Now the honest weaknesses, and they are serious. Making RNA from scratch under plausible early-Earth conditions remains very difficult in the laboratory. The components are hard to produce and to join, and RNA is fragile — it breaks down in water considerably faster than DNA does, which is awkward for a molecule supposed to have originated in an ocean. No ribozyme found or engineered so far can fully copy itself, though chemists have made ones that come part of the way. Because of this, some scientists think a simpler, sturdier molecule came first, and others argue that RNA and simple peptides emerged together.

The heart of it

RNA can both carry information and catalyse reactions, so a world of RNA alone escapes the chicken-and-egg problem of DNA needing proteins and proteins needing DNA.

3

Where the idea came from

The idea was suggested before there was any evidence for it. Around 1967 and 1968, three scientists working separately — Carl Woese, Francis Crick, who had co-discovered the structure of DNA, and Leslie Orgel — each proposed that RNA might have preceded both DNA and proteins. Their reasoning was mainly logical: RNA sits between the two in the modern cell. At the time nobody had ever seen an RNA molecule catalyse anything; every known enzyme was a protein, and the textbook position was that only proteins could be enzymes. The proposal remained speculation for over a decade.

Then, in the early 1980s, it was confirmed from an unexpected direction. Thomas Cech, working in Colorado on a pond organism, was studying how a stretch of RNA is cut out of a longer strand. He was trying to identify the protein enzyme responsible and could not find one, because there was none: the RNA was cutting itself. At about the same time Sidney Altman, at Yale, showed that in an enzyme made of both RNA and protein, the RNA part alone carried out the reaction. Both results met disbelief, and both men had to demonstrate carefully that no trace of protein was hiding in their samples. They shared the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1989.

Once ribozymes were real, the old speculation became a serious hypothesis. Walter Gilbert, a molecular biologist at Harvard, coined the name in an article in 1986, and "the RNA world" has been its name ever since.

4

What people get wrong

The main error is treating this as established fact. It is a hypothesis — a proposed explanation, consistent with a good deal of evidence, that has not been demonstrated. Nobody has watched an RNA world assemble itself, and nobody has yet made RNA that copies itself completely. It is the leading idea, which is a very different claim from being the correct one.

A second error is imagining RNA molecules floating loose in the open sea. Almost every version of the hypothesis needs some kind of compartment, because otherwise a successful copier's products drift away and there is nothing for selection to act on.

Commonly missed

Explain why the discovery that the ribosome's core is made of RNA rather than protein is taken as support for the RNA world hypothesis.

The trap is answering "because it shows RNA is important". Importance is not the point; what matters is the specific kind of evidence this is.

How to work it: Start from what the ribosome does — it builds every protein in every living thing, so it is about as fundamental and ancient as biological machinery gets. Now ask what a cell would use if it were designing this machine today, with the whole protein toolkit available. Proteins are more versatile catalysts, so a protein core would be the sensible choice. Instead the bond-forming step is done by RNA. The natural explanation is that the ribosome is older than the ability to make good protein enzymes, and was built when RNA was the only catalyst available; because it is so central, changing it later would have been catastrophic, so it was kept. It is a leftover, like an old foundation under a rebuilt house, and leftovers are evidence about the past. Note the limit: this is evidence consistent with an RNA world, not proof of one.

5

Connected ideas

The hypothesis is one part of the wider origin-of-life problem: the Urey-Miller experiment addressed the building blocks, the RNA world addresses copying, and cell membranes supply the compartment that most versions require. It is also a good example of what the word hypothesis properly means, and of how far an inference can be pushed from indirect evidence.
