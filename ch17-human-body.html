Chapter 17

The Human Body

Four systems, one job: keep every cell in your body supplied with oxygen and nutrients. Most marks are lost by mixing up which organ does which step — especially which part of the gut absorbs water.

17.1

The digestive system: one long tube

Your body cannot use a piece of bread as it is. Bread is made of molecules far too big to pass into your blood. Digestion is the breaking of large food molecules into small ones that can be absorbed.

In the mouth, teeth grind food into smaller pieces. That does not change the food chemically — it just gives the chemicals more surface to work on. Saliva contains an enzyme, a chemical that speeds up digestion, and this one begins breaking down starch. That is why a plain cracker turns slightly sweet if you hold it in your mouth.

The food is swallowed and squeezed down the oesophagus into the stomach, which makes acid. The acid kills most germs swallowed with the food, and it creates the sour conditions that a protease — the enzyme that digests protein — needs in order to work.

Next comes the small intestine, where most digestion is finished and nutrients are absorbed into the blood. The pancreas sits nearby; it carries no food, but it makes digestive enzymes and releases them into the small intestine. What is left enters the large intestine, whose main job is to absorb water. The dried remains are stored in the rectum until they leave the body.

The one to memorise

Small intestine → nutrients. Large intestine → water. Both are absorbing, but they absorb different things. Questions ask about water far more often than you would expect.

Commonly missed

A person's large intestine is not working properly. What is the most likely effect?

The trap is answering "they will be short of nutrients". They will not — nutrients were already absorbed earlier, in the small intestine, before the food ever reached here.

How to work it: ask what this organ takes out. It takes out water. If it fails, water stays in the waste, so the waste leaves the body watery. The person loses too much water and may become dehydrated.

17.2

The circulatory system: two loops, not one

This system has three parts: the heart (a pump), the blood vessels (the pipes) and the blood itself (the delivery van).

Blood does not go round in one big circle. It goes round twice, with the heart in the middle of both loops. This is double circulation. In the pulmonary loop, blood goes heart → lungs → heart, to collect oxygen. In the systemic loop, it goes heart → body → heart, to deliver that oxygen. Every drop passes through the heart twice on each full journey.

The pipes are easy once you know the rule. Arteries carry blood away from the heart — both words start with A. Veins carry it back in.

Two kinds of blood cell matter here. Red blood cells carry oxygen. They contain haemoglobin, which grabs onto oxygen and also gives blood its red colour. White blood cells fight infection, and their number rises when you have a wound or an illness, because more are needed to attack the germs. A high white cell count in a question is a clue that the person is infected.

Artery = Away

The direction of travel, not the oxygen, is what makes a vessel an artery or a vein. Arteries carry blood away from the heart. Veins bring it back. Nothing in the definition mentions oxygen at all.

Commonly missed

Which blood vessel carries deoxygenated blood away from the heart?

Most students learn the rough rule "arteries carry oxygenated blood" and then cannot find an answer, because "artery" and "deoxygenated" sound contradictory. They guess a vein.

How to work it: a vein can never be the answer, because the question says away from the heart, and that makes it an artery by definition. Which artery leaves the heart carrying blood that has already given up its oxygen to the body? The one heading to the lungs to collect more. The answer is the pulmonary artery — the exception that proves the direction rule matters more than the colour rule.

17.3

The respiratory system: getting air to the blood

Air enters through the nose, which does three jobs at once. Hairs and sticky mucus filter out dust and germs, blood vessels in the lining warm the air, and moisture is added so it is not drying when it reaches your lungs. Breathing through your mouth skips all three, which is why it can leave your throat sore.

Air then passes down the trachea (the windpipe), which splits into two bronchi, one per lung. These branch again and again like an upside-down tree, ending in millions of tiny air sacs called alveoli.

The alveoli are where the system pays off. Their walls are extremely thin and each is wrapped in blood vessels. Oxygen moves from the air inside into the blood, and carbon dioxide moves out of the blood to be breathed away. This happens by diffusion — substances spreading from where there is a lot of them to where there is less. Millions of tiny sacs give a far larger surface area than two plain bags would, so much more oxygen can cross at once.

Now the part that catches people out. Compare the air you breathe in with the air you breathe out. Oxygen falls and carbon dioxide rises sharply — but nitrogen stays at about 78%. Your body has no use for nitrogen, so it takes none out. It goes in and comes straight back out, unchanged.

Three lung diseases come up often. Asthma narrows the airways, so breathing becomes wheezy and difficult. Bronchitis is inflammation of the bronchi, which fill with mucus and cause a lasting cough. Pneumonia is an infection in which the alveoli fill with fluid, so less oxygen can cross into the blood.

Commonly missed

Which gas is present in roughly the same amount in inhaled and exhaled air?

People scan the options for oxygen or carbon dioxide, because those are the two gases the chapter is about. Both of those change a lot, so both are wrong.

How to work it: ask which gas your body has no use for. Nitrogen is the most common gas in air at about 78%, and your body cannot use it, so it neither absorbs it nor makes it. The answer is nitrogen. Water vapour is the opposite trap — exhaled air has clearly more of it, which is why a mirror mists up.

17.4

Skeleton and movement

Your skeleton does three jobs. It supports your body, giving it a shape to hang on. It protects soft organs — the skull is a hard box around the brain, and the ribs form a cage around the heart and lungs. And it allows movement, because bones give muscles something firm to pull on. Bones meet at joints: your elbow and knee bend one way only, while your shoulder and hip swivel in almost any direction.

Here is the fact that explains why we have so many muscles: a muscle can only pull, never push. A muscle works by getting shorter, and something that gets shorter can tug a bone towards it but never shove it away. So muscles work in pairs. To bend your elbow, the muscle at the front of the upper arm shortens and pulls the forearm up while the one at the back relaxes. To straighten it, the back muscle shortens and pulls while the front one relaxes.

Not all movement is your decision. Voluntary movements are ones you choose: walking, writing, chewing. Involuntary movements happen without you thinking — your heartbeat, your breathing at rest, and the squeezing that pushes food along your gut. You would not survive a night's sleep if your heart needed your permission to beat.

One more thing worth knowing: children's bones heal faster than adults' bones. A child's bones are still growing, so the bone tissue is more active and already busy making new bone. That building machinery is running, so it can repair a break faster than an adult's settled, finished bone.

Commonly missed

Which of these is an involuntary action?

The trap is breathing. Breathing appears on both lists, and that feels wrong, so students rule it out. But you can hold your breath on purpose (voluntary) and you keep breathing while asleep without deciding to (involuntary).

How to work it: ask "could I stop this for a whole hour by deciding to?" You can stop walking, writing or chewing — those are voluntary. You cannot stop your heartbeat or your digestion, so those are involuntary. If breathing is offered with no other involuntary option, it is the answer.

17.5

How the four systems work together

Learning the systems separately is how you start. The harder questions need you to see them as one supply chain, because no single system can keep a cell alive on its own.

Every cell needs two things delivered — oxygen and nutrients — and one thing taken away, carbon dioxide.

The respiratory system brings oxygen as far as the alveoli, and no further. The digestive system hands nutrients over at the wall of the small intestine, and no further. Neither can reach a muscle in your leg. Only the circulatory system goes everywhere, so it is the delivery service connecting the other two to every cell. The skeleton and muscles are what the supply chain exists to power — and the ribs protect the heart and lungs, so two systems are housed inside the fourth.

The joining sentence

The lungs collect oxygen, the small intestine collects nutrients, and the blood carries both to every cell while bringing carbon dioxide back to the lungs to be breathed out. Say that sentence and you have linked all four systems.

17.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

17.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

Both the small intestine and the large intestine absorb things. Explain the difference, and say what would happen if the large intestine stopped working.

Show answer

The small intestine absorbs nutrients from digested food into the blood. This is where most digestion is completed, helped by enzymes sent in by the pancreas.

The large intestine comes later and absorbs water from what is left over. By that point the useful nutrients have already been taken.

If the large intestine stopped working, water would not be removed from the waste, so it would leave the body watery. The person would lose too much water and could become dehydrated, even though they would still be getting their nutrients normally.

2

A doctor finds that a patient has a much higher number of white blood cells than usual. What does this suggest, and why?

Show answer

It suggests the patient has an infection, or a wound that germs have entered.

White blood cells are the cells that fight germs. When germs get into the body, it makes more of them so there are enough to attack the invaders. A rise in their number is a sign the body is defending itself.

Note that red blood cells would not change for this reason. Those carry oxygen, which is a different job entirely.

3

Why must muscles work in pairs? Use the elbow as your example.

Show answer

A muscle moves a bone by getting shorter and pulling on it. It cannot get longer and push, so a single muscle can only ever move a bone in one direction.

To bend the elbow, the muscle at the front of the upper arm shortens and pulls the forearm upwards, while the muscle at the back relaxes. To straighten the arm again, the muscle at the back shortens and pulls, while the front one relaxes.

Without the second muscle the arm could bend but never straighten, because nothing would be able to pull it back down.

4

A boy and his father both break an arm on the same day. The boy's arm heals sooner. Explain why.

Show answer

The boy's bones are still growing. His bone tissue is more active, meaning it is already producing new bone all the time as he gets taller.

Because that bone-building process is already running at full speed, it can be turned to repairing the break quickly. His father's bones have finished growing, so his bone tissue is much less active and takes longer to lay down the new bone needed to mend the break.

17.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 17.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about blood vessels and blood are correct?

  • 1All arteries carry blood that is rich in oxygen.
  • 2Arteries carry blood away from the heart.
  • 3Red blood cells contain haemoglobin, which carries oxygen.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. The word "all" is the giveaway. The pulmonary artery carries blood away from the heart to the lungs, and that blood has already given up its oxygen to the body. So it is an artery carrying deoxygenated blood. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is the actual definition of an artery, so it is correct. Statement 3 is correct — haemoglobin is the substance in red blood cells that grabs oxygen and gives blood its colour. Both consistent with C.

2

Which statements about the digestive system are correct?

  • 1Saliva contains an enzyme that starts breaking down starch.
  • 2Acid in the stomach kills many of the germs in food.
  • 3Water is absorbed mainly in the small intestine.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Saliva contains an enzyme that begins digesting starch while the food is still in the mouth. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. The small intestine absorbs nutrients. Water is absorbed mainly in the large intestine. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Stomach acid kills most germs swallowed with food, and it also gives protease the sour conditions it needs. So the answer is B.

3

Which statements about breathing and the lungs are correct?

  • 1Exhaled air contains about the same amount of nitrogen as inhaled air.
  • 2Gases move between the alveoli and the blood by diffusion.
  • 3Hairs in the nose help to trap dust before air reaches the lungs.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Nitrogen stays at about 78% in both, because the body has no use for it and neither absorbs nor produces it. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Nose hairs and mucus filter dust and germs out of the air, and the nose also warms and moistens it. That leaves A as the only option still containing statement 3.

Statement 2 — correct. Oxygen and carbon dioxide cross the thin alveoli walls by diffusion, spreading from where there is more to where there is less. So the answer is A.
