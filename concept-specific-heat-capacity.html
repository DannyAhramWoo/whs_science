Concept

Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 degree C.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Put a metal spoon in a bowl of hot soup and come back a minute later. The handle is too hot to hold comfortably, while the soup is still pleasantly warm. Both were heated by the same stove and both now sit at similar temperatures — yet the metal got hot in seconds and the soup took minutes on a burner to get there.

Or think of a beach on a summer afternoon. The sand burns your feet. The sea, under exactly the same sunshine all day, is cool enough to swim in. Substances differ in how much energy they demand for each degree of warming. Some are cheap to heat and some are expensive. Specific heat capacity is the number that says which.

Precisely: it is the energy, in joules (J), needed to raise the temperature of 1 kilogram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius. Its units are therefore joules per kilogram per degree C, written J per kg per degree C. Water's value is about 4200 J per kg per degree C, which is unusually high. Iron is about 450, copper about 390, and lead about 130. So warming a kilogram of water by one degree costs roughly ten times as much energy as warming a kilogram of iron by the same one degree.

A high value means stubborn: slow to warm up, and equally slow to cool down. A low value means quick in both directions.

2

How it works

The relationship is worth writing out in full words:

energy supplied = mass x specific heat capacity x temperature change

Take each part in turn. Energy supplied is in joules. Mass is in kilograms, and it appears because twice as much substance needs twice as much energy for the same rise. Specific heat capacity is the per-kilogram, per-degree cost of that particular substance. Temperature change is the final temperature minus the starting temperature, in degrees C — the change, not the final reading.

Here is a full worked example. How much energy is needed to heat 2 kg of water from 20 degrees C to 70 degrees C?

Step one, find the temperature change: 70 minus 20 = 50 degrees C. Step two, list the other quantities: mass = 2 kg, specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J per kg per degree C. Step three, multiply them together: 2 x 4200 x 50. Take it in order — 2 x 4200 = 8400, and 8400 x 50 = 420 000 J. That is 420 kilojoules, and a 2000 watt kettle supplying 2000 joules every second would need 420 000 divided by 2000, which is 210 seconds, or three and a half minutes. That is a realistic kettle time, which is a good sign the arithmetic is sound.

Now turn the relationship around, because this is the form that gets examined. If you fix the mass and fix the energy supplied and ask how much the temperature rises, then a large specific heat capacity gives a small rise. The relationship is inverted. Give 8400 J to 1 kg of water and it rises by 8400 divided by 4200 = 2 degrees C. Give the same 8400 J to 1 kg of lead and it rises by 8400 divided by 130 = about 65 degrees C. Same energy, same mass, a rise more than thirty times greater — because lead is cheap to heat.

Past IMSO papers set this as a table: four substances, equal masses, the same energy supplied to each, and the question asks which heats up most. There is no calculation to do. Read down the specific heat capacity column and pick the smallest number. If instead the question asks which heats up least, pick the largest. Deciding which way round the question runs, before touching the numbers, is the whole skill.

Water's high value shapes the world. Coastal places have mild climates because the sea absorbs enormous energy in summer while barely changing temperature, then releases it slowly through winter, so a seaside town has cooler summers and milder winters than an inland town at the same latitude. Car radiators use water because it carries away a great deal of energy for a modest rise in its own temperature. And a paper cup of water can be held over a flame without the paper burning: the water absorbs the energy and keeps the paper below its burning temperature for as long as water remains.

The heart of it

Energy = mass x specific heat capacity x temperature change, so for equal masses given equal energy, the substance with the lowest specific heat capacity gets hottest.

3

Where the idea came from

Before the 1760s, heat and temperature were widely treated as the same thing. It seemed obvious enough: hotter meant more heat. Clearing that up was one of the genuinely important steps in the science.

Joseph Black, a professor at Glasgow and later Edinburgh, made the distinction around the 1760s. He noticed that equal masses of different substances, left by the same fire for the same time, reached quite different temperatures. If heat and temperature were the same quantity, that could not happen. Black concluded that temperature measures how hot a thing is, while heat is a quantity that flows into it, and that different substances need different amounts of it to reach the same temperature. He called this their capacity for heat, and the name survives in our term today. He published little himself, and much of this reached the world through his lectures and students, so the dates are uncertain and usually given as "around 1760".

Black was working within the caloric theory, which held heat to be an invisible weightless fluid. That framework was wrong, and it was overturned by Benjamin Thompson's observation around 1798 that boring cannon barrels produced apparently endless heat from friction, and then by James Prescott Joule's experiments in the 1840s showing a fixed exchange rate between mechanical work and heat. Joule's name became the unit. Black's measurements remained perfectly valid — correct results surviving a wrong explanation.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is getting the direction backwards: assuming that a high specific heat capacity means a substance heats up quickly. It means the opposite. High capacity means it demands a lot of energy per degree, so for a given energy input it warms slowly.

The second is confusing heat with temperature. A bathful of warm water contains far more energy than a spark from a firework, though the spark is at a much higher temperature. Temperature says how hot; energy says how much.

The third is using the final temperature instead of the change. If something goes from 20 degrees C to 70 degrees C, the figure that belongs in the multiplication is 50, not 70.

Commonly missed

Four blocks of equal mass are given the same amount of energy by identical heaters. Their specific heat capacities are: aluminium 900, iron 450, copper 390 and lead 130 J per kg per degree C. Which block ends up hottest, and which shows the smallest rise?

The trap is that the numbers invite calculation, and many pupils reach for the largest value out of habit because larger usually means more. Here larger means more resistant.

How to work it: Rearrange the relationship in your head. Since energy = mass x specific heat capacity x temperature change, the temperature change equals the energy divided by both the mass and the specific heat capacity. Mass and energy are the same for all four, so temperature change depends only on one over the specific heat capacity — dividing by a bigger number gives a smaller answer. So the hottest block is the one with the lowest value: lead, at 130. The smallest rise belongs to the highest value: aluminium, at 900. As a check, lead's rise should be about 900 divided by 130, near seven times aluminium's. No calculator is needed at all — only the certainty that the relationship runs the opposite way to intuition.

5

Connected ideas

Specific heat capacity only makes sense once heat and temperature are kept apart, and it governs how quickly two objects in contact reach thermal equilibrium. It sits alongside thermal expansion as a way substances respond differently to the same heating, and it is one of the reasons conduction, convection and radiation move energy at the rates they do.
