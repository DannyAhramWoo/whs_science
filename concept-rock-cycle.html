Concept

The Rock Cycle

The rock cycle is the endless recycling of the Earth's rock between igneous, sedimentary and metamorphic forms, driven by heat from inside the planet and by weather at the surface.

Introduced in Chapter 12: The Planet Earth

1

What it means

Rock looks like the most permanent thing there is. A cliff seems fixed for ever. But over long enough time, rock is being destroyed and remade continuously, and any particular rock is only passing through its current form. That is what the rock cycle describes.

Rocks are sorted into three families by how they formed. Igneous rock forms when molten rock cools and hardens — the name comes from the Latin for fire. Granite and basalt are igneous. Sedimentary rock forms when small pieces of older rock, or the shells and remains of living things, settle in layers and are pressed and cemented together. Sandstone, limestone and shale are sedimentary. Metamorphic rock forms when an existing rock of any kind is changed by heat and pressure without ever fully melting — metamorphic means "changed in form". Marble and slate are metamorphic.

The word cycle matters. There is no starting point and no finishing point, and no arrow that must be followed. Any of the three kinds can become any other, given the right conditions.

2

How it works

Take one route round. Molten rock rises from the mantle and cools, forming igneous rock. If it cools slowly deep underground, the mineral crystals have time to grow large, which is why granite is visibly speckled. If it erupts as lava and cools in hours, the crystals are tiny, which is why basalt looks smooth and dark. So the texture of an igneous rock tells you how fast it cooled — a piece of evidence you can read directly.

Once that rock is exposed at the surface, weathering breaks it into fragments and erosion carries them away, usually by river to the sea. There the fragments settle as sediment, layer upon layer. As more piles on top, the weight squeezes the water out and dissolved minerals crystallise between the grains, gluing them together — compaction and cementation. The result is sedimentary rock, which is why it is nearly always layered, and why fossils are found in it: a shell or bone can settle with the sediment and be buried gently, whereas nothing survives being melted into igneous rock.

Bury that sedimentary rock deeper, or push it against a moving plate, and heat and pressure rearrange its minerals into denser, often banded forms. Limestone becomes marble; shale becomes slate; sandstone becomes quartzite. The rock does not melt — if it did, it would become magma and start the cycle again as igneous rock. Metamorphism is a change made in the solid state, which is why the original layering is often still faintly visible.

Push it deeper still and it melts, and the cycle closes.

But routes can be skipped. Igneous rock can be metamorphosed directly, without ever becoming sediment. Metamorphic rock can be weathered straight back into sediment. Sedimentary rock can melt without ever passing through the metamorphic stage. The engines behind all of this are just two: heat from inside the Earth, which melts, buries and moves rock, and energy from the Sun, which drives the weather that breaks rock down.

The timescales are difficult to hold in mind. Sediment might accumulate at a fraction of a millimetre a year; a complete circuit can take hundreds of millions of years. Some rock in Canada and Greenland is around 4 billion years old, while new basalt is forming at mid-ocean ridges today.

The heart of it

No rock is permanent — each of the three kinds can become either of the others, given enough heat, pressure or time at the surface.

3

Where the idea came from

The person most associated with this idea is James Hutton, a Scottish farmer and physician who studied rocks around Edinburgh in the late 1700s. What Hutton saw, and others had not, was that the processes acting on rock today — rain wearing hillsides down, rivers carrying mud to the sea, heat from below lifting and folding strata — were enough to explain everything, provided they had been going on for a very long time. He argued that the Earth was in a continuing cycle of destruction and renewal, and famously wrote in 1788 that in the rock record he could find "no vestige of a beginning, no prospect of an end". That sentence is genuine and appears in his published paper.

His key evidence came from unconformities — places where one set of rock layers sits on top of another set tilted at a different angle. At Siccar Point on the Scottish coast, which Hutton visited by boat in 1788, near-vertical layers are overlain by nearly horizontal ones. To produce that you need a full sequence: layers laid down flat, hardened, tilted and lifted, worn down by erosion, sunk beneath the sea, and then buried under fresh sediment. There is no way to compress that into a few thousand years.

This mattered enormously, because the accepted age of the Earth at the time was around 6,000 years, calculated from biblical genealogies — Archbishop James Ussher had famously dated the creation to 4004 BC. Hutton's rocks demanded vastly more. He could not say how much more; that had to wait for radioactive dating in the twentieth century, which gave the present figure of about 4.54 billion years.

Hutton wrote densely and was not widely read. His ideas reached the scientific world mainly through John Playfair, who restated them clearly in 1802, and Charles Lyell, whose Principles of Geology from 1830 made the slow-processes view standard. Lyell's book travelled with Charles Darwin on the Beagle, and the immense timescale it argued for was precisely what Darwin's theory of evolution required.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating the cycle as a fixed one-way sequence that must be followed in order — igneous, then sedimentary, then metamorphic. It is a network, not a queue. Every shortcut exists.

The other is confusing metamorphism with melting. Metamorphic rock is changed by heat and pressure but stays solid throughout. Once it melts it is magma, and whatever it forms on cooling is igneous. This is worth keeping straight, because it explains why metamorphic rocks often keep traces of their earlier structure while igneous rocks never do.

Commonly missed

Fossils are found in sedimentary rock but almost never in igneous rock. Explain why, using how each type forms.

The trap is answering that igneous rock is older, or that it is found deeper. Neither is reliably true, and neither is the reason.

How to work it: Ask what each rock does to a shell or bone caught up in it. Sedimentary rock forms from fragments settling gently in layers, usually under water, at ordinary temperatures. Remains can be buried in that sediment quickly enough to escape decay, and preserved as the sediment hardens around them. Igneous rock forms from molten material at temperatures of 700 to 1,300 degrees Celsius. Any remains caught in it are destroyed completely. The same reasoning explains why fossils are rare in metamorphic rock: the heat and pressure that reorganise the minerals usually distort or erase them, though faint traces sometimes survive in lightly altered rock.

5

Connected ideas

The surface half of the cycle is driven by weathering and erosion, while the deep half is driven by heat from the Earth's interior moving by convection. Reading rock as changed rather than fixed is itself an exercise in inference from evidence.
