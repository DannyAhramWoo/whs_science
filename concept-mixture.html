Concept

Mixtures and Pure Substances

A mixture contains two or more substances that keep their own identities and can be separated by physical means, while a pure substance contains only one kind of matter throughout.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

In everyday speech "pure" means clean, natural or good — pure orange juice, pure wool. In science it means something narrower and stricter: a pure substance contains only one kind of matter, with nothing else in it. On that definition, pure orange juice is not pure at all. It contains water, sugars, acids and much else, and is a mixture in the scientific sense. Distilled water is pure. Sea water is not.

A mixture is two or more substances present together, each keeping its own properties. Salt in water is still salt: it still tastes salty and it is still there. Air is a mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide and traces of others, and each gas behaves in air as it would alone.

The key test is that mixtures can be separated by physical means — filtering, evaporating, distilling, using a magnet, picking things apart. No chemical reaction is needed, because the substances were never chemically joined in the first place. They are simply sitting together.

This distinguishes a mixture from a compound, where two elements have chemically joined and produced something new with new properties. Water is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen, and it behaves like neither: hydrogen burns, oxygen supports burning, and water puts out fires. You cannot separate water into hydrogen and oxygen by filtering or evaporating.

2

How it works

Every separation method works by exploiting a difference in some property between the components. Once you see that, the methods stop being a list to memorise and become a single idea applied repeatedly.

Filtering exploits a difference in particle size. Sand and water poured through filter paper leaves the sand behind because sand grains are far too large to pass through the pores, while dissolved substances and the water itself go through. This is why filtering never separates a solution: dissolved particles are individual particles, smaller than any pore.

Evaporation exploits a difference in boiling point when you only want one component back. Heat salt solution and the water leaves as vapour while the salt, whose boiling point is enormously higher, stays behind as crystals. You recover the salt but lose the water.

Distillation uses the same difference but keeps both. The vapour is led through a cooled tube where it condenses back to liquid and is collected. This is how drinking water is made from sea water, and how alcoholic spirits are concentrated.

Magnetism exploits a difference in magnetic behaviour: a magnet drawn through a mixture of iron filings and sulfur powder lifts the iron and leaves the sulfur. And chromatography exploits differences in how strongly substances stick to paper, which is how the several dyes in a single black ink can be spread into separate coloured bands.

There is a second, subtler way to tell pure from impure, and it is more reliable than looking. A pure substance melts and boils at fixed, sharp temperatures. Pure water freezes at exactly 0 °C and boils at exactly 100 °C at normal atmospheric pressure. A mixture does neither: it melts and boils over a range, and the temperatures are shifted. Salt water freezes below 0 °C and boils above 100 °C. This is why salt is spread on icy roads — it lowers the freezing point so ice melts at temperatures where pure water would stay frozen. So if a sample melts sharply at the textbook temperature, that is good evidence it is pure; if it softens gradually over several degrees, it is not.

The heart of it

In a mixture the substances are merely together and keep their own properties, so any difference between them — size, boiling point, magnetism — can be used to pull them apart again.

3

Where the idea came from

Nobody discovered mixtures. What had to be developed, over a very long time, was the ability to make something pure and to know that you had.

The alchemists of the medieval Islamic world and later Europe spent centuries on exactly this problem. Their aim was mistaken — most were seeking to transmute cheap metals into gold, and to find a universal remedy — and their underlying theory, inherited partly from Aristotle, was wrong about what matter is. But the work forced them to become extremely good at practical separation. They refined distillation, built condensers, worked out crystallisation and repeated recrystallisation, and produced concentrated acids. Their laboratory equipment is the direct ancestor of what a chemistry lab uses now.

The reason purity mattered so much later is that you cannot do reliable chemistry with impure materials. If two chemists heat what they both call "salt" and one sample has other substances in it, they will get different results and neither will know why. The great advances of the 1700s — Joseph Black on gases, Antoine Lavoisier on combustion — depended on being able to obtain and identify pure substances, and on trusting that a measurement made in one laboratory meant the same as one made elsewhere.

The idea that a fixed melting point is a test of purity emerged from this same practical tradition rather than from any single experiment we can name. It is a good example of a tool that came out of craft knowledge and only later acquired a theoretical explanation.

4

What people get wrong

The main error is using "pure" in its everyday sense. Natural spring water is a mixture of water and dissolved minerals; fresh air is a mixture. Neither is pure scientifically, however clean or wholesome it may be.

The second is thinking a mixture must look mixed. A solution of salt in water is completely clear and uniform, and no amount of looking will reveal two substances. Being uniform is not the same as being pure.

The third is expecting filtering to work on solutions. Filter paper stops undissolved lumps; it cannot stop individual dissolved particles. Filtering salt water gives you salt water.

Commonly missed

A liquid is heated and found to boil steadily at 100 °C throughout, leaving nothing behind when it has all boiled away. A second liquid begins to boil at 102 °C and the boiling temperature creeps upwards as it goes, leaving a white solid behind. What can you conclude about each?

The trap is judging by appearance — both liquids look like clear water — and ignoring the two pieces of evidence actually given.

How to work it: Take the boiling behaviour first. A fixed, sharp boiling point of 100 °C is what a pure substance does, and 100 °C is water's value, so the first liquid is very likely pure water. The second boils above 100 °C and over a rising range, which is the signature of a mixture — a dissolved solute raises the boiling point, and as water leaves the remaining solution gets more concentrated, so the boiling point climbs further. The residue confirms it: something non-volatile was dissolved in it. So the first is pure water and the second is a solution.

5

Connected ideas

A solution is one particular kind of mixture, and the hardest to separate by simple means. The distinction rests on the particle model, which lets us picture different kinds of particle sitting side by side without joining. Separation methods rely on evaporation and boiling, and the whole topic depends on knowing the difference between physical and chemical change — since mixing is physical, and compound formation is chemical.

In one line

A mixture is substances sitting together while keeping their own properties, so it can always be separated physically by exploiting a difference between them, whereas a pure substance is one kind of matter only and shows it by melting and boiling at sharp, fixed temperatures.
