Concept

Ecosystem

An ecosystem is all the living things in a place together with the non-living surroundings they depend on, treated as one working whole.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Stand at the edge of a pond and list what you can see. Frogs, pondweed, water snails, a heron. That is a list of living things. But the frog also needs the water it swims in, the oxygen dissolved in it, the mud it buries itself in over winter and the sunlight that warms it. Leave those out and the list explains nothing.

An ecosystem is the list with the surroundings put back in: the living and the non-living things in one place, considered together, because each acts on the other.

Biologists build up to it in steps. An individual is one living thing: this frog. A population is all the members of one species in one place: every frog in the pond. A community is all the populations together, every living thing sharing the pond. An ecosystem is that community plus the water, mud, sunlight, dissolved oxygen and temperature. The biosphere is every ecosystem on Earth added together.

One line on that ladder is worth memorising. Individual, population and community are all lists of living things. The ecosystem is the first level including things that were never alive, which is exactly what the word was invented to do.

2

How it works

Compare two ecosystems. In a pond, the living part includes pondweed and algae making food from sunlight, water fleas eating the algae, snails, fish and frogs, a heron at the top, and bacteria in the mud breaking down whatever dies. The non-living part is the water, the oxygen and carbon dioxide dissolved in it, the mud and stones, the sunlight, and the temperature, which changes slowly because water warms and cools sluggishly.

In a patch of woodland the living part is oak and beech trees, bluebells, caterpillars, woodlice, mice, owls, and fungi in the leaf litter. The non-living part is soil, rainfall, air, wind, light and temperature, which swings far more sharply than the pond's. Those differences explain much of the difference in the living part: a woodland floor plant must survive deep shade, so bluebells flower early, before the leaves close overhead.

Two things move through every ecosystem, and they behave differently. Energy flows one way. It arrives as sunlight, a producer stores some as chemical energy, and at each feeding step most is released as heat during respiration and lost. It does not come back, which is why sunlight must keep arriving.

Nutrients are recycled. The carbon and nitrogen atoms in a leaf are not lost when it dies. Decomposers break the leaf down, the atoms return to soil and air, a root takes them up, and the same atoms are built into a new leaf. Earth gains almost no new carbon or nitrogen, so one stock is used over and over. Energy flows through and out; matter goes round and round.

The heart of it

An ecosystem is the first level that includes the non-living surroundings, and inside it energy flows one way and is lost as heat while nutrients are recycled indefinitely.

3

Where the idea came from

The word is younger than you might expect. The British botanist Arthur Tansley coined ecosystem in 1935, and he coined it in the middle of an argument.

The view he argued against belonged to the American ecologist Frederic Clements, who described a plant community as a kind of superorganism: a thing that grew, matured and had a proper adult form, with the individual plants as its parts. Tansley thought this dressed up a comparison as a fact. His deeper objection was that treating the community as a creature quietly left out the soil, the water and the climate, when those were doing much of the work. He wanted a term that forced the physical surroundings into the same unit as the organisms, and ecosystem was that term.

Naming it was one thing; making it measurable was another. Raymond Lindeman's paper of 1942 put energy at the centre, tracing how much passed from one feeding level to the next, which turned the ecosystem into something you could put numbers on. From the 1950s the textbooks of Eugene Odum carried the idea into classrooms everywhere.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking an ecosystem must be a certain size. A rotting log is an ecosystem: it has fungi, woodlice, beetle larvae and bacteria, plus moisture, temperature and the wood itself. So is a whole forest, and so is a rock pool. Where you draw the boundary is a choice made for convenience, not a line that exists in nature. Very little is truly sealed off — a bird flies out of the wood at dawn and back at dusk, carrying energy across whatever line you drew.

The second is confusing an ecosystem with a community, the level below. If an answer names only living things, it has described a community.

Commonly missed

A pupil writes: "The ecosystem of the pond is the frogs, fish, snails, pondweed and the heron." What has been left out, and what has actually been described?

The trap is that the list is correct as far as it goes, so it looks like a complete answer. Everything named really does live in the pond.

How to work it: Check the list for anything that was never alive. There is nothing, so this is the community, not the ecosystem. To make it an ecosystem, add the water, dissolved oxygen, mud and stones, sunlight and temperature. A quick check on any answer of this kind: if you cannot name one thing that has never been alive, you have not reached the ecosystem level.

5

Connected ideas

The ladder below the ecosystem is populations and communities, and its living and non-living halves are the biotic and abiotic factors. The place an organism occupies within one is its habitat, and the features fitting it there are its adaptations. Energy moves along food chains, and the recycling of matter is set out in the carbon cycle and the water cycle.
