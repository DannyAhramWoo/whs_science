Concept

Scattering of Light

Scattering is what happens when light strikes very small particles and is sent off in all directions instead of continuing in a straight line.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

Walk into a dusty attic where a single beam of sunlight comes through a gap in the shutters. You can see the beam itself, hanging in the air as a solid-looking shaft. Now think about what that means. Light travels in straight lines, so the light in that beam is heading for the floor, not for your eyes. You are standing to one side. You should see nothing.

You see it because of the dust. Each floating speck is struck by the beam and throws light out in every direction, and a little of that reaches your eye from every point along the beam. The beam becomes visible only where there is something in it to redirect the light sideways.

That redirection is scattering. Light meets a particle much smaller than a raindrop — a dust grain, a droplet of mist, a single molecule of air — and instead of passing through or bouncing off in one neat direction, it is flung outwards over a wide spread of angles.

Scattering is not the same as reflection. Reflection happens at a smooth surface large compared with the light, and it sends the light off in one predictable direction, which is why a mirror gives an image. Scattering happens at tiny particles and sends light everywhere at once, which is why the sky gives no image of anything — just an even glow.

2

How it works

The crucial extra fact is that scattering does not treat all colours equally. Sunlight is a mixture of all the colours, each with its own wavelength: red light has the longest wavelength of the visible colours, at roughly 700 nanometres, and violet and blue the shortest, at around 400 to 450 nanometres. When light meets particles far smaller than its own wavelength — and the nitrogen and oxygen molecules of the air are hundreds of times smaller — the short wavelengths are scattered far more strongly than the long ones. Blue is scattered several times as much as red.

Now look up. Sunlight enters the atmosphere from one direction. The reds and yellows mostly carry straight on. The blues are knocked sideways again and again by air molecules, bouncing around the sky until they arrive at your eye from every part of it. So the sky is blue because blue light is scattered out of the beam and spread across the whole dome above you. There is no blue paint up there and the air is not blue. You are seeing scattered sunlight. This also explains why the sky is black on the Moon, which has no atmosphere and therefore nothing to do the scattering, even in full sunlight.

Sunset is the same mechanism seen from the other end. When the Sun is low, its light enters at a shallow angle and must cross a far greater thickness of atmosphere to reach you — several times the path length it takes at noon. Over that long journey almost all the blue is scattered away sideways, out of the beam, and never arrives. What survives the trip is what scatters least: the reds and oranges. So the Sun and the clouds around it turn red at sunset not because anything adds red, but because the blue has been removed. Dust or smoke in the air scatters even more and deepens the colour, which is why sunsets are most vivid after a volcanic eruption or a bush fire.

Clouds are a useful contrast. A cloud is made of water droplets, and these are far larger than the wavelength of light. Large particles scatter all colours by roughly the same amount, so all colours come back together and mix. All colours mixed gives white, which is why clouds look white while the clear sky looks blue. A thick storm cloud looks grey only because so little light gets through it.

Be careful to keep four sky phenomena apart, because a matching question in past IMSO papers has set them against one another. Blue sky is scattering. A rainbow is refraction and dispersion, where light bends on entering a raindrop and the colours separate because each bends by a different amount. The colours swirling on a soap bubble come from interference between light reflected off the front and back of the thin film. Light staying inside an optical fibre is total internal reflection. Four different causes, one for each.

The heart of it

Small particles scatter short wavelengths most, so the sky is blue because blue is scattered towards us from all directions, and the sunset is red because the blue has been scattered away over a longer path.

3

Where the idea came from

Early explanations of the blue sky were all wrong in the same way: they assumed something up there must actually be blue. One idea was that we were seeing the darkness of space through a thin bright air; another that fine water vapour was tinted blue. Leonardo da Vinci around 1500 came closer, suggesting that fine particles lit from behind appear blue against darkness, though he could not say why.

John Tyndall, working in London in the 1860s, gave the first solid experimental grip on the problem. He passed white light through a tube of air carrying fine suspended particles and found the light coming out sideways was blue, while the light continuing straight through turned reddish. He had made a sunset in a tube. Tyndall concluded that dust and water droplets caused the sky's colour, which was right about the mechanism and wrong about the cause, since the sky would still be blue in perfectly clean air.

Lord Rayleigh supplied the mathematics in the 1870s. He showed that particles much smaller than the wavelength scatter light in proportion to one over the wavelength raised to the fourth power — so halving the wavelength scatters sixteen times as strongly — and that the molecules of the air alone are enough to do it without any dust. This is now called Rayleigh scattering. Where the particles are larger, as in mist or smoke, the effect is often still called Tyndall scattering.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is thinking the sky is blue because it reflects the sea. It is not. The sea is largely reflecting the sky, and the sky over a desert a thousand kilometres from any water is just as blue.

The second is thinking that something adds red at sunset. Nothing is added. Red is what remains once the blue has been scattered out over a long path through the atmosphere. Removing, not adding, is the right verb.

The third is calling scattering "reflection". Reflection is directional and needs a surface; scattering is in all directions from tiny particles. If a question asks why the sky is blue, "reflection" will not score.

Commonly missed

Explain why the daytime sky is blue but the clouds in it are white, given that both are made of things in the atmosphere and both are lit by the same white sunlight.

The trap is assuming one explanation must cover both, so a pupil who correctly says "blue scatters most" then cannot account for a white cloud sitting in the middle of it.

How to work it: Compare the size of the particles against the wavelength of light. The clear sky contains air molecules, which are hundreds of times smaller than a wavelength of light. At that size, scattering strongly favours short wavelengths, so blue is scattered towards us far more than red, and the sky looks blue. A cloud contains water droplets, which are larger than a wavelength of light. At that size the favouring disappears and every colour is scattered about equally, so all the colours come back mixed together, and a mixture of all colours is white. Same sunlight, same process, different particle size — and the particle size decides everything.

5

Connected ideas

Scattering only makes sense once you know that white light is a mixture of colours and that each colour is a wavelength, which is the business of colour and of waves. It sits alongside reflection and refraction as one of the things light does when it meets matter, and it is a property of the atmosphere that a sky on an airless world does not share.

In one line

Tiny particles scatter short wavelengths most strongly, which turns the whole sky blue by day and leaves only red light in the Sun's beam when it must cross the atmosphere at a shallow angle.
