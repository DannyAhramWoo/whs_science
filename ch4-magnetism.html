Chapter 4

Magnetism

Magnets pull on some metals and ignore others, and they reach across a gap to do it. The biggest trap in this topic is thinking that attraction proves something is a magnet. It does not.

4.1

Which materials a magnet can pull

A magnetic material is one that a magnet can attract. There are only four you need for these questions: iron, steel, nickel and cobalt. Steel counts because steel is mostly iron with a little carbon mixed in.

Everything else is non-magnetic. A magnet will not pull wood, plastic, glass, rubber or paper. That part feels obvious. The part that catches people is that a magnet will not pull copper, aluminium, brass, silver or gold either — and those are all metals.

So "metal" and "magnetic" are two different ideas. Every magnetic material happens to be a metal, but only a few metals are magnetic. A drinks can made of aluminium sits there while a steel paperclip beside it jumps to the magnet.

Commonly missed

A pupil sorts a pile of objects using a magnet. Which of these will the magnet pick up: a copper coin, an aluminium can, a steel nail, a brass key?

All four are metal, so it is tempting to say the magnet picks up all of them. The trap works because the everyday word "metal" and the science word "magnetic" get muddled together.

How to work it: run through the short list — iron, steel, nickel, cobalt. Only the steel nail is on it. Copper, aluminium and brass are not, so the magnet leaves all three behind. If an option offers "all the metals", that is almost always the wrong answer.

4.2

Poles, and the rule that follows from them

Every magnet has two ends where its pull is strongest. These are the poles. One is the north pole, the other the south pole. You cannot have a magnet with only one pole. Snap a bar magnet in half and you do not get a lonely N and a lonely S — you get two smaller magnets, each with its own N and S.

Bring two magnets together and one rule decides what happens: like poles repel, unlike poles attract. N facing N pushes apart. S facing S pushes apart. N facing S pulls together.

Repelling is the important half. A magnet can attract many things, but it can only push away another magnet. Nothing else gets pushed. Hold a magnet near a steel nail and the nail is always pulled in, never shoved away.

Only magnets get repelled

Attraction happens between a magnet and any magnetic material. Repulsion only ever happens between two magnets. That one difference is the key to the next section.

4.3

Testing whether a bar is really a magnet

Here is the classic setup. You have two identical grey bars. One is a magnet, the other is an ordinary unmagnetised iron bar. They look the same. How do you tell which is which?

Most people reach for attraction. Hold the bars near each other — if they pull together, that must be the magnet. But this proves nothing. A magnet attracts unmagnetised iron just as happily as it attracts another magnet. So the bars will pull together no matter which way round they are, and you learn nothing at all.

The answer is the repulsion test. Turn one bar around and try both ways. If at some point the two bars push each other apart, both bars must be magnets. Iron that is not magnetised can never push. If they attract both ways round, one of them is plain iron.

Commonly missed

Bar X attracts bar Y. Which statement must be true?

The tempting answer is "both X and Y are magnets". It is wrong. Attraction has three possible causes: two magnets facing unlike poles, a magnet and a piece of unmagnetised iron, or the same pair the other way round. You cannot tell which from attraction alone.

How to work it: the only thing attraction proves is that at least one of the two is a magnet, and the other is at least magnetic material. To prove both are magnets you need repulsion. Whenever a question gives you attraction and asks what "must" be true, be suspicious of any option claiming both objects are magnets.

4.4

The magnetic field: where the force reaches

Magnetic force is a non-contact force. It works across a gap, with nothing at all in between. A magnet held above a paperclip lifts it before the two touch.

The space around a magnet where this force can be felt is called the magnetic field. You cannot see it, so we draw it as field lines. By agreement the lines always start at the north pole and curve round to the south pole.

Where the lines are crowded close together, the field is strong. That happens at the poles, which is why a paperclip sticks best to the ends of a bar magnet and barely sticks to the middle. Move further away and the lines spread out, so the force gets weaker. Far enough away and the magnet has no useful effect at all.

Reading a field diagram

Two things to check every time: the arrows point from N to S outside the magnet, and the lines are closest together at the poles. A diagram that gets either of these wrong is the wrong answer.

4.5

Electromagnets: magnetism you can switch off

Wind a coil of wire around an iron bar and connect the coil to a battery. While the current flows, the iron bar behaves as a magnet. Break the circuit and the magnetism vanishes. This is an electromagnet, and the iron bar in the middle is called the core.

Three things make an electromagnet stronger, and questions ask for them constantly:

More turns of wire in the coil. More current, which usually means more batteries. And a soft iron core instead of no core at all. Any one of these increases the pull; doing all three increases it a lot.

The real advantage is not strength, though. It is control. A permanent magnet such as a bar magnet is magnetic all the time and you cannot turn it off. An electromagnet is only magnetic while the switch is closed. That is exactly what a scrapyard crane needs: switch on to lift a car body, drive it across the yard, switch off to drop it. A doorbell and an electric bell work the same way — the electromagnet pulls an arm to strike the gong, the circuit breaks, the arm springs back, and the cycle repeats.

Stronger, three ways

More turns, more current, and an iron core. Note what is not on the list: making the wire longer without adding turns, or using a copper core. Copper is not magnetic, so a copper core does nothing.

Commonly missed

Which is the better choice for a crane that sorts scrap metal: a very strong permanent magnet, or an electromagnet?

"Strong" sounds like the winning word, so the permanent magnet looks attractive. But strength is not the problem the crane has.

How to work it: ask what the machine needs to do. The crane must pick up and then let go. A permanent magnet cannot let go — the scrap would stay stuck to it. The electromagnet wins because it can be switched off. Whenever a use involves releasing the load, or turning the pull on and off, the answer is the electromagnet.

4.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

4.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A magnet is dipped into a box of mixed objects. It picks up some paperclips but leaves the copper wire and the aluminium foil behind. Explain why.

Show answer

Paperclips are made of steel, and steel is mostly iron. Iron is one of the four magnetic materials, so the magnet attracts them.

Copper and aluminium are metals, but they are not magnetic materials. Only iron, steel, nickel and cobalt are attracted to a magnet. Being made of metal is not enough on its own.

2

You have two identical iron bars. One is a magnet and one is not. You are allowed to use only the two bars. How do you find out which is the magnet, and why does simply seeing if they attract not work?

Show answer

Bring the two ends together, then turn one bar round and try again. If the bars ever push each other apart, both are magnets. If they attract whichever way round you hold them, one bar is unmagnetised iron.

Attraction is useless as a test because a magnet attracts unmagnetised iron just as strongly as it attracts another magnet. So you would see attraction either way and learn nothing.

To find which single bar is the magnet, hang each bar by a thread near the middle of the other. Only the true magnet will repel a known pole. Repulsion is the only proof.

3

An electromagnet lifts eight paperclips. Give three changes that would let it lift more, and explain why each works.

Show answer

Add more turns of wire. Each turn adds its own share to the field, so more turns make a stronger field from the same current.

Increase the current, for example by adding a second battery. A larger current through the same coil produces a stronger field.

Use an iron core, or a thicker one. Iron concentrates the field produced by the coil, which greatly increases the pull compared with a coil around nothing.

4

A steel paperclip is hanging in the air below a magnet, not touching it. Name the forces on the paperclip, say which is a non-contact force, and explain how you know the magnetic force is not larger than the weight.

Show answer

Two forces act: the magnetic force pulling the clip upwards and its weight pulling it down. Both are non-contact forces, since neither needs the clip to touch anything.

The clip is hanging still, so its motion is not changing. That means the forces are balanced and the two must be exactly equal.

If the magnetic force were larger, the clip would rise up to the magnet. Since it stays where it is, the magnetic force cannot be the larger one.

4.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 4.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about magnetic materials are correct?

  • 1All metals are attracted to a magnet.
  • 2Steel is attracted to a magnet.
  • 3Copper and aluminium are not attracted to a magnet.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Copper, aluminium and brass are metals and none of them is attracted. Only iron, steel, nickel and cobalt are. The word "all" is the giveaway. And once statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C — you can stop here.

Checking anyway: steel is mostly iron, so statement 2 is correct, and statement 3 says exactly what makes statement 1 false, so it is correct too. Consistent with C.

2

Bar P is brought near bar Q and the two bars push each other apart. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Both P and Q are magnets.
  • 2The two facing ends are like poles.
  • 3Q could be a bar of unmagnetised iron.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Repulsion only ever happens between two magnets, so both bars must be magnets. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Unmagnetised iron can only be attracted, never repelled. It contradicts statement 1. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Like poles repel, so if the bars push apart the facing ends must be both N or both S. So the answer is B.

3

Which statements about electromagnets are correct?

  • 1Adding more turns of wire to the coil makes the electromagnet stronger.
  • 2Increasing the current in the coil makes the electromagnet stronger.
  • 3The magnetism can be switched off by breaking the circuit.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Each extra turn adds to the field, so more turns give a stronger magnet. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Without current there is no field, so breaking the circuit removes the magnetism. Correct statement 3 means the answer is A.

Statement 2 — correct as well, since a larger current produces a stronger field. All three are right, so A stands.
