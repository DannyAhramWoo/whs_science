Concept

Phases of the Moon

The phases of the Moon are the changing shapes we see as the Moon orbits the Earth, because we view its sunlit half from different angles.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Watch the Moon for a month and it changes shape. It grows from nothing to a thin curved sliver, thickens to a half circle, fills out to a complete disc, then shrinks back the same way. The whole cycle takes about 29.5 days. These shapes are the phases, and they come in a fixed order that never varies.

The essential thing to understand first is that the Moon makes no light of its own. It is a ball of rock, and it is only visible because sunlight falls on it and bounces off towards us. Anything that shines by reflecting another source is called a non-luminous object, and the Moon is the largest one most of us ever see.

Now the key point. Because the Sun is a single source of light off to one side, exactly half the Moon is lit at every single moment — the half facing the Sun. That never changes. What changes is how much of the lit half we can see from Earth, and that depends on where the Moon has got to in its orbit. A phase is not a change in the Moon. It is a change in our viewing angle.

2

How it works

Picture the Sun far away to the left, shining on everything. The Earth sits in the middle and the Moon travels in a big circle around it, taking about 27.3 days to complete one orbit relative to the stars.

When the Moon is on the far side of the Earth from the Sun, the Sun is behind us and shining straight past us onto the Moon's near face. We see the whole lit half: a full moon. When the Moon has travelled round to sit between the Earth and the Sun, its lit half faces away from us and its dark half faces us, so we see almost nothing: a new moon. At the two quarter positions, the Moon is off to one side, and we see the lit half edge-on — half lit, half dark — which looks to us like a half circle. Confusingly, these are called first quarter and third quarter, because the Moon is a quarter and three quarters of the way round its orbit, not because a quarter is lit.

The in-between shapes have names worth knowing. Crescent means less than half lit; gibbous means more than half but not full. Waxing means growing, waning means shrinking. So the full sequence is: new, waxing crescent, first quarter, waxing gibbous, full, waning gibbous, third quarter, waning crescent, and back to new.

There is a useful test you can do without any equipment. Look at a crescent moon and note which side is bright, then find the Sun, or work out where it has set. The bright edge always faces the Sun. It has to — that is the side the light is coming from. This one observation is enough to show that the phases are about lighting and geometry.

Notice also that the phase tells you when the Moon is in the sky. A full moon is opposite the Sun, so it rises as the Sun sets and is up all night. A new moon is in the same direction as the Sun, so it is up in the daytime and lost in the glare. A first quarter moon is a quarter turn away, so it is high in the evening sky.

The heart of it

Half the Moon is always lit; the phase is simply how much of that lit half is turned towards us.

3

Where the idea came from

This is one of the oldest correct scientific explanations we have. The ancient Greeks had it right, and no single person deserves the credit — several thinkers arrived at pieces of it during the fifth and fourth centuries BC.

Anaxagoras, in Athens around 450 BC, argued that the Moon is made of rock, similar in nature to the Earth, and shines by reflected sunlight rather than by its own power. Ancient sources report that he was prosecuted for impiety over claims of this kind — the Moon was widely regarded as divine, and saying it was a lump of stone was an offence against the gods. The details of that trial come to us second-hand and centuries later, so we cannot be sure exactly what he was charged with, but the story reflects a real hostility to the idea.

Aristarchus of Samos, around 270 BC, went further and turned the phases into a measuring tool. He reasoned that at exactly half moon, the Sun, Moon and Earth must form a right angle at the Moon. By measuring the angle between the Sun and the Moon in the sky at that moment, he could work out the shape of the triangle and hence how much further away the Sun is than the Moon. The method is completely sound. His answer was badly wrong — about 19 times, when the true figure is nearer 400 — because judging the exact instant of half moon by eye is very difficult and a small error in the angle produces an enormous error in the result. Even so, he had established that the Sun is far bigger and further away than it looks, which is part of why he proposed a Sun-centred system.

4

What people get wrong

The classic error is thinking the phases are caused by the Earth's shadow falling on the Moon. This is a reasonable guess, but it cannot be right, for a simple reason: the Earth's shadow points directly away from the Sun, so it can only reach the Moon when the Moon is on the opposite side of the Earth from the Sun. That is the full moon position — the one time in the month when the Moon looks completely bright. When the Earth's shadow does land on the Moon, we get a lunar eclipse, and that is a different, rarer event lasting hours rather than weeks.

A second error is thinking there is a permanently dark side of the Moon. The Moon always keeps the same face towards Earth, so there is a far side we never see from here, but every part of the Moon gets sunlight during the month. The far side has day and night just as the near side does.

Commonly missed

A student says the Moon shows phases because the Earth's shadow covers part of it. Give one observation that shows this cannot be correct.

The trap is that shadows are a familiar way for things to go dark, so the explanation feels right until you check the geometry.

How to work it: Work out where the Earth's shadow points — directly away from the Sun. So the shadow can only fall on the Moon when the Moon is opposite the Sun. But that is exactly the position where we see a full moon, fully bright. If the shadow theory were correct, the Moon would be darkest when it is in fact brightest. A second answer works too: phases follow a smooth monthly cycle, but the Moon spends most of the month nowhere near the Earth's shadow.

5

Connected ideas

The phases depend on the Moon reflecting sunlight rather than producing it, and on the Moon's revolution around the Earth. Eclipses happen at the two phases where the three bodies line up exactly, and the Moon stays in orbit at all because of gravity.
