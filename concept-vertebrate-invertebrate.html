Concept

Vertebrates and Invertebrates

Vertebrates have a backbone of linked bones running down the body; invertebrates are every animal without one.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

Run your fingers down your back and you can feel a row of hard bumps under the skin. Those are the vertebrae: separate bones stacked in a column, each able to tilt a little against its neighbours. The column is the backbone, or vertebral column, and any animal with one is a vertebrate. Fish, frogs, snakes, birds and every mammal have one, and so do you.

Invertebrate means "without a backbone": insects, spiders, snails, worms, crabs, jellyfish, octopuses and starfish. The pair is odd, one group defined by having a thing and the other by lacking it, and the lacking group is far bigger. Around ninety-five of every hundred known animal species are invertebrates. Vertebrates only feel like the main event because we are one.

So the two words are not matched partners: "vertebrate" names a genuine family whose members are all related, "invertebrate" everything left over. A crab and a jellyfish are both invertebrates, yet no more related to each other than either is to you.

2

How it works

Why does a backbone matter enough to be the first split? Every animal above a certain size faces three problems: holding the body up against gravity, anchoring muscles to pull against, and protecting the nerve cord from the brain. A backbone solves all three at once: the stacked vertebrae carry the weight, muscles attach to bony bumps along them, and a channel through the column shelters the spinal cord like a pipe around a cable.

The critical feature is that the skeleton is inside. Bone can be added to and reshaped while the animal is alive, so a vertebrate grows continuously to a great size without stopping to rebuild. This is why the largest animals that ever lived have all been vertebrates.

Invertebrates solved the same problems differently, at a price. Insects, spiders and crabs wear an exoskeleton, a hard outer case. Muscles pull against it from within, but it cannot grow: the animal must shed the case and swell before the fresh one hardens, and during those hours it is soft and nearly defenceless. That limit is much of why there are no insects the size of dogs.

Other invertebrates avoid skeletons entirely. An earthworm holds its shape with fluid squeezed inside a muscular tube, as a balloon holds its shape with air: cheap to build, but unable to support much weight out of water.

The heart of it

A backbone is an internal support that grows with the animal, which is why vertebrates reach sizes no exoskeleton or fluid-filled body could hold up.

3

Where the idea came from

The word "invertebrate" is younger than you might expect. The French naturalist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck coined it around 1800, while working on the animal collections in Paris. Before him, animals without backbones were largely ignored. Naturalists studied birds, fish and mammals in detail because they were large, familiar, huntable and easy to preserve. Worms, insects and shellfish were lumped into a vague heap and left alone.

Lamarck did the first serious work on that heap, arguing the animals were not one undifferentiated mass but many distinct groups, deserving the careful separation given to vertebrates. Pulling molluscs, insects, crustaceans and worms apart was a real advance, and much of it has survived.

This is the useful lesson in his story. Lamarck is more often remembered for a different idea about how species change over generations, which was wrong in its central mechanism. But being wrong about one thing did not make him wrong about everything. His patient sorting of the animals nobody else would look at was solid work, and it stands. Judge each claim on its own rather than judging the person and then the claims.

One further honest point: who first drew the backbone line is hard to trace, because people had noticed for centuries that some animals had bony spines and others did not. We cannot be sure of a single origin for the observation. Lamarck supplied the name for the leftover group and the serious study it had never had.

4

What people get wrong

The biggest error is thinking invertebrate means "soft" or "small". A crab is armoured hard enough to hurt your hand and a giant squid can be metres long, yet both are invertebrates: the test is a backbone, not hardness or size.

The second error is treating invertebrates as one kind of animal. They are not a family but a leftover pile, so "invertebrates reproduce like this" is almost always false, the pile holding creatures as different as a snail is from a spider.

Commonly missed

A crab has a hard skeleton, moves on jointed legs and grows to a good size. A pupil concludes it must be a vertebrate. Where has the reasoning gone wrong, and what would you check?

It is a trap because every fact the pupil cites is true. The error is the definition: they have quietly replaced "has a backbone" with "has a hard skeleton", a different test.

How to work it: Return to the actual criterion, a column of linked vertebrae inside the body housing a nerve cord, then check where the crab's hard parts are. They form a case outside with the soft tissue within, an exoskeleton, the opposite arrangement, with no bony column inside. The crab is therefore an invertebrate, and the clinching evidence is that it must moult its whole case to grow, which no vertebrate has to do.

5

Connected ideas

This split is classification's opening move, and a dichotomous key almost always asks it first because it is quick to check and splits the animals into two useful halves. The backbone also belongs to the skeleton and muscle system, where the reason for stacked separate bones rather than one solid rod becomes clear. And which skeleton an animal has is itself an adaptation, shaping where it can succeed.

In one line

Vertebrates have an internal column of linked bones that supports the body, anchors the muscles, shelters the nerve cord and grows with the animal; invertebrates are the far larger leftover group, solving those problems with an outer case, fluid pressure, or nothing.
