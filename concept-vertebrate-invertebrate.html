Concept

Vertebrates and Invertebrates

Vertebrates are animals with a backbone made of linked bones running down the body; invertebrates are every animal that has no backbone at all.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

Run your fingers down the middle of your back and you can feel a row of hard bumps under the skin. Those are the vertebrae: separate bones stacked in a column, each able to tilt a little against its neighbours. The whole column is the backbone, or vertebral column, and any animal that has one is a vertebrate. Fish, frogs, snakes, birds and every mammal have one. So do you.

Invertebrate means "without a backbone". Insects, spiders, snails, worms, crabs, jellyfish, octopuses and starfish are all invertebrates. This pair of groups is odd: one is defined by having a thing, the other by lacking it, and the lacking group is enormously bigger. Around ninety-five out of every hundred known animal species are invertebrates. Vertebrates only feel like the main event because we happen to be one.

So the two words are not matched partners. "Vertebrate" names a genuine family whose members are all related; "invertebrate" names everything left over once that family is removed. A crab and a jellyfish are both invertebrates, but they are no more closely related to each other than either is to you.

2

How it works

Why does a backbone matter enough to be the first split? Every animal above a certain size faces three problems: something must hold the body up against gravity, give muscles a firm anchor to pull against, and protect the nerve cord carrying signals from the brain. A backbone solves all three at once. The stacked vertebrae carry the body's weight, muscles attach to bony bumps along them, and a channel through the column shelters the spinal cord like a pipe around a cable.

The critical feature is that the skeleton is inside. Bone can be added to and reshaped while the animal is alive, so a vertebrate can grow continuously to a very large size without stopping to rebuild. This is why the largest animals that have ever lived have all been vertebrates.

Invertebrates solved the same three problems differently, and their solutions carry a price. Insects, spiders and crabs wear an exoskeleton, a hard case on the outside. It protects well and muscles pull against it from within, but it cannot grow. The animal must shed the whole case and swell to a new size before the fresh one hardens, and during those hours it is soft and nearly defenceless. That limit is a large part of why there are no insects the size of dogs.

Other invertebrates avoid skeletons entirely. An earthworm holds its shape with fluid squeezed inside a muscular tube, as a balloon holds its shape with air: cheap to build, but unable to support much weight out of water. The split is thus an engineering divide, skeleton inside, outside, or none at all, with different consequences for size, growth and where the animal can live.

The heart of it

A backbone is an internal support that can grow with the animal, which is why vertebrates can reach sizes no exoskeleton or fluid-filled body could ever hold up.

3

Where the idea came from

The word "invertebrate" is younger than you might expect. It was coined by the French naturalist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck around the turn of the nineteenth century, roughly 1800, while he worked on the animal collections in Paris. Before him, animals without backbones had been largely ignored. Naturalists studied birds, fish and mammals in detail because those animals were large, familiar, huntable and easy to preserve. Worms, insects and shellfish were lumped into a vague heap and left alone.

Lamarck did the first serious work on that neglected heap. He examined the animals closely and argued that they were not one undifferentiated mass but many distinct groups, deserving the same careful separation given to animals with backbones. Pulling molluscs, insects, crustaceans and worms apart was a real advance, and much of that separation has survived.

This is the useful lesson in his story. Lamarck is more often remembered for a different idea about how species change over generations, which turned out to be wrong in its central mechanism. But being wrong about one thing did not make him wrong about everything. His patient sorting of the animals nobody else would look at was solid work, and it stands. Judge each claim on its own rather than judging the person and then the claims.

One further honest point: who first drew the backbone line is hard to trace, because people had noticed for many centuries that some animals had bony spines and others did not. We cannot be sure of a single origin for the observation itself. What Lamarck supplied was the name for the leftover group and the serious study it had never had.

4

What people get wrong

The biggest error is thinking invertebrate means "soft" or "small". A crab is armoured hard enough to hurt your hand and a giant squid can be many metres long, yet both are invertebrates. The definition is about a backbone specifically, not hardness, size or having any skeleton at all.

The second error is treating invertebrates as one kind of animal. They are not a family but a leftover pile. Saying "invertebrates reproduce like this" is almost always false, because the pile contains creatures as different as a snail is from a spider.

A third slip is confusing a shell with a backbone. A snail's shell sits outside the body and protects it, but it contains no vertebrae and houses no nerve cord.

Commonly missed

A crab has a hard skeleton, moves quickly on jointed legs and grows to a good size. A pupil concludes it must be a vertebrate. Where has the reasoning gone wrong, and what would you check?

It is a trap because every fact the pupil cites is true. The error is in the definition used: they have quietly replaced "has a backbone" with "has a hard skeleton", which is a different test.

How to work it: Go back to the actual criterion, a column of linked vertebrae inside the body housing a nerve cord. Then check where the crab's hard parts are. They form a case on the outside with the soft tissue within, which is an exoskeleton and the opposite arrangement. There is no bony column down the inside. The crab is therefore an invertebrate, and the clinching evidence is that it must moult its whole case to grow, which no vertebrate ever has to do.

5

Connected ideas

This split is classification's opening move, and a dichotomous key almost always uses it as the first question because it is quick to check and divides the animals into two useful halves. The backbone also belongs to the skeleton and muscle system, where the reason for stacked separate bones rather than one solid rod becomes clear. Which skeleton an animal has is itself an adaptation, shaping where each kind of animal can succeed.

In one line

Vertebrates have an internal column of linked bones that supports the body, anchors the muscles, shelters the nerve cord and can grow with the animal, while invertebrates are the far larger leftover group solving those problems with an outer case, with fluid pressure, or not at all.
