Chapter 19

Ecosystems

Living things do not live alone. They eat, get eaten, compete and depend on each other. The single biggest mark-loser here is the arrow in a food chain — it points from the eaten to the eater, because it shows where the energy goes.

19.1

Levels of organisation: from cell to ecosystem

Biology is built in layers, and each layer is made of the one below it. Questions often give you the eight words jumbled and ask you to sort them from smallest to largest, so learn the ladder by heart.

A cell is the smallest unit of life. Many cells of the same kind make a tissue. Several tissues make an organ, such as a heart or a leaf. Organs that share one job make an organ system, such as the digestive system. All the systems together make one whole organism — one rabbit, one oak tree.

Above the organism the layers stop being about bodies and start being about groups. A population is all the members of one species living in an area — all the rabbits in a field. A community is all the populations living there together — the rabbits, the grass, the foxes, the worms. An ecosystem is that community plus the non-living surroundings: the soil, the water, the air, the sunlight. The place where a particular organism lives is its habitat.

Population versus community

A population is one species. A community is every species in the area. So "all the frogs in the pond" is a population; "all the frogs, fish, weeds and insects in the pond" is a community. Add the water, mud and sunlight and you have an ecosystem.

Commonly missed

A pond contains fish, water plants, insects, water and mud. Which term best describes the fish, the plants and the insects taken together?

The trap is choosing "ecosystem", because the question has just listed the water and mud and your eye picks them up. But the question asks only about the living items.

How to work it: more than one species is listed, so it is not a population. The non-living parts are excluded, so it is not an ecosystem. Several populations of living things together is a community.

19.2

Biotic and abiotic factors, and who makes the food

Everything that affects an organism falls into two groups. Biotic factors are living: predators, prey, competitors, disease. Abiotic factors are non-living: light, temperature, water, oxygen, soil, wind. A quick test — if it was never alive, it is abiotic.

Within the living side, organisms are sorted by how they get their food. Producers make their own food from sunlight, water and carbon dioxide by photosynthesis. Green plants and algae are the producers. Consumers cannot make food, so they eat other organisms. A herbivore eats only plants, a carnivore eats only animals, and an omnivore eats both.

A third group is easy to forget. Decomposers — bacteria and fungi — break down dead plants, dead animals and droppings, returning nutrients to the soil where plants take them up again. Without them the soil would run out of nutrients, the producers would fail, and everything above them would fail too.

Every chain starts with a producer

No food chain begins with an animal. If a chain seems to start with a rabbit, a plant has been left off the front. Trace it back — the energy has to come from sunlight through a producer.

19.3

Food chains, food webs and the arrow that catches people out

A food chain shows what eats what. The arrows do not mean "eats". They show the direction the energy flows — from the organism that is eaten to the organism that eats it. So you read grass → grasshopper as "energy passes from the grass into the grasshopper". Writing the arrow the other way round is the most common error on this topic, and it costs the whole mark.

Real habitats are not single chains. A food web joins many chains together, because most animals eat more than one thing. Webs are useful for a favourite question type: what happens if one organism is removed?

Work it in three steps. First, whatever the removed organism ate is no longer being eaten, so those numbers rise. Second, whatever ate the removed organism has lost a food source, so those numbers fall. Third, follow the effect outwards one more step.

Try it on the web above. Suppose a disease wipes out the grasshoppers. Grass is no longer eaten by them, so grass numbers rise. Frogs lose their only food in this web, so frog numbers fall sharply. Snakes lose one of their two foods, so they eat more mice and mouse numbers fall. Hawks lose frogs but still have snakes, so hawks drop a little rather than disappearing. The animal with only one food source is always hit hardest.

Commonly missed

In the chain lettuce → caterpillar → thrush → hawk, all the thrushes are removed. What happens to the number of caterpillars and the amount of lettuce?

The trap is answering only about the caterpillars and stopping. Questions like this almost always want the knock-on effect as well.

How to work it: nothing is eating the caterpillars now, so caterpillars increase. More caterpillars means more lettuce being eaten, so lettuce decreases. Each step down the chain flips the direction of the effect.

19.4

Why energy runs out, and why predators and prey rise and fall

At every step of a food chain most of the energy is lost. An animal uses energy to move, to stay warm and to keep its body working, and all of that escapes as heat. Some energy leaves in droppings and in the parts that are not eaten, such as bones and fur. Only about a tenth of the energy passes on to the next level.

That single fact explains two things. It explains why food chains are short — after four or five steps there is almost no energy left to support another animal. It also explains why there are far fewer hawks than there are blades of grass. A top predator needs an enormous base of plants beneath it.

Energy loss also puts a ceiling on predator numbers, and that leads to a pattern you will be asked to read off a graph. In a predator–prey cycle the two populations rise and fall in turn, but the predator always lags behind. Plenty of prey means predators are well fed, so predators increase. More predators eat more prey, so prey falls. With less food the predators starve and their numbers fall too. With fewer predators the prey can recover, and the cycle begins again.

Reading the graph

Given two unlabelled curves, the prey is the one that peaks first and is usually higher. The predator peaks a little later and stays lower, because energy is lost at each step so the habitat cannot support as many.

Commonly missed

A graph shows the prey population falling. What will the predator population do next, and why?

The trap is saying "the predators fall because the prey fall" straight away. That skips a step and is often marked wrong, because just after the prey begins to fall the predators are still increasing — they are still well fed.

How to work it: the prey falls because predator numbers grew. The predators keep rising for a short while, then run short of food and fall. Say the reason, not just the direction.

19.5

Adaptation: why an organism fits its habitat

An adaptation is a feature that helps an organism survive in its habitat. The key word is helps it survive. In an answer you must say what the feature is and what it does, or the mark is not given.

Desert plants face one problem: too little water. A cactus stores water in a thick fleshy stem. Its leaves are reduced to needles, which cuts the surface area so far less water escapes, and the spines also keep animals from eating it. Roots either spread wide and shallow to catch brief rain, or go very deep to reach water underground.

Polar animals face the opposite problem: losing heat. A thick layer of fat under the skin and dense fur both act as insulation, slowing heat loss. Small ears and a compact body shape reduce the surface area through which heat can escape — which is why an Arctic fox has short round ears and a desert fox has huge ones.

Water brings its own demands. Fish have gills to take in oxygen dissolved in water, and a streamlined body so water flows past easily. Birds have air sacs that make them lighter for flight. Some fish in muddy, oxygen-poor water have a labyrinth organ that lets them gulp air from the surface.

This is the classic finch example. On a group of islands, finches that started out similar ended up with different beaks on different islands, each shape matching the food available there. It works because members of one species are not identical — there is variation. When conditions change, the individuals whose features happen to suit the new conditions survive and breed, and over many generations the whole population shifts. Variation is what gives a species something to work with.

Answering an adaptation question

Always give feature plus benefit. "The polar bear has thick fur" earns nothing. "The polar bear has thick fur, which traps a layer of air and slows heat loss in the cold" earns the mark.

19.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

19.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

In the food chain leaf → snail → bird, explain what the arrows mean. Why is it wrong to draw them the other way round?

Show answer

Each arrow shows the direction in which energy flows. Energy stored in the leaf passes into the snail when the snail eats it, and then into the bird when the bird eats the snail.

Drawing them the other way would say that energy travels from the bird into the snail and from the snail into the leaf, which is the opposite of what happens. The arrow always points from the organism that is eaten towards the one doing the eating.

2

A farmer removes all the decomposers from a field. Predict what happens over the next few years and explain why.

Show answer

Dead leaves, dead animals and droppings would stop being broken down, so they would build up on the surface of the soil.

More seriously, the nutrients locked inside that dead material would never return to the soil. The plants would slowly run short of nutrients and grow poorly or die.

Because the plants are the producers, every consumer above them would then lose its food supply, and the whole community would collapse from the bottom up.

3

Why are there usually far fewer hawks in a grassland than there are grasshoppers?

Show answer

Energy is lost at every step of a food chain. Animals use most of the energy in their food for movement, warmth and body processes, and that energy escapes as heat. More is lost in droppings and uneaten parts.

Only about a tenth of the energy passes to the next level. The hawk is two or more steps above the grasshopper, so only a small fraction of the grassland's energy reaches it.

That small amount of energy can only support a small number of hawks, so top predators are always rare compared with the organisms below them.

4

A desert fox has very large ears. An Arctic fox has small ones. Explain how each is adapted to its habitat.

Show answer

The desert fox's large ears give a big surface area. Heat from the blood passes out through that surface, which helps the fox lose heat and stay cool in a hot habitat.

The Arctic fox's small ears give a small surface area, so far less heat escapes. In a freezing habitat the problem is keeping heat in, not losing it.

The same body part solves opposite problems, simply by changing size. That is what an adaptation is — a feature that suits the conditions the animal actually lives in.

19.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 19.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which of the following are abiotic factors in a pond?

  • 1The bacteria living in the mud
  • 2The temperature of the water
  • 3The amount of light reaching the bottom
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Bacteria are living things, so they are a biotic factor, not an abiotic one. The moment statement 1 is wrong the answer is C, and you can stop.

Checking anyway: temperature and light were never alive, so both are abiotic. Statements 2 and 3 are correct, which agrees with C.

2

In the food chain grass → rabbit → fox, all the foxes are removed. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The number of rabbits will increase.
  • 2The amount of grass will decrease.
  • 3The rabbits will begin to make their own food by photosynthesis.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Nothing is eating the rabbits now, so their numbers rise. The answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Rabbits are consumers. An animal cannot start photosynthesising because its predator disappeared. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. More rabbits eat more grass, so the grass decreases. The answer is B.

Watch for this: a statement that changes what kind of organism something is, rather than how many there are, is almost always the wrong one.

3

Which statements about a food chain are correct?

  • 1Every food chain begins with a producer.
  • 2The arrows show the direction in which energy flows.
  • 3Energy is lost as heat at each step, so chains are usually short.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. The energy has to enter the chain from sunlight, and only a producer can capture it. The answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Animals lose most of their food energy as heat, so after a few steps there is too little left to support another level. Correct statement 3 means the answer is A.

Statement 2 — correct. The arrow points from the eaten to the eater because that is the way the energy travels. All three are right, confirming A.
