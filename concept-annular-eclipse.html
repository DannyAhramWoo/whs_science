Concept

Why Some Eclipses Are Annular

An annular eclipse happens when the Moon passes in front of the Sun while near the far end of its elliptical orbit, so it looks too small to cover the Sun completely and leaves a bright ring of sunlight showing all around it.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Hold a coin at arm's length and you can just about blot out a distant streetlamp. Bring the coin nearer your eye and it hides far more; hold it further away and the lamp peeps out around the edge. The coin has not changed size. Only its distance from you has, and distance is what decides how big something looks. Astronomers call how big a thing looks its apparent size, measured as an angle across the sky rather than in metres.

A solar eclipse happens when the Moon passes directly between Earth and the Sun and casts its shadow on us. In a total solar eclipse the Moon's disc covers the Sun completely and the sky goes dark. In an annular eclipse the Moon sits dead centre in front of the Sun but is not quite wide enough to cover it, so a complete bright ring remains all the way round. The word comes from the Latin annulus, meaning ring, and it is nothing to do with the word annual.

The difference between those two outcomes is nothing more than how far away the Moon happens to be that day.

2

How it works

The Moon's orbit round Earth is an ellipse — a slightly squashed circle — not a perfect circle, with Earth off to one side rather than in the middle. So the Moon's distance from us changes through the month. At its nearest, called perigee, it is roughly 363,000 kilometres away. At its furthest, apogee, it is roughly 405,000 kilometres. That is a change of about a tenth, which is enough to make the Moon look noticeably bigger or smaller, although the change is too gradual for the eye to catch from one night to the next.

Now put that together with a remarkable coincidence. The Sun is about 400 times wider than the Moon, and it also happens to be about 400 times further away. Those two factors very nearly cancel, so from Earth the Sun and Moon look almost exactly the same size in the sky. This is pure chance — there is no reason it had to work out — and it is the only reason total solar eclipses are possible at all. On a planet whose moon looked much smaller, every solar eclipse would be annular.

Because the match is so close, it takes very little to tip the balance either way. If an eclipse happens when the Moon is near perigee, the Moon looks slightly larger than the Sun, covers it entirely, and the eclipse is total. If it happens near apogee, the Moon looks slightly smaller, cannot quite cover it, and the eclipse is annular. Roughly speaking, a little over a third of central solar eclipses are annular.

Three kinds are worth keeping straight. A total eclipse: the Moon covers the whole Sun, the sky darkens to something like deep twilight, and the Sun's faint outer atmosphere becomes visible. An annular eclipse: the Moon is centred on the Sun but too small, leaving an unbroken ring of sunlight, and the daylight only dims a little because a good deal of the Sun is still shining. A partial eclipse: the Moon is not lined up centrally, so it takes a bite out of one side of the Sun and the remaining bright part is a crescent, not a ring. The quickest test is the shape of what is left. Ring means annular; crescent or bite means partial; nothing means total.

One more question needs answering. The Moon goes round Earth about once a month, so why is there not an eclipse every month? Because the Moon's orbit is tilted by about five degrees relative to the plane in which Earth orbits the Sun. Picture two hoops, slightly crossed rather than lying flat on each other. Most months the Moon passes above or below the direct line to the Sun, and its shadow misses Earth entirely. Only when the Moon happens to be crossing that plane at the same moment it passes between us and the Sun does the shadow land on us. That alignment comes round a few times a year, not monthly.

Past competition papers, IMSO ones included, have set this from a picture: a dark disc surrounded by a complete bright ring, with the question asking what the Moon's distance must be at that moment. The ring tells you the Moon is near apogee.

The heart of it

The Sun and Moon look almost the same size from Earth by coincidence, so when the Moon's elliptical orbit has carried it near its furthest point it looks slightly too small to cover the Sun and leaves a ring of light.

3

Where the idea came from

Eclipses have been recorded for thousands of years, in China, Babylon and Greece, long before anyone understood what caused them. Babylonian astronomers worked out by around 500 BCE that eclipses repeat in a cycle of about 18 years and 11 days, later called the saros. They could predict eclipses without knowing why they happened, purely from the pattern in their records.

The Greeks worked out the mechanism. Around 450 BCE Anaxagoras argued that eclipses are shadows: the Moon blocking the Sun, or Earth's shadow falling on the Moon. In the third century BCE Aristarchus of Samos used eclipse observations to estimate the sizes and distances of the Sun and Moon. His method was sound, but his measurements were far too rough, and he concluded the Sun was about 20 times further away than the Moon rather than 400 times.

Kepler's discovery, published in 1609, that orbits are ellipses rather than circles was what finally explained the difference between total and annular eclipses. Once the Moon's distance is understood to vary through the month, its changing apparent size follows immediately, and so does the ring.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking the Moon must be closer than usual to make a ring appear. It is the reverse. A ring means the Moon looks too small, and something looks small when it is far away, so an annular eclipse means the Moon is near its furthest point.

The second is confusing annular with partial. Both leave sunlight visible, but a partial eclipse leaves a crescent because the alignment is off centre, while an annular eclipse leaves a complete ring because the alignment is good and only the size falls short.

The third is expecting an eclipse every new moon. The five-degree tilt of the Moon's orbit means its shadow usually misses Earth completely.

Safety, and this is not optional. Never look directly at the Sun, at any time, and that includes during any eclipse of any kind. Sunlight can damage the retina permanently, and because the retina has no pain sensors you would feel nothing while it happened. An annular eclipse is especially dangerous because the sky dims enough to feel comfortable while a bright ring of Sun is still shining. Sunglasses, smoked glass, photographic film and looking through a telescope or binoculars are all unsafe, and the last of those is far worse than using no equipment at all. The safe method is pinhole projection: make a small hole in card, hold it so sunlight passes through, and let the image fall onto a second card or a wall, then look at that second surface. You look at the projected image on the surface, with your back to the Sun. You never look through the hole.

Commonly missed

During one solar eclipse the Moon appears as a dark disc with a complete bright ring of sunlight around it. Where in its orbit is the Moon, and why is the eclipse not total?

The trap is the assumption that a more dramatic-looking eclipse means the Moon is nearer. Readers reach for "close" because the Moon is passing in front of the Sun, and the ring is a striking sight.

How to work it: Start from what the ring tells you. A ring is left because the Moon's disc is not wide enough to cover the Sun's. So the Moon's apparent size is smaller than usual. Apparent size falls as distance grows, so the Moon must be near the far end of its elliptical orbit, at or close to apogee. The alignment itself is fine — a poor alignment would give a crescent, not a ring — so the only thing missing is size, and that comes from distance.
