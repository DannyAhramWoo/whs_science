Concept

Genes, DNA and Inheritance

DNA is a long molecule inside almost every cell that carries the instructions for building and running a living thing, and a copy of it is passed from parents to offspring.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Children resemble their parents. Eye colour, the shape of an earlobe, whether the tongue rolls — these travel down families, so something physical must carry that information between generations.

That something is DNA, short for deoxyribonucleic acid: an extremely long molecule sitting inside the nucleus, the control centre of the cell. It holds instructions written in a code of four chemical letters, shortened to A, T, C and G, and the order of those letters is the message, much as the order of letters makes a word.

Three words are stacked inside one another. A gene is a section of DNA carrying the instruction for one thing, such as a single protein. A chromosome is a whole length of DNA wound tightly around proteins so it packs into the nucleus. DNA is the material, a gene a section of it, a chromosome a bundle holding many genes. A human body cell holds 46 chromosomes in 23 pairs, paired because one of each came from the mother and one from the father.

2

How it works

DNA is shaped like a twisted ladder, a double helix: two strands winding around each other, joined across the middle by pairs of the chemical letters. A always pairs with T, C always with G. That fixed pairing means either strand can serve as a pattern to rebuild the other, so splitting the ladder down the middle leaves each half specifying its missing partner exactly. This is how a cell copies its DNA before dividing.

Now the distinction that matters most. Body cells, or somatic cells — skin, muscle, liver, white blood cells — carry all 46 chromosomes, the full 23 pairs. Egg and sperm cells carry only 23 chromosomes, one from each pair, not 23 pairs. When a sperm fertilises an egg, 23 plus 23 gives 46 again. If sex cells carried 46 each, every generation would double the number.

DNA does its job through proteins. A gene's instruction is first copied into a related molecule called RNA, a step named transcription — literally, copying out. That copy leaves the nucleus and is read by the cell's protein-building machinery, which links amino acids in the order the code specifies. So a gene does not directly make an eye brown; it makes a protein, and the protein makes the pigment.

Because every person's DNA sequence differs slightly, a person can be identified from it. In DNA fingerprinting a sample is cut into fragments spread out by size into a pattern of bands, like a barcode. Two samples from the same person match, and a child's bands should all be found in one parent or the other, which is how parentage is tested. Reading such a pattern turns up in past competition papers, including IMSO material. It follows that a forensic scientist needing a full profile must use a body cell, and among blood cells only white blood cells, since mature red blood cells have no nucleus and therefore no DNA.

Not everything about you is inherited. An inherited characteristic comes through DNA from parents, such as blood group. An acquired characteristic is gained during life — a scar, a learned language, muscles built by training — and is not written into DNA or passed on.

Genes can also be moved deliberately. A genetically modified organism, or GMO, is one whose DNA has been altered by adding or changing a gene — a crop given a gene resisting an insect pest, say. The molecule changed is always DNA, not protein, fat or carbohydrate, since DNA is where instructions are stored. Whether such crops should be grown widely is argued over, and reasonable people disagree.

The heart of it

Body cells carry 46 chromosomes in 23 pairs, while egg and sperm cells carry 23 singles, so that fertilisation restores the full 46 rather than doubling it.

3

Where the idea came from

In the 1860s Gregor Mendel, a monk at Brno in what is now the Czech Republic, bred pea plants and counted their offspring in thousands. Traits such as tall and short did not blend but reappeared in steady ratios, meaning inheritance came in separate units. His work was almost entirely ignored for around 35 years until botanists rediscovered it about 1900. He had the rules right without knowing what the units were made of.

In 1869 Friedrich Miescher extracted an unfamiliar substance from the nuclei of white blood cells and called it "nuclein". It was DNA. Almost nobody thought it important, since DNA seemed too plain a molecule to carry instructions, and most researchers backed proteins instead. That guess held for decades.

In 1953 James Watson and Francis Crick published the double-helix structure at Cambridge. Their key evidence was an X-ray photograph of DNA, Photo 51, taken in Rosalind Franklin's laboratory at King's College London and shown to them by a colleague without her knowledge or permission; her unpublished measurements reached them the same way. Franklin died of cancer in 1958, aged 37. The Nobel Prize went to Watson, Crick and Maurice Wilkins in 1962. It is not awarded after death, so she could not have shared it, but whether her contribution was fairly credited at the time is a separate question, and the usual answer now is that it was not.

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent slip is with the number 23. People learn "23" and "46" and attach them to the wrong cells, or say a sex cell has "23 pairs". A sex cell has 23 singles, one from each pair; 23 pairs would mean 46, the body-cell number.

The second is assuming any cell will do for a DNA test. Mature red blood cells have lost their nucleus in order to carry more oxygen, so contain no DNA at all; blood works as evidence because of the white cells in it. The third is thinking a gene directly produces a visible feature, when it produces a protein and the protein produces the feature.

Commonly missed

A scientist needs a complete set of a person's chromosomes. Which is suitable: a sperm cell, a red blood cell, or a white blood cell?

The trap is that all three are real human cells, and blood is the obvious source of evidence, so a reader picks a blood cell without asking which sort.

How to work it: Ask two questions of each cell: does it have a nucleus, and does it hold the full 46? A sperm cell has a nucleus but only 23 chromosomes, so the set is incomplete. A mature red blood cell has no nucleus, so no DNA at all. A white blood cell is a body cell with a nucleus, carrying all 46 in 23 pairs, so it is the answer.

5

Connected ideas

DNA sits in the nucleus, so how a cell is built and bounded matters here. Shared DNA is also what modern classification groups organisms by, and the same molecule is what viruses and bacteria carry.
