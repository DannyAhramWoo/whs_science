Chapter 16

Photosynthesis and Respiration

Plants make their own food using light, and every living thing releases energy from food. The biggest mistake in this whole topic is believing that plants photosynthesise by day and respire by night. They respire every hour of every day.

16.1

How a plant makes its own food

Animals eat. Plants cannot, so a plant has to build its own food out of raw materials. The process is called photosynthesis: photo means light, synthesis means putting together.

A plant needs two raw materials. Carbon dioxide comes from the air and enters the leaf through tiny holes called stomata on the underside. Water comes from the soil, taken in by the roots and carried up the stem to the leaves.

Neither of those carries energy. The energy comes from sunlight, caught by chlorophyll — a green substance packed inside tiny structures called chloroplasts. Chlorophyll absorbs the light and uses it to join the carbon dioxide and water together.

What comes out is glucose, a sugar, which is the plant's food. Any glucose the plant does not use straight away is converted into starch and stored. Oxygen comes out too, and the plant releases most of it into the air through the stomata.

Learn this sentence

Carbon dioxide + water, using light energy trapped by chlorophyll, makes glucose + oxygen. If you can say that without hesitating, half of this chapter is already secure.

16.2

Where it happens, and how to prove it

Photosynthesis happens only in the green parts of a plant, mainly the leaves. The reason is simple: green is the colour of chlorophyll. No chlorophyll means no way to capture light, so no photosynthesis. That is why roots, brown stems and white patches on variegated leaves make no food at all.

You cannot see glucose, so instead you test for the starch the leaf stored. Add a few drops of iodine solution. Iodine is orange-brown, but it turns blue-black wherever starch is present.

The classic experiment uses that test to prove light is needed. First the plant sits in the dark for a day or two until it has used up its stored starch — this is called destarching. Without that step the test proves nothing, because starch made last week would still be in the leaf. Then part of one leaf is covered with black card, the plant is left in sunlight, and the leaf is tested afterwards.

Commonly missed

A leaf is covered on one side with black card, left in sunlight for six hours, then tested with iodine. The whole leaf turns blue-black. What went wrong?

Students usually answer "the card fell off" or "light got through". Neither is the standard answer. The trap is that the experiment was set up wrongly from the start.

How to work it: the plant was not destarched. Starch made on previous days was still stored throughout the leaf, so iodine finds starch everywhere and the result tells you nothing. Any starch experiment must begin with 24–48 hours in the dark.

16.3

What changes the rate

A plant does not photosynthesise at a fixed speed. Three conditions change the rate — how much food it makes each hour.

Light intensity. Brighter light supplies more energy, so the rate climbs. It does not climb forever. Past a certain brightness the plant cannot use any more light and the rate levels off.

Carbon dioxide concentration. Carbon dioxide is a raw material, and there is very little of it in air — well under one part in a hundred. Supply more and the rate rises. This is why greenhouse growers sometimes pump carbon dioxide into the air.

Temperature. A warm plant works faster than a cold one, up to a point. Too hot and the leaf is damaged, so the rate falls sharply.

Commonly missed

Which change would increase the rate of photosynthesis in a plant: (a) increasing the oxygen in the air, or (b) increasing the carbon dioxide in the air?

Oxygen looks like a good answer because it sounds healthy and because students remember oxygen appearing somewhere in this topic. It does appear — on the wrong side of the arrow.

How to work it: before choosing, ask whether the substance goes in or comes out. Oxygen is a product. Supplying extra of a product does nothing, in the same way that adding more crumbs does not help you bake more bread. The answer is (b), carbon dioxide, because that is a raw material.

16.4

Respiration: releasing the energy again

Food is useless until the energy inside it is released, and the process that does that is respiration. Every living thing respires: humans, fish, worms, mushrooms, bacteria and plants.

Respiration takes in glucose and oxygen. It gives out carbon dioxide, water and energy. Your body uses that energy to move, grow, repair itself and stay warm. A plant uses it to grow and to move water and minerals around.

Put the two side by side and they are mirror images. Photosynthesis builds glucose and locks energy inside it; respiration breaks glucose apart and lets that energy out. The inputs of one are the outputs of the other.

Not the same as breathing

Respiration is a chemical reaction inside cells, not breathing. Breathing just moves air in and out of your lungs. A plant has no lungs and never breathes, yet it respires in every cell, day and night.

16.5

Day and night: what a plant is really doing

Here is the point that decides more marks than any other in this topic. A plant respires all the time, day and night, without a pause. Respiration never stops in a living thing, because energy is needed every second to stay alive.

What changes is photosynthesis, which needs light and so happens only in daylight. During the day both processes run at once, and photosynthesis normally runs much faster. The plant therefore makes more oxygen than it uses and uses more carbon dioxide than it makes, so from outside it looks as though it only takes in carbon dioxide and gives out oxygen.

At night photosynthesis shuts down and only respiration is left. Now the plant takes in oxygen and gives out carbon dioxide, exactly like an animal.

The sentence that must never appear in your answer

"Plants photosynthesise during the day and respire at night." This is wrong. Plants respire during the day as well; photosynthesis simply hides it, because the plant makes far more oxygen than it uses up. Say instead: respiration happens all the time, photosynthesis only in the light.

Commonly missed

A sealed jar contains a green plant and is left in bright light. Six hours later the oxygen level in the jar has risen. What does this show about respiration in the plant?

Almost everyone answers "the plant was not respiring, because oxygen went up". That is the trap. A rise in oxygen tells you which process was winning, not which processes were running.

How to work it: both processes ran. Photosynthesis produced oxygen faster than respiration used it, so the level rose. The correct answer is that respiration was still taking place, but photosynthesis was faster.

16.6

Measuring the rate with a pondweed

Rate is hard to measure on a land plant, because the oxygen escapes invisibly into the air. Underwater it is easy. Shine a lamp on a piece of pondweed in a beaker and oxygen comes off as a stream of bubbles you can count. More bubbles per minute means a faster rate. Move the lamp closer and they speed up; move it away and they slow down.

One detail catches people out. The water holds only a little dissolved carbon dioxide, and the pondweed soon uses it up — after which extra light makes no difference. So a spoonful of sodium hydrogencarbonate is dissolved in the water. It slowly releases carbon dioxide, keeping the supply topped up so that light is the only thing being varied.

Why the chemical is added

Sodium hydrogencarbonate supplies extra carbon dioxide. If a question asks why it is added, the answer is always the same: so that carbon dioxide does not become the factor limiting the rate, leaving light as the only variable being tested.

16.7

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

16.8

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A plant is kept in a dark cupboard for a week and dies. Explain why, using both photosynthesis and respiration in your answer.

Show answer

Without light the plant cannot photosynthesise, so it makes no new glucose. Its supply of food stops.

Respiration, however, carries on the whole time, because the plant still needs energy to stay alive. It keeps breaking down the starch it had stored.

Eventually the stored food runs out, there is no energy left to release, and the plant dies. Notice that respiration did not stop just because it was dark.

2

A leaf has green edges and a white centre. The whole leaf is exposed to sunlight and then tested with iodine. Predict the result and explain it.

Show answer

Only the green edges turn blue-black. The white centre stays orange-brown.

The green parts contain chlorophyll, so they could absorb light energy, photosynthesise and store starch. Iodine finds starch there and turns blue-black.

The white centre has no chlorophyll, so it could not capture light and made no glucose and no starch, even though light was falling on it. This shows chlorophyll is needed for photosynthesis.

3

A gardener seals a healthy plant inside a clear glass box and stands it on a sunny windowsill. Will the plant use up all the oxygen and suffocate? Explain.

Show answer

No. The plant does use oxygen, because it respires all day long, but in the light it also photosynthesises and photosynthesis produces oxygen faster than respiration uses it.

So the oxygen level inside the box rises rather than falls. The carbon dioxide level falls, and in fact that is what will eventually limit the plant.

At night the answer would be different: photosynthesis stops, only respiration remains, and the oxygen level would drop.

4

A student counts bubbles from pondweed with the lamp at 10 cm, 20 cm and 30 cm. The bubble rate stays the same at all three distances. Suggest what is wrong with the experiment.

Show answer

Something other than light must be holding the rate back. The most likely cause is that the water has run short of dissolved carbon dioxide.

If carbon dioxide is the factor limiting the rate, then changing the light makes no difference at all — the plant already has more light than it can use. Adding sodium hydrogencarbonate to the water would fix this.

A second possibility is that the water became too warm from the lamp, or too cold, so temperature was limiting instead.

16.9

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 16.7. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about a green plant at midnight are correct?

  • 1The plant has stopped respiring.
  • 2The plant is taking in oxygen from the air.
  • 3The plant is giving out carbon dioxide.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Respiration never stops in a living thing. It is photosynthesis that stops in the dark, not respiration. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C.

Checking anyway: at night only respiration is running, so the plant takes in oxygen and releases carbon dioxide. Statements 2 and 3 are both correct, which agrees with C.

2

Which of these would increase the rate of photosynthesis in a pondweed in a beaker?

  • 1Moving the lamp closer to the beaker.
  • 2Dissolving sodium hydrogencarbonate in the water.
  • 3Bubbling extra oxygen through the water.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. A closer lamp means brighter light and more energy for the plant to use. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Oxygen is a product of photosynthesis, not a raw material. Adding more of it changes nothing. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Sodium hydrogencarbonate releases carbon dioxide, which is a raw material, so the rate rises. The answer is B.

3

Which statements about photosynthesis and respiration are correct?

  • 1Photosynthesis stores energy in food, while respiration releases energy from food.
  • 2Photosynthesis happens only in cells containing chlorophyll.
  • 3Both processes need light in order to happen.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. That is exactly the difference between them: one builds glucose and locks energy in, the other breaks glucose down and lets energy out. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Only photosynthesis needs light. Respiration runs in the dark, in roots underground and inside animals. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Chlorophyll is what absorbs the light, so a cell without it cannot photosynthesise. The answer is B.
