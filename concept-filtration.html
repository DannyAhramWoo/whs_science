Concept

Filtration

Filtration separates an insoluble solid from a liquid by passing the mixture through a material with holes too small for the solid.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Pour muddy water through a folded coffee filter and clear water drips out while a brown sludge stays behind in the paper. That is filtration, so familiar in everyday life that the science of it often goes unexamined.

Filtration separates a solid from a liquid, but only a solid that is insoluble — one that has not dissolved. Sand in water is insoluble: the grains sit there as grains, and left alone they settle to the bottom. Salt in water has dissolved, its particles spread out evenly among the water particles with no lumps left anywhere. That distinction decides everything about whether filtration will help you.

Two words describe the result. The residue is what stays behind in the filter paper. The filtrate is the liquid that passed through. In muddy water, the mud is the residue and the clear water is the filtrate.

2

How it works

Filter paper looks solid but is a dense mat of tangled fibres with countless tiny gaps between them. Filtration works because those gaps sit at a useful size: much bigger than the particles of the liquid, so it trickles through with no difficulty, but much smaller than the grains of undissolved solid, which cannot follow. It is a sieve with holes far too small to see.

Apply the same reasoning to dissolved salt and you see why the answer changes. When salt dissolves it does not become invisible lumps; its particles come apart and mingle among the water particles, so they are of the same order of smallness as the water itself. A hole that lets water through must let them through too, and nothing about the paper can pick out one and not the other. This is why filtration never separates a dissolved substance from its liquid, and no amount of finer paper or slower pouring will change it.

A useful check is to ask what you would see in the beaker. If the mixture is cloudy or settles into layers when left, there is undissolved solid and filtration will catch it. If it is clear all the way through — even if coloured — everything is dissolved and the filter will catch nothing.

Filtration is everywhere once you look. Waterworks pass water through beds of sand and gravel to trap grit. A coffee filter holds back the grounds, while a vacuum cleaner filters the other way round, catching dust and letting air pass. Your body does it too: the kidneys act as natural filters, holding useful large things back in the blood while letting water and small wastes through.

The heart of it

Filtration catches what has not dissolved, because the holes are bigger than the liquid particles and smaller than the solid lumps.

3

Where the idea came from

Nobody discovered filtration. It is one of those techniques so useful and so obvious in outline that many peoples arrived at it separately. Ancient Egyptian and Greek writers describe straining wine and drinking water through cloth, and letting muddy water stand so the silt sinks before the clear water is poured off. These were the standard answers to dirty water for thousands of years, long before anyone could say why they worked.

Refining these methods was part of the everyday craft of alchemy, where straining, settling and distilling were routine. The Alexandrian alchemist Maria the Jewess, also known as Mary the Prophetess, is credited with several pieces of apparatus, including the gentle water bath still called the bain-marie. Her dates are genuinely uncertain and are usually given as somewhere between the first and third century AD; we cannot be sure, and her own writings do not survive.

What was missing was the explanation. Straining removed the visible dirt, so the sensible conclusion was that it removed dirt in general — and yet salty water stayed stubbornly salty however carefully it was strained. That puzzle only resolves once you accept that dissolved substances have broken up into particles as small as those of the liquid. Filtration then stops looking like a general cleaning process and starts looking like what it is: a size filter, and nothing more.

4

What people get wrong

By far the commonest mistake is expecting filtration to remove a dissolved substance. It is an easy slip, because "filter" in everyday speech suggests purifying. But a filter can only catch what is too big to pass, and dissolved particles are not too big.

A related slip is thinking clear means pure. Clear only means nothing suspended in it is large enough to scatter light. A clear liquid may have a great deal dissolved in it, which is exactly when filtration is useless and evaporation is the method you want.

Commonly missed

Some salt is stirred into water until it all disappears. The mixture is then poured through filter paper. What is left in the filter paper, and what is in the filtrate?

It is a trap because a solid went into the beaker, and it feels as though a solid must therefore come out somewhere.

How to work it: Ask first whether the solid dissolved. It did — it disappeared and the liquid is clear, so there are no lumps of salt anywhere. Filtration catches only undissolved solid, and since the salt particles are now spread out among the water particles they pass through the holes just as easily as the water does. So the filter paper holds nothing, and the filtrate is salty water, exactly the mixture that went in. To recover the salt you would evaporate the water instead.

5

Connected ideas

Filtration is one method among several for separating mixtures, and it only makes sense alongside the others. Knowing what a solution is explains why dissolved substances cannot be filtered out, and evaporation is the method that handles them. The particle model supplies the underlying reason the holes work as they do, and the kidney is the best example of the same principle at work in a living body.
