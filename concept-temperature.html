Concept

Temperature

Temperature is a measure of how fast the particles in a substance are moving on average.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Everyone knows what temperature is until they are asked to define it. "How hot something is" is true but circular. The useful definition is about particles: temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance — kinetic energy being the energy something has because it is moving.

Two words in that definition are doing careful work. Average matters because the particles do not all move at the same speed. In any cup of water some particles are dawdling and a few are moving very fast indeed, and temperature reports the middle of that spread, not any individual. Kinetic matters because temperature is about motion specifically, not about how much energy the object holds in total.

That second point is the crucial difference from heat. A bathtub of lukewarm water and a cup of the same water are at the same temperature — their particles move at the same average speed — but the bath contains vastly more thermal energy, because it has vastly more particles. Temperature does not depend on how much stuff there is. Total thermal energy does.

Temperature is measured with a thermometer, in degrees Celsius (°C) for everyday use, where 0 °C is the freezing point of pure water and 100 °C its boiling point at normal air pressure.

2

How it works

A traditional liquid thermometer works by thermal expansion. The liquid inside — alcohol with dye, or historically mercury — is in a bulb connected to a very narrow tube. When the liquid warms, its particles move faster and push each other slightly further apart, so the liquid takes up more room and is forced up the tube. The tube is narrow on purpose: a small change in volume becomes a long, easily read movement up the scale.

Notice what a thermometer actually does. It does not sense the temperature of the object from a distance. It comes into contact, exchanges heat with the object until the two stop changing, and then reports its own temperature. This is why you must leave a thermometer in place long enough, and why a large thermometer in a small sample can change the very thing it is measuring.

There is a floor to temperature, which is a striking thought. Since temperature measures particle motion, there must be a point at which motion is as small as it can possibly be, and you cannot go lower. That point is absolute zero, at about −273 °C. It has never been reached, only approached to within a whisker in laboratories, and it is genuinely unreachable rather than merely difficult. This is why scientists often use the kelvin scale, which starts at absolute zero: 0 K is −273 °C, and each kelvin is the same size as a degree Celsius, so water freezes at 273 K. On that scale there are no negative temperatures, and doubling the temperature really does mean doubling the average particle energy.

There is no ceiling. Nothing prevents particles moving faster and faster, so temperature has a bottom but no top — an asymmetry that surprises most people the first time they meet it.

The heart of it

Temperature reports the average speed of particles, so it does not care how many particles there are.

3

Where the idea came from

Measuring temperature came long before understanding it. Around 1600 Galileo Galilei and several contemporaries built thermoscopes — glass bulbs with a tube standing in liquid, in which the liquid rose or fell as the air in the bulb cooled or warmed. These showed changes but had no agreed scale, so one person's reading meant nothing to another. Worse, they were affected by air pressure as well as temperature, which nobody yet knew.

Sealed liquid-in-glass thermometers came later in the 1600s, and the scales followed. Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit, a German instrument maker in Amsterdam, produced reliable mercury thermometers around 1714 and a scale based on a salt-ice mixture and, approximately, human body temperature. Anders Celsius, a Swedish astronomer, proposed his hundred-point scale in 1742 — and here is an oddity worth knowing: he originally put 0 at the boiling point of water and 100 at freezing, so his scale ran backwards to ours. It was inverted after his death, and the version we use is not quite the one he published.

The connection between temperature and particle motion arrived much later, through the kinetic theory of gases developed by figures including Rudolf Clausius, James Clerk Maxwell and Ludwig Boltzmann in the second half of the 1800s. Maxwell's work in the 1860s showed that particles in a gas have a whole spread of speeds rather than a single one, which is precisely why temperature had to be defined as an average. Boltzmann's insistence that matter was made of atoms at all was fiercely opposed by respected scientists during his lifetime; atoms were not universally accepted until the early 1900s, after his death in 1906.

4

What people get wrong

The main confusion is with heat, and it comes from everyday speech, where "heat" and "temperature" are used interchangeably. In science they are different: temperature is an average per particle, heat is a total amount of energy transferred. Adding heat usually raises temperature, but not always — ice absorbs a great deal of heat while melting and stays at 0 °C throughout.

A second confusion comes from touch. Metal at room temperature feels colder than wood at the same temperature, and it is tempting to conclude it really is colder. It is not. Metal conducts heat away from your skin much faster, so your hand cools quicker and reports "cold". Your skin measures the rate of heat loss, not temperature, which makes it an unreliable thermometer.

Commonly missed

A beaker holds 100 g of water at 80 °C. It is poured into a second beaker already holding 100 g of water at 80 °C. What is the temperature of the combined 200 g of water?

The trap is the habit of adding quantities in a question. Two lots of 80 invites 160 °C, or, from those who suspect a trick, an averaging manoeuvre that lands on 40 °C.

How to work it: Ask what temperature measures — the average particle speed. Every particle in both beakers already has the same average energy, and pouring them together does not make any particle move faster. You now have twice as many particles moving at the same average speed, so twice the thermal energy but the same average. The answer is 80 °C. Thermal energy adds; temperature does not.

5

Connected ideas

Temperature and heat are the pair this chapter exists to separate, and a difference in temperature is what makes heat flow at all. When two objects reach the same temperature they are in thermal equilibrium and the flow stops. Temperature is really a statement about the particle model of matter, and raising it far enough produces a change of state.

In one line

Temperature is the average kinetic energy of a substance's particles, which is why two identical cups of hot water poured together are no hotter than one.
