Concept

Evaporation and Boiling

Evaporation and boiling both turn a liquid into a gas, but evaporation happens only at the surface and at any temperature, while boiling happens throughout the liquid and only at one particular temperature.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

A puddle dries up on a cool day without ever coming close to boiling. Washing on a line dries in the wind. Neither of these is boiling — nothing is bubbling and the temperature is nowhere near 100 °C. This is evaporation: liquid turning to gas at the surface, at any temperature at all.

Boiling is different in several definite ways. It happens only at the boiling point — 100 °C for water at normal atmospheric pressure. It happens throughout the liquid, not just at the surface, which is what the bubbles are: pockets of vapour forming down in the body of the liquid and rising. And it needs a continuous supply of heat, whereas evaporation does not.

Both produce the same thing. The gas coming off a boiling kettle and the gas leaving a drying puddle are both water vapour, made of exactly the same particles. Only the manner of leaving differs.

One point of language: the visible white cloud above a kettle is not steam. True steam is an invisible gas. The cloud is water vapour that has already cooled and condensed into tiny liquid droplets — so what you can see is the vapour turning back into liquid, not the vapour itself.

2

How it works

The particles in a liquid do not all move at the same speed. Some are slow, some are fast, and most are somewhere in between. Only a particle at the surface, moving fast enough and in the right direction, can break free of its neighbours' attraction and escape into the air. That is evaporation, and you can see immediately why it works at any temperature: at any temperature above absolute zero there are always some particles fast enough, just far fewer when it is cold.

This picture predicts everything about the rate. Raise the temperature and a larger proportion of particles are fast enough, so evaporation speeds up. Increase the surface area — spread the puddle out, hang the washing wide — and more particles are at the surface where escape is possible, so it speeds up. Blow air across it and escaped particles are carried away before they can drift back in, so it speeds up. Raise the humidity and the air is already crowded with water vapour, so more particles return and the net rate falls, which is why washing dries badly on a damp day.

Now for a consequence that is easy to miss. The particles that escape are the fastest ones. Removing the fastest particles lowers the average speed of those left behind — and average particle speed is what temperature measures. So evaporation cools the liquid that is left. This is not a side effect; it follows necessarily. It is why sweating cools you, why you feel cold stepping out of a swimming pool, and why a wet cloth around a bottle keeps the contents cool in hot weather.

Boiling is a different mechanism. Above the liquid there is air pressing down. A bubble of vapour can only form and survive inside the liquid if the pressure of the vapour inside it is enough to push back against the atmosphere. As you heat the liquid its vapour pressure rises, and at the boiling point it finally equals atmospheric pressure. Bubbles can then form anywhere in the liquid, not just at the surface, and they rise and burst.

This immediately explains something otherwise baffling: the boiling point depends on air pressure. High on a mountain the air presses less hard, so water boils at a lower temperature — around 90 °C at 3,000 m. Food cooks more slowly there, because the water it is cooking in is cooler, not because there is anything wrong with the heat. A pressure cooker does the reverse, raising the pressure so water boils at perhaps 120 °C and food cooks faster.

The heart of it

Evaporation is fast particles escaping from the surface at any temperature, which cools what remains; boiling is vapour bubbles forming throughout the liquid, which happens only when its vapour pressure matches the pressure of the air above.

3

Where the idea came from

Evaporation has been used for as long as anyone has made salt from sea water, so the effect was never in doubt. What needed discovering was the energy involved, and that came from Joseph Black in Glasgow in the 1760s.

Black had already found that melting ice absorbs a great deal of heat without any rise in temperature. He turned to boiling and found the same thing on a larger scale: once water reaches 100 °C, the temperature refuses to go higher however long you heat it, and a very large amount of heat is consumed in turning it to vapour. He called this hidden energy latent heat, and he measured it.

The result was genuinely surprising to his contemporaries, because heat and temperature were not clearly separated in most people's minds. It seemed obvious that heat added must show as temperature. Black demonstrated that during a change of state it does not.

Two honest caveats. Black explained his findings using the caloric theory, in which heat was an invisible fluid — a theory later abandoned, though his measurements survived it unchanged. And the modern explanation of evaporative cooling in terms of the fastest particles escaping was not Black's; it belongs to the kinetic theory developed in the following century, by Maxwell, Boltzmann and others. Black established that the energy was there and how much of it there was, without knowing what heat actually is.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating evaporation as boiling that has not got going yet, or thinking a liquid must be heated to evaporate. Puddles dry at 5 °C. Evaporation needs no heating at all; heating only makes it faster.

The second is misreading the bubbles that appear in a pan well before boiling. Those are dissolved air coming out of solution as the water warms, not water vapour. Boiling bubbles form only at the boiling point and are made of vapour.

The third is calling the visible cloud from a kettle steam, when it is actually condensed droplets. Look closely and there is a short clear gap just above the spout: that gap is the real steam.

Commonly missed

Two identical thermometers are placed side by side in a warm room. One bulb is wrapped in a piece of wet cloth. Both are left for ten minutes. Which reads the lower temperature, and why?

The trap is expecting both to settle at room temperature, since both are in the same room and nothing is being cooled by any obvious means.

How to work it: The one with the wet cloth reads lower. Water is evaporating from the cloth, and only the fastest-moving particles have enough energy to escape from the surface. Once those leave, the average energy of the particles remaining in the cloth is lower — and temperature is exactly a measure of that average. So the cloth, and therefore the bulb inside it, is cooled below room temperature. This continues as long as evaporation continues, and it is the principle behind sweating. It also gets more pronounced in dry or breezy conditions, because escaped particles are carried away rather than returning.

5

Connected ideas

Both processes are changes of state, and both need the particle model to explain why some particles can escape and others cannot. Evaporative cooling links directly to heat and temperature, since it works by removing the fastest particles and so lowering the average. Evaporation is also the step that lifts water into the sky in the water cycle, and it is the standard way of recovering a dissolved solid from a solution.
