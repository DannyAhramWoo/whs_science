Concept

Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of every object to keep doing exactly what it is already doing, whether that is staying still or moving steadily in a straight line.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

You have felt inertia many times without naming it. A bus pulls away and you sway backwards. It brakes and you pitch forwards. Neither time did anything push you — the bus moved and you, briefly, did not.

That is inertia. Objects resist having their motion changed. The word comes from Latin and originally meant something close to laziness or inactivity, which is a fair description: an object left alone will not take up any new state of motion on its own initiative.

The important half of the definition is the half people drop. Inertia is not only about staying still. A stationary object resists being started, and a moving object resists being stopped, slowed, sped up or turned. Both are the same reluctance, and they are equally strong. A trolley that is hard to get going is exactly as hard to bring to a halt once it is rolling.

Inertia is not a force. Nothing pushes you back into your seat when a car accelerates. What actually happens is that the seat pushes you forward, and your body's inertia means it takes a moment to respond. Calling inertia a force is one of the quickest ways to lose a mark on this topic.

2

How it works

How much inertia does something have? The answer is beautifully simple: exactly as much as its mass. Mass is the measure of inertia. A 10 kg object is ten times harder to start, stop or turn than a 1 kg object, given the same force. That is the whole rule.

This gives mass a second meaning worth holding on to. You probably met mass as "how much matter there is in an object". Equally true, and often more useful, is "how strongly the object resists a change in its motion". The two definitions turn out to describe the same quantity, and that is not obvious — it is a genuine discovery about the universe rather than a matter of wording.

Watch it decide real outcomes. Two shopping trolleys, one empty and one full of tins, given the same firm push. The empty one shoots away; the loaded one barely shifts. Same force, different mass, different result. Now push both to the same speed and try to stop them with your hands: the loaded one hurts. It resists stopping just as stubbornly as it resisted starting.

Inertia also explains why a car crash is dangerous in a way that a car journey is not. Travelling at 60 km per hour does no harm at all — you and the car are moving together and nothing is changing. The danger is entirely in the change. In a collision the car stops in a fraction of a second, but your body's inertia carries it forward at the original speed until something stops it. A seat belt exists to be that something, spreading the stopping force across your chest and hips over a slightly longer time instead of letting the windscreen do it all at once. Headrests exist for the mirror-image reason: in a rear-end collision the seat pushes your body forward and your head, left to its own inertia, would stay behind.

There is a lovely everyday demonstration in a party trick. Pull a tablecloth out from under a set of crockery very fast and the crockery stays. The plates have inertia, so they need a force acting for a reasonable length of time to get moving. Yank the cloth quickly and friction only acts on them for an instant — not long enough to change their motion much. Pull slowly and the same friction has time to work, and everything goes over the edge.

The heart of it

Inertia is not a force and not a push — it is the plain fact that motion does not change on its own, and mass is the measure of how strongly an object insists on that.

3

Where the idea came from

Inertia had to be discovered, and it took over two thousand years, because everyday life hides it almost perfectly.

Aristotle taught that a moving object needs a continuous push to keep moving, and that once the push stops the motion stops. That view stood essentially unchallenged for centuries. Given what anyone could observe, it was a reasonable conclusion. On the ground, things really do stop. Aristotle was not being foolish; he was generalising honestly from a world absolutely saturated with friction, where the true behaviour of an undisturbed object never shows itself.

Galileo Galilei found the way round the problem in the early 1600s, not by arguing better but by reducing friction and watching the trend. Rolling balls down one smooth slope and up another, he saw a ball climb back to nearly the height it had started from, and get nearer to that height the smoother he made the surfaces. If he lowered the second slope, the ball simply travelled further to reach the same height. Extending the pattern, a ball on a perfectly smooth level surface would have to travel for ever. The idea that motion decays was revealed as an artefact of friction, not a law of nature.

Isaac Newton made it the opening statement of his Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica in 1687, where it became what we now call the first law of motion: an object stays at rest or moves at constant speed in a straight line unless a force acts on it. Note what a strange law it is. It describes what happens when nothing happens — which is exactly the case nobody had thought needed explaining.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is treating inertia as a force that pushes you around in vehicles. It is not. In a braking car nothing pushes you forwards; you were already going forwards and the car left you behind. Write "the passenger continues moving forward because of inertia", never "inertia pushes the passenger forward".

The second is thinking inertia only applies to stationary things, so that a moving object somehow has less of it. It has exactly the same. Objects resist stopping precisely as much as they resist starting.

The third is confusing inertia with weight. Take a heavy crate to the Moon and its weight falls to about a sixth, so lifting it becomes easy. Its inertia does not change at all, because its mass has not changed. Pushing it sideways across a smooth floor would be just as hard on the Moon as on Earth.

Commonly missed

A ball rests on the flat floor of a bus that is travelling at a steady speed. The driver suddenly brakes. Which way does the ball roll, and why?

The trap is that the ball starts moving forwards even though nothing pushed it forwards, which feels as though it needs a force to explain it. Many people answer that braking throws the ball forward.

How to work it: before the brakes, the ball and the bus were both moving forwards at the same steady speed. Braking is a force applied to the bus, not to the ball. The ball has inertia, so it keeps moving forwards at its original speed while the bus slows beneath it. Relative to the bus, the ball therefore rolls forwards. Nothing pushed it — the floor simply stopped keeping up with it.

5

Connected ideas

Inertia is the reason force is defined the way it is: a force is needed to overcome inertia and change motion. Mass is the direct measure of how much inertia an object has, which is why the two ideas cannot really be learned apart. Balanced forces make sense only once inertia is understood, since it explains why zero net force leaves motion unchanged instead of letting it fade. Friction is what disguises inertia in daily life, and weight is the force that people most often confuse it with.
