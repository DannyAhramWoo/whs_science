Concept

Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of every object to keep doing exactly what it is already doing, whether that is staying still or moving steadily in a straight line.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

You have felt inertia without naming it. A bus pulls away and you sway backwards. It brakes and you pitch forwards. Neither time did anything push you — the bus moved and you, briefly, did not.

That is inertia. Objects resist having their motion changed. The word comes from Latin and meant something close to laziness, which is fair: an object left alone takes up no new motion on its own.

The important half of the definition is the half people drop. Inertia is not only about staying still. A stationary object resists being started, and a moving object resists being stopped, sped up or turned, equally strongly. A trolley that is hard to get going is exactly as hard to halt once rolling.

Inertia is not a force. Nothing pushes you back into your seat when a car accelerates; the seat pushes you forward, and your body's inertia means it takes a moment to respond. Calling inertia a force is one of the quickest ways to lose a mark on this topic.

2

How it works

How much inertia does something have? Exactly as much as its mass. Mass is the measure of inertia: a 10 kg object is ten times harder to start, stop or turn than a 1 kg one, given the same force. That is the whole rule.

This gives mass a second meaning. You probably met mass as "how much matter there is in an object". Equally true, and often more useful, is "how strongly it resists a change in its motion". That these describe the same quantity is a genuine discovery about the universe, not a matter of wording.

Give two shopping trolleys, one empty and one full of tins, the same firm push: the empty one shoots away, the loaded one barely shifts. Now push both to the same speed and stop them by hand — the loaded one hurts, resisting stopping as stubbornly as it resisted starting.

Inertia also explains why a car crash is dangerous in a way that a car journey is not. Travelling at 60 km per hour does no harm — you and the car move together and nothing changes. The danger is entirely in the change. In a collision the car stops in a fraction of a second, but your body's inertia carries it forward at the original speed until something stops it. A seat belt is that something, spreading the stopping force over a longer time instead of letting the windscreen do it all at once.

The heart of it

Inertia is not a force and not a push — it is the plain fact that motion does not change on its own, and mass is the measure of how strongly an object insists on that.

3

Where the idea came from

Inertia took over two thousand years to discover, because everyday life hides it almost perfectly.

Aristotle taught that a moving object needs a continuous push to keep moving, and that once the push stops the motion stops. Given what anyone could observe, that was reasonable: on the ground, things really do stop. He was generalising honestly from a world saturated with friction, where the true behaviour of an undisturbed object never shows itself.

Galileo Galilei found the way round the problem in the early 1600s, not by arguing better but by reducing friction and watching the trend. Rolling balls down one smooth slope and up another, he saw a ball climb back to nearly its starting height, and nearer to it the smoother the surfaces. Lowering the second slope, the ball travelled further to reach that height. Extending the pattern, a ball on a perfectly smooth level surface would travel for ever. Motion decaying was an artefact of friction, not a law of nature.

Isaac Newton made it the opening statement of his Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica in 1687, where it became the first law of motion: an object stays at rest or moves at constant speed in a straight line unless a force acts on it. Note what a strange law it is. It describes what happens when nothing happens — exactly the case nobody had thought needed explaining.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is treating inertia as a force that pushes you around in vehicles. In a braking car nothing pushes you forwards; you were already going forwards and the car left you behind. Write "the passenger continues moving forward because of inertia", never "inertia pushes the passenger".

The second is confusing inertia with weight. Take a heavy crate to the Moon and its weight falls to about a sixth, so lifting it becomes easy. Its inertia does not change, because its mass has not changed. Pushing it sideways across a smooth floor would be just as hard on the Moon as on Earth.

Commonly missed

A ball rests on the flat floor of a bus that is travelling at a steady speed. The driver suddenly brakes. Which way does the ball roll, and why?

The trap is that the ball starts moving forwards even though nothing pushed it, which feels as though it needs a force to explain it. Many people answer that braking throws the ball forward.

How to work it: before the brakes, ball and bus were both moving forwards at the same steady speed. Braking is a force applied to the bus, not to the ball. The ball has inertia, so it keeps moving forwards at its original speed while the bus slows beneath it. Relative to the bus, it therefore rolls forwards. Nothing pushed it — the floor simply stopped keeping up with it.

Connected ideas

Inertia is why force is defined as it is: a force is needed to overcome inertia and change motion. Mass is the direct measure of how much inertia an object has, which is why the two cannot be learned apart. Balanced forces make sense only once inertia is understood, since it explains why zero net force leaves motion unchanged instead of letting it fade. Friction disguises inertia in daily life, and weight is the force people most often confuse it with.
