Concept

Digestion

Digestion is the breaking down of food into pieces small enough to pass through the gut wall into the blood.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

When you swallow a mouthful of bread, that bread is not yet part of you. It sits in a tube running through your body, and the inside of that tube is still the outside world. Nothing has entered you until it crosses the wall into your blood.

The problem is size. A starch molecule is a chain of hundreds of sugar units joined end to end, far too big to slip through the lining of the gut. The same is true of protein and fat. So the food must be taken apart, link by link, until the pieces fit through.

Two kinds of taking-apart happen. Physical digestion is the cutting, crushing and churning done by teeth and the stomach. It makes the lumps smaller but does not change what they are made of — chewed bread is still bread. Chemical digestion breaks the chemical joins inside the molecules, and this is what changes starch into sugar. Both matter, and they help each other.

2

How it works

Chemical digestion is done by enzymes. An enzyme is a substance made by the body that speeds up one chemical change without being used up. Each handles one kind of food: amylase in saliva works on starch, protease on protein, lipase on fat. No single enzyme dissolves everything, which is why the body must produce several.

Enzymes can only work on a surface they touch. Imagine a cube of bread: the enzyme reaches the outside, but the middle is untouched until the outside is stripped away. Cut that cube into eight and you create new surfaces where none existed, doubling the area the enzyme can attack without adding any bread. Chewing does this many times over, so it does not merely ease swallowing — it makes chemical digestion faster.

Each stage has conditions that suit its enzymes. In the mouth, saliva begins on starch. In the stomach, glands release hydrochloric acid, which kills many bacteria in the food and is the condition in which the stomach's protein-digesting enzyme works best. Move that enzyme somewhere non-acidic and it stops working, so the acid is not simply a defence but part of the machinery.

The mixture then passes into the small intestine, where fluid from the pancreas and bile from the liver arrive. Bile is not an enzyme and breaks no chemical joins; it breaks large fat drops into tiny droplets, the same surface-area trick, so lipase has far more surface to work on. Most digestion and nearly all absorption happen here, in a tube several metres long lined with millions of finger-like projections.

What is left reaches the large intestine, where water is reclaimed and the remainder compacted. Undigested fibre passes out, but its bulk was useful, giving the gut muscles something to push against.

The heart of it

Food is not part of you until it is small enough to cross the gut wall, and almost every trick the digestive system uses creates more surface for enzymes to work on.

3

Where the idea came from

For a long time nobody could say what happened inside a living stomach, because there was no way to look into one. Some argued the stomach ground food up mechanically; others thought it rotted or fermented. Neither side could settle it, because neither could watch.

Then, in the 1820s, an army surgeon in North America named William Beaumont gained a troubling opportunity. Alexis St Martin had survived a gunshot wound to the abdomen, which healed leaving a permanent opening into his stomach. Beaumont could see in. Over years he lowered food in on a string, drew it out at intervals to see how far it had broken down, and sampled the fluid. This showed digestion in the stomach was chemical, done by a fluid, not a grinding.

It matters to say plainly that St Martin had little choice. He was poor, dependent on Beaumont, and for periods worked as his servant while the experiments continued. He is reported to have wanted them stopped and to have left more than once. By any modern standard the ethics were poor: a person cannot properly agree when refusing would cost him his living. The knowledge was real and useful. That does not settle whether it should have been obtained that way, and honest science includes admitting that.

Beaumont did not have the full picture either. He knew a fluid was doing the work and that it was acidic, but the understanding of enzymes as specific substances, each acting on one food, came later and from many people rather than one.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest confusion is between physical and chemical digestion. Chewing, churning and bile are physical: they change the size of the pieces, not the substances. Only enzymes change one substance into another. If asked what turns starch into sugar, the answer is never chewing, however much it helps.

The second is thinking the stomach does most of the work. Its job matters, but the small intestine is where most digestion is completed and nearly all absorption happens.

Commonly missed

A pupil says that bile digests fat. Explain why this statement is not correct, and say what bile actually does.

The trap is that bile plainly has something to do with fat, so the statement feels right. It is counted as an enzyme when it is not one.

How to work it: Digesting means breaking chemical joins, and only enzymes do that. Bile breaks large fat drops into small droplets, a physical change — the fat is still fat. That increases the surface exposed, so lipase works on far more at once and the fat is digested faster. Bile helps digestion; it does not perform it.

5

Connected ideas

Digestion supplies fuel that is only released when it meets oxygen inside cells during respiration. The absorbed food is carried away by circulation, which is why the gut is wrapped in blood vessels. The starch you digest was built by plants during photosynthesis, so almost every meal traces back to sunlight.
