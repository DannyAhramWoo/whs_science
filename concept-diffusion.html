Concept

Diffusion

Diffusion is the spreading out of particles from where they are crowded to where they are sparse, caused by nothing more than their own random movement.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Someone opens a bottle of perfume at the far end of a still room, and after a while you can smell it. Nobody fanned it towards you. Drop a crystal of purple dye into a glass of water and leave it completely undisturbed: hours later the whole glass is evenly coloured. In both cases the substance has moved from where it was concentrated to where it was not, without being pushed.

Diffusion is the name for this. Two words are worth defining properly. Concentration means how many particles there are in a given space. A concentration gradient is a difference in concentration between one place and another. The rule is that diffusion moves particles down the gradient — from high concentration to low — until the concentration is even everywhere.

Diffusion happens in gases and in liquids. It does not happen in solids to any noticeable degree, because solid particles cannot travel past their neighbours. It is fastest in gases, where the particles are free and fast, and much slower in liquids, where every particle is hemmed in by others.

2

How it works

The most important thing to understand is that no particle is trying to spread out. Particles have no sense of where it is crowded. Each one simply moves in a straight line until it collides with something, bounces off in a new direction, and carries on. The direction of every individual journey is random.

So why does the crowd move in a definite direction? Because of counting. Picture an imaginary line drawn through the room, with a lot of perfume particles on the left and almost none on the right. Each particle near the line has an equal chance of drifting left or right. But there are many more particles on the left, so many more cross rightwards than cross leftwards. There is a net flow to the right, even though nothing individually intends to go right. Once the concentration is even, particles are still crossing the line constantly in both directions — but now the numbers match, so nothing appears to change. Diffusion does not stop at that point; it merely becomes invisible.

This is why diffusion is one-way in practice but not by any rule forbidding the reverse. Evenly spread perfume could in principle all wander back into the bottle. It is simply overwhelmingly unlikely with that many particles, in the same way that a hundred tossed coins could in principle all come up heads.

Two factors change the rate. Temperature is one: heating gives particles more energy, so they move faster, collide more and spread more quickly. This is why a smell fills a warm room faster than a cold one, and why sugar dissolves and spreads through hot tea far faster than iced water.

The other is the mass of the particle. At a given temperature, lighter particles move faster on average than heavier ones, because it takes less energy of motion to get a light particle up to speed. So a gas made of light particles diffuses faster. Hydrogen, with the lightest particles of any gas, diffuses noticeably faster than carbon dioxide, whose particles are about twenty-two times heavier.

Diffusion is not merely a laboratory curiosity. It is how oxygen crosses from the air in your lungs into your blood, and how carbon dioxide crosses the other way — no pump is involved at that final step, only a concentration gradient. It is how carbon dioxide gets into a leaf for photosynthesis. Life depends on it working reliably.

The heart of it

Particles moving completely at random produce an orderly one-way spread, simply because more particles leave a crowded region than enter it.

3

Where the idea came from

Thomas Graham, a Scottish chemist working in Glasgow and later London, studied the movement of gases through the 1830s and set the subject on a quantitative footing. Rather than simply noting that gases mix, he measured how fast different gases passed through a porous barrier — a plug of plaster, or a fine crack — and compared the rates.

What he found is that the gases did not all pass at the same rate, and that the differences followed a pattern related to the density of the gas: lighter gases got through faster, by a definite mathematical relationship rather than a vague tendency. This result is still known as Graham's law. It was important beyond gases themselves, because it fitted neatly with the emerging picture of gases as swarms of moving particles, and gave that picture something numerical to be tested against. Graham went on to study the diffusion of dissolved substances in liquids as well, work that laid foundations for the chemistry of colloids.

It is worth being clear about the limits of what he did. Graham did not discover that gases mix, which had been known for a long time. He also did not, by himself, prove the particle theory. What he supplied was careful measurement where there had previously been only observation — and measurement is what lets you tell a good theory from a plausible-sounding one. His results made sense on a particle picture, and rather less sense without it.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest misunderstanding is describing diffusion as though particles want to spread out, or as though there is a force pushing them from crowded regions. There is no such force and no such intention. The apparent direction is a statistical result of random motion, and saying so is not a quibble — it is the whole explanation.

The second is thinking diffusion stops once mixing is complete. The particles carry on moving exactly as before. What stops is the net change, because the flow each way is now equal.

The third is confusing diffusion with convection or with stirring. If you stir the dye, or if warm currents carry the perfume, the substance spreads much faster — but that is bulk movement of the fluid, not diffusion. True diffusion is what happens with no currents at all, and it is comparatively slow.

Commonly missed

A drop of food colouring is placed in a beaker of cold water and a second, identical drop in a beaker of hot water. Neither is stirred. In which does the colour spread faster, and why?

The trap is answering correctly but explaining it as "hot water dissolves things better", which is about solubility, not about rate of spreading.

How to work it: The colour spreads faster in the hot water. The particles in hot water have more energy, so both the water particles and the colouring particles move faster. Faster random movement means each particle covers more ground in a given time, so the colouring particles wander away from the crowded drop more quickly and the concentration evens out sooner. The final result is the same in both beakers — evenly coloured water — it is only the time taken that differs.

5

Connected ideas

Diffusion is the particle model made visible: it is direct evidence that particles move on their own. It explains how a solute spreads evenly once dissolved, and it depends on state, since it is fast in gases, slow in liquids and effectively absent in solids. Temperature controls its rate, because temperature is a measure of particle speed. And it is the mechanism by which gases cross into and out of the blood in breathing.
