Chapter 10

Earth, Moon and Sun

Three objects, two movements and two kinds of shadow. Nearly every mark lost here comes from swapping solar and lunar round, so this chapter fixes that first and then makes sure it stays fixed.

10.1

Rotation and revolution are not the same word

The Earth is doing two things at once, all the time. Keeping the two words apart is the foundation of this whole topic.

Rotation means spinning on its own axis. The axis is an imaginary line through the North and South Poles. One full spin takes 24 hours. This is what gives us day and night. The half of the Earth facing the Sun has day; the half turned away is in its own shadow and has night. Nothing is switching on and off — the Earth is simply turning.

Revolution means travelling all the way round the Sun in an orbit. One full trip takes about 365 days, which is what we call a year. This is what gives us the seasons, not day and night.

A useful picture: you can spin on the spot in the kitchen without going anywhere — that is rotation. You can also walk a lap round the kitchen table — that is revolution. The Earth does both at the same time.

Rotate = spin

Both words start with the same idea of going round, so link each one to its time. Rotation → 24 hours → day and night. Revolution → 365 days → one year. If a question mentions day and night, it is asking about rotation, never about the orbit.

Commonly missed

Which movement of the Earth causes day and night?

Many students answer "the Earth moving round the Sun". It sounds reasonable, because the Sun is involved. But the orbit takes a whole year, and day and night change every few hours — the timings do not match.

How to work it: match the time to the effect. Day and night repeat every 24 hours, and the only 24-hour movement is the spin. So the answer is rotation on its axis. Use this timing check every time: if the effect repeats daily it is rotation, if it repeats yearly it is revolution.

10.2

The Moon borrows its light

The Moon travels round the Earth in an orbit of its own. One full trip takes about 28 days, which is roughly a month. So the Earth carries the Moon along with it as it goes round the Sun.

Here is the part people get wrong. The Moon is not a source of light. It makes no light of its own at all. It is a ball of grey rock, rather like a huge pebble. It only looks bright because sunlight lands on it and reflects off to us. The Moon is more like a mirror than a torch.

The Sun, by contrast, is a genuine luminous object — it produces its own light. This is why the same question can be asked twice on the same paper: "name a luminous object" and "name a non-luminous object we can see". The Sun is the first. The Moon is the second.

Luminous or not

The Sun is luminous — it makes its own light. The Moon is non-luminous — it reflects the Sun's light. If the Sun stopped shining, the Moon would go completely dark.

10.3

Why the Moon changes shape

Half of the Moon is always lit by the Sun, just as half of the Earth always has daytime. That never changes. What changes is how much of the lit half we can see from Earth, and that depends on where the Moon has got to in its orbit.

These changing views are the phases of the Moon. When the Moon sits between us and the Sun, its lit side faces away from us and we see almost nothing — a new moon. As it moves round we catch a thin sliver, called a crescent, then a half moon, then a fat shape called a gibbous moon, and finally the whole lit face: a full moon. Then the sequence runs backwards. The full cycle takes about 28 days, the same as one orbit.

Notice that the Moon is not being covered by anything during a phase. Nothing is casting a shadow on it. We are simply looking at the lit half from a different angle each night.

Commonly missed

A student says the phases of the Moon are caused by the Earth's shadow falling on the Moon. Explain why this is wrong.

The trap is that it almost works. A shadow would explain a curved dark edge, so the idea feels sensible.

How to work it: use the timings. Phases take 28 days to go round once, and at new moon the Moon is on the Sun's side of the Earth — the Earth's shadow points the other way entirely, so it cannot possibly reach. The Earth's shadow does land on the Moon sometimes, but that is a lunar eclipse, it lasts a couple of hours, and it happens at full moon rather than new moon.

10.4

The two eclipses, the right way round

An eclipse happens when the Sun, Earth and Moon line up so that one of them casts a shadow on another. There are exactly two, and the whole difference is which object is in the middle.

In a solar eclipse, the Moon is in the middle. It passes between the Sun and the Earth and blocks the Sun from view. The Moon's shadow lands on the Earth. Because the Moon must be on the Sun's side of us, a solar eclipse can only happen at new moon. The Moon's shadow is small, so only people standing inside that little patch of shadow see it — perhaps a strip a few hundred kilometres wide. And it happens during the day, because you must be able to see the Sun to watch it disappear.

In a lunar eclipse, the Earth is in the middle. The Earth passes between the Sun and the Moon and blocks the sunlight that normally reaches the Moon. The Earth's shadow lands on the Moon. This needs the Moon to be on the opposite side of us from the Sun, so a lunar eclipse only happens at full moon. The Earth is much bigger than the Moon and casts a wide shadow, so everyone on the whole night side of Earth can see it at once, for an hour or more.

The memory hook

sOLar = the Sun is hidden. LUNAr = the Moon is hidden. Whichever object is being hidden from view is the one that gives the eclipse its name. It is never named after the object doing the hiding. So in a solar eclipse the Sun disappears, which means the Moon must be in front of it.

Commonly missed

Diagram question: the Sun is on the left, then a small circle, then a larger circle. Which eclipse is shown?

The trap is that students look for the word "moon" or "sun" in the options and guess. The diagram may not even be labelled.

How to work it: find the middle object, because that is what casts the shadow. Here the middle circle is small, so it is the Moon, so the Moon's shadow lands on the Earth behind it. The Sun is being hidden. That makes it a solar eclipse. Then check yourself with the phase: a solar eclipse needs a new moon, and the Moon is indeed on the Sun's side of the Earth. Consistent.

10.5

Why not one every month, and how dark the shadow gets

Here is a fair question. The Moon reaches new moon once every 28 days and full moon once every 28 days. So why do we not get a solar eclipse and a lunar eclipse every single month?

The answer is that the Moon's orbit is tilted by about 5 degrees compared with the Earth's orbit round the Sun. The three objects are almost never in a perfectly straight line. Most months the Moon passes a little above the Sun, or a little below it, and its shadow misses the Earth completely — it sails past into empty space. Only when the tilt happens to bring all three into line do we get an eclipse, which is why they are rare enough to make the news.

A shadow has two parts, and this explains why some eclipses are total and some are only partial. The dark middle of the shadow, where the light is blocked completely, is the umbra. The lighter edge around it, where only some of the light is blocked, is the penumbra. Stand in the umbra during a solar eclipse and the Sun vanishes altogether: a total eclipse. Stand in the penumbra and you see the Sun with a bite taken out of it: a partial eclipse.

The question that separates people

Eclipses are rare because the Moon's orbit is tilted about 5 degrees, so the Sun, Earth and Moon are hardly ever in an exact straight line. Nearly every month the shadow misses. Knowing this is the difference between understanding eclipses and having memorised them.

10.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

10.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

Explain the difference between the rotation and the revolution of the Earth, and say what each one causes.

Show answer

Rotation is the Earth spinning on its own axis, an imaginary line through the poles. One spin takes 24 hours. The side facing the Sun has day and the side turned away has night, so rotation is what causes day and night.

Revolution is the Earth travelling all the way round the Sun in its orbit. One trip takes about 365 days, which we call a year, and it is what gives us the seasons.

Both are happening at the same moment. Spinning does not move you anywhere; orbiting does.

2

A solar eclipse is happening. Draw the order of the three objects in your head and explain why the Sun disappears from view.

Show answer

The order is Sun – Moon – Earth. The Moon is in the middle, so it passes directly between the Sun and the Earth.

Light from the Sun cannot travel through the Moon, because light travels in straight lines and the Moon is opaque. The Moon therefore casts a shadow, and that shadow lands on part of the Earth. Anyone standing inside it looks up and finds the Sun blocked.

The name matches the object being hidden: it is the Sun that disappears, so it is called a solar eclipse.

3

The Moon is not a source of light, yet it is the brightest thing in the night sky. Explain.

Show answer

The Moon is a ball of rock and produces no light of its own, so it is non-luminous. Sunlight travels across space, lands on the Moon's surface and reflects off it. Some of that reflected light reaches our eyes, and that is what we see.

It looks so bright mainly because it is close to us — far closer than any star — so the reflected light has less distance to spread out over.

4

There is a full moon roughly every 28 days. Explain why we do not see a lunar eclipse every 28 days as well.

Show answer

A lunar eclipse needs the Sun, Earth and Moon in an almost perfect straight line, so that the Earth's shadow actually lands on the Moon.

The Moon's orbit around the Earth is tilted by about 5 degrees compared with the Earth's orbit around the Sun. So at most full moons the Moon sits slightly above or slightly below the Earth's shadow, and the shadow misses it. The Moon stays fully lit and we just see an ordinary full moon.

Only when the tilt happens to line all three up does the shadow land on the Moon, which is why eclipses are uncommon.

10.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 10.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about a lunar eclipse are correct?

  • 1The Moon passes between the Sun and the Earth.
  • 2The Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.
  • 3It can only happen when there is a full moon.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. That describes a solar eclipse. In a lunar eclipse it is the Earth that sits in the middle. The moment statement 1 is wrong the answer must be C, and you can stop.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is correct, since the middle object casts the shadow and here that is the Earth. Statement 3 is correct, because the Moon must be on the opposite side of the Earth from the Sun to fall into the shadow, and that position is exactly what a full moon is. Consistent with C.

2

Which statements about the Earth and the Moon are correct?

  • 1The Earth rotates on its axis once every 24 hours.
  • 2The Moon takes about 28 days to orbit the Earth.
  • 3The Moon gives out its own light.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. One rotation is 24 hours, and it is what gives us day and night. So the answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. The Moon is non-luminous rock. It only reflects sunlight. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. One orbit of the Moon takes about 28 days, which is also the length of one full cycle of phases. So the answer is B.

3

Which statements about a solar eclipse are correct?

  • 1The Moon's shadow falls on part of the Earth.
  • 2It happens when there is a new moon.
  • 3Someone standing in the umbra sees the Sun completely blocked.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. The Moon is the middle object, so it is the one casting the shadow, and that shadow lands on the Earth. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. The umbra is the dark core of the shadow where all the light is blocked, so the Sun is hidden completely. That points to A.

Statement 2 — correct. The Moon has to be between us and the Sun, so its lit side faces away from Earth. That position is a new moon. All three are right, so the answer is A.
