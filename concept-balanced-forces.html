Concept

Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

Forces are balanced when they cancel out completely and nothing is left over, and unbalanced when something is left over — and only the leftover changes how an object moves.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

Almost nothing has just one force on it. A book on a table has its weight pulling down and the table pushing up. A cyclist has the push from the pedals, air resistance, friction at the wheels and weight, all at once. So the useful question is never "what forces are acting" on its own. It is "what is left after they have all been added up".

That leftover has a name: the net force, sometimes called the resultant force. Adding forces is not quite ordinary addition, because direction counts. Two forces of 10 N pointing the same way give a net force of 20 N. Two forces of 10 N pointing at each other give a net force of zero — they cancel exactly.

When the net force is zero, the forces are balanced. When the net force is anything other than zero, they are unbalanced. Everything else on this page follows from that single distinction.

2

How it works

Here is the part that costs the most marks, so it is worth stating slowly. Balanced forces do not mean "not moving". They mean "not changing". An object with zero net force keeps doing precisely whatever it was already doing. If it was stationary, it stays stationary. If it was travelling in a straight line at a steady speed, it carries on at that same steady speed in that same straight line.

Those two outcomes feel like opposites — one thing is sitting still, the other is hurtling along — but as far as forces are concerned they are the same case. In both, nothing about the motion is changing. That is all zero net force ever guarantees.

Work through a lorry on a motorway at a steady 80 km per hour. Its engine drives it forward, say with 4000 N. Air resistance and rolling friction push back on it. Because the speed is steady, we can deduce those backward forces total exactly 4000 N — not because anyone measured them, but because if they came to less the lorry would be speeding up, and if they came to more it would be slowing down. Steady speed forces the numbers to match. The same logic applies vertically: weight down, road pushing up, equal, or the lorry would sink or rise.

Now unbalance it. The driver presses the accelerator until the engine gives 5000 N while resistance is still 4000 N. There is 1000 N left over, and that leftover makes the lorry speed up. As it goes faster, air resistance grows — pushing through air gets harder the quicker you go — until the backward forces have climbed to 5000 N again. At that moment the leftover is gone, the forces are balanced once more, and the lorry settles at a new higher steady speed. This is exactly how a falling parachutist reaches a constant speed too: weight stays the same, air resistance grows with speed until it matches.

Notice that this gives you a way of reading questions backwards. You are usually told what an object is doing, and asked about the forces. Speed or direction changing means unbalanced, guaranteed. Steady speed in a straight line, or complete stillness, means balanced, guaranteed. You do not need to know a single number.

The heart of it

Zero net force does not mean "at rest" — it means "unchanging", and standing still and cruising at a steady speed in a straight line are both examples of unchanging.

3

Where the idea came from

Working out when forces cancel came long before working out what happens when they do not. In 1586 the Flemish engineer Simon Stevin published a book in Dutch called De Beghinselen der Weeghconst, meaning The Elements of the Art of Weighing. In it he analysed forces in equilibrium — that is, forces in balance — with a rigour nobody had managed before.

His most admired argument is the "wreath of spheres". Imagine a triangular block with two sloping sides of different steepness, and a closed loop of identical balls on a string draped over it, hanging down underneath. Stevin argued the loop must stay still. If it did not, it would slide round, and since the arrangement looks the same after it has slid round by one ball, it would go on sliding for ever, giving endless motion for nothing. Since that is absurd, it must stay put. From that he could work out how the pull along a slope depends on the slope's steepness — without any experiment at all, purely from the impossibility of perpetual motion.

What took much longer was the second half of the idea. Stevin's world was one of ropes, pulleys and stationary loads, where "balanced" and "standing still" always went together. Accepting that steady motion in a straight line belongs in the very same category — that a cruising ship and a resting stone are in the identical state of zero net force — required abandoning the ancient assumption that motion itself needs a cause. That step was not completed until Galileo's work on smooth inclined planes and Newton's Principia of 1687. The two halves of the idea were separated by a century.

4

What people get wrong

The dominant mistake is reading "balanced" as "stationary". It leads directly to marking statements like "the forces on a car moving at constant speed are unbalanced" as correct, which they are not. Train yourself to translate: balanced means nothing about the motion is changing.

A second mistake is assuming that two equal forces must cancel. They only cancel if they point in exactly opposite directions. Two equal forces pointing the same way add up to double, and two equal forces at an angle leave something over as well.

A third is forgetting the vertical forces. A box being pushed along the floor has four forces, not two — push, friction, weight and the upward push from the floor. The vertical pair usually balance, which is exactly why the box neither sinks nor lifts.

Commonly missed

A skydiver has fallen for some time and is now dropping at a constant speed. Are the forces on her balanced or unbalanced, and what does that say about air resistance?

The trap is the falling. Everything about the scene shouts "moving fast, so something must be winning", and the word gravity makes people want weight to be the larger force.

How to work it: start from what she is doing, not from what forces you can name. Her speed is constant and her direction is not changing, so nothing about her motion is changing, so the net force is zero and the forces are balanced. Only two forces act — weight downwards and air resistance upwards — so for the net to be zero, air resistance must be exactly equal to her weight. She keeps falling not because a force is pushing her down but because she was already falling and nothing is changing that.

5

Connected ideas

This idea is built directly on force, since balancing is just what happens when several forces act together. Friction is very often the force doing the balancing on a real surface, and weight is almost always one of the forces in the sum. Inertia explains why zero net force leaves motion unchanged rather than fading away, and gravity supplies the downward force in nearly every example you will meet.

In one line

Forces are balanced when they add to zero, and that means the motion is not changing — which covers standing perfectly still and cruising at a steady speed equally well.
