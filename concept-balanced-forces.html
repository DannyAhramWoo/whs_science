Concept

Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

Forces are balanced when they cancel out completely and nothing is left over, and unbalanced when something is left over — and only the leftover changes how an object moves.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

Almost nothing has just one force on it. A book on a table has its weight pulling down and the table pushing up. A cyclist has the pedals' push, air resistance, friction and weight all at once. So the useful question is never "what forces are acting" but "what is left after they are added up".

That leftover has a name: the net force, sometimes called the resultant force. Adding forces is not ordinary addition, because direction counts. Two 10 N forces pointing the same way give 20 N. Two 10 N forces pointing at each other give zero — they cancel exactly.

When the net force is zero, the forces are balanced. When the net force is anything other than zero, they are unbalanced. Everything else on this page follows from that single distinction.

2

How it works

Here is the part that costs the most marks. Balanced forces do not mean "not moving". They mean "not changing". An object with zero net force keeps doing whatever it was doing: if stationary it stays stationary, and if travelling in a straight line at a steady speed it carries on at that speed in that line.

Those two outcomes feel like opposites — one sitting still, the other hurtling along — but as far as forces are concerned they are the same case. Nothing about the motion is changing. That is all zero net force guarantees.

Work through a lorry on a motorway at a steady 80 km per hour. Its engine drives it forward with, say, 4000 N, while air resistance and rolling friction push back. Because the speed is steady, those backward forces must total exactly 4000 N — not because anyone measured them, but because any less and it would speed up, any more and it would slow down.

Now unbalance it. The driver accelerates until the engine gives 5000 N while resistance is still 4000 N. The 1000 N left over makes the lorry speed up. As it goes faster, air resistance grows — pushing through air gets harder the quicker you go — until the backward forces reach 5000 N. The leftover is gone, the forces are balanced, and the lorry settles at a new higher steady speed.

This lets you read questions backwards. You are usually told what an object is doing and asked about the forces. Speed or direction changing means unbalanced; steady speed in a straight line, or stillness, means balanced. You do not need a single number.

The heart of it

Zero net force does not mean "at rest" — it means "unchanging", and standing still and cruising at a steady speed in a straight line are both examples of unchanging.

3

Where the idea came from

Working out when forces cancel came long before working out what happens when they do not. In 1586 the Flemish engineer Simon Stevin published De Beghinselen der Weeghconst, The Elements of the Art of Weighing. In it he analysed forces in equilibrium — forces in balance — with a rigour nobody had managed before.

His most admired argument is the "wreath of spheres". Imagine a triangular block with two sloping sides of different steepness, and a closed loop of identical balls on a string draped over it. Stevin argued the loop must stay still. If it slid round, then since the arrangement looks the same after sliding by one ball, it would slide for ever, giving endless motion for nothing. That is absurd, so it stays put. From that he worked out how the pull along a slope depends on its steepness — purely from the impossibility of perpetual motion.

The second half of the idea took much longer. Stevin's world was one of ropes, pulleys and stationary loads, where "balanced" and "standing still" always went together. Accepting that steady motion in a straight line belongs in the same category — a cruising ship and a resting stone both in a state of zero net force — meant abandoning the ancient assumption that motion needs a cause. That step waited for Galileo's inclined planes and Newton's Principia of 1687, a century later.

4

What people get wrong

The dominant mistake is reading "balanced" as "stationary", which leads to accepting claims like "the forces on a car at constant speed are unbalanced". Translate every time: balanced means nothing about the motion is changing.

A second is assuming two equal forces must cancel. They cancel only if they point in exactly opposite directions. Two pointing the same way add to double, and two at an angle leave something over.

A third is forgetting the vertical forces. A box pushed along the floor has four, not two — push, friction, weight and the floor's upward push. The vertical pair balance, which is why the box neither sinks nor lifts.

Commonly missed

A skydiver has fallen for some time and is now dropping at a constant speed. Are the forces on her balanced or unbalanced, and what does that say about air resistance?

The trap is the falling. Everything about the scene shouts "moving fast, so something must be winning", and the word gravity makes people want weight to be larger.

How to work it: start from what she is doing, not from the forces you can name. Her speed and direction are constant, so nothing about her motion is changing, so the net force is zero and the forces are balanced. Only two forces act — weight downwards and air resistance upwards — so for the net to be zero, air resistance must exactly equal her weight. She keeps falling not because a force pushes her down but because she was already falling and nothing is changing that.

5

Connected ideas

This idea is built directly on force, since balancing is what happens when several forces act together. Friction is often the force doing the balancing on a real surface, and weight is almost always one of the forces in the sum. Inertia explains why zero net force leaves motion unchanged rather than fading, and gravity supplies the downward force in nearly every example.

In one line

Forces are balanced when they add to zero, and that means the motion is not changing — which covers standing perfectly still and cruising at a steady speed equally well.
