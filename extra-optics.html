Light That Bends and Bounces

Light usually travels in straight lines. But the moment it crosses from air into water or glass, it changes direction — and that single fact explains rainbows, why a swimming pool looks shallower than it is, and why some people need spectacles.

Read this first

This chapter is not on the exam syllabus. Nothing here will be tested, so do not revise it the way you revise the numbered chapters.

It is here for two reasons. These ideas turn up early, often before anyone explains them properly, and it is much better to meet them once clearly than to half-recognise the words. And they pay off immediately: once you know why light bends, you can explain a rainbow, a pool that is deeper than it looks, a diamond that sparkles and a pair of glasses, all with the same one idea.

1

Why light bends at all

Light is fast. Through empty space it is the fastest thing there is. But it does not go at that top speed through everything. Send it into water and it slows down. Send it into glass and it slows down more. Nothing pushes it or grabs it — light simply has a different speed in different materials.

That change of speed is the whole secret. If a beam of light meets the surface of the glass head on, straight in, it slows down and carries on in the same direction. Nothing interesting happens. But if it arrives at an angle, one edge of the beam reaches the glass before the other edge does. That edge slows first. The rest of the beam is still going quickly. And a line that slows at one end swings round.

The marching band

Picture a row of marchers, shoulder to shoulder, walking across a football pitch towards a strip of thick mud. They reach the mud at an angle, so the marcher on the left steps in it first and slows to a trudge. Her neighbours are still striding along on grass. To stay in line, the whole row has to swing round towards her side.

Nobody told them to turn. The turn happened because one end slowed before the other. Light does exactly the same thing at the surface of water or glass, and this is called refraction — the bending of light when it changes speed.

The normal, and which way things bend

To describe the bending, we need a line to measure from. That line is called the normal. It is simply an imaginary line drawn at right angles to the surface, at the exact spot where the light hits. It is usually drawn dashed, because it is not real — it is a reference line, like the centre line painted on a road.

With that line in place, the rule is short:

  • Going into a material where light is slower (air into glass), the ray bends towards the normal.
  • Coming out into a material where light is faster (glass back into air), the ray bends away from the normal.

Two things worth getting right

The first is a word. People often say light slows down in a denser material, and then picture something heavy. That is the wrong kind of density. What matters here is optical density: how much a material slows light down. It is not the same as mass density, which is how much a lump of the stuff weighs for its size. Some very heavy substances hardly bend light at all, and some light ones bend it a great deal. Water is far lighter than many metals but slows light noticeably. So say optically denser when you mean "light goes slower in it", and keep ordinary density for weight and volume.

The second is about comparisons. Scientists use a number called the refractive index to say how much a material slows light. But a number like that is meaningless on its own, because it is always a comparison — and you have to name what it is being compared with. Usually the comparison is with a vacuum, or with air, which is nearly the same thing. Compared with air, glass and water are both slower. But you can equally compare two materials with each other, and then the answer can go either way round: light travelling out of glass and into water is speeding up, not slowing down. Never quote a refractive index without saying compared with what.

2

Seeing things where they are not

Put a straw in a glass of water and look from the side. The straw appears to snap in half at the water line. It has not, of course. The light coming from the underwater part of the straw bends as it leaves the water and enters the air, so it arrives at your eye travelling in a different direction from the one it started in.

Your brain has one firm assumption about light: it came here in a straight line. That assumption is right almost all the time, and your brain does not check it. So it traces the light backwards in a straight line and decides the straw must be somewhere along that line. The underwater half ends up looking shifted and the straw looks broken.

The same thing makes a swimming pool look shallower than it really is. Light from the tiles at the bottom bends outwards as it leaves the water, your brain traces it straight back, and the bottom appears to be higher up than it is. A pool that looks waist deep from the edge can be well over your head. That is not a trick of the water being murky. It happens even in perfectly clear water, and it is worth remembering before jumping in anywhere unfamiliar.

People who fish with a spear have known this for a very long time. If you aim straight at the fish you see, you miss above it every time. You have to aim below the fish you can see, because the real fish is deeper than its image. Knowing the physics is not required to learn that — but it explains why the correction is always in the same direction.

3

When light cannot get out

Now stay underwater and look upwards, and try tilting your view further and further towards the horizontal. Something strange happens.

Remember the rule: light leaving water for air bends away from the normal. So the ray always comes out at a wider angle than it went in. Tilt the ray a bit more, and the escaping ray tilts more still. Keep going and you reach a point where the escaping ray has bent so far that it is running flat along the surface, skimming it, barely leaving at all.

Tilt past that point and there is nowhere left for the ray to go. It cannot bend more than flat. So none of it escapes: all of it reflects back down into the water, as cleanly as if the surface were a mirror. This is total internal reflection — total because no light gets out, internal because the light stays inside the material, and reflection because that is what happens to it instead.

The angle where the change happens is the critical angle. It is different for different materials. For water it is roughly 49 degrees, and for ordinary glass roughly 42 degrees. Both are approximate: the exact value depends on the kind of glass and even on the colour of the light.

Two everyday sights come straight from this.

A diamond sparkles because light travels unusually slowly inside it, which makes its critical angle small — around 24 degrees. Almost any ray that gets in ends up steeper than that when it reaches a face, so it bounces around inside instead of leaving. A cut diamond is shaped so that the light rattles about and finally comes out of the top, all at once, in bright flashes. Glass jewellery cannot do this nearly as well because its critical angle is much larger and the light simply leaks out.

The underside of a water surface looks like a mirror. If you have ever been underwater in a pool and looked up at a shallow angle, you will have seen the bottom of the pool reflected above you. Nothing has been silvered. The light is simply hitting the surface past the critical angle and cannot get through.

4

Light down a pipe

Total internal reflection sounds like a curiosity. It is actually the basis of one of the most useful inventions of the last century.

An optical fibre is a thread of very pure glass, thinner than a hair. Shine light in at one end and it travels down the middle. Whenever it wanders sideways and meets the wall of the fibre, it hits at a shallow angle — well past the critical angle — so it is totally internally reflected and sent back into the middle. It bounces its way along, thousands of times, and comes out at the far end.

The clever part is that this still works if the fibre is bent. As long as the bend is gentle, every strike on the wall is still past the critical angle, so the light follows the curve. Light travelling round a corner, without a single mirror.

Bundles of these fibres carry almost all long-distance internet traffic. Messages are sent as flashes of light rather than as electricity in copper wire, and light carries far more information far further before it fades. The cables that link continents lie on the sea floor and are made of glass.

Doctors use the same idea in an endoscope: a flexible tube with fibres inside, which can be passed into the body. Some fibres carry light in to illuminate what is there, and others carry the picture back out. It lets a doctor see inside a patient without cutting them open.

Prisms instead of mirrors

Total internal reflection also turns up in things you can hold. A periscope — the tube used to see over a wall or out of a submarine — can be built with two ordinary mirrors, but a good one uses two triangular blocks of glass instead. Light enters the flat face of a block, strikes the sloping back face at more than the critical angle, and is turned through a right angle without a mirror at all. Binoculars use the same trick to fold a long light path into a short body.

Why bother, when a mirror is cheaper? Because an ordinary mirror has its silver behind a sheet of glass, so a little light reflects off the front of the glass too, giving a faint second image. The silver also tarnishes with age. A prism has neither problem: total internal reflection is total, and there is nothing to wear out.

5

Lenses

Everything so far has involved flat surfaces. A lens is a piece of glass with curved surfaces, and the curve is what makes it useful.

Here is the reason a curve behaves differently from a flat sheet. The bending of a ray depends on the angle at which it meets the surface. A flat surface presents the same angle everywhere, so every ray in a parallel beam is bent by the same amount and the beam stays parallel — just shifted a little. A curved surface is tilted differently at every point. A ray arriving near the edge meets a steeply tilted piece of glass and is bent a lot. A ray arriving at the middle meets a nearly flat piece and is hardly bent at all. So the rays are no longer bent equally, and the beam changes shape.

There are two shapes to know.

A convex lens is fatter in the middle than at the edges. Its edges are tilted so that they bend rays inwards, and the more they are bent the further out they started. Parallel rays going in all come together at a single point on the far side, called the focus. A convex lens is a converging lens: it gathers light.

A concave lens is thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges. Its edges tilt the other way, so they bend rays outwards. Parallel rays going in come out spreading apart. A concave lens is a diverging lens: it scatters light.

Where you meet them

A magnifying glass is a single convex lens held close to something small. The light reaching your eye arrives spread wider than it otherwise would, so the object fills more of your view and looks bigger.

Your eye has a convex lens in it, just behind the pupil, and it focuses light onto the back of the eyeball where the light-sensitive cells are. Small muscles squeeze it fatter or let it go thinner, which is how you can switch from reading a page to looking out of a window without anything blurring for long.

Spectacles help when the eye's own lens brings light to a focus in slightly the wrong place. If it focuses too soon, a concave lens in front spreads the light out a little first, pushing the focus back to where it belongs. If it focuses too late, a convex lens starts gathering the light early. That is the whole idea behind a prescription: a small correction applied before the light ever reaches the eye.

A camera uses a convex lens to throw a sharp picture onto a sensor, and a telescope uses a large one to collect far more light than your eye can on its own, which is why a big telescope can see faint things a small one cannot.

6

Splitting light

One detail has been left out so far. Light of different colours does not slow down by quite the same amount when it enters glass. Violet light is slowed the most; red light the least, with the other colours in between.

Since bending depends on slowing, this means each colour bends by a slightly different amount at the same surface. Violet turns furthest, red turns least. Send ordinary white light — which is all the colours travelling together — into a triangular block of glass called a prism, and it comes out as a fan, with the colours in order: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet. That fan is called a spectrum, and the spreading is called dispersion.

The prism does not add anything. The colours were in the white light the whole time, and the prism simply sorts them by how much each one bends. That is why the same block can put them back together again if you send the fan into a second prism the other way round.

Rainbows

A rainbow is this same effect, done by rain instead of glass, millions of times over.

Each raindrop is a tiny sphere of water and acts like a small round prism. Sunlight enters the front of the drop and bends, splitting the colours apart. It then travels to the back of the drop, where it strikes the inside surface and reflects off it. It comes back out through the front, bending and spreading once more on the way. So each drop takes in white sunlight and sends out a small fan of separated colours, backwards, towards the direction the light came from.

This is why a rainbow is always behind you when you face it, with the Sun at your back. Each drop sends its colours out at its own particular angle, so the red you see comes from one set of drops and the violet from a different set lower down. You are not looking at one drop with a rainbow in it; you are collecting one colour each from an enormous number of drops.

Sometimes a second, fainter bow appears above the first. That one is made by light that reflected twice inside each drop instead of once. Every reflection loses a little light, so the second bow is dimmer — and the extra bounce turns the order of the colours round, so the secondary bow is reversed compared with the main one. If you ever see two, check the order. It is a satisfying thing to be able to predict.

7

Questions to think about

These have no marks attached and no single correct wording. Try answering out loud, in full sentences, before opening anything.

1

A beam of light shines straight down onto the flat top of a glass block, exactly at right angles to the surface. It slows down as it enters. Why does it not bend?

Show answer

Because bending needs one side of the beam to slow before the other. Coming in square on, the whole width of the beam crosses the surface at the same instant, so every part slows at the same moment and there is nothing to make the beam swing round.

Going back to the marching band: if the whole row steps into the mud together, everybody slows together and the line stays straight. It is only the angled arrival that turns them.

Note that the light really is slower inside the glass. Slowing and bending are not the same thing — slowing always happens, bending only happens when the beam arrives at an angle.

2

Someone says "light travels more slowly in denser materials". What is wrong with that sentence, and how would you fix it?

Show answer

The problem is the word "denser". In everyday use it means heavy for its size — mass density. That is not the property that slows light down.

The property that matters is optical density: how much a material slows light. The two do not march in step. There are very heavy materials that bend light only slightly, and much lighter ones that bend it strongly. Knowing what a substance weighs for its size does not tell you what it will do to a ray of light.

A better sentence: "light travels more slowly in optically denser materials", or simply "light travels more slowly in some materials than others, and the ones that slow it most bend it most".

3

Total internal reflection happens when light tries to leave glass for air, but never when light goes from air into glass. Why can it only work one way round?

Show answer

Because the effect depends on the ray bending away from the normal, and that only happens when light is speeding up as it crosses the boundary.

Leaving glass for air, the ray bends away from the normal, so it comes out at a wider angle than it went in. Tilt the incoming ray enough and the outgoing one would have to bend past flat, which is impossible — so instead none escapes.

Going the other way, air into glass, the ray bends towards the normal, coming out at a narrower angle than it went in. There is no limit to run into. However shallow the incoming ray, there is always a legal direction for it inside the glass.

The general rule: total internal reflection can only happen when light is trying to move from a slower material into a faster one.

4

Why does a rainbow have its colours in the order red on the outside and violet on the inside — and why is the second bow, when you see one, the other way round?

Show answer

Each drop splits sunlight because violet is slowed most and so bends most, and red is slowed least and bends least. The two colours therefore leave the drop at different angles. Since you only receive one colour from any given drop, red reaches you from drops higher in the sky and violet from drops lower down, which puts red on the outer edge of the arc.

The secondary bow is made by light that bounced twice inside the drop rather than once. The extra bounce flips the geometry, so the colours emerge in the opposite order and the bow appears reversed.

It is also fainter, because a little light escapes at every reflection. Two reflections mean two chances to lose some, so much less of the original sunlight makes it to your eye.

5

A friend says an optical fibre must have a mirror lining, because light goes round the bends. What would you say?

Show answer

There is no lining. The glass wall itself does the job, using total internal reflection. Light travelling along a thin fibre always meets the wall at a very shallow angle — far past the critical angle — so none of it escapes and all of it is reflected back inside.

It is also better than a mirror. Every real mirror absorbs a small fraction of the light it reflects, and over thousands of bounces those small losses would add up to nothing arriving. Total internal reflection loses nothing at the surface, which is what makes a cable thousands of kilometres long possible at all.

The one catch is that the bend must be gentle. Bend a fibre too sharply and the light starts meeting the wall too steeply, gets past the critical angle the wrong way, and leaks out.

Why this is worth knowing

Almost everything in this chapter came from one small fact: light travels at different speeds in different materials. That is all. Nothing else was added. From that single starting point came the broken straw, the shallow-looking pool, the fish that is not where it appears, the mirror on the underside of a pond, the sparkle of a diamond, the glass threads carrying messages under the ocean, the lens in your eye, the lenses on your face, and every rainbow you will ever see.

That is the pattern worth noticing. Good science is rarely a long list of separate facts to remember. It is usually a small number of ideas that keep turning up in different clothes. If you understand why the marching band turns when it hits the mud, you have not learned one thing — you have learned the reason behind a dozen things that had seemed unrelated.

None of this will be examined. But the next time somebody tells you a swimming pool is deeper than it looks, or points at a double rainbow, you will know exactly why, and you will have worked it out rather than been told.
