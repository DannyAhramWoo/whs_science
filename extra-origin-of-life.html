The Origin of Life

How did life start on a planet that began as molten rock with a poisonous atmosphere? Nobody knows for certain, and that is what makes this one of the most interesting questions in science.

Read this first

It is here because the ideas are genuinely worth knowing, and because being able to talk about an open question — one where scientists still disagree — is a different and more valuable skill than reciting an answer.

1

A planet nobody could live on

Earth formed about 4.6 billion years ago, and for a long stretch after that it was hot molten rock. There was no ground to stand on and no sea to swim in. If you could somehow have visited, you would have died in seconds.

Volcanoes were everywhere, and they poured out gas: carbon dioxide, nitrogen and enormous quantities of water vapour. That gas built up into a thick early atmosphere — but not an atmosphere you could breathe. It was closer to Venus today than to the air outside your window. Crucially, there was no free oxygen at all.

Then the surface slowly cooled. A crust formed. The water vapour high above condensed and fell as rain, and it rained for an extremely long time — long enough to fill the ocean basins. Those oceans are where the rest of this story happens.

The reversal at the heart of this chapter

We usually imagine living things fitting themselves to a planet. Here it happened the other way round as well. Life began on a world that could not have supported us — and then changed that world until it could.

2

The chemical soup

Life most probably began in the oceans, and one reason to think so is that nothing exotic was needed. The elements that make up living things — carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphorus and sulphur — were all present on early Earth. They were simply arranged in a different mixture.

Two energy sources were doing the work. Lightning tore through the thick atmosphere, and ultraviolet light poured down unfiltered, because there was no ozone layer yet to stop it. Both drove chemical reactions among the volcanic gases. The simple molecules that formed washed down into the sea, where they became more concentrated and reacted with each other again.

The Urey-Miller experiment

In 1953, at the University of Chicago, a graduate student named Stanley Miller and his supervisor Harold Urey decided to test the idea rather than argue about it. They sealed water, methane, ammonia and hydrogen inside glassware, heated the water so it evaporated and circulated, and passed electric sparks through the gas as artificial lightning.

Within a week the water had darkened to brown. When they analysed it they found amino acids — the small units from which proteins are built — along with formaldehyde, hydrogen cyanide and urea. Nobody had put those in. They had assembled themselves.

Be honest about what it showed

The experiment proved that the building blocks of life form easily under those conditions. It did not make life — nothing in that flask was alive, and nothing in it could copy itself. Worse, scientists now think the real early atmosphere was probably somewhat different from the mixture Miller chose.

It is still a landmark, but for a reason worth stating precisely: it turned a philosophical question into an experimental one. Before 1953 you argued about the origin of life. After 1953 you could test claims about it in a laboratory.

3

From building blocks to something alive

Having amino acids is not the same as having life, and the gap between them is where the real difficulty sits.

In the 1920s two scientists working independently — Alexander Oparin in Russia and J.B.S. Haldane in Britain — proposed the same broad answer. Life, they suggested, did not appear in one step. It arose gradually out of non-living chemistry: simple molecules first, then longer chains that could store information and speed up reactions, then a membrane to hold everything together, and an energy source to keep it running. This is the Oparin-Haldane hypothesis, and almost every modern idea is a descendant of it.

Those longer chains are called polymers. A polymer is nothing more mysterious than a long chain built from small repeating units joined end to end, like a paper chain made from identical strips. Proteins are polymers of amino acids. DNA and RNA are polymers too.

The chicken-and-egg problem

Here is the knot. In every living cell today, DNA holds the instructions — but DNA cannot copy itself. It needs proteins to do that. And proteins cannot be made without the instructions held in DNA. Each one requires the other to already exist. Neither could plausibly have gone first.

The most popular way out is the RNA world hypothesis. RNA is a close relative of DNA, and it has an unusual property: it can carry information in the order of its units, and it can also speed up chemical reactions the way a protein does. One molecule, both jobs. If something like RNA came first, no chicken and no egg is needed — a single kind of molecule could have held the instructions and carried them out.

Once a molecule could copy itself, even imperfectly, the door opens. Copies that worked slightly better made more copies. That is the beginning of change and improvement, and it does not require anything to be alive in the ordinary sense.

Say this plainly

The RNA world is a hypothesis, not settled fact. It is an elegant solution to a real problem, and there is laboratory evidence that RNA can do both jobs. But no one has watched it happen, and there are researchers who think something simpler came before RNA. Treating it as proved would be a mistake.

4

Why a bag was needed

A self-copying molecule floating loose in an ocean is in trouble. It drifts apart from the other molecules it needs. It is diluted to nothing. It is broken up by ultraviolet light. To get anywhere, it needed a container.

The container turns out to be the easy part, and this is one of the most satisfying details in the whole story. Phospholipid molecules have two ends with opposite tastes: a head that is attracted to water and two tails that avoid it. Drop a lot of them into water and they cannot help sorting themselves out. The heads turn outwards towards the water, the tails huddle inwards away from it, and the result is a double layer — a bilayer. A flat sheet of bilayer has awkward exposed edges, so the sheet curls round and closes into a hollow bubble.

A bubble like that does three useful things at once. It keeps a useful set of molecules together instead of letting them scatter. It concentrates them, and concentrated chemicals react faster. And it puts a barrier between what is inside and whatever is outside.

Those bubbles, with self-copying molecules inside, are as close to a first cell as we can currently describe. Every cell in your body still has a membrane built on exactly this principle, out of exactly this kind of molecule.

5

Vents in the dark

So far the story has been set in shallow sunlit water, with lightning overhead. There is a serious rival account that puts the whole thing somewhere else entirely: at hydrothermal vents on the deep ocean floor, where hot mineral-rich water pours out through cracks in the crust.

The case for vents rests on three points. First, they prove the principle: living things thrive there right now, in total darkness with no oxygen, taking their energy from sulphur compounds instead of light. Life does not need sunlight. Second, vents supply steady energy, unlike lightning, which is violent and occasional. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the deep sea is sheltered. The early surface was being scoured by ultraviolet light and bombarded by meteorites — not an easy place for something fragile to persist.

Two live possibilities

Warm surface pools with lightning, or deep hydrothermal vents. Both are serious scientific proposals with real evidence behind them, and scientists have not settled which is right. It is also possible that neither is exactly right, or that the answer involves both. This is not a case of one correct answer and one wrong one that you simply have not been told yet.

6

The first evidence, and the air we breathe

Everything so far has been reasoning about what could have happened. The oldest direct physical evidence we have of life comes from stromatolites: layered, dome-shaped structures built up over years by thick mats of cyanobacteria, sometimes called blue-green algae. The mat traps sediment, grows upwards through it, and traps more — leaving a stack of fine layers behind, one on top of the next.

Fossil stromatolites about 3.5 billion years old have been found, and there is evidence that cells may have existed as early as 3.8 billion years ago. Set against a planet that formed 4.6 billion years ago, life appeared remarkably soon after there was anywhere for it to live.

What those cyanobacteria did next reshaped the planet. They carried out photosynthesis, and photosynthesis releases oxygen as waste. A single mat makes very little. But there were vast numbers of them and, more importantly, there was an unimaginable amount of time. Slowly, over hundreds of millions of years, oxygen accumulated in the oceans and then in the air.

Three things followed. Oxygen made it possible for larger, more complex, more energy-hungry organisms to exist. Some of that oxygen high in the atmosphere formed the ozone layer, which absorbs ultraviolet light — and only once that shield existed did living on land become survivable. Life had, in effect, built its own roof.

The point of the whole chapter

Life did not just adapt to Earth. It rebuilt Earth. The breathable atmosphere, the ozone shield and the possibility of life on land are all products of living things, not conditions that were waiting for them.

7

Questions to think about

These have no marks attached and no single correct wording. Try answering out loud, in full sentences, before opening anything.

1

The Urey-Miller experiment did not produce life, and the atmosphere it used was probably not the real one. Why is it still considered one of the most important experiments of the twentieth century?

Show answer

Because of what it changed about the question, not because of what it made. Before it, the origin of life was something people argued about from opinion. Miller and Urey showed it could be put in a flask and tested.

It also settled one real doubt: people had assumed the building blocks of life must be difficult to make. The experiment showed amino acids appear within days from ordinary gases and a spark. That removed one obstacle even though many remain.

A fair summary is that its value lies in the method it introduced rather than in its result — and that a good experiment can matter enormously while still being wrong in its details.

2

Explain the DNA and protein chicken-and-egg problem in your own words, and say why it is not a small technical detail.

Show answer

DNA carries the instructions but cannot copy itself without proteins. Proteins cannot be built without instructions from DNA. Each needs the other already in place, so neither can be the starting point.

It matters because it blocks the obvious story. You cannot simply say the parts of a cell gathered gradually, since the two most central parts each depend on the other being finished. Any account of the origin of life has to break this loop or it does not get started at all.

The RNA world is one proposed escape: a single molecule that can do both jobs needs no partner to exist first.

3

Suppose you had to decide between the warm-pool idea and the hydrothermal vent idea. What evidence would actually change your mind, and how might someone go and look for it?

Show answer

There is no fixed answer here — what matters is whether your evidence would genuinely distinguish the two, rather than merely being consistent with one of them.

Reasonable things to want: chemistry that only works at vent temperatures and pressures and fails in a surface pool, or the reverse. Rocks of the right age that record where the earliest cells were living. Vent minerals that make the key reactions happen far faster than anything a pool can offer.

Weak evidence, by contrast, is anything both ideas predict equally. Finding that bacteria live at vents today shows life can survive there. It does not show life began there, because those bacteria could have arrived later.

4

Cyanobacteria filled the air with oxygen, which was poisonous to most of the organisms alive at the time. Was this a disaster or an opportunity? Argue both sides.

Show answer

A disaster: for organisms adapted to a world without oxygen, a rising level of it was lethal. Enormous numbers of them would have died out or been pushed into the few places oxygen could not reach — deep mud, deep rock, the guts of other creatures — where their descendants still live.

An opportunity: reactions using oxygen release far more energy, which is what made large and active organisms possible. Ozone formed and shielded the surface, opening the land.

The useful conclusion is that it was both at once, depending on which organism you ask, and that a change in the environment does not have a single verdict attached to it.

One to try

1

Which of the following best describes the role of the RNA World Hypothesis in understanding the origin of life?

  • AIt suggests that proteins were the first self-replicating molecules before DNA evolved.
  • BIt proposes that RNA molecules could store genetic information and catalyse reactions, making them key in early life.
  • CIt states that lipids formed the first cell membranes, enabling metabolic reactions.
  • DIt argues that volcanic hydrothermal vents were the sole environments for life's origin.
Show answer

Answer: B

B is correct. The whole point of the hypothesis is that one molecule does both jobs: RNA can hold information in the order of its units and can also speed up reactions. That is what breaks the chicken-and-egg deadlock between DNA and proteins.

A is wrong. Proteins are very good at speeding up reactions but they do not store and copy genetic information, so they cannot self-replicate. This option describes the problem the hypothesis exists to avoid.

C is wrong. Lipids forming membranes is a true and important statement — it is section 4 of this chapter — but it belongs to a different idea. The RNA world is about information and catalysis, not containers.

D is wrong. Vents are a proposal about where life began. The RNA world is a proposal about which molecule came first. The two are separate questions, and the word "sole" should make you suspicious in any case.

The general lesson: three of the four options are perfectly respectable science. They are wrong only because they answer a different question from the one asked. Check what the question is about before checking whether a statement is true.

Why this is worth knowing

Most of what you learn in science is settled. Somebody worked it out, it has been checked many times, and your job is to understand it and use it. That is a reasonable way to spend most of your time, but it gives a misleading picture of what science is actually like. It makes it look like a finished building rather than a construction site.

This topic is a construction site. Careful, honest, well-informed people disagree about where life began and about which molecule came first, and they disagree because the evidence does not yet decide it. Being able to hold an open question in your head — to say what is known, what is merely likely, what is guesswork, and what would settle it — is a harder skill than remembering an answer, and it lasts longer. Every answer you have been given was once a question like this one.
