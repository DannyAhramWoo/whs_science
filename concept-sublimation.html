Concept

Sublimation

Sublimation is a solid turning straight into a gas without ever becoming a liquid.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

The usual story of the states of matter goes in order: heat a solid and it melts to a liquid, heat the liquid and it boils to a gas. Sublimation breaks that order. Some solids skip the middle step entirely and become gas directly, with no puddle at any point.

Dry ice is the clearest example. It is solid carbon dioxide, and left on a table it steadily shrinks away to nothing while the table stays dry — which is where the name comes from. Nothing melted, because carbon dioxide has no liquid stage at ordinary air pressure. The white cloud pouring off it is not the carbon dioxide, which is invisible, but water vapour from the air condensing in the cold.

The reverse change has its own name: deposition, a gas turning straight back into a solid. Frost on a cold window is deposition. Water vapour meets glass cold enough that it forms ice crystals directly, which is why frost appears as feathery patterns rather than droplets that later froze.

2

How it works

The particle model explains it well. In a solid, the particles are packed closely and held by strong forces of attraction, vibrating in fixed positions, and heating makes them vibrate harder. In most substances they first loosen enough to slide past one another, which is melting, and only later break free altogether, which is boiling. In a substance that sublimes, the surface particles break away completely before the solid ever loosens into a liquid, so the liquid stage never appears.

Solid iodine is the classic laboratory case. Warmed gently, dark grey crystals give off a striking violet vapour, and if that vapour touches a cold surface it forms crystals again directly — sublimation and deposition in one demonstration. Naphthalene, used in mothballs, does the same more slowly, which is why a mothball shrinks over months without anything ever being wet.

Sublimation happens outdoors too. Washing left on a line on a dry, cold, windy day can freeze stiff and then become dry without ever thawing: the ice goes straight to vapour and is carried off. Freeze-drying uses this deliberately — food is frozen and the ice drawn off as vapour, so it dries without going soggy.

Because it is so selective, sublimation is a useful way of separating a mixture. If one substance sublimes and the other does not, gentle warming sends only the first away as gas, and you can collect it again on a cold surface by deposition. A mixture of iodine and sand separates this way.

One point of care: sublimation is a physical change. The substance stays the same throughout, nothing new is made, and cooling reverses it.

The heart of it

In sublimation the surface particles of a solid break free completely before the solid ever loosens into a liquid, so it becomes gas directly.

3

Where the idea came from

The word sublimation is much older than the science behind it. It was standard alchemical vocabulary for centuries: alchemists routinely heated solids and collected the material that reappeared as crystals on the cool upper part of the vessel. They used it constantly to purify substances, and they were right that it worked. What they could not give was an explanation, because they had no picture of matter as particles with attractions between them.

The most memorable example arrived in the early 1800s. Iodine was discovered by the French chemist Bernard Courtois in 1811, in a saltpetre works where he was processing seaweed ash. According to the usual account he added too much acid and a violet vapour rose from the vessel — a story that may be tidied up in the retelling, though the vapour is real and anyone can see it. It was striking enough that the substance was named after the Greek word for violet.

The modern explanation had to wait for the particle model to become firm during the nineteenth century. Only once scientists accepted that solids, liquids and gases differ in how tightly their particles are held, rather than being three kinds of stuff, did it become clear that there is nothing strange about skipping the liquid stage. Solid, liquid, gas is a common pattern, not a law every substance must obey.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is assuming every substance must melt before becoming a gas, so that any shrinking solid must have quietly melted at some point. Dry ice is the answer: the surface it sits on stays completely dry.

The second is confusing the white cloud above dry ice with the carbon dioxide, which is invisible; the cloud is water from the air, condensed by the cold. The third is reaching for "condensation" when the correct word for gas going straight to solid is deposition.

Commonly missed

A block of dry ice on a metal tray gets steadily smaller and finally disappears, but the tray is completely dry throughout. Someone says the dry ice must have melted and the liquid evaporated quickly. Are they correct?

It is a trap because "melted then evaporated" is the familiar route and does explain a disappearing solid, so it feels safe.

How to work it: Test the claim against the evidence. If the block had melted, there would have been liquid on the tray at some stage, and the tray would have been wet at least briefly. It never was, so no liquid stage occurred. The solid carbon dioxide turned directly into carbon dioxide gas, which is invisible and drifted away. That is sublimation, and it is why the substance is called dry ice.

5

Connected ideas

Sublimation is one of the changes of state, so it belongs with melting, boiling, freezing and condensation, and it is best understood through the particle model. Because only some substances do it, it also serves as a method of separating mixtures. Diffusion explains why you can smell a mothball across a room, and evaporation is its nearest relative: the same escape of surface particles, from a liquid instead of a solid.
