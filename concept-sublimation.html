Concept

Sublimation

Sublimation is a solid turning straight into a gas without ever becoming a liquid.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

The usual story of the states of matter goes in order. Heat a solid and it melts to a liquid; heat the liquid and it boils to a gas. Sublimation breaks the order. Some solids skip the middle step entirely and become gas directly, with no puddle at any point.

Dry ice is the clearest example. Dry ice is solid carbon dioxide, and left on a table it steadily shrinks away to nothing while the table stays dry — which is where the name comes from. Nothing melted, because carbon dioxide has no liquid stage at ordinary air pressure. It goes solid to gas and that is all it does. The white cloud that pours off it is not the carbon dioxide itself, which is invisible, but ordinary water vapour from the air condensing in the cold.

The reverse change also happens, and it has its own name: deposition, a gas turning straight back into a solid without becoming liquid on the way. Frost on a cold window on a winter morning is deposition. Water vapour in the air meets glass cold enough that it forms ice crystals directly, which is why frost appears as feathery crystal patterns rather than as droplets that later froze.

2

How it works

The particle model explains it well. In a solid, the particles are packed closely and held together by strong forces of attraction, vibrating in fixed positions. Heating makes them vibrate harder. In most substances the particles first loosen enough to slide past one another, which is melting, and only later break free altogether, which is boiling. In a substance that sublimes, the particles at the surface break away completely before the solid as a whole ever loosens into a liquid, so the liquid stage never appears.

Solid iodine is the classic laboratory case. Warmed gently, dark grey crystals give off a striking violet-purple vapour, and if that vapour touches a cold surface it forms grey crystals again directly — sublimation and deposition in one demonstration. Naphthalene, used in mothballs, does the same thing more slowly, which is why a mothball in a wardrobe shrinks over months and why you can smell it from across the room without anything ever being wet.

Sublimation happens quietly outdoors too. Washing left on a line on a dry, cold, windy day can freeze stiff and then, over hours, become dry without ever thawing: the ice goes straight to vapour and is carried off by the wind. The same slow loss shrinks snow on a mountain in bright sunshine even when the air stays below freezing. Freeze-drying makes deliberate use of it — food is frozen and then the ice is drawn off as vapour, so the food dries without ever going soggy, keeping its shape and flavour.

Because it is so selective, sublimation is a useful way of separating a mixture. If a mixture contains one substance that sublimes and one that does not, gentle warming sends only the first away as gas, and if you want it back you can collect it on a cold surface by deposition. A mixture of iodine and sand, or of ammonium chloride and salt, separates this way. Nothing else in the mixture is affected, because nothing else has anywhere to go.

One point of care: sublimation is a physical change, a change of state. The substance is the same substance throughout. Sublimed iodine is still iodine; dry ice becomes carbon dioxide gas, which was already what it was. Nothing new is made, and the change can be reversed by cooling.

The heart of it

In sublimation the surface particles of a solid break free completely before the solid ever loosens into a liquid, so it becomes gas directly.

3

Where the idea came from

The word sublimation is much older than the science behind it. It was standard alchemical vocabulary for centuries: alchemists routinely heated solids and collected the material that reappeared as crystals on the cool upper part of the vessel, and they called that process sublimation. They used it constantly as a way of purifying substances, and they were right that it worked. What they could not give was an explanation, because they had no picture of matter as particles with attractions between them.

The most memorable example arrived in the early 1800s. Iodine was discovered by the French chemist Bernard Courtois in 1811, in a saltpetre works where he was processing seaweed ash. According to the usual account he added too much acid and a violet vapour rose from the vessel — a story that may well be tidied up in the retelling, though the violet vapour is real and anyone can see it. The vapour was striking enough that the substance was named after the Greek word for violet.

The modern explanation had to wait for the particle model of matter to become firm during the nineteenth century. Only once scientists accepted that solids, liquids and gases differ in how tightly their particles are held, rather than being three different kinds of stuff, did it become clear that there is nothing strange about skipping the liquid stage. The solid, liquid, gas order is a common pattern, not a law that every substance must obey.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is assuming every substance must melt before it can become a gas, so that any shrinking solid must have quietly melted at some point. Dry ice is the answer to that: the surface it sits on stays completely dry.

The second is confusing the white cloud above dry ice with the carbon dioxide. Carbon dioxide gas is invisible. The visible cloud is water from the surrounding air, condensed by the cold. The third is forgetting that sublimation has a reverse, and reaching for "condensation" when the correct word for gas going straight to solid is deposition.

Commonly missed

A block of dry ice is placed on a metal tray and left for an hour. The block gets steadily smaller and finally disappears, but the tray is completely dry throughout. A student says the dry ice must have melted and the liquid evaporated quickly. Is she correct?

It is a trap because "melted then evaporated" is the familiar route and it does explain a disappearing solid, so it feels safe.

How to work it: Test the claim against the evidence. If the block had melted, there would have been liquid on the tray at some stage, and a liquid cold enough to be dry ice would not vanish instantly — the tray would have been wet at least briefly. It never was. So no liquid stage occurred. The solid carbon dioxide turned directly into carbon dioxide gas, which is invisible and drifted away. That is sublimation, and it is why the substance is called dry ice.

5

Connected ideas

Sublimation is one of the changes of state, so it belongs with melting, boiling, freezing and condensation, and it is best understood through the particle model. Because only some substances do it, it also serves as a method of separating mixtures. Diffusion explains why you can smell a mothball across a room once the vapour has formed, and evaporation is its nearest relative, being the same escape of surface particles from a liquid instead of a solid.
