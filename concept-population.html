Concept

Populations and Communities

A population is all the members of one kind of living thing in one place, and a community is all the populations living there together.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

If you count the rabbits in one wood, you have counted a population. A population is all the individuals of a single species that live in the same area and can breed with one another. The rabbits in that wood and the rabbits in a wood fifty miles away are usually treated as two populations, because they hardly ever meet.

Now count everything alive in that same wood: rabbits, foxes, oak trees, worms, mushrooms, beetles. All those populations together make a community. A community is the living part of a place. Add in the non-living part — the soil, the rainfall, the temperature, the light — and you have an ecosystem. So the three words fit inside each other: individuals make a population, populations make a community, and a community plus its surroundings makes an ecosystem.

Why bother separating them? Because they answer different questions. If you ask why the rabbits died out, you are asking a population question and you can study rabbits alone. If you ask why the whole wood changed, you are asking a community question, and studying rabbits alone will not tell you.

2

How it works

Populations change by exactly four routes, and it helps to know that the list is complete. Numbers go up through births and through immigration, which means individuals arriving from elsewhere. Numbers go down through deaths and through emigration, individuals leaving. Nothing else can change a population. Any explanation you give must work through one of those four.

A population that had unlimited food and no predators would grow in a particular shape. Suppose a pair of animals produces four offspring and every animal survives to breed. Two becomes four, four becomes eight, eight becomes sixteen. This is doubling, and doubling gets out of hand very quickly: twenty doublings from a single pair gives over a million. Nothing in nature grows like this for long, which tells you that something must always be holding populations back.

What holds them back are limiting factors. Food runs short. Space runs out. Predators find them. Disease spreads more easily when animals are crowded together. Notice that several of those get worse as the population gets bigger — a crowded population starves faster and catches disease faster. That is a self-correcting arrangement. Growth creates the very conditions that stop growth, so numbers tend to level off rather than rise for ever.

The level they settle around is called the carrying capacity — the number the habitat can support with the food, water and space it has. It is not a fixed number carved into the ground. A wet summer with more grass raises it; a hard winter lowers it. Real populations wobble above and below their carrying capacity rather than sitting exactly on it, because the corrections always arrive late.

Counting a whole population is usually impossible, so ecologists sample. A common method is the quadrat: place a square frame of known size on the ground at random spots, count what is inside, and scale up. If you find an average of 6 daisies per square metre and the field is 400 square metres, you estimate 2,400 daisies. The method works only if the placing is genuinely random, which is why people are told not to choose their spots — a person choosing will drift towards interesting patches without meaning to.

The heart of it

Populations do not grow for ever because the crowding that comes with growth is itself what limits growth.

3

Where the idea came from

Treating population as something you could put numbers on, and even write equations for, was a change in how biology was done. Two mathematicians arrived at the same tool independently in the 1920s: Alfred Lotka and Vito Volterra. Working separately, each produced a set of equations describing how the numbers of two species that interact should rise and fall over time, and each predicted a lag — a delay between the peak of one population and the peak of the other.

What prompted Volterra was not a laboratory. It was a puzzle about fish catches in the Adriatic Sea during the First World War. Fishing had been reduced during the war, and the proportions of the different fish being landed had shifted in a way nobody expected. The question was put to him, and he answered it with mathematics rather than with more fishing.

This is worth pausing on, because it shows something about how populations are studied. Neither man began by counting animals in a field. They began with a pattern in existing records and asked what rules could produce it. That is a legitimate way to do science, but it has an obvious weakness: equations can fit a pattern without being the true explanation. The Lotka–Volterra equations assume things that are not true of real habitats — that there is nothing else in the food web, that the environment never changes, that individuals are all identical. Real populations therefore only roughly follow them. The equations remain useful because they show what the interaction alone would do, which lets you see when something else must be at work.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest confusion is using "population" for everything living in a place. That is a community. Population is strictly one species. If a question asks about the population of a pond and lists frogs, fish and pondweed, it is testing whether you noticed.

The second is imagining carrying capacity as a ceiling that numbers can never pass. Populations overshoot it regularly. Animals breed in a good season, the numbers rise above what the habitat can feed, and then a die-back brings them down again. The carrying capacity is the level numbers return towards, not a wall.

Commonly missed

A population of deer on an island rises steadily for ten years, then falls sharply in the eleventh year, then settles at a level lower than the peak but higher than the starting number. Suggest why the sharp fall happened and why the final level is lower than the peak.

The trap is answering "they ran out of food" and stopping, which does not explain the second half of the question at all.

How to work it: The rise happened because births exceeded deaths while food was plentiful. The sharp fall happened because the population overshot the carrying capacity — there were more deer than the plants could feed, so many starved. The final level is lower than the peak because the peak was never sustainable in the first place. It may also be lower than the carrying capacity was originally, if the overcrowded deer damaged the plants by overgrazing, which would reduce what the island can support in future.

5

Connected ideas

Populations are the numbers that sit at each level of a food chain, and the clearest case of two populations pushing each other up and down is a predator and its prey. What allows a population to survive its particular habitat at all is adaptation. Counting populations well is also a lesson in method, because it depends entirely on sampling without bias.
