Concept

Populations and Communities

A population is all the members of one kind of living thing in one place; a community is all the populations living there together.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Count the rabbits in one wood and you have counted a population. A population is all the individuals of one species living in the same area and able to breed with one another. The rabbits in that wood and those fifty miles away are usually treated as two populations, because they hardly ever meet.

Now count everything alive in that wood: rabbits, foxes, oak trees, worms, mushrooms, beetles. Those populations together make a community, the living part of a place. Add the non-living part — soil, rainfall, temperature, light — and you have an ecosystem. The words nest: individuals make a population, populations make a community, a community plus its surroundings makes an ecosystem.

Why separate them? They answer different questions. Asking why the rabbits died out is a population question, answerable by studying rabbits alone. Asking why the whole wood changed is a community question, and rabbits alone will not tell you.

2

How it works

Populations change by exactly four routes, and the list is complete. Numbers rise through births and immigration, individuals arriving, and fall through deaths and emigration, individuals leaving. Nothing else can change a population, so any explanation must work through one of those four.

A population with unlimited food and no predators would grow in a particular shape. If a pair produces four offspring and all survive to breed, two becomes four, then eight, then sixteen. Doubling gets out of hand quickly — twenty doublings from one pair gives over a million. Nothing grows like this for long, which tells you something must always hold populations back.

What holds them back are limiting factors: food runs short, space runs out, predators find them, disease spreads more easily among crowded animals. Several of those worsen as the population grows, so a crowded population starves and sickens faster. That is self-correcting — growth creates the very conditions that stop growth, so numbers level off rather than rise for ever.

The level they settle around is the carrying capacity, the number the habitat can support with its food, water and space. It is not fixed: a wet summer raises it, a hard winter lowers it. Real populations wobble above and below it rather than sitting on it, because the corrections always arrive late.

Counting a whole population is usually impossible, so ecologists sample. The quadrat method places a square frame of known size at random spots, counts what is inside, and scales up: 6 daisies per square metre across a 400 square metre field estimates 2,400. It works only if the placing is genuinely random, which is why people are told not to choose their spots — anyone choosing drifts towards interesting patches without meaning to.

The heart of it

Populations do not grow for ever because the crowding that comes with growth is itself what limits growth.

3

Where the idea came from

Treating population as something you could put numbers on, and even write equations for, changed how biology was done. Two mathematicians arrived at the same tool independently in the 1920s: Alfred Lotka and Vito Volterra. Each produced equations describing how the numbers of two interacting species should rise and fall over time, and each predicted a lag — a delay between the peak of one population and the peak of the other.

What prompted Volterra was not a laboratory but a puzzle about fish catches in the Adriatic Sea during the First World War. Fishing had been reduced, and the proportions of fish being landed had shifted in a way nobody expected. The question was put to him, and he answered it with mathematics rather than more fishing.

That is worth pausing on. Neither man began by counting animals in a field; both began with a pattern in existing records and asked what rules could produce it. This is legitimate science with an obvious weakness: equations can fit a pattern without being the true explanation. The Lotka–Volterra equations assume things untrue of real habitats — nothing else in the food web, an unchanging environment, identical individuals — so real populations only roughly follow them. They remain useful because they show what the interaction alone would do, which lets you see when something else must be at work.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest confusion is using "population" for everything living in a place. That is a community; population is strictly one species. A question listing frogs, fish and pondweed as a pond's population is testing whether you noticed.

The second is imagining carrying capacity as a ceiling numbers can never pass. Populations overshoot regularly: animals breed in a good season, numbers rise above what the habitat can feed, and a die-back brings them down. Carrying capacity is the level numbers return towards, not a wall.

Commonly missed

A population of deer on an island rises steadily for ten years, falls sharply in the eleventh, then settles below the peak but above the starting number. Suggest why the sharp fall happened and why the final level is lower than the peak.

The trap is answering "they ran out of food" and stopping, which does not explain the second half of the question.

How to work it: The rise happened because births exceeded deaths while food was plentiful. The fall happened because the population overshot the carrying capacity — more deer than the plants could feed, so many starved. The final level is below the peak because the peak was never sustainable, and it may sit below the original carrying capacity too, if overgrazing damaged the plants and reduced what the island can support in future.

5

Connected ideas

Populations are the numbers at each level of a food chain, and the clearest case of two populations pushing each other up and down is a predator and its prey. What lets a population survive its habitat is adaptation. Counting populations well is also a lesson in method, since it depends on sampling without bias.
