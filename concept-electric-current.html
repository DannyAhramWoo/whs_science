Concept

Electric Current

Electric current is the flow of electric charge through a material.

Introduced in Chapter 3: Electric Circuits

1

What it means

You already know what a current is in a river. Water moves past a fixed point, and if you stood on a bridge you could count how much water went by each second. An electric current is the same idea, but what moves is not water. It is charge — a property carried by the tiny particles inside matter, in particular by electrons, the small negatively charged particles that sit on the outside of every atom.

In a metal wire, most electrons are locked to their atoms, but a few in each atom are loose. Those loose electrons can drift from atom to atom. When a battery is connected, it pushes on those loose electrons all along the wire at once, and they begin drifting in a shared direction. That drift is the current.

Because current is a flow, it needs a complete loop. If you cut the wire anywhere, charge has nowhere to go, so the flow stops everywhere at once — not just at the cut. This is why a single broken switch kills the whole circuit, and it is the single most useful fact in the whole topic.

Current is measured in amperes (amps, symbol A) using an ammeter. One amp is a very large number of electrons per second, which is why circuits in a classroom usually run at a fraction of an amp.

2

How it works

The battery does not manufacture electrons. The wire is already full of them, packed in tight, before you connect anything. What the battery supplies is a push — chemical reactions inside it pull electrons off one terminal and pile them onto the other, creating a difference in electrical pressure between the two ends. That pressure difference is called voltage, and it is what sets the electrons moving.

Here is the part that surprises people. Individual electrons drift extremely slowly — often less than a millimetre per second in a household wire. Yet the bulb lights instantly when you close the switch. The reason is that the wire is already full. The push travels along the wire at nearly the speed of light, so every electron in the loop starts moving at almost the same instant, including the ones already inside the bulb. It works like a long pipe already full of water: open the tap and water comes out the far end straight away, even though no single drop travelled the whole length.

The current is the same everywhere in a single loop. If 0.3 A flows out of the battery, 0.3 A flows through the bulb and 0.3 A flows back in. Nothing is "used up" — the charge that leaves comes back. What is used up is energy: each electron arrives back at the battery with less energy than it left with, having given that energy to the bulb as light and heat. Charge is conserved; energy is transferred. Mixing these two up causes more mistakes than anything else in this chapter.

A worked case. Two identical bulbs in one loop with one battery. Because the loop is single, the same current passes through both, so both glow with equal brightness. Add a third bulb and every bulb dims, because more components in the path make it harder for charge to flow, so the current in the whole loop falls — and it falls for all three equally.

The heart of it

Current is charge in motion around a complete loop, and it is not consumed on the way — only the energy it carries is.

3

Where the idea came from

In the 1780s in Bologna, the anatomist Luigi Galvani noticed that a dead frog's leg twitched when touched with metal instruments during an electrical experiment. He repeated it many times and concluded that the frog itself stored a special vital fluid he called animal electricity. Living tissue, he thought, was the source.

Alessandro Volta, a physicist at Pavia, disagreed. He noticed that the twitch appeared when two different metals touched the moist tissue, and suspected the metals, not the frog, were producing the electricity. To prove it, he removed the animal entirely: in 1800 he stacked discs of zinc and copper separated by brine-soaked cardboard, and got a steady flow of electricity with no frog anywhere near. That stack — the voltaic pile — was the first battery, and it is why we say "volt".

Galvani was wrong about the frog, but he was not simply foolish. Nerves really do signal using electrical impulses, so he had stumbled near a genuine fact without the tools to recognise what he was seeing. This is a common pattern in science: a wrong explanation of a real observation is still worth far more than no observation at all.

One more correction came much later. Early scientists guessed current flowed from the positive terminal to the negative one, and drew all their arrows that way. When electrons were identified around 1897, it turned out that in a metal wire the moving particles are negative and travel the opposite way. By then the arrows were in every book, so they were kept. The arrows on a circuit diagram show conventional current, and are simply backwards compared with the electrons. Nothing breaks because of this — but it is worth knowing it is a historical accident, not a fact about nature.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is imagining current as something the battery pours into the circuit and the bulb swallows, so that less current comes out of a bulb than went in. Nothing in a circuit eats charge. Picture a bicycle chain instead: the whole chain moves together, no links vanish at the sprocket, but the sprocket still receives energy from it.

The second error is thinking the electrons themselves race from the battery to the bulb before it can light. They do not. The wire is already stocked with electrons, and the bulb lights because the ones already inside it start moving.

Commonly missed

An ammeter placed before a bulb reads 0.4 A. A second ammeter is placed after the same bulb in the same single loop. What will it read?

The trap is the word "after". It invites you to think the bulb has consumed some of the current, so the second reading should be lower.

How to work it: Ask first whether there is any junction between the two meters where charge could leave the path. In a single loop there is none, so every electron passing the first meter must pass the second. Charge is not created or destroyed, so the reading is also 0.4 A. The bulb takes energy from the charge, not the charge itself — the current is unchanged and only the energy carried has dropped.

5

Connected ideas

Current only flows through materials that allow it, which is the difference between a conductor and an insulator. How much current flows depends on the resistance in the path, and how the current divides depends on whether components sit in series or in parallel. A current also creates a magnetic field around itself, which is the whole basis of the electromagnet, and the energy the current carries is a case of energy transfer.
