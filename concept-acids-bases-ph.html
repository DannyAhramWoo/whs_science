Concept

Acids, Bases and the pH Scale

Acids and bases are chemical opposites that cancel each other out, and the pH scale is the number line that says how far towards one extreme a solution sits.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Lemon juice makes your mouth screw up. Soap feels slippery between your fingers. Those two everyday facts are opposite ends of the same idea. Lemon juice is an acid, soap solution is a base, and if you mix enough of one into the other, both stop behaving that way.

An acid is a substance that, dissolved in water, releases hydrogen particles called hydrogen ions. An ion is simply a particle carrying an electric charge. Common acids include hydrochloric acid, HCl, which is the acid in your stomach, sulfuric acid, H2SO4, in car batteries, and citric acid in lemons and limes. A base is a substance that does the reverse: it takes those hydrogen ions out of the water again. A base that dissolves in water is also called an alkali. Sodium hydroxide, NaOH, is a strong one; the magnesium hydroxide in indigestion tablets is a mild one.

Because they are opposites, they cancel. Mixing an acid with a base is called neutralisation, and it produces a salt and water. A salt here does not only mean the salt on chips; it is the general name for the compound left when an acid has been neutralised. Hydrochloric acid plus sodium hydroxide gives sodium chloride, NaCl, plus water, H2O — table salt and water from two dangerous liquids.

Safety, and this is not optional. Laboratory chemicals are never identified by taste, and never by putting your nose over them. The sour taste of lemon and the bitterness of tonic water are facts about food, not laboratory methods. Strong acids and strong bases burn skin and eyes badly, and a strong base can be as damaging as a strong acid despite feeling soapy rather than sharp. Chemicals are identified with indicators and meters.

2

How it works

The pH scale runs from 0 to 14 and measures how acidic or alkaline a solution is. Below 7 is acidic, and the lower the number the more strongly acidic. Exactly 7 is neutral, which is where pure water sits. Above 7 is alkaline, and the higher the number the more strongly alkaline.

One point about the scale needs saying plainly, because it is easy to miss. Each step of 1 is a tenfold change, not a change of one unit. A solution of pH 3 is ten times more acidic than one of pH 4, and one hundred times more acidic than one of pH 5. So the gap between stomach acid at about pH 2 and orange juice at about pH 4 is not a small one; the stomach acid is roughly a hundred times stronger.

To find pH without a meter we use an indicator, a dye that changes colour depending on acidity. Litmus is the simplest: red in acid, blue in alkali. It only sorts solutions into two boxes. Universal indicator is a blend of dyes that runs through a range of colours, so it gives an approximate number: red at pH 1 to 2, orange and yellow around 3 to 6, green at 7, blue-green and blue around 8 to 11, and purple at 12 to 14.

Rough everyday values worth carrying in your head: battery acid about 1, stomach acid about 2, lemon juice about 2, vinegar about 3, black coffee about 5, milk about 6.5, pure water 7, blood about 7.4, baking soda solution about 9, soap about 10, oven cleaner about 13.

Acids also give themselves away by how they react. Acid plus reactive metal gives a salt plus hydrogen gas — drop magnesium into hydrochloric acid and it fizzes as hydrogen bubbles off. Acid plus carbonate gives a salt, water and carbon dioxide gas. That is why vinegar on baking soda foams over, and why a chemist testing an unknown white powder with dilute acid takes fizzing that turns limewater milky as evidence of a carbonate. Acid solutions also conduct electricity, because the ions in them are charged particles free to move.

The neutralisation idea has a direct use that examiners like. An antacid is a tablet for indigestion caused by excess stomach acid. For it to work it must have a pH above 7, because only a base can neutralise an acid. A tablet with pH 6 would add acid to the problem. Note the reasoning is about the property, not about the label on the packet: given a table of tablets and their pH values, the correct answer is whichever one is above 7 — and preferably only mildly above, since something at pH 13 in your stomach would do harm. Papers have set exactly this comparison, for instance in an IMSO 2017 theory paper.

The heart of it

Acids and bases are opposites that neutralise each other to make a salt and water, and pH puts a number on which side of neutral a solution is, with every step of 1 meaning ten times.

3

Where the idea came from

People knew sour things and slippery things long before anyone knew why. The first careful scientific work on it came from Robert Boyle in Oxford and London in the 1660s. Boyle noticed that certain plant extracts — including syrup of violets and a dye from lichen — changed colour in a repeatable way depending on which liquid he added them to. He wrote this up as a practical test, and in doing so he described the first acid-base indicators. That is the direct ancestor of the litmus paper on a school bench.

Boyle had the test long before anyone had the explanation. For the next two centuries chemists argued about what actually made a substance acidic. Lavoisier in the 1770s and 1780s guessed it was oxygen, and was confident enough to name the gas after the idea: oxygen comes from Greek roots meaning acid-former. He was wrong. Hydrochloric acid, HCl, contains no oxygen at all, and it is one of the sharpest acids in the laboratory. The hydrogen explanation only came together in the nineteenth century.

The scale itself is much more recent. Soren Sorensen, working at the Carlsberg laboratory in Copenhagen, introduced pH in 1909. His problem was practical: brewing beer depends on acidity, and saying merely acidic was not precise enough to control. He built a scale on the concentration of hydrogen ions. Because those concentrations range over an enormous span, he compressed them using powers of ten — which is exactly why one step on the finished scale means a tenfold change, rather than being an odd feature bolted on afterwards.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating the pH scale as if it were an ordinary ruler, so that pH 4 sounds only slightly more acidic than pH 5. It is ten times more acidic. Say tenfold to yourself every time you move one step.

The second is confusing strong with concentrated. A strong acid is one that releases its hydrogen ions readily; a concentrated one is one with a lot of acid in a little water. A dilute strong acid can be gentler than a concentrated weak one.

The third is assuming a higher pH is somehow safer. Alkalis at pH 13 or 14 damage skin and eyes badly. Neutral is safe; both extremes are not.

Commonly missed

Four indigestion tablets are dissolved in water and their pH values measured: W is 6.5, X is 8.0, Y is 7.0, Z is 4.5. Which tablet would best relieve indigestion caused by too much stomach acid, and why?

It is a trap because Y at pH 7.0 looks like the tidy answer — neutral, safe, balanced — and pupils reach for it on the grounds that neutral must be the goal.

How to work it: Ask what job the tablet must do. It must neutralise acid that is already there, and only a base can do that. A base has pH above 7. W at 6.5 and Z at 4.5 are themselves acidic and would make things worse. Y at 7.0 is neutral: it dilutes the acid but cannot cancel it, because neutralisation needs a base to react with. Only X, at pH 8.0, is a base, so X is the answer. It is also mildly basic rather than violently so, which is what you want in something being swallowed.

5

Connected ideas

Neutralisation is a chemical change, because new substances with new properties are made and you cannot separate them again by physical means. Acids do their work dissolved in water, so the idea of a solution underlies all of it, and the fact that acid solutions conduct electricity connects to how charged particles carry a current. The fizzing of an acid on a carbonate is the standard way of making carbon dioxide for a laboratory test.
