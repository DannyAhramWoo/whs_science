Science Stories

The Physical World

Every idea in the physics chapters was worked out by somebody, usually slowly and usually with a wrong turn or two along the way. These are the episodes behind the facts — the frog legs, the cannon barrels, the ramps and the prisms. Read them and the science stops being a list of things that are simply true, and becomes a set of arguments that people eventually won.

Chapter 1

The man who could not build a clock

Around 1604, in Padua, Galileo Galilei wanted to know how falling things speed up. There was an obvious problem. A dropped ball hits the floor in under a second, and in 1604 nobody owned a clock that could measure less than a second. Pendulum clocks did not yet exist. Galileo could see the thing he wanted to study and could not time it.

So he slowed it down. He took a long wooden beam, cut a straight groove down the middle, lined it with smooth parchment, and propped one end up a little way. A bronze ball rolling down that gentle slope is doing the same thing as a falling ball, only lazily enough to watch. He let it go from a quarter of the way up, then half, then the whole length, and recorded each run.

For timing, he used a large vessel of water with a thin pipe at the bottom. He opened the pipe when the ball started, closed it when the ball arrived, then weighed the water that had escaped. More water meant more time. It sounds crude, and it was, but he repeated each run many times and the weights agreed closely.

There is a second account, harder to verify, that he tied gut frets across the ramp and listened for the ball clicking over them, adjusting them until the clicks fell into an even rhythm. Galileo's father was a professional musician, and a trained ear catches a mistimed beat far better than an eye catches a moving ball. Whether he really did this, we cannot be sure. What he found was that distance grew with the square of the time — the ball covered 1, then 3, then 5 units in successive equal intervals.

What this tells us

When something is too fast, too small or too far away to measure directly, the trick is not better eyesight — it is finding a slower situation that obeys the same rule.

Chapter 2

A hammer and a feather, watched by millions

Galileo had claimed that if you removed the air, a heavy object and a light one would fall together. He could not remove the air. Neither could anyone else for another fifty years, and even then only from a small glass jar. The claim sat there, believed by physicists, quietly doubted by nearly everyone else, because it so plainly contradicts what happens when you drop a stone and a leaf.

On 2 August 1971, at the end of the Apollo 15 mission, Commander David Scott stood on the Moon at the foot of Hadley Rille with a television camera running. The Moon has no air worth mentioning. In his right hand he held a geological hammer weighing about 1.3 kilograms. In his left he held a falcon feather, chosen because the lunar module was named Falcon, weighing about 30 grams.

He said that a gentleman named Galileo had made a rather significant discovery a long time ago, and that he would confirm it. Then he let go of both at the same moment. They fell slowly, because gravity on the Moon is about one-sixth of ours, and they hit the grey dust together.

It was not a real experiment. Nobody doubted the outcome, the release was not perfectly simultaneous, and the film is not precise enough to measure anything useful. It was a demonstration, and demonstrations have their own value. Millions of people who would never accept it from a textbook watched a hammer and a feather land at the same instant and had nothing left to argue with. Both are still lying there.

What this tells us

Mass and weight are different things: the hammer had far more mass than the feather, yet on the Moon both weighed very little, and both fell at exactly the same rate.

Chapter 3

The quarrel over the twitching frog

In Bologna in the 1780s, Luigi Galvani was dissecting frogs. He noticed that a dead frog's leg would kick when it was touched with a metal instrument, especially near an electrical machine that was sparking across the room. Later he hung frog legs from brass hooks on an iron railing and found they twitched there too, with no machine anywhere.

Galvani drew a conclusion that seemed reasonable. The electricity, he decided, was inside the frog. Living tissue held a special kind of electricity of its own — animal electricity — and the metal merely released it. He published this in 1791 and it caused a sensation. It suggested that life itself might be electrical.

Alessandro Volta, a physicist in Pavia, was impressed at first and then increasingly unconvinced. He noticed something Galvani had treated as incidental: the twitching was strongest when two different metals were involved. Brass hook, iron railing. Volta's idea was that the electricity came from the junction of the two metals, and the frog leg was simply a very sensitive detector, wet and salty and good at conducting. The frog was the meter, not the battery.

The argument ran for years and was often bad-tempered. Volta settled it in 1800 by removing the frog entirely. He stacked discs of zinc and copper in alternating order, separated by cardboard soaked in brine, and got a steady current out of a pile with nothing living in it. This was the first battery.

Galvani was wrong about the source. But he was not wrong about everything — nerves and muscles genuinely do work by electrical signals, which is roughly what he was groping towards.

What this tells us

A battery works because two different materials are separated by something that lets charge move — which is why a circuit needs a complete loop and a source with two distinct terminals.

Chapter 4

The twitch of a needle in a lecture room

Hans Christian Oersted was a professor in Copenhagen who had spent years suspecting, on rather philosophical grounds, that electricity and magnetism must be connected. He had no evidence. Others had looked for a link and found nothing, and by 1820 the general view was that the two had nothing to do with each other.

In April 1820, during a lecture to advanced students, Oersted was demonstrating something else entirely — the heating of a wire by a current. A compass happened to be lying on the bench nearby. When he closed the circuit, the needle moved. Not much. Reports say it was a small deflection, and that most of the students did not notice it at all.

Oersted noticed. He also, to his credit, did not announce anything for three months. He went back to the laboratory and worked on it properly with stronger batteries, and found something genuinely odd: the needle did not point towards the wire or away from it. It settled at right angles, wrapping itself around the wire. The magnetic effect went in circles.

That was so strange that it delayed acceptance. Every known force acted along a line between two objects. This one acted sideways. Oersted published a short Latin pamphlet in July 1820, and within months Ampère in Paris had built the mathematics and Faraday was on his way to the electric motor.

The story is often told as pure luck. Not quite. The compass was on that bench because Oersted had been half-looking for exactly this for years. Luck arrived at the one bench in Europe prepared to recognise it.

What this tells us

An electric current always produces a magnetic field around itself — which is the whole basis of the electromagnet, and the reason a coil with a current in it behaves like a magnet.

Chapter 5

The crown, the bath, and a story we cannot check

The tale goes like this. King Hiero II of Syracuse, in the third century BC, gave a goldsmith gold to make a crown. He suspected the smith had kept some gold and mixed in cheaper silver. He asked Archimedes to find out, without damaging the crown. Archimedes, stepping into a full bath and watching the water spill over the edge, realised that the overflow measured the volume of his own body. He leapt out and ran through the streets shouting a Greek word meaning "I have found it".

Be careful with this one. Archimedes wrote a great deal and never mentioned it. The story appears in Vitruvius, a Roman architect writing roughly two hundred years after Archimedes died. Two centuries is a long time for a story to improve in the telling, and the naked run through Syracuse has the ring of a good anecdote rather than a record.

There is a further difficulty. Measuring a crown's volume by displaced water would need accuracy beyond what was practical then — the difference between a gold crown and an adulterated one is tiny. The method Archimedes actually describes in On Floating Bodies is better: weigh the crown in air, then weigh it while submerged. The upthrust makes it seem lighter, and how much lighter depends on its density. That comparison is far more sensitive, and needs only a good balance.

So the bath may be embroidered, or invented. What is not in doubt is the principle he set down in writing: an object in a fluid is pushed up by a force equal to the weight of fluid it displaces. Ships are still designed with it. Whether the goldsmith was guilty, nobody knows.

What this tells us

Whether something floats depends on density, not size — and upthrust from the displaced fluid is what holds a floating object up.

Chapter 6

The cannon that would not stop getting hot

For most of the eighteenth century, heat was thought to be a substance. It was called caloric: an invisible, weightless fluid that soaked into things and made them hot, and flowed out when they cooled. It was a decent theory. It explained why heat moves from hot things to cold ones, and why a hot object cools — it is running out of caloric. Serious chemists believed it.

In the 1790s Benjamin Thompson, an American who had taken the British side in the revolution and ended up as Count Rumford in the service of the Elector of Bavaria, was supervising the boring of cannon at the Munich arsenal. Boring a cannon means driving a blunt steel bit into solid brass while horses turn the drive. The metal gets extremely hot. Under caloric theory this made sense: shaving the brass into chips supposedly squeezed the caloric out.

Rumford tested it. He used a deliberately dull borer that produced almost no chips, and the heat came out anyway. He surrounded the cylinder with a wooden box holding about two and a half gallons of water, and let the horses walk. After two and a half hours the water boiled. He reported that the onlookers were astonished, and admitted he took a childish pleasure in their astonishment.

The point was that the heat never ran out. As long as the horses walked, heat kept coming. No finite amount of fluid inside the brass could do that. Rumford concluded that heat was not a substance at all but a kind of motion.

He did not win immediately. Caloric survived another forty years, partly because Rumford could not say precisely how much motion made how much heat. That took Joule.

What this tells us

Heat is not stuff an object contains — it is energy on the move, and work done against friction can produce it without limit.

Chapter 7

Newton shuts out the light

In 1666, with Cambridge closed by plague, Isaac Newton was at home in Lincolnshire with a glass prism bought at a fair. Prisms were toys. Everyone knew they threw coloured patterns on the wall, and the accepted explanation was that the glass somehow dyed or corrupted the light — white light was pure, and passing through glass spoiled it into colours.

Newton darkened his room, cut a small round hole in the shutter, and let one narrow beam of sunlight through onto the prism. The patch that landed on the far wall was not round. It was a long stripe, five times longer than it was wide, running from red at one end to violet at the other. That shape was the clue. If the glass were merely dyeing the light, why should the beam be stretched out in one direction?

Then came the part that settled it, which he called the experimentum crucis. He put a board with a small hole in the path of the spread-out colours, so only one colour — pure red — passed through, and sent that red beam into a second prism. If prisms manufacture colour, the second should have altered it further. It did not. The red bent a little more, and stayed red.

Finally he took the whole spread of colours and brought them back together with a lens, and got white light again. Colour was not being added by the glass. It was already in the sunlight, and the prism simply bent each colour by a different amount, spreading a mixture into its parts.

Not everyone accepted this. Robert Hooke rejected it outright, and the resulting quarrel was bitter enough that Newton sat on much of his optical work for thirty years.

What this tells us

White light is a mixture of colours already present in it, and a prism separates them rather than creating them.

Chapter 8

The brewer with the very good thermometers

James Prescott Joule ran a brewery in Salford, near Manchester, and did physics in the parts of the day the brewery left him. This turned out to matter. Brewing depends on temperature control, and Joule had thermometers finer than most laboratories used — he could read differences of around one two-hundredth of a degree, at a time when many scientists would not have trusted a fiftieth.

In the 1840s he built an apparatus of almost aggressive simplicity. An insulated barrel of water. Inside it, a paddle wheel. Outside, a cord over a pulley carrying known weights. Let the weights fall a measured distance, and the paddle churns the water. Then read the thermometer.

The warming was minuscule. A weight falling the height of a room raises the water by a fraction of a degree, which is why nobody had done the experiment convincingly before — most people could not see the effect at all. Joule ran it over and over, correcting for heat leaking through the walls, and arrived at a number: a fixed amount of mechanical work always produced the same fixed amount of heat.

When he presented this in 1843 the reception was cool. He was a brewer, not a university man, and his claimed precision looked implausible. William Thomson, later Lord Kelvin, heard him at Oxford in 1847, took him seriously, and helped bring the work into the mainstream.

The honeymoon story is charming and probably too neat: Joule is said to have carried thermometers to the Alps in 1847 to test whether water at the foot of a waterfall is warmer than at the top. Thomson recalled meeting him there with a very long thermometer. Whether it was truly a honeymoon experiment we cannot be sure, and the effect, though real, was far too small to measure at those falls.

What this tells us

Energy changes form rather than disappearing: the movement of the falling weight does not vanish, it turns into heat in the water.

Chapter 9

Reading sunlight out of a lump of coal

Coal was mined for centuries before anyone knew what it was. It burned, it came out of the ground, and that was enough. Various theories circulated: that it was a mineral like any other, or a bitumen that had oozed underground.

The evidence was sitting in the mines all along. Coal seams are threaded with the shapes of plants — fern fronds, bark with a diamond pattern, hollow stems, sometimes whole upright tree trunks running through the roof of a gallery. In the 1830s and 1840s, geologists in the coalfields of Britain and continental Europe catalogued these fossils and made the argument stick: a coal seam is a compressed swamp forest, buried and squeezed for some three hundred million years. The word Carboniferous, coined in 1822, means coal-bearing.

The second half of the idea came from a different direction. Once it was understood that plants build themselves using the energy of sunlight, and once Joule and others had established that energy is conserved as it changes form, the two halves could be put together. Julius Robert Mayer, a German physician who was among the first to state the conservation of energy, wrote in the 1840s that plants capture sunlight and store it, and that this stored sunlight is what we later burn.

Mayer's work was ignored for years and he was treated badly for it. But the conclusion holds. The heat from a coal fire is sunlight that fell on a swamp before there were dinosaurs, was captured by ferns, buried, and kept. Burning it releases energy that has been in storage for three hundred million years — which is also the reason it cannot be replaced on any timescale that helps us.

What this tells us

Nearly all our energy traces back to the Sun, and fossil fuels are non-renewable simply because the sunlight in them took millions of years to store.

Why these stories matter

Not one of these people started out knowing the answer. Galvani was wrong about his frogs. Caloric was believed by careful, intelligent chemists for a hundred years. Newton had to fight for an idea about prisms that now takes one sentence to state, and Joule was dismissed because he made beer for a living. Science was not handed down finished — it was argued into shape by people who guessed, tested, quarrelled, and revised.

Notice also which mistakes were useful. Galvani's animal electricity was wrong, but it was wrong in a way that could be checked, and checking it produced the battery. Caloric was wrong, but it was specific enough that Rumford's cannon could break it. A clear idea that turns out to be false is worth far more than a vague statement that is technically right, because only the clear one can be tested. Being precise and being wrong is how the work gets done.
