Concept

Population Growth Curves

A population growth curve is a graph of numbers against time, and its shape tells you whether a population is starting slowly, multiplying freely, or has run into the limits of what its habitat can supply.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Put a few bacteria into a flask of nutrient broth, count them every hour, and plot the counts against time. What you get is not a straight line. It is a curve with a distinct shape, and that shape repeats whenever any population enters a new place with room to grow — bacteria in a flask, rabbits let loose on an island, yeast in a bottle of sugar solution.

A population is all the individuals of one species living in one area. It changes for four reasons only: births and immigration (individuals arriving) push it up; deaths and emigration (individuals leaving) push it down. Every curve you will ever read is those four things in combination.

The characteristic shape is called the S-shaped curve, or the logistic curve, because it looks like a letter S lying slightly flattened: slow, then steep, then flat. Reading the phases off a graph, naming them, and explaining what causes each is exactly what gets examined.

2

How it works

The S-curve has four phases. Learn the names alongside the reasons.

Lag phase. The curve is nearly flat at the start. Numbers barely rise. There is plenty of food and space, so nothing is holding growth back — the delay is that the organisms are settling in. Bacteria are making the enzymes they need to digest this particular food; larger animals are establishing territory and reaching breeding age. There are also very few individuals, so even rapid doubling adds few in absolute terms. Two becoming four is a doubling, but it is still only two extra.

Exponential phase. The curve turns sharply upward and becomes steep. This is also called the log phase or the rapid growth phase. Resources are still abundant, and now there are enough individuals for doubling to add large numbers. Each generation multiplies the whole population: 100 becomes 200, then 400, then 800. This is exponential growth, which means growth by repeated multiplication rather than by adding a fixed amount. It is the fastest the population will ever grow, and it cannot last.

Stationary phase. The curve levels off into a horizontal line, sometimes wobbling slightly up and down. The population has reached the largest size the habitat can support indefinitely, which is called the carrying capacity. Numbers are not frozen — births and deaths continue — but they now roughly balance, so the total stays about the same.

Decline phase. In a closed container the curve eventually falls. The food is exhausted and the waste products the organisms have excreted have built up to poisonous levels. Deaths exceed births. This phase appears in the bacterial flask because nothing new comes in and nothing is removed; a natural habitat is usually replenished, so it often stays at the stationary level instead.

What flattens the curve are limiting factors, and you should be able to name four. Food runs short as more individuals compete for the same supply. Space runs out — nesting sites, territory, room to grow. Disease spreads far more easily in a crowded population, because individuals contact one another more often. Predators increase in response to abundant prey, and eat more of them. Notice that the first three get worse specifically because the population is dense; this is why the brake applies itself automatically as numbers rise.

Contrast this with the J-shaped curve. Here growth continues exponentially with no levelling off at all, so the graph looks like a letter J: flat, then almost vertical. This happens when a population meets no limiting factor for a while — a species introduced to a new country with no natural predators, or algae in a lake suddenly flooded with nutrients. A J-curve is not a stable state. It ends in a crash: the population overshoots the carrying capacity, consuming resources faster than they are replaced, and then numbers collapse steeply as the exhausted habitat cannot feed them. The crash can drop the population far below the carrying capacity, because the overshoot damaged the habitat itself — grassland grazed down to bare soil supports fewer animals afterwards than it did before.

A related graph is the age structure pyramid, which plots how many people fall into each age band, youngest at the bottom. A country with a very wide base — a large proportion of children — is growing rapidly, because those children will become parents and there are many of them relative to the older bands. A country with a narrow base and a bulging middle or top has a stable or shrinking population. The reasoning is the same in both graphs: read the shape, then say what the shape implies about future numbers.

The heart of it

Populations grow exponentially only while resources last, and level off at the carrying capacity because the very crowding they create brings food shortage, disease and predators to bear.

3

Where the idea came from

The starting point was Thomas Malthus, an English clergyman and scholar, whose Essay on the Principle of Population appeared in 1798. His argument was that populations grow by multiplication while food supply grows only by addition, so population must always press against the limits of food. His specific predictions about human famine proved wrong, largely because he could not foresee the agricultural and industrial changes ahead. But the underlying insight — that unchecked multiplication must eventually collide with finite resources — was sound, and it directly influenced both Darwin and Wallace.

Malthus had no mathematics for what happens when the collision occurs. That was supplied by the Belgian mathematician Pierre-François Verhulst, who published the logistic equation around 1838 while trying to model Belgium's population. His curve rose exponentially at first and then bent over to approach a ceiling, which is the S-curve as we still draw it. His work was largely ignored and was rediscovered independently in the 1920s by Raymond Pearl and Lowell Reed in the United States, who did not initially know of it.

The honest caveat is that real populations rarely follow the smooth curve exactly. They overshoot, oscillate around the carrying capacity, and are disturbed by weather and disease. The logistic curve is a model — a simplified picture that captures the main behaviour — not a prediction of what any particular species will do.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is misreading the lag phase as "nothing is growing" or "conditions are poor". Conditions in the lag phase are excellent; that is why the exponential phase follows. Growth is slow because numbers are tiny and organisms are adjusting.

The second is reading the flat stationary phase as "no births and no deaths". Both continue at a high rate. What has happened is that they have come into balance. A flat line means births equal deaths, not that nothing is happening.

The third is confusing the two shapes. An S-curve levels off because limiting factors applied in time; a J-curve does not, and therefore ends in a crash. If a graph shows a steep rise followed by a steep fall with no plateau between, that is overshoot, and the explanation is that resources were consumed faster than they could be replaced.

Past IMSO papers have tested this by labelling points on a bacterial growth curve and asking for the phase names, and by presenting age structure pyramids for several countries and asking which is growing fastest.

Commonly missed

Deer are introduced to an island with no predators. Their numbers rise steeply for twenty years, peak far above the level the island later supports, then fall sharply and settle at a lower number than the peak. Explain the shape.

The trap is answering only "they ran out of food". That explains the fall but not why the population settles below the peak rather than back at it.

How to work it: Take it in three parts. The steep rise is exponential growth with no predators and abundant vegetation, so almost no limiting factor applies — a J-shaped section. The peak far above the sustainable level is overshoot: deer kept breeding while eating the plants faster than the plants regrew, so the damage was already done before numbers stopped rising. The sharp fall is starvation, deaths exceeding births. It settles lower than the peak because the overgrazing reduced the vegetation itself, so the island's carrying capacity is now genuinely lower than it was before the deer arrived. A good answer names overshoot and says the carrying capacity was damaged, not merely reached.

5

Connected ideas

These curves are the mathematics of how populations behave, and the levelling off is usually caused by predator and prey pressure or by competition within an ecosystem. Bacteria in a flask are the standard example because microorganisms grow fast enough to show all four phases in a day. Overproduction of offspring is also the second step of natural selection, and a nutrient-driven J-curve is what an algal bloom in eutrophication actually is.

In one line

A population grows slowly at first, then multiplies exponentially while resources last, then levels off at the carrying capacity as food, space, disease and predators apply the brakes — and if nothing applies them in time it makes a J-shaped curve, overshoots, and crashes.
