Concept

Population Growth Curves

A population growth curve is a graph of numbers against time, and its shape tells you whether a population is starting slowly, multiplying freely, or has run into the limits of what its habitat can supply.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Put a few bacteria into a flask of nutrient broth, count them every hour, and plot the counts against time. What you get is not a straight line but a curve with a distinct shape, and that shape repeats whenever a population enters a new place with room to grow.

A population is all the individuals of one species in one area. Births and immigration push it up; deaths and emigration push it down.

The characteristic shape is the S-shaped curve, or logistic curve, because it looks like a flattened letter S: slow, then steep, then flat. Reading the phases off a graph and naming them is what gets examined.

2

How it works

The S-curve has four phases. Learn the names with the reasons.

Lag phase. The curve is nearly flat. There is plenty of food and space, so nothing holds growth back — the organisms are settling in, bacteria making the enzymes needed to digest this food. There are also very few individuals, so even doubling adds little: two becoming four is only two extra.

Exponential phase. The curve turns sharply upward. Resources are still abundant, and now there are enough individuals for doubling to add large numbers: 100 becomes 200, then 400, then 800. This is exponential growth, growth by repeated multiplication rather than by adding a fixed amount, and it cannot last.

Stationary phase. The curve levels into a horizontal line. The population has reached the largest size the habitat can support indefinitely, the carrying capacity. Births and deaths continue but now balance, so the total holds steady.

Decline phase. In a closed container the curve eventually falls: food is exhausted and excreted waste has built up to poisonous levels, so deaths exceed births. A natural habitat is usually replenished and stays at the stationary level instead.

What flattens the curve are limiting factors: food runs short as more individuals compete for it, space runs out, disease spreads more easily in a crowd, and predators increase in response to abundant prey. The first three worsen precisely because the population is dense, which is why the brake applies itself as numbers rise.

Contrast the J-shaped curve, where growth continues exponentially with no levelling off, as when a species is introduced with no natural predators. A J-curve is not stable. It ends in a crash: the population overshoots the carrying capacity, consuming resources faster than they are replaced, and numbers collapse as the exhausted habitat cannot feed them, often falling below the original carrying capacity because the overshoot damaged the habitat itself.

A related graph is the age structure pyramid, plotting people by age band, youngest at the bottom. A wide base means rapid growth; a narrow base means a stable or shrinking population.

The heart of it

Populations grow exponentially only while resources last, and level off at the carrying capacity because the very crowding they create brings food shortage, disease and predators to bear.

3

Where the idea came from

The starting point was Thomas Malthus, whose Essay on the Principle of Population appeared in 1798. He argued that populations grow by multiplication while food supply grows only by addition, so population must press against the limits of food. His famine predictions proved wrong, but the insight that unchecked multiplication must collide with finite resources was sound, and it influenced Darwin.

Malthus had no mathematics for the collision. That came from the Belgian mathematician Pierre-François Verhulst, who published the logistic equation around 1838 while modelling Belgium's population. His curve rose exponentially then bent over to approach a ceiling — the S-curve as we still draw it. It was largely ignored, and rediscovered in the 1920s.

The honest caveat is that real populations rarely follow the smooth curve exactly. They overshoot, oscillate around the carrying capacity, and are disturbed by weather and disease. It is a model, not a prediction.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is misreading the lag phase as "conditions are poor". Conditions there are excellent, which is why the exponential phase follows.

The second is reading the flat stationary phase as "no births and no deaths". Both continue at a high rate and have come into balance: a flat line means births equal deaths.

The third is confusing the shapes. An S-curve levels off because limiting factors applied in time; a J-curve does not, and ends in a crash. A steep rise then a steep fall with no plateau is overshoot.

Past IMSO papers have labelled points on a bacterial growth curve and asked for the phase names.

Commonly missed

Deer are introduced to an island with no predators. Their numbers rise steeply for twenty years, peak far above the level the island later supports, then fall sharply and settle lower than the peak. Explain the shape.

The trap is answering only "they ran out of food". That explains the fall but not why the population settles below the peak rather than back at it.

How to work it: Three parts. The steep rise is exponential growth with no predators and abundant vegetation — a J-shaped section. The peak above the sustainable level is overshoot: the deer kept breeding while eating plants faster than they regrew. The sharp fall is starvation. It settles lower than the peak because overgrazing reduced the vegetation itself, so the carrying capacity is now genuinely lower than before the deer arrived. A good answer names overshoot and says the carrying capacity was damaged, not merely reached.

5

Connected ideas

These curves describe how populations behave, and the levelling off is usually caused by predator and prey pressure or competition within an ecosystem. Bacteria are the standard example, since microorganisms show all four phases in a day. An algal bloom in eutrophication is a nutrient-driven J-curve.

In one line

A population grows slowly at first, multiplies exponentially while resources last, then levels off at the carrying capacity as food, space, disease and predators apply the brakes — and if nothing applies them in time it makes a J-shaped curve, overshoots, and crashes.
