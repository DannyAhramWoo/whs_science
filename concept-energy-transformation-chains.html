Concept

Energy Transformation Chains

An energy transformation chain lists the forms energy takes as it passes through a device, from its starting store to the job it finally does.

Introduced in Chapter 8: Energy and Its Changes

1

What it means

Switch on a torch and a bulb glows. That energy was already stored in the battery's chemicals, and it was handed along a short chain until it left as light.

Writing a chain means naming each form in order, with arrows between them. For the torch: chemical energy in the battery becomes electrical energy in the wires, which becomes light energy at the bulb. A transformation is one of those arrows.

The idea works for anything: a wind turbine, a falling stone, a power station. Writing the chain tells you what a device really does.

2

How it works

The rule underneath everything is the conservation of energy: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only changed from one form into another. The total at the end of the chain always equals the total at the start. What changes is the form, and how useful that form is.

Take a coal-fired power station, which is the standard example and worth learning stage by stage. The coal holds chemical energy — energy locked in the way its atoms are bonded together. Burning it in the boiler releases that as thermal energy, meaning heat, which boils water into steam. The rushing steam turns a turbine, so thermal energy becomes kinetic energy, the energy of movement. The spinning turbine drives a generator, which turns kinetic energy into electrical energy. Written out: chemical, then thermal, then kinetic, then electrical. Four forms, three arrows, one sentence per component.

For an unfamiliar device, work through it part by part. A hairdryer: electrical energy in the cable becomes thermal energy at the element and kinetic energy at the fan, plus unwanted sound.

At every arrow, some energy goes somewhere useless, nearly always as heat. This is not a fault in the machine. It always happens, so the chain loses value along its length. If 100 J of chemical energy enters the boiler, perhaps 40 J emerges as electrical energy and 60 J leaves as waste heat.

Efficiency is the fraction that stays useful. A machine that turns most of its input into the form you wanted is efficient; one losing most as heat is inefficient. Longer chains tend to be less efficient, because each extra arrow is another chance to lose some. Burning fuel to make electricity, then running a heater, warms a room worse than burning the fuel in the room.

Chains also let you audit energy with numbers. A roller-coaster car at the top of a drop has 5000 J of gravitational potential energy. Partway down it has 3000 J of kinetic energy and 1200 J of potential energy. 3000 plus 1200 is 4200 J. Subtract that from 5000 J and 800 J is missing. It has not vanished. Friction and air resistance have turned it into heat in the track, the wheels and the air.

The heart of it

The total energy never changes along a chain, but at every step some turns into heat, so the useful amount only ever falls.

3

Where the idea came from

Before the 1840s, heat and motion were thought separate. Heat was believed to be an invisible weightless fluid called caloric, flowing from hot bodies into cold ones. On that view there was nothing to transform.

An early crack appeared around 1798, when Benjamin Thompson, also known as Count Rumford, supervised the boring of cannon barrels in Munich. The drilling produced heat without limit, for as long as the horses kept turning the drill. A fluid squeezed from the metal should run out.

The measurement that settled it came from James Prescott Joule in England during the 1840s. Joule ran a paddle wheel in an insulated barrel of water, driven by falling weights, and measured the tiny temperature rise. He could then say how much mechanical work produced how much heat: the mechanical equivalent of heat. Heat and work could now be counted in one currency, and the unit of energy carries his name.

Joule's results were doubted at first, because the temperature changes were tiny and established scientists distrusted his thermometers. Over the next decade the measurements held up and caloric was abandoned.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking energy gets "used up". A flat phone battery has not destroyed its energy; it has sent it out as light, sound and heat, spread into the surroundings beyond recovery. Read "used up" as "spread too thinly to be useful".

The second is skipping stages. Saying a power station turns chemical energy into electrical energy is true but useless, since it hides the boiler and the turbine. Name a form for each component.

The third is forgetting the waste term in a numbers question. One IMSO paper follows a roller-coaster down a drop and requires the missing energy to be found by subtracting the kinetic and remaining potential energy from the starting total.

Commonly missed

A hydroelectric dam holds water 60 m above its turbines. Write the energy transformation chain, and explain why the electrical energy produced is always less than the potential energy the water started with.

The trap is the answer "potential to electrical". It skips the fall and the turbine, the parts doing the work, and leaves the second half unanswered.

How to work it: take the components in order. The stored water has gravitational potential energy. Falling turns that into kinetic energy. The moving water spins the turbine, which drives a generator, turning kinetic energy into electrical energy. So: gravitational potential, then kinetic, then electrical. As for the shortfall, at every arrow some energy leaves as heat and sound: water rubbing pipe walls, warming bearings, the humming turbine. Conservation still holds. The total is unchanged; only the useful share has dropped.

5

Connected ideas

Every chain applies conservation of energy, the rule that makes the books balance. Kinetic and potential energy swap most visibly inside a chain, and heat is what the waste almost always becomes. Work moves energy between stores, and fossil fuels start most industrial chains.

In one line
