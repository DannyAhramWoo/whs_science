Concept

Solubility and Solubility Curves

Solubility is the largest mass of a substance that will dissolve in a fixed amount of solvent at a given temperature, and a solubility curve is the graph of how that limit changes as the temperature changes.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Stir a spoon of sugar into a glass of cold water and it vanishes. Stir in a second and a third and they vanish too. Keep going and eventually a layer of sugar sits on the bottom and refuses to move however long you stir. The water has taken all it can hold. That limit is solubility.

Some words first. The substance being dissolved is the solute, the liquid doing the dissolving is the solvent, and the result is a solution. Solubility is the maximum mass of solute that will dissolve in a stated mass of solvent at a stated temperature. It is usually written as grams per 100 g of water, and both conditions must be stated, because the number is meaningless without them.

A solution holding all it can at that temperature is saturated. Add more solute to a saturated solution and it simply sits there undissolved. A solution that could still take more is unsaturated.

Note what solubility is not. It is not about speed. Stirring, warming and grinding the solute into powder all make dissolving happen faster, but none of them changes the final amount that will dissolve. Only the temperature, and the choice of solute and solvent, set the limit.

2

How it works

For most solids, solubility rises with temperature. Hot water dissolves more sugar, more copper sulfate, more potassium nitrate than cold water does. The reason is that the solvent particles are moving faster at higher temperature, so they collide with the solid harder and more often, and are better able to pull its particles away and keep them surrounded and apart.

Gases do the opposite, and this reversal is what papers test. A gas dissolves less in hot water than in cold. A dissolved gas particle is only loosely held among the water particles, and heating gives it enough energy to escape back into the air. This is not a small exception; it explains several everyday things at once.

A fizzy drink left in the sun goes flat, because the dissolved carbon dioxide comes out of solution as the liquid warms — you can watch the bubbles form on the inside of the glass. River and lake water in a hot summer holds less dissolved oxygen, so fish are more likely to suffocate in warm shallow water than in cold deep water. Warm water discharged from a power station into a river reduces the oxygen available to everything living in it. And bubbles appear inside a pan of water long before it boils, because the air dissolved in the cold water is being driven out first.

A solubility curve puts this on a graph: temperature along the horizontal axis, solubility in grams per 100 g of water up the vertical axis. Each substance has its own curve. A point on the curve is a saturated solution. A point below the curve is unsaturated, meaning more could still dissolve. A point above the curve cannot last, and the excess appears as solid crystals.

Reading one is two skills. To find the solubility at a temperature, go up from that temperature to the curve and across to the vertical axis. To find how much crystallises out on cooling, read the solubility at the start temperature, read it again at the end temperature, and subtract.

Worked example. Potassium nitrate has a solubility of about 110 g per 100 g of water at 60 degrees C and about 32 g per 100 g of water at 20 degrees C. A saturated solution is made with 100 g of water at 60 degrees C and then cooled to 20 degrees C. How much solid appears?

At 60 degrees C the water holds 110 g. At 20 degrees C it can hold only 32 g. The difference, 110 − 32 = 78 g, can no longer stay dissolved, so 78 g of potassium nitrate crystallises out. That is the whole calculation, and it is worth noticing that it is a subtraction and nothing more.

Now scale it. If the solution had been made with 50 g of water instead of 100 g, every figure halves: it would hold 55 g at 60 degrees C and 16 g at 20 degrees C, so 55 − 16 = 39 g would crystallise. Always check how much solvent the question actually gives you, because the table is quoted per 100 g and the question rarely uses exactly 100 g.

Papers also ask candidates to plot the curve themselves from a table of results and then read a value beyond the last data point, which is called extrapolating — extending the line in the direction it is already going. When plotting, marks go for a sensible scale that uses most of the paper, for both axes labelled with quantity and unit, and for a smooth curve through the points rather than dot-to-dot lines. Those presentation marks are worth as much as the reading itself, and this is how IMSO 2009 papers set the topic. Extrapolation should also be treated with care, since you are predicting where no measurements were taken.

The heart of it

Heating lets a solvent hold more of most solids but less of any gas, and a solubility curve is simply the line showing where saturation sits at each temperature.

3

Where the idea came from

Solubility has no single discoverer, because people exploited it long before they measured it. Salt has been recovered from seawater by evaporation for thousands of years, and sugar refiners have grown crystals by cooling concentrated syrups since the medieval period. Both trades depended on knowing that a hot solution holds more than a cold one, but what they had was a craft rule, not a number.

Turning it into a number was eighteenth and nineteenth century work, and it depended on the balance. Once chemists weighed the solid before dissolving and the residue after evaporating, solubility became a measurable property that could be tabulated for each substance at each temperature. Those tables are the raw material of every solubility curve drawn since.

The behaviour of gases was sorted out later. William Henry, working in Manchester around 1803, found that the mass of a gas dissolving in a liquid is proportional to the pressure of that gas above the liquid — now called Henry's law. It is why a fizzy drink is bottled under pressure and fizzes the moment the cap releases it. Henry worked closely with John Dalton in the same city, and a theory of gases as separate particles made it easier to picture a dissolved gas escaping. The temperature effect on gases, though, was known from observation well before anyone could explain it.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is generalising from solids to gases. Hot dissolves more is true for sugar and salt and wrong for carbon dioxide and oxygen. Deal with them as two separate rules.

The second is confusing rate with amount. A pupil asked why sugar dissolves better in hot tea often answers that stirring is easier, but the real point is that the limit itself is higher.

The third is forgetting to scale for the mass of solvent, since curves are drawn per 100 g of water while questions often supply 50 g or 200 g.

Commonly missed

A bottle of fizzy drink is left in a warm car all afternoon. When opened it fizzes violently and then tastes flat. A second identical bottle kept in the fridge opens quietly and still tastes fizzy. Explain both differences.

It is a trap because the warm bottle fizzes more on opening, which looks like evidence that warmth helped the gas dissolve, when it shows the opposite.

How to work it: Carbon dioxide is a gas, and gases become less soluble as temperature rises. In the warm bottle the water can no longer hold all its dissolved carbon dioxide, so gas comes out of solution and builds up pressure in the space above the liquid. Releasing the cap releases that pressure suddenly, which is the violent fizz — it is gas leaving, not gas dissolved. Having left, it is gone, so the drink tastes flat. The cold bottle kept more of its carbon dioxide dissolved, so less pressure built up and more fizz remained in the liquid.

5

Connected ideas

Solubility is the quantity behind every solution, and it decides which separation methods will work, since a dissolved solid cannot be filtered but can be recovered by evaporating or by cooling until it crystallises. It also explains why one component runs further than another in chromatography and why alcohol suits some solutes better than water. The particle model supplies the reason temperature matters at all, and the plotting itself is an exercise in handling variables.

In one line

Solubility is the most that will dissolve in a fixed amount of solvent at a stated temperature, it rises with temperature for most solids and falls for gases, and the mass that crystallises on cooling is just the solubility at the start minus the solubility at the end.
