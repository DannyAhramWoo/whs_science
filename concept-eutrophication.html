Concept

Eutrophication

Eutrophication is what happens when extra nutrients wash into a lake or river, feeding an explosion of algae that ends, several steps later, with the water running out of oxygen and the fish suffocating.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

A pond turns bright green in a few weeks. A month later the fish are floating. Farmers spread fertiliser on the field beside it, rain washed some into the water, and the pond died. That sequence has one name: eutrophication.

The word comes from Greek roots meaning "well nourished", which sounds like a good thing, and that is the point of the name. The water is not poisoned; it has been over-fed. Nutrients useful in small amounts arrive in large amounts.

Two terms first. Nutrients here means the minerals plants need to grow, chiefly nitrates and phosphates — what fertiliser is made of and what sewage is rich in. Dissolved oxygen means oxygen gas held within the water itself. Fish do not breathe the oxygen inside water molecules; their gills take dissolved oxygen from the water, and there is not much of it. That small supply is where the story ends.

2

How it works

Learn this as an ordered chain. The marks are for the steps in order.

1 — Runoff. Rain falls on farmland, dissolves nitrates and phosphates from fertiliser, and carries them into a stream and then a lake. Untreated sewage delivers the same chemicals. This is the only step involving humans.

2 — Nutrient enrichment. The water now holds far more nitrate and phosphate than usual. Normally the nutrient supply limits how much algae can grow; remove that limit and growth runs free.

3 — Algal bloom. Algae are simple plant-like organisms floating in the water. Given unlimited nutrients and surface sunlight they multiply enormously within days, until the surface is a thick green mat.

4 — Light is blocked. The mat sits on top and sunlight cannot pass through, so the rooted plants on the lake bed are now in darkness.

5 — The submerged plants die. Without light they cannot photosynthesise, so they die. The algae die soon after, since a bloom that dense exhausts the nutrients and shades its own lower layers. The lake now holds a great deal of dead plant material.

6 — Decomposers multiply. Decomposers are bacteria and fungi feeding on dead matter. Presented with a feast, their numbers explode.

7 — Oxygen is used up. This is the step that matters. Decomposer bacteria respire: they break down food to release energy, taking in oxygen to do it. Billions respiring together strip the dissolved oxygen from the water.

8 — The fish suffocate. Fish need dissolved oxygen at their gills and there is now almost none, so they die along with insect larvae, snails and nearly everything else needing oxygen.

Two related cases are tested the same way. Sewage in a river skips to step 6, being itself food for decomposers, so oxygen falls below the outfall and recovers downstream. Thermal pollution — warm water from a power station — lowers dissolved oxygen because warm water holds less dissolved gas.

The heart of it

Nutrients feed algae, dead algae feed decomposers, and it is the decomposers' respiration that empties the water of oxygen and kills the fish.

3

Where the idea came from

No one person discovered eutrophication. It was pieced together through the mid twentieth century as lakes in industrialised countries visibly deteriorated and limnologists — scientists who study inland waters — worked out why.

The argument that settled it was over which nutrient to blame. Through the 1960s detergent manufacturers argued that carbon, not the phosphate in their products, was the limiting nutrient. The Canadian ecologist David Schindler answered it in Ontario in the early 1970s by dividing a lake with a curtain and adding phosphate to one half only. An aerial photograph showed one side green, the other clear, and phosphate controls in detergents followed in several countries.

The earlier mistake is worth noting. Nutrient enrichment was long thought harmless or even beneficial, since it produced more plant growth and, people assumed, more fish. That thinking stopped at step 3. Following the chain through to what decomposers do to oxygen is what turned a supposed benefit into a recognised form of pollution.

4

What people get wrong

Almost everyone blames the algae. "The algae poisoned the fish" or "the algae used up all the oxygen" loses the marks, and it is the most examined trap in this topic.

Living algae photosynthesise, which adds oxygen to the water in daylight, so they are not the killers. The oxygen is taken by decomposer bacteria respiring as they break down the dead algae and plants. The algae's crime is blocking light and then dying in quantity; the killing is done by what feeds on the corpses.

Past IMSO papers have asked for this chain as a structured written answer.

Commonly missed

A lake beside a farm turns green in spring. By late summer the water is clearer but most of the fish are dead. Explain why the fish died after the algae had gone.

The trap is the timing: the fish die when the water looks better, so anyone who thinks the algae are the killer cannot explain the delay.

How to work it: The water cleared because the algae died, not because the problem ended. Their bodies sank, and decomposer bacteria fed on that mass of dead algae and bottom plants and multiplied. Those bacteria respire, taking dissolved oxygen from the water until it fell below what fish gills need. The delay is the point: deaths follow the decomposition, which follows the bloom, so the fish die after the green has gone.

5

Connected ideas

Eutrophication is an ecosystem knocked out of balance by one non-living factor, arriving through the water cycle. The organisms doing the damage are microorganisms, and the oxygen they consume goes into respiration. The result is a collapse in populations at every trophic level above the producers.

In one line

Fertiliser or sewage washes nutrients into water, algae bloom and block the light, the plants below die, and decomposers feeding on the dead matter respire away the dissolved oxygen until the fish suffocate — so the oxygen loss, not the algae, kills them.
