Concept

Eutrophication

Eutrophication is what happens when extra nutrients wash into a lake or river, feeding an explosion of algae that ends, several steps later, with the water running out of oxygen and the fish suffocating.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

A pond turns bright green in a few weeks. A month later the fish are floating. Farmers spread fertiliser on the field beside it, rain washed some into the water, and the pond died. That whole sequence has one name: eutrophication.

The word comes from Greek roots meaning "well nourished", which sounds like a good thing. That is the point of the name. The water is not poisoned. It has been over-fed. Plant nutrients that would be useful in small amounts arrive in large amounts, and the consequences run away.

Two words you need before the mechanism makes sense. Nutrients here means the mineral substances plants need to grow, chiefly nitrates and phosphates — the very things fertiliser is made of, and the very things human and animal sewage is rich in. And dissolved oxygen means oxygen gas held within the water itself. Fish do not breathe the oxygen in water molecules; they use their gills to take dissolved oxygen out of the water, and there is not much of it. That small supply is where the whole story ends.

2

How it works

Learn this as an ordered chain. Papers ask for it as a written answer worth several marks, and the marks are for the steps in order.

Step 1 — runoff. Rain falls on farmland. Water soaks through the soil or runs over it, dissolving nitrates and phosphates from fertiliser, and carries them into a stream, then a lake. Untreated sewage does the same job with the same chemicals. This is called runoff, and it is the only step involving humans.

Step 2 — nutrient enrichment. The lake water now holds far more nitrate and phosphate than usual. Normally the amount of nutrient is what limits how much algae can grow. Remove that limit and growth is free to run.

Step 3 — algal bloom. Algae are simple plant-like organisms floating in the water. Given unlimited nutrients and plenty of sunlight at the surface, they multiply enormously in days. The surface becomes a thick green mat. This is an algal bloom.

Step 4 — light is blocked. The mat sits on top. Sunlight cannot reach through it. The rooted plants growing on the lake bed are now in darkness.

Step 5 — the submerged plants die. Without light they cannot photosynthesise, so they cannot make food, and they die. Shortly afterwards the algae die too, because a bloom that dense exhausts even the extra nutrients and shades its own lower layers. Now the lake holds an unusual quantity of dead plant material.

Step 6 — decomposers multiply. Decomposers are bacteria and fungi that feed on dead matter. Suddenly presented with a feast, their population explodes.

Step 7 — oxygen is used up. Here is the step that matters. Decomposer bacteria are living things, and living things respire — they break down food to release energy, and to do it they take in oxygen. Billions of them respiring together strip the dissolved oxygen out of the water.

Step 8 — the fish suffocate. Fish need dissolved oxygen at their gills. There is now almost none. They suffocate and die, along with insect larvae, snails and almost everything else that needs oxygen. What survives is bacteria that can live without it.

Two related cases are tested the same way. Sewage discharged into a river skips straight to step 6, because sewage is itself organic matter for decomposers, so oxygen falls sharply just downstream of the outfall and recovers further along. Thermal pollution — warm water released by a power station or factory — lowers dissolved oxygen too, because warm water holds less dissolved gas than cold water, and it raises fish metabolism so they need more oxygen at the moment there is less.

The heart of it

Nutrients feed algae, dead algae feed decomposers, and it is the decomposers' respiration that empties the water of oxygen and kills the fish.

3

Where the idea came from

No one person discovered eutrophication. It was pieced together through the middle of the twentieth century as lakes in industrialised countries visibly deteriorated and limnologists — scientists who study inland waters — worked out why.

The argument that settled it concerned which nutrient to blame. Through the 1960s there was a genuine dispute: detergent manufacturers argued that carbon, not the phosphate in their products, was the limiting nutrient. The Canadian ecologist David Schindler answered it experimentally at the Experimental Lakes Area in Ontario in the early 1970s by dividing a whole lake with a curtain and adding phosphate to one half only. An aerial photograph showed one side green and the other clear. Whole-lake experiments of that kind are rare and unusually convincing, and phosphate controls in detergents followed in several countries.

The earlier mistake is worth noting. For a long time nutrient enrichment was regarded as harmless or even beneficial, since it produced more plant growth and, people assumed, more fish. The thinking stopped at step 3. Following the chain through to what decomposers do to oxygen is exactly what turned a supposed benefit into a recognised form of pollution.

4

What people get wrong

Almost everyone blames the algae. The answer "the algae poisoned the fish" or "the algae used up all the oxygen" loses the marks, and it is the single most examined trap in this topic.

Be precise about why. Living algae photosynthesise, which adds oxygen to the water in daylight. They are not the killers. The oxygen is taken by the decomposer bacteria respiring as they break down the dead algae and dead plants. The algae's crime is blocking light and then dying in enormous quantity. The killing is done by what feeds on the corpses. There are algal species that release genuine toxins, but that is a separate phenomenon and not the mechanism of eutrophication.

The second error is skipping steps. A four-mark answer wants four links, and jumping from fertiliser straight to dead fish gets one. Write the chain out.

Past IMSO papers have asked for this chain as a structured written answer, and have approached the same idea sideways through sewage discharge lowering dissolved oxygen.

Commonly missed

A lake beside a farm turns green in spring. By late summer the water is clearer again but most of the fish are dead. Explain why the fish died after the algae had gone.

The trap is the timing. The fish die when the water looks better, so anyone who thinks the algae are the killer cannot explain the delay.

How to work it: The water cleared because the algae died, not because the problem ended. Their dead bodies sank. Decomposer bacteria fed on that mass of dead algae and dead bottom plants, and multiplied. Those bacteria respire, taking dissolved oxygen from the water. Oxygen fell below what fish gills need, so the fish suffocated. The delay is the point: the deaths follow the decomposition, which follows the bloom, so the fish die after the green has gone.

5

Connected ideas

Eutrophication is a whole ecosystem knocked out of balance by a change in one non-living factor, and it travels through the water cycle to reach the lake. The organisms doing the damage are microorganisms, and the oxygen they consume goes into respiration. The result is a collapse in the populations at every trophic level above the producers.

In one line

Fertiliser or sewage washes nutrients into water, algae bloom and block the light, the plants below die, and decomposers feeding on all that dead matter respire away the dissolved oxygen until the fish suffocate — so the oxygen loss, not the algae, is what kills them.
