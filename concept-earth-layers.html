Concept

The Layers of the Earth

The Earth is built of layers — crust, mantle, outer core and inner core — which differ in what they are made of, how hot they are and whether they are solid or liquid.

Introduced in Chapter 12: The Planet Earth

1

What it means

Standing on the ground, the Earth feels like solid rock all the way down. It is not. Cut the planet in half and you would find a structure more like an onion, or a peach: a thin skin, a very thick middle, and a dense centre.

The crust is the outer skin, and it is astonishingly thin — between about 5 km under the oceans and about 70 km under mountain ranges, compared with the Earth's radius of 6,371 km. If the Earth were an apple, the crust would be about as thick as the apple's skin. It is made of solid rock, and it is the only part we have ever seen.

The mantle lies beneath, about 2,900 km thick, and holds roughly 84 per cent of the Earth's volume. It is rock rich in iron and magnesium, and although it is solid in the sense that seismic waves pass through it as through a solid, it is hot enough to creep and flow extremely slowly over millions of years — a few centimetres a year, about the rate fingernails grow.

The outer core, from about 2,900 km to 5,150 km down, is molten iron and nickel. The inner core at the centre is iron and nickel too, but solid, and its temperature is thought to be somewhere near 5,400 degrees Celsius — comparable to the surface of the Sun.

2

How it works

The obvious question is why the layers are arranged as they are. Early in its history, about 4.5 billion years ago, the young Earth was hot enough to be largely molten, and in a liquid, dense material sinks and less dense material floats. Iron and nickel, being dense, sank towards the centre; lighter silicate rock rose. This sorting is called differentiation, and it is the reason the Earth has a dense metal core and a light rocky crust. It is the same principle as oil floating on water, run on a planetary scale.

The inner core presents a puzzle: it is the hottest part of the Earth, so why is it solid while the cooler outer core is liquid? The answer is pressure. Melting point depends not only on temperature but on pressure, and squeezing atoms together makes it harder for them to break free into a liquid. At the centre the pressure is over three million times atmospheric pressure, which raises iron's melting point above even those enormous temperatures. Move outwards into the outer core and the pressure drops enough that the iron melts, even though it is slightly cooler.

These layers do things. The liquid outer core is electrically conducting metal in constant motion, stirred by heat escaping from the inner core and by the Earth's rotation. Moving conductor generates electric currents, and electric currents generate magnetic fields — so the outer core is the source of the Earth's magnetic field, the thing that makes compasses work and shields the planet from much of the solar wind. Heat escaping through the mantle drives slow convection currents there, and those currents drag the plates of the crust about, causing earthquakes, volcanoes and mountain building.

The heat itself has two sources: leftover heat from the Earth's violent formation, and heat released continuously by the decay of radioactive elements such as uranium, thorium and potassium in the rock.

The heart of it

The Earth's layers sorted themselves by density when the planet was molten, and the state of each layer depends on pressure as well as temperature.

3

Where the idea came from

Here is the striking fact about this whole subject: nobody has ever been down there. The deepest hole ever drilled is the Kola Superdeep Borehole in north-west Russia, which reached about 12.26 km by 1989 after nearly twenty years of work, before the rock became too hot and behaved too much like plastic to drill further. That is roughly 0.2 per cent of the way to the centre, and it never left the crust. Everything we claim to know about the interior is inferred indirectly.

The main tool is earthquake waves. An earthquake sends vibrations through the whole planet, and they are recorded by seismographs all over the world. The waves travel at different speeds through different materials, and they bend when they cross a boundary — just as light bends passing from air into water. So the pattern of arrival times at hundreds of stations can be worked backwards into a picture of what the waves must have passed through. Two kinds of wave matter especially: P-waves, which travel through solids and liquids, and S-waves, which cannot travel through liquid at all. In 1906 Richard Dixon Oldham noticed that S-waves fail to arrive on the far side of the Earth, leaving a "shadow zone" — powerful evidence that something in the middle is liquid. In 1909 Andrija Mohorovičić identified the sharp boundary between crust and mantle, still called the Moho.

The inner core was found by the Danish seismologist Inge Lehmann in 1936. She was studying P-waves that arrived in the supposed shadow zone where, according to the accepted model of a wholly liquid core, no such waves should have appeared. Rather than dismissing them, she worked out that a solid ball at the centre, with a boundary that reflected and bent the waves, would explain the readings exactly. She was working in a field that gave women very few opportunities, and she published the paper under the terse title "P′" — arguably the most quietly confident title in the history of geology. Her conclusion was confirmed in the 1970s when far more sensitive instrument networks became available. She lived to 104.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest misconception is that the mantle is a sea of molten lava that the crust floats on. It is not. Apart from a few small partly-melted pockets, the mantle is solid rock — we know because S-waves travel through it, and S-waves cannot cross a liquid. It flows, but as a solid flows: extremely slowly, like a glacier of ice, which is undoubtedly solid yet creeps downhill. Lava is mantle rock that has melted on its way to the surface because the pressure dropped, not a sample of a molten layer.

The second is the assumption that hotter always means more liquid. Pressure is the other half of the story, and at the centre of the Earth it wins.

Commonly missed

The inner core is hotter than the outer core, yet the inner core is solid and the outer core is liquid. Explain.

The trap is the everyday rule that heating something makes it melt. That rule holds only when pressure is held constant, and here it is not.

How to work it: Melting happens when particles gain enough energy to break free of their fixed positions and move past one another. Pressure works against this by squeezing the particles tightly together, so under very high pressure a substance needs a higher temperature to melt — the melting point rises. At the Earth's centre the weight of everything above produces pressure over three million times that of the atmosphere, pushing iron's melting point above the temperature that is actually there. So the inner core stays solid. Moving outwards, the pressure falls faster than the temperature does, until at the top of the outer core the melting point has dropped below the local temperature, and the iron is liquid.

Connected ideas

The layers sorted themselves by density, they are stirred by convection carrying heat outwards, and the churning liquid outer core generates the Earth's magnetic field. Movement of the crust above the mantle drives the rock cycle.

In one line

The Earth has a thin rocky crust, a thick solid but slowly flowing mantle, a liquid iron outer core and a solid iron inner core kept solid by immense pressure — and we know this almost entirely from earthquake waves.
