Chapter 12

The Planet Earth

Inside the Earth, the rocks on its surface and the air above it. The single biggest confusion in this topic is between weathering and erosion — two words that sound related but describe different jobs.

12.1

What the Earth is made of inside

Nobody has ever drilled through the Earth. The deepest hole ever made goes down about 12 km, which sounds enormous until you learn that the centre of the Earth is about 6 400 km down. We know what is inside from the way earthquake waves bend as they pass through.

The Earth has four layers. Going inwards from where you are standing:

The crust is the thin, solid rocky shell we live on. Under the oceans it is only about 5 km thick; under mountains it can reach 70 km. Compared with the whole planet that is nothing. If the Earth were an apple, the crust would be about as thick as the apple's skin.

The mantle is the thickest layer by far — roughly 2 900 km of hot rock. It is not liquid. It is solid rock that is so hot it can creep and flow, but only a few centimetres a year, about the speed your fingernails grow. The outer core below it is liquid metal, mostly iron and nickel. The inner core at the centre is metal again, but here the pressure is so enormous that it is squeezed solid even though it is the hottest part of the whole planet, around 5 000 °C.

Two things change with depth

Going down through the layers, both the temperature and the density increase. The inner core is the hottest and the densest part of the Earth. It is solid not because it is cool, but because the weight of everything above squeezes it solid.

12.2

Three families of rock

Every rock on Earth belongs to one of three groups. The group is decided by how the rock formed, not by what it looks like or how hard it is.

Igneous rock forms when molten rock cools and hardens. If it cools slowly deep underground, the crystals have time to grow large, which is why granite has visible speckles. If it cools quickly at the surface after a volcano erupts, the crystals stay tiny and you get smooth dark basalt. Crystal size tells you the cooling speed.

Sedimentary rock forms when small pieces of sediment — sand, mud, crushed shells — settle in layers, usually underwater, and are pressed together by the weight of more layers on top. Sandstone and limestone form this way. Because the sediment settles gently, dead plants and animals can be buried in it without being destroyed, so fossils are found almost only in sedimentary rock.

Metamorphic rock is an existing rock that has been changed by heat and pressure while staying solid. Limestone becomes marble. Shale becomes slate. The ingredients are the same; the rock has been rearranged, not melted.

Fossils give the rock away

If a rock contains a fossil, it is almost certainly sedimentary. Molten rock would burn the remains away, and the heat and pressure of metamorphism would crush them. Only gentle burial in layers preserves a shape.

12.3

The rock cycle

No rock stays the same for ever. Given enough time, any of the three types can turn into any other. What decides the outcome is the process that acts on it.

There are only three processes to remember, and each one leads to a particular type:

Melting, then cooling gives igneous rock — always. Weathering and erosion, followed by the pieces settling and being pressed together, gives sedimentary rock. Heat and pressure without melting gives metamorphic rock. That is the whole cycle.

A common question hands you a starting rock and one process and asks what comes out. You do not need to know the starting rock at all. Granite that melts and cools becomes igneous. Marble that is broken down, washed into a lake and squashed becomes sedimentary. The process decides.

Commonly missed

Limestone deep underground is squeezed and heated until it becomes marble. Which process has taken place?

The trap is the word "heated". People see heat, think of a volcano, and answer melting — which would make the marble igneous. But melting destroys the rock completely and produces magma, and magma cannot cool into marble.

How to work it: ask whether the rock stayed solid. Limestone that stayed solid and was rearranged by heat and pressure has been metamorphosed, so marble is a metamorphic rock. Test yourself with the pair: limestone → marble, shale → slate. Both are solid-state changes.

12.4

Weathering, erosion and soil

These two words are used almost interchangeably in ordinary speech, and that is exactly why questions target them.

Weathering is the breaking down of rock where it stands. Nothing is carried anywhere. Rain water seeps into a crack, freezes overnight, expands and widens the crack a little. Plant roots push into a gap and prise it open. Slightly acidic rain slowly dissolves limestone. All of this happens on the spot.

Erosion is the carrying away of the loosened pieces by moving water, wind, ice or gravity. A river takes broken grains downstream. Wind blows sand across a desert. Erosion needs something moving to do the transporting.

Weathering usually comes first and erosion second, which is why they get muddled. Over a very long time, weathered rock mixes with the decayed remains of plants and animals — called humus — and the mixture becomes soil. Soil is therefore part mineral and part once-living material, which is why it can feed plants when bare crushed rock cannot.

One question separates them

Ask: did the pieces move somewhere else? No movement means weathering. Movement means erosion. Ice cracking a cliff apart is weathering; the river below carrying those fragments to the sea is erosion.

Commonly missed

Water gets into a crack in a rock on a mountain, freezes at night and widens the crack. Over months the rock splits. What is this an example of?

Because water is involved, and water is the usual agent of erosion, people answer erosion. But look at what the water actually did: it sat in one place and expanded. Nothing was carried off the mountain.

How to work it: the water is acting as a wedge, not as a lorry. This is weathering. Erosion would only begin later, when the split-off fragments tumble away or are washed downhill.

12.5

The atmosphere

The atmosphere is the layer of gases held around the Earth by gravity. Its composition is worth learning as a number, because questions ask for it directly: about 78% nitrogen, about 21% oxygen, and roughly 1% everything else — argon, carbon dioxide and water vapour. Nitrogen is the biggest share by a long way, which surprises people who assume the air is mostly the gas they breathe.

Climb upwards and the air gets thinner, because there is less air above pressing down. The temperature is less obvious. It does not simply drop for ever. In the lowest layer, the troposphere, where all our weather happens, it gets colder as you rise — which is why mountain tops keep their snow. Above that, in the stratosphere, the temperature starts going back up.

The reason is the ozone layer, which sits in the stratosphere. Ozone absorbs most of the harmful ultraviolet light arriving from the Sun. Absorbing that energy warms the air around it, so temperature rises with height through this layer. The ozone layer is the reason we are not badly burnt by sunlight, and the reason the graph bends.

Reading these graphs

On an atmosphere graph, altitude is usually on the vertical axis and temperature on the horizontal — the opposite of most graphs you have drawn. Always check which axis is which before deciding whether the air is warming or cooling.

12.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

12.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

The inner core is the hottest part of the Earth, yet it is solid while the outer core above it is liquid. Explain how that is possible.

Show answer

Whether something is solid or liquid depends on pressure as well as temperature. High pressure squeezes particles so tightly together that they cannot slide past each other, which keeps the material solid at temperatures that would otherwise melt it.

The inner core sits at the very centre, with the entire weight of the planet pressing in on it. That pressure is far greater than in the outer core, so even at around 5 000 °C the metal stays solid.

2

A geologist splits open a rock and finds the shell of a sea creature inside. What type of rock is it, and why could it not be either of the other two?

Show answer

It is sedimentary. Sedimentary rock forms when sediment settles in gentle layers, so a shell can be buried and preserved with its shape intact.

It cannot be igneous, because igneous rock forms from molten rock and the heat would destroy the shell completely. It is very unlikely to be metamorphic, because the heat and pressure that change a rock also squash and distort any fossil inside it.

3

Waves pound a cliff until chunks break off, and the sea then drags the chunks along the shore. Identify which part is weathering and which is erosion.

Show answer

The waves battering the cliff and breaking chunks off it is weathering — the rock is being broken down where it stands.

The sea then dragging the chunks along the shore is erosion, because the loosened material is now being transported somewhere else.

The same sea does both jobs, so you cannot decide by naming the agent. Decide by asking whether anything moved.

4

Air is about 78% nitrogen and only about 21% oxygen. Why does the temperature stop falling and start rising once you climb above the troposphere?

Show answer

In the troposphere the air is warmed mainly from below, by the ground soaking up sunlight, so the further you get from the ground the colder it becomes.

Higher up, in the stratosphere, sits the ozone layer. Ozone absorbs ultraviolet light from the Sun, and that absorbed energy heats the surrounding air. So in this layer there is a heat source above you rather than below you, and the temperature climbs with height instead of dropping.

12.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 12.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about the layers of the Earth are correct?

  • 1The crust is the thickest layer of the Earth.
  • 2The outer core is made of liquid metal.
  • 3Temperature increases as you go deeper into the Earth.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. The crust is the thinnest layer, as little as 5 km under the oceans. The mantle is the thickest, at roughly 2 900 km. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C.

Checking anyway: the outer core is liquid iron and nickel, so 2 is correct, and it does get hotter with depth, so 3 is correct. Consistent with C.

2

A block of limestone is buried deep underground, where it is squeezed and heated for millions of years until it becomes marble. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Marble is a metamorphic rock.
  • 2Heat and pressure caused the change.
  • 3The limestone must have melted completely first.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. A rock changed by heat and pressure while staying solid is metamorphic, and limestone → marble is the standard example. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. If the rock had melted completely it would have become magma, and magma cools into igneous rock, not marble. Metamorphism happens in the solid state. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Burial supplies both the pressure of the rock above and the heat of the depths. So the answer is B.

3

Which statements about the atmosphere are correct?

  • 1Nitrogen makes up the largest share of the air.
  • 2The ozone layer absorbs harmful ultraviolet light from the Sun.
  • 3Air becomes thinner as altitude increases.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Nitrogen is about 78% of the air, well ahead of oxygen at about 21%. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. There is less air above pressing down as you climb, so the air spreads out and thins. That points to A.

Statement 2 — correct. The ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbs most incoming ultraviolet light, which is also why that layer warms with height. All three are right, so the answer is A.
