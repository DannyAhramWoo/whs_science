Study skills

Reading a Diagram Under Pressure

In competition papers, more than seventy questions in a single set of past papers cannot be answered without reading a figure. Those questions are not testing whether you know the topic. They are testing whether you have a procedure for looking.

Start

This page is not more facts

Every other page on this site adds something to what you know. This one does not. It adds a procedure — a fixed order of moves you make when a figure appears above a question.

That sounds like a small thing. It is not. In a hard paper, the recall questions and the figure questions test almost the same science, but they separate people completely. Someone can name every part of a flower and still lose both marks on a numbered flower diagram, because naming and locating are different jobs. The first is memory. The second is a search, done under time pressure, on a picture drawn by somebody else.

Papers are built to punish the gap. The wrong answers are not silly. They are usually the structure sitting right next to the correct one. A fast eye lands on the neighbour. Only a finger lands on the right one.

So the procedure below is deliberately slow at the start and fast at the end. Roughly ten seconds of setting up — naming the whole figure, finding the direction, tracing one path — buys back a minute of confusion later. Practise the steps until they run without thinking, because under pressure you will only do what is automatic.

What separates the two groups

Knowing the science gets you to the point where you can recognise the right answer if you look at the right place. The procedure is what gets your eyes to the right place. People who score well on figure questions are not remembering more. They are looking in a fixed order.

1

The one habit that fixes most of it

If you read only one section of this page, read this one. Across every kind of figure, the same wrong-answer design keeps coming back, and it is worth naming out loud.

The dominant trap: answering the structure next door

The wrong option is almost never far away. It is touching the right one, inside it, or one step along from it. Papers are designed this way on purpose, because it catches exactly the person who recognises the topic but never traced the picture.

Here is the same trap wearing four different costumes.

  • In a labelled body diagram: a small ball drawn inside a cup-shaped structure. Two names, two structures, one touching the other. Point at the wrong one and the mark is gone even though you knew both words.
  • In a flower: the head of a stamen and the stalk holding it up. They are joined. Naming the stalk when you meant the head is a single centimetre of error.
  • In a cycle diagram: condensation and evaporation are the same two boxes with the arrow pointing the other way. Read the arrow backwards and you do not lose one mark, you lose both at once.
  • In a food web: the arrow means "is eaten by", not "eats". Read it as "eats" and every single trophic level in the whole web flips.

Notice what these have in common. In each case the student knew the science. What failed was the looking.

The countermeasure

Before you commit to any answer on any figure, do this. It takes about five seconds.

  1. Put a fingertip on a place in the figure you are completely sure about. Not part 1 — the part you are surest of.
  2. Move the finger along the figure to the thing you intend to name. Actually move it. Do not do this with your eyes.
  3. Say aloud, or mouth silently, what that thing connects to on each side: "the tube comes into it from here, and leaves it going there."
  4. Only now write the answer down.

Step 3 is the one people skip, and it is the one that works. A structure has neighbours. If you can state both neighbours correctly, you are on the right structure. If you cannot state them, you were recognising, not reading.

The second trap, close behind

Answering the figure's topic instead of the question asked.

A figure showing one named cycle can sit above a question about a completely different substance. The heading pulls your answer towards it. In one past paper a question printed under a nitrogen cycle diagram asked which gas plants take in to make food — and the answer is carbon dioxide, not nitrogen.

How to beat it: read the question stem once more with the figure covered by your hand. If the question still makes sense with the picture hidden, the picture is not the answer.

Trap patterns in this section are drawn from IMSO past papers, 2008 to 2019.

2

Numbered anatomy diagrams

How to recognise it: a cut-through or outline drawing of a living thing or an organ, with parts marked 1, 2, 3 or A, B, C, and leader lines pointing at them. The question asks which number is a named part, or which numbers share a property.

The instinct is to start at part 1 and work along. Resist it. Part 1 is chosen by the examiner, not by you, and it is often the hardest label on the figure.

The procedure

  1. Name the whole thing first. Say it: "this is a flower cut down the middle." If you cannot name the whole picture, no part label can be trusted.
  2. Find the orientation. Which edge is outside or top? Which is inside or bottom? Diagrams are almost always drawn in the direction the flow or the light travels.
  3. Find the direction of the flow, and check whether the numbering follows it. Numbering usually runs along the flow or top to bottom. If it does, you can predict roughly what each number is before looking closely.
  4. Anchor on the part you are surest of — not part 1. One confident anchor plus counting beats guessing six labels.
  5. Turn the question into something you can see. "Contains chloroplasts" becomes "is green, and is inside rather than the clear outer skin". Convert the science word into a shape or a position before you point.
  6. Check every option, not just the promising one. If options are overlapping groups, the numbers they do not share are the ones that decide the answer. Work out only those.
  7. Check the leader line actually touches the structure. A line ending in a gap means the answer is the space itself, not the wall around it.
Traps in this type

Container versus contents. A ball drawn inside a cup. Ask yourself: is the question about the bag, or the thing in the bag?

Where versus what causes. Being asked where something happens and answering with the organ that does it. One organ makes a substance, a different one stores it, and papers exist specifically to test that split.

Paired-answer questions. If you must name two parts for one mark, you get nothing for one right. Find the one you know, then go back and verify the second properly.

Size. Papers warn that diagrams are not to scale. The biggest blob is not automatically the biggest organ.

1

The diagram shows a flower cut down the middle, with five parts numbered. Which number points to the part where pollen is made?

Show answer

Answer: 2

Step 1 — name the whole thing. Say it aloud: "a flower, cut down the middle, seen from the side." Now every label has a context.

Step 2 — orientation. The stalk is at the bottom, the open part is at the top. So the top of the picture is where insects and pollen arrive.

Step 3 — anchor. Do not start at 1. The part I am surest of is the big curved sheet on the outside — that is a petal, and 1 points at it. Good, my reading of the figure works.

Step 4 — trace. Put a finger at the base of the flower and move up the short side stalk. It rises from the base, and at its top sits a small oval head. Say it: "a stalk, and on top of the stalk, a head." That whole structure is a stamen. The number 2 points at the head. The number 3 points at the stalk.

Step 5 — turn the question into something visible. "Where pollen is made" means "the little bag at the top of a stamen that splits open and spills powder". A stalk cannot spill powder — it only holds things up. So it is the head, which is 2.

Why 3 is the trap. It is joined to 2. They touch. This is exactly the next-door error from section 1, and it is the single most common way to lose this mark. Tracing from the base upwards is what separates them.

Why 4 is wrong. 4 is the tip of the tall central column — that is where pollen lands, not where it is made. This is the where-versus-what-causes trap: arriving and being produced are different events in different places.

Why 5 is wrong. 5 is the swollen base of the central column. That holds the female cells. Different job, opposite end of the process.

3

Graphs

How to recognise it: two axes and a line. This is the most common figure type of all — in one set of past papers, seventeen questions depended on reading a graph. It is worth being very good at.

There are two flavours. In a graph-reading question the graph is given and you must extract something. In a graph-selection question you are given a story and four graphs, and you must pick the one that fits. The second kind needs a different opening move, so it has its own step below.

The procedure

  1. Read both axis labels and their units aloud before you look at the line. Most graph errors are axis errors, and they happen in the first two seconds.
  2. State the graph as one sentence: "this shows how temperature changes as time passes." If you cannot say the sentence, do not go on.
  3. Say what the shape means in words, before touching any numbers. Flat means not changing. Rising means getting bigger. Steeper means changing faster.
  4. Mark every turning point. Anywhere the line changes slope, something physically happened in the story. Match each event in the question to one turning point, in order. This answers most "at which point" questions with no arithmetic at all.
  5. Only now read values, and read them by tracing across from the axis with a finger or a ruler edge, not by eye.
  6. For graph-selection: predict your own shape first, in words — "goes down fast, then levels off" — and only then hunt for the graph that matches your sentence. Never compare the four options against each other.
  7. For "what can be inferred": ban anything the graph has no axis for. If the axes are time and temperature, no conclusion about taste, cost or safety can possibly be supported.
Traps in this type

Flat does not mean zero. On a speed-time graph a flat line means moving at a steady speed. On a distance-time graph a flat line means stopped. Same shape, opposite meaning. Check the vertical axis every time.

Steepest is not highest. "When was it fastest" and "when was it speeding up most" are different questions with different answers.

Over-inference. In selection questions about conclusions, the wrong options are usually true-sounding science that the axes simply do not show.

Familiar shapes. In graph-selection items, wrong options are drawn as the shapes you have seen most often, such as a straight line through the origin. Predicting first is the only defence.

Several lines on one grid. Find the key, then put a finger on the named line and keep it there.

Units. Answers can differ by a factor of ten or a hundred on units alone.

2

Hot wax in a tube is left to cool in a room. The graph shows its temperature over twelve minutes. Between which two points is the wax turning from liquid to solid, and how do you know from the shape alone?

Show answer

Answer: between Q and R

Step 1 — axes aloud. "Across the bottom, time in minutes. Up the side, temperature in degrees C." Ten seconds, and now I cannot make an axis error.

Step 2 — one sentence. "This shows how the temperature of the wax changes as time passes." Said it. Move on.

Step 3 — shape in words, no numbers yet. Going along the line: it drops steeply, then it goes completely flat, then it drops again but more gently. Three sections. Say that out loud before touching a single value.

Step 4 — turning points as events. There are two turning points, at Q and at R. So two things happened to the wax. The flat section between them is the important one, because flat means the temperature is not changing at all, even though the wax is still sitting in a cool room losing heat the whole time.

That is the key reasoning. Heat is leaving, but the temperature is not falling. So the heat leaving must be doing some other job instead of cooling the wax down. The job it is doing is changing the wax from liquid to solid. A substance stays at one temperature while it changes state.

So the freezing happens between Q and R, and the flat temperature — about 60 degrees, read by tracing across from the axis with a finger — is the freezing point of this wax.

What each other section is. P to Q is liquid wax cooling down. R to S is solid wax cooling down. Neither is a change of state, because in both the temperature is falling.

The trap avoided. A flat line invites you to say "nothing is happening here". The opposite is true: the flat section is the only part of this graph where the wax is doing something interesting. Always ask what a flat line means on these particular axes.

4

Data tables

How to recognise it: rows and columns of numbers or results, with a question asking for a pattern, a prediction, or which item is the biggest or best.

The procedure

  1. Read the column headings and their units, then say what one single row means as a sentence: "at 30 degrees, 42 grams dissolved."
  2. Work out which column is varied and which is measured. One is the cause, one is the effect. Mark them.
  3. Find the row-to-row pattern before anything clever. Does the measured column go up by the same amount each time? Does it double? Neither? For prediction questions the constant step is the answer.
  4. For "which is biggest" questions, write the rule down as a direction first. Some quantities work backwards. Decide whether you want the largest or the smallest number before you scan the column.
  5. For tables with several trials, write one conclusion per row before reading the next row. Never hold three rows in your head at once.
  6. Check your conclusion against every row, including the ones you did not use. A correct deduction fits all of them.
Traps in this type

The backwards quantity. The most reliable table trap of all. Some measurements mean "how much is needed", so a small number means the effect happens easily. Students scan for the largest number automatically. Write the rule down first.

Row read as column. Especially when the answer options are themselves little tables.

Straight-line extrapolation. If the steps between rows are not equal, the pattern is not straight, and you cannot just keep adding.

Missing the reference point. Some tables need a fact you supply yourself, such as room temperature being about 25 degrees. Fix that number before you start.

Stopping after one row. A multi-trial table has several trials precisely because no single one settles it. Every wrong option is what you get by stopping early.

3

A student stirs sugar into 100 g of water at different temperatures and records the greatest mass that dissolves. Predict the mass that would dissolve at 70 degrees, and state one thing this table cannot tell you.

Show answer

Answer: about 274 g

Step 1 — one row as a sentence. Take the third row and say it: "at 30 degrees, 218 grams of sugar dissolved in 100 grams of water." Now the table means something instead of being a block of digits.

Step 2 — which column is which. The student chose the temperatures, so temperature is the varied column, the cause. The mass dissolved is what came out of the experiment, so it is the measured column, the effect.

Step 3 — the row-to-row pattern, before anything clever. Work down the measured column and write the gaps: 204 minus 190 is 14. 218 minus 204 is 14. Then 14, 14, 14 again. Every step is the same, and every temperature step is the same 10 degrees.

So the pattern is: every 10 degrees hotter, another 14 g dissolves. That sentence is the whole answer.

Step 4 — use it. 70 degrees is one step past 60. So 260 plus 14 gives about 274 g.

Step 5 — check against every row. Going back down: 190, add 14 gives 204, correct. Add 14 gives 218, correct. All the way to 260, correct. The rule fits every row, so it can be trusted one step beyond.

What the table cannot tell you. Anything with no column. It cannot tell you how fast the sugar dissolved, because time was never recorded — only the final amount. It also cannot tell you what happens at 90 degrees with any confidence, because that is three steps beyond the data and the pattern might bend.

The trap avoided. Say "about 274 g", not "exactly 274 g". You are stepping outside the measured range, and a real pattern often curves once you leave the data. Papers usually accept a range for this reason.

5

Cycle and flow diagrams

How to recognise it: boxes or pictures joined by arrows, usually forming a loop, sometimes with side branches leading away from it.

An arrow is a verb

Every arrow in a flow diagram is a doing word hiding as a line. If you cannot turn an arrow into a sentence — "water rises from the sea into the air" — then you have not read it. You have only seen it.

The procedure

  1. Read the caption first. It usually tells you what the cycle is, and half the marks live there.
  2. Trace each arrow with a fingertip and say it as a full sentence out loud. One arrow at a time.
  3. Find the closed loop. A cycle is a circle plus some side branches. Identify the circle first, because the harder question is almost always about a side branch.
  4. Locate the start and the end before you order anything: what leaves first, what comes back last. Then fill in the middle.
  5. For "which box does X", match the box by what goes in and what comes out, not by its name.
  6. If one figure serves several questions, read all of them before answering any. A later question often tells you how to read the figure.
Traps in this type

Reading an arrow backwards. The classic. Two processes can be exactly the same pair of boxes with the arrow reversed, so getting the direction wrong swaps two answers at once.

Decoys in the matching list. If you must match five arrows to six named processes, the extra name exists to be chosen. Two of the names will sound almost identical.

Answering the cycle's name. Covered in section 1, and it bites hardest here.

Ordering questions with a plausible wrong start. When every option contains the same words in a different order, knowing the words is worth nothing. Only the order scores.

4

The diagram shows the water cycle with four arrows lettered W, X, Y and Z. Which letter is condensation, and which single letter would you have to change to turn this into a diagram of evaporation happening twice?

Show answer

Answer: X is condensation; changing Z would give a second evaporation

Step 1 — caption first. It says the water cycle. That is free information. I now know the loop closes and that water returns to where it started.

Step 2 — say every arrow as a sentence. Finger on each in turn:

W: "water leaves the sea and rises as invisible vapour." X: "rising vapour cools high up and gathers into the cloud." Y: "water falls from the cloud onto the land." Z: "water runs across the land back down into the sea."

Four sentences. Notice that every one of them has a direction built into it. That is what makes an arrow a verb.

Step 3 — find the loop. Sea, up to vapour, into cloud, down onto land, back to sea. It closes. Good — the figure is consistent and I have read it correctly.

Now answer part one. Condensation means a gas turning into a liquid. Which of my four sentences describes that? Only X: vapour, which is a gas, becoming the water droplets that make a cloud. So X is condensation.

Why W is the trap. W and X are next to each other, both point upwards, and both involve water going towards the sky. But W is liquid becoming gas — that is evaporation, the exact opposite change. This is the reversed-arrow trap from section 1: two neighbouring arrows describing opposite processes. Saying each one aloud as a sentence is what pulls them apart.

Part two. To get evaporation happening twice I need a second arrow where liquid water turns into vapour and goes up. Look at the arrows I have. Y is falling rain, and rain cannot be turned into evaporation without reversing gravity. Z is liquid water sitting on the land, which is exactly the situation evaporation starts from. So Z is the one to change: point it upwards from the land instead of sideways into the sea, and it becomes water evaporating from puddles, soil and plants.

The general lesson. Both parts were solved by the same move — turning each arrow into a sentence with a direction in it. Neither part needed any fact you did not already know.

6

Food webs

How to recognise it: names of living things joined by arrows, with no boxes and no loop.

Settle this before anything else

In a food web the arrow points from the eaten to the eater. It shows which way the energy travels. Grass points to rabbit, because the grass's energy ends up in the rabbit. Read it as "eats" and every level in the entire web turns upside down.

The procedure

  1. Confirm the arrow convention on one obvious pair before anything else. Find a plant and the animal that eats it, and check the arrow runs plant to animal.
  2. Mark the producers. Anything with no arrow coming into it is a producer, level 1. They are your only anchor.
  3. Number outward from the producers. Whatever eats a producer is level 2. Whatever eats a level 2 is level 3. Write the numbers on the paper.
  4. Look for multi-path animals. Anything with arrows arriving from both a plant and an animal sits on two levels at once.
  5. For "if this is removed" questions, trace exactly one step each way and stop. Its food increases, its predator decreases. The word "immediately" means one step only.
  6. For "smallest population", go to the top. Energy is lost at every level, so the top has fewest individuals.
Traps in this type

Reversed arrows. The whole question, every time.

Counting creatures instead of roles. An animal that eats both a plant and an animal is one creature but two levels. Answer options are usually close together so that missing one such animal costs the mark.

Immediate versus eventual. One word in the stem decides whether you trace one step or several.

Biggest animal, biggest population. Untrue, and papers punish it directly.

5

The diagram shows a garden food web. Which animal feeds at two different levels, and what would happen immediately to the number of caterpillars if all the thrushes were removed?

Show answer

Answer: the owl feeds at two levels; caterpillars would increase

Step 1 — confirm the convention. Lettuce points to slug. A slug eats lettuce, not the other way round, so the arrow really does run from the eaten to the eater. Now I can trust every other arrow on the figure.

Step 2 — mark the producers. Which names have no arrows arriving? Lettuce and berry bush. Both are plants, which fits. They are level 1.

Step 3 — number outward. Slug eats lettuce, so slug is level 2. Caterpillar eats lettuce and berries, so caterpillar is level 2. Mouse eats berries, so mouse is level 2. Thrush eats slugs and caterpillars, which are level 2, so thrush is level 3.

Now the owl. Follow its two incoming arrows separately. One comes from the mouse, which is level 2 — so eating a mouse makes the owl level 3. The other comes from the thrush, which is level 3 — so eating a thrush makes the owl level 4.

So the owl is the multi-level animal. One bird, two levels, depending on what it happens to eat that night. Tracing each incoming arrow one at a time is what finds this. Glancing at the web does not.

Why the caterpillar is the trap here. The caterpillar also has two arrows coming in, from lettuce and from the berry bush, so it looks like the answer. But both of those are plants, so both make it level 2. Two arrows is not the same as two levels. Check what each arrow comes from, not how many there are.

Part two — remove the thrushes. The word is "immediately", so I trace exactly one step and stop. One step down from the thrush leads to the slug and the caterpillar. Nothing is eating the caterpillars any more, so the number of caterpillars would increase.

One step, then stop. It is true that more caterpillars would later mean less lettuce, and that owls would have less to eat — but those are two and three steps away. That is what "eventually" would have meant. "Immediately" means one step.

7

Comparing set-ups

How to recognise it: three or four small pictures of apparatus, each slightly different, with a question asking which ones prove a particular thing.

This is a fair test question wearing a picture. The whole thing turns on finding a pair that differs in exactly one way.

The procedure

  1. Write out what each set-up has and has not got, as a short list beside each picture. On paper. Not in your head.
  2. Underline the one factor the question is about. "Is light needed?" — then light is the factor.
  3. Find the pair that differs in that factor and in nothing else. Compare the two lists item by item.
  4. Sanity-check: if these two gave different results, is there only one possible reason? If you can think of a second reason, the pair is wrong.
  5. If asked to design the comparison, copy the given set-up and delete exactly one thing — the thing being tested. The answer is a clone minus one variable.
  6. For sealed containers, do one container at a time. List who is inside, ask who uses each gas and who makes it, decide, then move on. Never compare until all are decided.
Traps in this type

A pair differing in two variables. The most common wrong option, and it is attractive because it obviously produces a difference in outcome. A difference you cannot explain proves nothing.

The most extreme pair. Everything against nothing. It shows a difference and identifies no cause.

Deleting the wrong thing. When building a control, removing anything other than the tested factor changes two variables at once.

6

Four jars are set up with cress seeds. Which two jars together show whether light is needed for the seeds to grow green?

Show answer

Answer: A and B

Step 1 — list what each jar has. Write it out, do not hold it in your head:

A: water yes, light yes. B: water yes, light no. C: water no, light yes. D: water no, light no.

Four lines. Ten seconds. This is the whole question, and everything after it is easy.

Step 2 — underline the factor. The question asks about light. So light is the one thing that is allowed to differ between my chosen pair. Water must be identical in both.

Step 3 — find the pair. Go along the lists comparing two at a time. A and B: water yes in both, light differs. That is exactly one difference. That is the pair.

Step 4 — sanity check. If A goes green and B does not, is there only one possible reason? Yes. Both jars had water, both were warm, both had the same seeds. Light is the only thing left. The conclusion is forced, and a forced conclusion is what a fair test is for.

Why C and D is wrong. It also differs in light only, which is why it looks tempting. But both jars are dry, so neither will grow at all, and you learn nothing about light from two dead jars. A comparison has to be able to produce a difference.

Why A and D is wrong. This is the two-variable trap, and it is the most common wrong answer. A has water and light. D has neither. If they turn out different, you cannot say whether it was the water or the light, because both changed. It looks like the strongest comparison and it proves the least.

Why A and C is wrong. That pair differs in water, not light. It is a good fair test of the wrong question.

8

Tracing a path

How to recognise it: nothing is labelled, there is no data, and no variable is being tested. The figure just shows an arrangement of objects — mirrors, walls, magnets, pipes, a heater in a room — and you must work out where something goes through it.

These questions feel unfair because there is nothing to read. There is nothing to read because the answer is a line you have to draw yourself.

The procedure

  1. Name what is moving. Light, air, water, or a magnetic field. Say it. Everything else follows from that one word.
  2. Draw the path on the paper with a pencil, starting from the source. Light starts at the lamp or the object. Hot air starts at the heater. Water starts upstream. Draw the line before you reason about the answer.
  3. Apply the one rule for that thing. Light goes in straight lines and bounces off a mirror at the same angle. Hot air rises because it is less dense. Water speeds up where a pipe narrows, because the same amount must get through a smaller gap.
  4. For "why did it not work", look for where your drawn line is blocked or misses its target.
  5. For mirrors, work backwards from the eye. Trace from the eye to the mirror, bounce, and carry straight on. That is where the object must be. Never trace forwards from a guessed position.
  6. If a question says "using your answer above", double-check the first answer before building on it. Getting the first one wrong loses both.
Traps in this type

Checking only half the path. With mirrors, students check that light gets from the mirror to the eye and forget that it must also reach the mirror in the first place, in a straight unblocked line.

Tracing forwards from a guess. Always work backwards from the eye.

Wide pipe, fast water. The intuition that a big pipe carries water better beats the physics for most people. Same amount of water, bigger opening, so it moves slower.

Warm and dense together. Warm air is less dense. Half the wrong options in these questions exist to catch people who pair them the other way.

7

A student looks into the lower mirror of a periscope and sees a bird. Three positions are marked outside the tube. Which one is the bird actually at?

Show answer

Answer: K

Step 1 — name what is moving. Light. Not the eye, not the mirrors. Once I have said "light", the rule I need is fixed: it travels in straight lines and bounces off a mirror.

Step 2 — start at the eye, not at the bird. This is the move that makes periscope questions easy. I do not know where the bird is, so I cannot start there. I do know exactly where the eye is.

Step 3 — trace backwards, one straight segment at a time. Finger on the eye. Straight left to right into the lower opening, until it meets the lower mirror. The mirror is tilted, so the line turns and goes straight up the tube. Up it goes until it meets the upper mirror. That mirror is tilted the other way, so the line turns again and goes straight out sideways through the top opening.

Step 4 — carry straight on. The line leaves the top opening travelling horizontally. Light does not curve, so it keeps going horizontally for ever. Whatever it hits is what the eye sees.

Step 5 — read off the answer. The line leaves level with the top opening and stays level. K is the position at that same level. So the bird is at K.

Why J is the trap. J is higher up, and a bird ought to be high, so it feels right. But the drawn line never rises after it leaves the tube. Feeling right is not the test — the test is whether your traced line arrives there. Once the line is on the paper, J is obviously wrong.

Why L is wrong. L is level with the bottom of the tube, on the far side. Light from L would have to pass straight through the solid tube wall to reach the lower mirror. Trace it and you run into the wall. This is the blocked-path check from step 4 of the procedure.

The general lesson. None of this needed a fact about mirrors beyond "straight lines, and it bounces". What it needed was a line drawn on the paper, starting from the one place you are certain about.

9

Classification trees and keys

How to recognise it: a branching diagram, or a numbered list of either/or choices. The question asks which group something belongs to, or which two things are most closely related.

A dichotomous key is the friendliest figure type in any paper, as long as you refuse to skip ahead.

The procedure

  1. Find the root — the single point everything branches from — and read the split written there. That first split is the most important division in the whole diagram.
  2. Answer one either/or at a time, about your own organism, before moving on. Never jump to a name you recognise further down.
  3. For "which two are most similar", count branch points, not sideways distance. Two things that share a more recent junction are more closely related. Count how far back you must go to reach the junction they share.
  4. For a two-way grid, answer both questions separately, then read off the cell. Do not try to recognise the cell first.
  5. Answer only from the figure. A fact you know from elsewhere is still wrong if the question asks what the figure shows.
Traps in this type

Sideways distance. Two names printed next to each other on a tree can be distantly related. Only the branch points count.

Outside knowledge. If the stem says "from the diagram", then a true statement the diagram does not show is a wrong answer.

Skipping a level and landing in the group next door.

Answering only one question in a grid key. You need both answers to land in the right cell.

8

Use the key to identify creature W: it has a soft body with no legs at all, and it carries a shell on its back. Which group does it belong to?

Show answer

Answer: group 1

Step 1 — find the root and read it. The whole tree starts from one point at the top, and the first split asks a single question: legs, or no legs? That is the most important division in this key, because it decides which half of the diagram I am even looking at.

Step 2 — answer that question, and only that question, about W. The description says "no legs at all". So I go left. Finger on the left branch.

Notice what I did not do. I did not look at the word "shell", even though it is the most memorable thing in the description and I can already see it printed further down. Answer the question you are at, then move.

Step 3 — the next either/or. Having gone left, the branch asks about a shell. W carries a shell on its back. So I go left again, and arrive at group 1, the snail-like group. That fits: a snail has a soft body, no legs, and a shell.

Why group 4 is the trap. Group 4 holds spider-like and centipede-like creatures, and a centipede has a hard covering that people loosely call a shell. If you jump straight to the word "shell" and hunt for it, you can land there. But you can only reach group 4 by going right at the very first split, which means the creature has legs. W has none, so that whole half of the tree was ruled out at step 1 and you never needed to look at it.

Why group 2 is wrong. Group 2 is the correct half of the tree — no legs — but it is the no-shell branch. It is the neighbour of the right answer, one either/or away. This is the next-door trap again: the wrong answer is one step from the correct one, and skipping the second question is all it takes to land on it.

The general lesson. A key cannot be got wrong if you answer every question in order and never read ahead. All the errors come from recognising a word further down the page and jumping to it.

Circuit diagrams

How to recognise it: a battery, some bulbs, some wires drawn as straight lines with square corners, and often a switch. The question asks which bulbs light, which is brightest, what happens when a switch closes, or which of four circuits matches a table of results.

These are the hardest figure questions in the papers, and they are usually the ones people skip. They are worth learning properly instead, because they run on a small number of rules that never change. Everything below is built from four ideas, and the first three you may already have. Take them in order and do not skip the middle one.

The four ideas underneath every circuit question

Idea 1 — current needs a complete loop. Current is the flow of charge round a circuit. It has to leave one end of the battery, go all the way round, and arrive back at the other end. If there is any break in that path — an open switch, a gap, a wire that leads nowhere — nothing flows and nothing lights. A loop that has no break is called a closed loop.

Idea 2 — in a single loop, the current is the same everywhere. This surprises people. It feels as though the bulb nearest the battery should get the current first and take the most. It does not. Think of water going round a closed ring of pipe: the same amount passes every point each second, because it has nowhere else to go. Components joined one after another in a single loop are in series. Identical bulbs in series are all equally bright, whatever order they sit in.

Idea 3 — at a junction, the current splits. This is the idea that gets left out, and it is the one that unlocks the hard questions. Where a wire divides into two branches, the current divides too. Whatever goes in must come out, so the two branch currents add back up to the current that arrived. Components on separate branches are in parallel.

Now the useful consequence. The two branches do not have to get equal shares. Current takes the easier branch more readily, and a branch is easier when there is less in the way to slow it down. A branch containing one bulb is easier than a branch containing two. And a branch made of plain wire with nothing in it at all is easiest of all — so easy that almost the entire current goes that way and hardly any is left for the other branch.

Idea 4 — more current through a bulb means a brighter bulb. That is the only link between the reasoning and what you actually see. Brightness is your meter for current.

The removal test — the most useful sentence on this page for circuits

Take a bulb out and see what survives. If removing one bulb kills the others, it was in series with them. If removing it leaves the others lit, it was on its own parallel branch. A whole table of observations can be turned into a list of statements this way, and that is how you deduce a circuit you were never shown.

The procedure

  1. Trace the loop with a finger, starting at one end of the battery, and see whether you can get back to the other end. If you cannot, nothing lights, and no other reasoning is needed.
  2. Count the loops. One loop means series: same current everywhere, and removing anything stops everything. More than one loop means there are parallel branches, and each branch can work on its own.
  3. Label every component "on the main line" or "on a branch". The main line is the part every loop passes through. Everything on the main line is in series with the whole circuit.
  4. For a switch, ask what new path it creates when it closes, and whether that path goes around a bulb. A path around a bulb steals its current, so that bulb dims or goes out — while bulbs on the shared main line get brighter, because more current is now flowing overall.
  5. For an observation table, turn each row into a plain sentence first — "removing F killed G and H, so F is on the main line" — and only then test the four candidate circuits against your sentences.
  6. Check that your answer explains every row, not just the first one. A circuit that fits two rows out of three is the wrong circuit.
Traps in this type

Brightness follows position. People assume the bulb nearest the battery is brightest, or that current gets "used up" along the way. In a series loop the current is identical at every point, so identical bulbs are equally bright.

Missing a short circuit. A closed switch that offers a plain-wire path around a bulb. Almost all the current takes the easy route and the bypassed bulb goes out. This is the single most common way a circuit question catches people.

Assuming a closed switch means everything gets brighter. Closing a switch can make some bulbs dimmer or dark. It depends entirely on what new path it opens.

Confusing open and closed. Open means there is a gap and nothing flows. Closed means the metal touches and the path is complete. It is worth saying "closed means complete" to yourself once before you start.

Stopping after one row of an observation table. The table has several rows because no single row identifies the circuit. Every wrong option is what you get by stopping early.

Three identical bulbs are labelled J, K and L. A student unscrews one bulb at a time and records what the other two do. Which of the four circuits matches all three results?

Show answer

Answer: circuit 2

Step 5 first — turn every row into a sentence. This is the whole technique. Do not look at the four circuits yet. Cover them with your hand if it helps.

Row 1: removing J killed both the others. So J must be carrying the current that the others depend on. J is on the main line, in series with everything.

Row 2: removing K left the others lit. So current can still get round without passing through K. K must be on a branch of its own.

Row 3: removing L left the others lit. Same reasoning. L is on a branch of its own.

Three sentences. Notice that I now know the answer before I have looked at a single circuit: one bulb on the main line, two bulbs on branches, and the main-line bulb is J.

Now test the candidates against all three sentences.

Circuit 1 fails at row 2. All three bulbs sit in one single loop, so they are all in series. Removing any one of them breaks the only loop and everything goes out. That matches row 1 but contradicts rows 2 and 3, where the others stayed lit. Rejected.

Circuit 3 fails at row 1. All three bulbs are on their own separate branches. Removing any one leaves the other two branches complete, so the others stay lit every time. That matches rows 2 and 3 but contradicts row 1, where removing J killed everything. Rejected.

Circuit 4 fails at rows 1 and 3. It has the right shape — one bulb on the main line, two on branches — but the wrong bulb is on the main line. Here L is the one everything depends on. That predicts removing L kills the others, and removing J does not. The table says the exact opposite. Rejected.

Circuit 2 fits all three. J sits on the main line, so every path from the battery goes through it and removing J stops everything, matching row 1. K and L sit on separate branches, so removing either one leaves the other branch complete and current still flows through J and the surviving bulb, matching rows 2 and 3. Three rows, three matches.

Why circuit 4 is the trap. It is not a random wrong answer. It is circuit 2 with two of the labels swapped, which is the next-door trap from section 1 appearing in a circuit. Anyone who works out the correct shape — one on the line, two on branches — and then picks the first circuit with that shape will choose wrongly half the time. Getting the shape right is only half the job. You must also check which named bulb is where.

What current-splitting adds here. Think about brightness while K and L are both in place. The current flows through J, reaches the junction, and splits between the K branch and the L branch. So J carries the whole current while K and L each carry only part of it, which means J is the brightest bulb. Now unscrew L. The current that was going down the L branch has nowhere to go, so all of it goes through K instead — and K gets brighter than it was before. That is a genuinely surprising result, and it is exactly the kind of follow-up these questions ask.

When the question hands you the rule

Some questions open with a word you have never seen. A body part of a prawn. A type of orbit. A measurement of a wing. The instinct is to freeze, because it looks like a topic nobody taught you.

Look again at the sentences before the question. In these papers, that scary word is almost always defined right there in the stem, along with the exact rule you need. The vocabulary is decoration. The reasoning underneath is at your level.

Real examples from past papers, described rather than quoted: one question defines a prawn's modified front leg, tells you how many segments an ordinary leg has, and asks you to count. Another defines two directions of orbit in two plain sentences and then asks a question you can answer from those sentences alone. A third defines a property of metals, gives a table, and asks which metal heats up most — the definition supplied is the method.

What to do

1. Read the stem twice. Slowly. The second reading is where the rule appears.
2. Underline the sentence that defines something or states a rule. There is almost always exactly one.
3. Write that rule in your own words in the margin, in five words if you can.
4. Now ignore the frightening word completely and apply your rule.

Here is the useful part. A question dressed in unfamiliar vocabulary often has a lower success rate than it deserves, because people give up at the first strange word. If you can stay calm long enough to reach the definition, these are among the most winnable questions in the paper. The difficulty is entirely in the costume.

One warning. If you read the stem twice and genuinely cannot find a supplied rule, then the question does need outside knowledge, and it is a different situation. Move on and come back. The point is to check before you panic, not to assume every hard word is free.

The whole page in one page

If you remember nothing else, remember the first section. Before you commit to any answer on any figure: put a finger on something you are sure of, trace to the thing you mean to name, and say what it connects to on each side. The wrong answer is nearly always the one next door, and a finger is the only thing that tells them apart.

The rest is the same idea, shaped for each kind of figure.

  • Numbered diagrams: name the whole picture first, find the direction of flow, anchor on the part you are surest of — never on part 1.
  • Graphs: axes aloud, one sentence, then what the shape means in words. Numbers last. Predict before you choose.
  • Tables: one row as a sentence, then the row-to-row pattern, before anything clever.
  • Cycles: an arrow is a verb. Say each one as a sentence and the direction stops being guesswork.
  • Food webs: the arrow means "is eaten by". Confirm it on one pair, then number outward from the plants.
  • Set-ups: list what each one has, then find the pair differing in exactly one thing.
  • Paths: name what is moving, then draw the line on the paper. For mirrors, start at the eye.
  • Keys: answer each either/or in turn and never read ahead.
  • Circuits: trace the loop, count the loops, then use the removal test. Kills the others means series. Leaves them lit means its own branch.
  • Strange words: read the stem twice and find the rule it gave you.

None of this is extra science. Every one of these moves is something you can do with a finger and ten seconds, on a question about a topic you already know. That is the point. The science was never the missing part.
