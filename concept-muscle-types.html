Concept

The Three Kinds of Muscle

The body has three different muscle tissues — skeletal muscle that you choose to move, cardiac muscle that beats in the heart without tiring, and smooth muscle that works slowly inside your organs without you ever noticing.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Decide to lift your arm and it lifts. Try to decide to stop your heart and nothing happens. Both jobs are done by muscle, yet you command one and cannot touch the other. That difference is real, and it exists because they are not the same tissue.

All muscle does one thing: it contracts, meaning it gets shorter and pulls. A muscle can never push. It pulls, then relaxes back. Everything a muscle achieves is built out of pulls arranged cleverly.

Beyond that shared ability, the body has three distinct kinds, and they differ in four ways worth checking every time: what they look like under a microscope, whether you control them, how fast they act and how quickly they tire, and where they are found.

Two words do most of the work here. Voluntary means under conscious control — you decide, and it happens. Involuntary means it happens without your decision, run automatically by the nervous system. This one distinction, voluntary against involuntary, is the thing most often tested, and it is also the most useful, because it usually identifies the muscle on its own.

2

How it works

Skeletal muscle. Found attached to the bones of the skeleton — biceps, thigh muscles, the muscles of your face and jaw. Under a microscope it shows clear stripes across the fibres, so it is called striated, meaning striped, and its cells are long and unusually have several nuclei each. It is voluntary: you decide to move and it moves. It contracts quickly and powerfully, and it tires — hold a heavy book at arm's length for twenty minutes and the ache tells you so. It is joined to bone by tendons, tough non-stretchy cords. Non-stretchy matters: if a tendon stretched, the muscle's pull would be absorbed by the tendon instead of moving the bone.

Antagonistic pairs. Because a muscle can only pull, one muscle can never both bend and straighten a joint. So skeletal muscles work in opposing pairs called antagonistic pairs, and the classic example is at the elbow. The biceps sits on the front of the upper arm; the triceps sits at the back. To bend the elbow and raise the forearm, the biceps contracts and shortens, pulling the forearm up — and at the same time the triceps relaxes, letting it happen. To straighten the arm, the reverse: the triceps contracts, pulling the forearm back down, while the biceps relaxes. One contracts while the other relaxes, always. If both contracted hard at once the joint would simply lock in place. This is worth saying carefully because a very common answer is that the biceps relaxes to straighten the arm — true, but incomplete, since relaxing alone does nothing. Something has to pull the other way, and that is the triceps.

Cardiac muscle. Found in one place only: the wall of the heart. Nowhere else in the body has it. It is faintly striated, less obviously than skeletal muscle, and its cells are branched and joined end to end, which lets an electrical signal spread rapidly through the whole wall so the chambers contract together rather than in a ragged sequence. It is involuntary. Its most remarkable property is that it does not tire: it has contracted and relaxed roughly once a second for your entire life and will continue without rest. It manages this because it is exceptionally well supplied with blood and packed with mitochondria, the parts of a cell that release energy using oxygen, so it never has to work without oxygen the way a sprinting leg muscle does.

Cardiac muscle also has its own rhythm. The beat is generated inside the heart itself, by a patch of specialised cells called the pacemaker, not by an instruction from the brain. Nerves and hormones can speed the heart up or slow it down, but they are adjusting a rhythm that already exists. This is why heart muscle in a laboratory dish can go on contracting rhythmically with no nerves attached at all, and why a transplanted heart, whose nerves have been cut, still beats.

Smooth muscle. Found in the walls of hollow organs and tubes: the gut, the blood vessels, the bladder, the airways, the iris of the eye. Under a microscope it has no stripes at all — hence smooth — and its cells are short and spindle-shaped with one nucleus each. It is involuntary. It contracts slowly and can hold a contraction for a long time without tiring, which suits jobs that need steady sustained force rather than a quick burst.

Its most important job is peristalsis, the wave of contraction that pushes food along the gut. Rings of smooth muscle contract behind the food and relax in front of it, so a squeeze travels down the tube and the contents are pushed ahead of it, rather like squeezing toothpaste along from the closed end. Peristalsis is why you can swallow while lying down, and why an astronaut can eat in zero gravity — food is not falling down the gut, it is being pushed. Smooth muscle in the blood vessel walls is also what performs vasodilation and vasoconstriction during temperature control.

A useful summary. Skeletal: striped, voluntary, fast, tires, on bones. Cardiac: faintly striped, involuntary, steady, never tires, heart only. Smooth: unstriped, involuntary, slow, sustained, in the organs and vessels.

The heart of it

Only skeletal muscle is voluntary, and it is the only muscle attached to bones — so if a task is one you chose to do, the muscle doing it is skeletal, and if it happens without your deciding, it is cardiac in the heart or smooth everywhere else.

3

Where the idea came from

The Greek physician Galen, working in the second century, understood that muscles pull rather than push and that they work in opposing pairs — a genuinely good observation. His explanation of how was wrong: he believed a fluid he called animal spirits flowed from the brain down hollow nerves to inflate the muscle, making it swell and shorten. That picture lasted for well over a thousand years.

It was tested in the seventeenth century by the Dutch scientist Jan Swammerdam, in an experiment that is a model of clear thinking. If a muscle swells because something flows into it, its total volume must increase when it contracts. He sealed a frog muscle in a glass tube with water and a narrow neck, made it contract, and watched the water level. It did not rise. The muscle changed shape without changing volume, so nothing was flowing in. A long-accepted theory was undone by measuring the one quantity that had to change if it were true.

The three types were distinguished later, once microscopes were good enough to see the stripes, and this was the work of many anatomists across the nineteenth century rather than one discoverer. The heart's own rhythm was established in the same period: physiologists found that a heart removed from an animal continued to beat, which showed the drive could not be coming from the brain. The pacemaker region itself was identified in 1907 by Arthur Keith and Martin Flack in England.

How a muscle actually shortens was only settled in 1954, when two independent research teams in England — one including Hugh Huxley and Jean Hanson, the other Andrew Huxley and Rolf Niedergerke — proposed that muscle contains two sets of overlapping filaments that slide past each other. The filaments themselves do not shorten; they slide further over one another, so the whole muscle gets shorter. That is why the volume never changes, and Swammerdam's puzzling result finally made sense nearly three centuries after he obtained it.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking muscles can push. They cannot. Every push your body makes is a pull from the other side of a joint, which is the entire reason antagonistic pairs exist.

The second is assuming that anything effortful is voluntary and anything effortless is not. Effort is not the test. Digesting a meal takes real work from smooth muscle for hours and you feel none of it, while holding a light object still is voluntary though it costs little. The test is whether you decided.

The third is treating "muscle" as one tissue and answering about all three at once — for instance saying muscles get tired, which is true of skeletal muscle and untrue of cardiac.

Commonly missed

A student holds a heavy book out in front of her for twenty minutes. Which type of muscle is doing this work: skeletal, cardiac or smooth?

The trap is the twenty minutes. Sustained holding sounds like the slow, tireless, long-lasting work that smooth muscle is described as doing, and the arm is not visibly moving, so it does not feel like the quick powerful action associated with skeletal muscle. The word "heavy" also suggests strain, which pulls people towards involuntary tissue.

How to work it: Ignore the duration and ask the only question that separates the types reliably — did she decide to do it? She did. That makes it voluntary, and skeletal muscle is the only voluntary muscle, so the answer is skeletal. Check it two more ways. Where is the muscle? In the arm, attached to bones by tendons, and only skeletal muscle attaches to bone. Does it tire? After twenty minutes her arm aches badly, and cardiac and smooth muscle do not fatigue like that. All three tests agree. Holding still is not the absence of work; the muscle is contracting the whole time to resist gravity.

This angle — telling skeletal, cardiac and smooth muscle apart from a described everyday task, where the deciding clue is voluntary against involuntary control — has appeared in IMSO papers, including 2019.

Connected ideas

The skeleton page covers how bones and joints turn a muscle's pull into movement. Cardiac muscle is the engine of circulation, and smooth muscle in the vessel walls performs the vasodilation and vasoconstriction of temperature control, as well as the shivering counterpart in skeletal muscle. Smooth muscle drives peristalsis through the gut, which belongs to digestion. All muscle is powered by respiration, and building muscle needs protein from the diet, while contraction itself needs calcium.

In one line

Skeletal muscle is striped, voluntary and attached to bones where it works in antagonistic pairs like the biceps and triceps; cardiac muscle is found only in the heart, is involuntary and never tires because it sets its own rhythm; and smooth muscle is unstriped and involuntary, working slowly in the gut and blood vessels to drive peristalsis.
