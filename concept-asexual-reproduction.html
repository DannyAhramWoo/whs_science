Concept

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction is reproduction from a single parent, producing offspring that are genetically identical to it.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

Cut a shoot from a houseplant, push it into damp soil, and in a few weeks it grows roots and becomes a plant. No seed, no flower, no pollen, no second plant. The new plant is not the offspring of two parents. It is a piece of one parent that carried on growing.

That is asexual reproduction: one parent, no joining of sex cells, and offspring that carry exactly the same instructions as the parent. Those instructions are held in genes, the chemical code inside every cell that decides what an organism is like. Because no genes have been mixed with anyone else's, the offspring are clones — organisms genetically identical to the one they came from.

Compare that with sexual reproduction, which needs two parents. A sex cell from each — pollen and ovule in a plant, sperm and egg in an animal — join together in fertilisation. The offspring gets a mixture of two sets of genes, so it resembles both parents without being a copy of either. That mixing is the whole difference, and everything else follows from it.

Asexual reproduction is not a rare curiosity. It is how most bacteria multiply, how many fungi spread, and how a great many plants fill a patch of ground. Every dandelion in a lawn, every strawberry plant in a bed, may be a copy of one original.

2

How it works

In plants, asexual reproduction usually means growing a new plant from part of an old one, which is called vegetative reproduction.

Runners. A strawberry plant sends out a horizontal stem across the soil surface. Where it touches down, roots grow and a new plant forms. The connecting stem then withers, and the daughter plant lives on its own. Spider plants do the same in the air.

Tubers. A potato is not a root but a swollen underground stem, packed with stored starch. The dimples on it, the "eyes", are buds. Plant one potato and each eye can sprout a new shoot, feeding on the stored food until leaves are made. So a bag of potatoes from a single plant is a bag of clones.

Bulbs. An onion or a daffodil bulb is a very short stem wrapped in thick, food-filled leaf bases. Inside, side buds grow into new bulbs, and one bulb becomes a clump over the years.

Cuttings. The gardener's version. A piece of stem is cut and rooted, sometimes helped by a hormone powder. Growers use it because it guarantees the new plant is exactly like the parent: a grape variety worth having is reproduced by cuttings so that every plant gives the same fruit.

Grafting. A shoot of one plant, called the scion, is joined to the rooted stem of another, called the rootstock, so the tissues fuse and grow as one. Nearly every apple tree in an orchard is a graft. The fruit-bearing top is a clone of a chosen variety, while the roots below come from a tougher plant. Grow an apple from a pip instead and you get something unpredictable, because a pip is the product of sexual reproduction.

In other organisms, the methods are different but the principle is identical.

Binary fission in bacteria. The cell copies its genetic material and splits into two equal cells, each a copy of the original. In good conditions this can happen every twenty minutes, which is why a small contamination in warm food becomes millions of bacteria overnight.

Budding in yeast and hydra. A small bulge appears on the parent's body, grows, and eventually breaks away as an independent individual. Yeast, a single-celled fungus, buds off smaller daughter cells. Hydra, a tiny freshwater animal, grows a miniature hydra on its side which detaches and swims off.

Regeneration and fragmentation. Break a starfish arm off with a piece of the central disc attached and it can regrow into a whole starfish. A flatworm called a planarian cut into pieces can regrow into several complete worms. Each new individual has only one parent and identical genes, so this counts as reproduction, not repair.

The trade-off. Asexual reproduction is fast, needs no partner, needs no pollinator and wastes nothing on flowers or seeds — an organism alone in a good place can fill it. But every offspring is identical, so there is no variation, meaning no differences between individuals. Variation is a population's insurance. If a new disease arrives that one plant cannot resist, none of them can, because they are all the same plant. A whole crop or a whole population can be destroyed at once. Sexual reproduction is slower and needs a partner, but shuffles genes so offspring differ, and some may survive what kills the rest.

The heart of it

One parent means identical offspring, and identical offspring means speed without variation — so an asexual population is efficient until something arrives that it cannot resist.

3

Where the idea came from

Farmers used asexual reproduction for thousands of years before anyone could explain it. Grafting was practised in China and around the Mediterranean well over two thousand years ago, and Roman writers described it as a routine orchard technique. They knew it worked and knew it kept a good variety true. They had no notion of genes, and some believed grafting mixed the natures of the two plants.

Understanding came with the microscope. In the 1670s Antonie van Leeuwenhoek saw single-celled organisms he called animalcules and watched some divide. Through the 1800s biologists worked out that cells arise only by division of existing cells, and by the later part of that century how a cell copies and shares out its contents was being drawn in detail.

One widely repeated story deserves caution. Regeneration in freshwater hydra was reported by Abraham Trembley in the 1740s, and caused a sensation because an animal cut in pieces appeared to become several animals — something many thought impossible. These experiments have been retold so often that details vary between accounts, so treat colourful versions carefully. What is firmly established is that Trembley's work made regeneration a serious subject of study.

Why identical offspring are risky was demonstrated, painfully, by the Irish potato famine of the 1840s. Much of the crop was a small number of varieties multiplied from tubers, so it was close to genetically uniform. When a water mould that causes potato blight spread through it, there was little resistance anywhere and the crop failed repeatedly, with about a million deaths.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking asexual reproduction is somehow primitive, a thing only simple organisms do. It is a strategy with clear advantages, used by plants and animals that also reproduce sexually — a strawberry plant does both, flowering in one season and running along the ground in the same one.

The second, and the one built into these questions, is confusing reproduction with growth. Reproduction makes a new individual. Growth and development make an existing individual bigger or more specialised. Cells dividing inside a body are not cloning the organism, and this distinction is exactly what an IMSO paper of 2016 tested by asking which listed process is not natural cloning.

Commonly missed

Which of these is NOT an example of natural cloning: a planarian worm cut in two growing into two worms; a starfish regrowing from a severed arm; a bacterium dividing into two; a fertilised egg cell developing into a muscle cell?

It is a trap because every option involves cells dividing and producing genetically identical cells, so all four look alike at first glance. The last option is especially tempting to keep, since the cells really are identical copies.

How to work it: Apply one test to each option — at the end of it, is there one organism or more than one? Planarian: one worm becomes two separate worms, so that is cloning. Starfish: one starfish becomes two, so that is cloning. Bacterium: one cell is a whole organism, and it becomes two organisms, so that is cloning. Fertilised egg becoming a muscle cell: at the start there is one developing organism, and at the end there is still one, just with more and more varied cells inside it. No new individual was produced. That is development, not reproduction, so it is the odd one out. There is a second clue too: a fertilised egg came from fertilisation, so two parents were already involved.

5

Connected ideas

Asexual reproduction is the counterpart to reproduction by flowers and seeds, and the contrast between them is really a contrast about genes and variation. Because it produces no variation, it links directly to natural selection, which needs differences between individuals to work on. Binary fission is how microorganisms multiply so quickly, and how living things reproduce is one of the features used to sort them into groups.
