Chapter 18

Sorting Living Things

Scientists sort living things into groups so that any organism can be found, named and compared. The trap this chapter fixes is judging an animal by how it looks — a penguin is not a fish, a dolphin is not a fish, and neither of them is cold-blooded.

18.1

What makes something living

Before you can sort living things, you have to decide what counts as living. Scientists use seven characteristics of living things. A thing is alive only if it does all seven.

They are: movement, respiration (releasing energy from food), sensitivity (responding to changes around it), growth, reproduction, excretion (getting rid of waste made inside the body) and nutrition (taking in food).

Two are easy to muddle. Respiration is not breathing. Breathing moves air in and out; respiration happens inside every cell, releasing energy from food. A plant respires day and night without lungs. And excretion means removing waste the body itself made, such as urine or carbon dioxide — not undigested food.

Commonly missed

A millipede curls into a tight coil when it is touched. Which characteristic of living things does this show?

The trap is that the millipede visibly moves, so "movement" looks right. But every response involves some movement, which would make "sensitivity" a pointless word.

How to work it: ask what caused the change. Something happened outside the animal — a touch — and the animal detected it and reacted. A change outside plus a reaction equals sensitivity. Choose movement only when the animal moves on its own, with no stimulus mentioned.

18.2

Why we classify, and the vertebrate groups

Classification means sorting living things into groups by shared features. We do it for three reasons: to organise the million-plus known species so they are not just a heap; to identify an unknown organism by matching its features; and to see relationships, because things placed in the same group usually share an ancestor. Giving names is a result of classifying, not the reason for it.

Animals split first into vertebrates, which have a backbone, and invertebrates, which do not. The vertebrates then split into five classes. The features that separate them are always the same four: skin covering, how they breathe, eggs or live young, and warm- or cold-blooded.

Insects are invertebrates

Insects, spiders, worms, snails and crabs have no backbone, so they are invertebrates. A beetle's hard outer case is on the outside — it is not a skeleton inside the body. The five vertebrate groups are only fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds and mammals. Nothing else belongs.

18.3

Warm-blooded and cold-blooded

A warm-blooded animal keeps its body at a steady temperature whatever the weather. Its own body produces the heat. A cold-blooded animal does not: its body temperature rises and falls with its surroundings, which is why a lizard basks on a warm rock in the morning.

Only two groups are warm-blooded: birds and mammals. The other three — fish, amphibians and reptiles — are all cold-blooded. That is the whole rule, and it has no exceptions at this level.

The word "cold-blooded" causes trouble, because it sounds like "lives somewhere cold". It actually means "body temperature not controlled". A crocodile in a hot swamp is cold-blooded; a penguin on the ice is warm-blooded.

Commonly missed

Which of these animals is cold-blooded: a penguin, a dolphin or a frog?

Both wrong options are built to look right. A penguin lives in one of the coldest places on Earth, so "cold-blooded" feels natural. A dolphin swims, has a smooth streamlined body and fins, so it looks like a fish.

How to work it: never answer from habitat or body shape. A penguin has feathers and lays eggs, so it is a bird — warm-blooded. A dolphin has lungs, gives birth to live young and feeds them milk, so it is a mammal — warm-blooded. A frog has moist skin and starts life as a tadpole with gills, so it is an amphibian. The frog is the cold-blooded one.

18.4

Plant groups: seeds or spores

Plants split first by how they reproduce. Flowering plants produce flowers, and inside the flower they make seeds. A mango tree, a rose and a blade of grass are all flowering plants, even though grass flowers are small and dull.

Non-flowering plants never make flowers. Ferns and mosses belong here, and they reproduce using spores — tiny dust-like specks, usually released from the underside of a fern frond or from a capsule on a stalk above a moss. A spore is not a seed. A seed carries a tiny baby plant and a food store; a spore is a single cell with neither.

Ferns and mosses differ from each other too. A fern has proper roots and vascular bundles — tubes carrying water through the plant — and that plumbing is what lets ferns grow tall. A moss has neither, only thin thread-like anchors, so water must soak in from the surface. That is why mosses stay low and grow only in damp, shady places.

Spores, not seeds

Only flowering plants make seeds, because seeds form inside flowers. No flower means no seed. Ferns and mosses therefore reproduce by spores, and a question that offers "ferns reproduce by seeds" is always wrong.

18.5

Dichotomous keys and scientific names

A dichotomous key is a set of paired either/or questions used to identify an unknown organism. "Dichotomous" means "cut into two": every step gives exactly two choices, and each choice either sends you to the next question or gives you an answer. You never skip a step and you never go back.

To use one, start at question 1 with your specimen in front of you. Read both options, decide which is true, and follow only that branch. Suppose you have an animal with a backbone, dry scaly skin, and it lays eggs on land. Step 1: backbone — yes, so go to step 2. Step 2: feathers or hair — no, so go to step 3. Step 3: moist skin — no, it is dry, so go to step 4. Step 4: does it lay eggs on land — yes. Reptile. Four questions, no guessing.

Once identified, every organism gets a binomial name: two parts, the genus first and the species second. A tiger is Panthera tigris and a lion is Panthera leo. They share the genus Panthera, so they are close relatives, but the second word differs so they are different species. A dog, Canis familiaris, and a wildcat, Felis sylvestris, differ in both words, so they are not even in the same genus and are much further apart.

Those two ranks sit at the bottom of a ladder that runs from broad to narrow: kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, species. Each step down holds fewer organisms that are more alike. A kingdom holds millions; a species holds only one kind.

Reading a scientific name

Compare the first word to judge how closely two organisms are related. Same first word means same genus, so they are close. Different first words mean the split happened higher up the ladder, so they are distant.

18.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

18.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

A dolphin lives in the sea, has a streamlined body and fins. Explain why it is a mammal and not a fish.

Show answer

Shape and habitat are not classification features. What matters is skin covering, breathing, young and body temperature.

A dolphin breathes air with lungs through a blowhole and would drown if held under water, while a fish takes oxygen from water using gills. A dolphin gives birth to live young and feeds them milk; almost all fish lay eggs. A dolphin also has a little hair as a baby, and it is warm-blooded.

On every feature that counts, it matches mammals. It looks like a fish only because that shape suits swimming.

2

A gardener finds a small green plant growing flat on damp shady soil. It has no flowers, no true roots and no tubes inside its stem. What group is it in, and how does it reproduce?

Show answer

It is a moss, a non-flowering plant. The clues are no flowers, no true roots and no vascular bundles.

Because it has no flowers it cannot make seeds, so it reproduces by spores released from a capsule on a short stalk.

Its lack of tubes also explains where it is growing. Water must soak in directly from the surface, so a moss can only survive in damp shady places and can never grow tall. A fern, which does have tubes and true roots, can.

3

Two animals are named Panthera tigris and Panthera leo. A third is Canis familiaris. Which two are more closely related, and how do you know?

Show answer

Panthera tigris and Panthera leo are more closely related.

In a scientific name the first word is the genus and the second is the species. The tiger and the lion share the genus Panthera, so they only separate at the very last step of the ladder.

Canis familiaris has a different first word, so the dog separates from the other two much further up — at family level or above. Different genus means a much older split.

4

Why do scientists classify living things? Give two reasons that are not "to give them names".

Show answer

To identify. With groups defined by clear features, an unknown organism can be worked out by matching its features against a key, instead of being compared with every known species one by one.

To see relationships. Organisms placed in the same group share features because they share ancestors, so the grouping shows how life is connected.

Classification also organises a huge amount of information so it can be searched. Naming is the result of classifying, not the purpose.

18.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 18.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about animal groups are correct?

  • 1Insects are vertebrates because they have a hard body case.
  • 2All mammals are warm-blooded.
  • 3Amphibians have moist skin and start life in water.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. A vertebrate must have a backbone inside its body. An insect's hard case is on the outside, so insects are invertebrates. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: mammals are warm-blooded, so 2 is correct. Amphibians such as frogs have moist skin and hatch as tadpoles with gills, so 3 is correct. Consistent with C.

2

Which statements about plants are correct?

  • 1Ferns reproduce by spores.
  • 2Ferns have vascular bundles but mosses do not.
  • 3Mosses produce seeds inside small flowers.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. Ferns have no flowers, so they cannot make seeds; they release spores from the underside of their fronds. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Mosses are non-flowering, so they have no flowers and no seeds. They use spores. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Ferns have true roots and tubes carrying water, which is why they grow tall; mosses have neither, which is why they stay flat and damp. So the answer is B.

3

A student uses a dichotomous key to identify a penguin. Which statements are correct?

  • 1Each step of a dichotomous key offers exactly two choices.
  • 2Having feathers places the penguin in the bird group.
  • 3The penguin is warm-blooded.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. "Dichotomous" means cut into two, so every step splits into exactly two options. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. A penguin is a bird, and all birds are warm-blooded. The cold habitat is irrelevant. Since 1 and 3 are both correct, the answer must be A.

Statement 2 — correct. Feathers are found in no other group, so feathers alone settle it. The answer is A.
