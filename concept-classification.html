Concept

Classification

Classification is sorting living things into groups whose members share features, so that knowing the group tells you something true about it.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

You classify things all day without noticing. Because the cutlery drawer keeps forks apart from spoons, you can guess what you have not seen: hand someone an unfamiliar object from the fork slot and they expect prongs.

Science works on the same idea, but chooses groups far more carefully. The point is not tidiness but prediction: a good group is one whose members share so much that a fact learned about one is likely true of the others. Knowing an animal is a mammal, you can predict without checking that it has a backbone, is warm-blooded and feeds its young on milk.

A bad group gives you nothing. Sorting animals into "brown" and "not brown" is consistent, yet says nothing about how they breathe, eat or reproduce. A group is judged by what it lets you predict, not by how easily it applies.

2

How it works

Living things are classified in boxes inside boxes. The big boxes are kingdoms — animals, plants, fungi and some very small organisms. Inside a kingdom the boxes get smaller and their members more similar, until you reach the species, roughly a group similar enough to breed together and produce young that can breed in turn.

At kingdom level you can say only that an animal eats other living things and can usually move; at the group containing cats you can add sharp teeth, retractable claws and forward-facing eyes. Wide groups cover a lot but say little, narrow groups the reverse, because the fewer the members the more they share.

The hard part is choosing which features to sort on, because organisms have hundreds and most are misleading. A dolphin and a shark are both smooth, grey, fast and torpedo-shaped, so sorted on shape they belong together. But a dolphin breathes air, is warm-blooded and feeds its young on milk, while a shark does none of these: the shape is shared because both must push through water, not because they are related. So scientists sort on features that are hard to change — how the skeleton is arranged, how the young develop, how the organism reproduces — rather than colour, size or shape, because only the deep ones track real relationships.

Reading a branching tree

Those nested boxes are often drawn instead as a branching tree, called a cladogram. Read it from the left: a single line splits, each half splits again, and the tips on the right are the groups being compared. Every point where a line divides is a shared ancestor — a population that lived long ago and gave rise to everything above that point.

One rule decides every question asked about such a tree. Two groups are more closely related when the branch point they share is more recent, meaning further to the right. Nothing else counts. It does not matter how near two tips sit on the page, and it does not matter how alike the two animals look, because the artist could have drawn the same tree with the branches swung round like a mobile and the relationships would be unchanged.

Take four invented groups of small burrowing animals: sandmoles, dunerats, spinehogs and glidermice. The first split separates glidermice from the other three, so glidermice branch off earliest. The remaining line splits again, putting spinehogs on one side and leaving sandmoles and dunerats together. Those two then split at the last and most recent branch point.

Now suppose the question says sandmoles and glidermice are drawn next to each other on the page and both have grey fur, and asks which pairing is the closest. The tempting answer is sandmole and glidermouse. It is wrong twice over. Their nearest shared branch point is the very first split, the oldest on the tree, so they are the most distant pair shown. Grey fur is a shallow feature of the kind that misled us with the dolphin and the shark. The correct answer is sandmole and dunerat, because their shared branch point is the most recent one. Trace back from each tip with a finger until the two paths meet, and note where they meet — the pair that meets furthest right wins.

The heart of it

A group is worth having only if belonging to it lets you predict things you have not checked, which is why scientists sort on deep, hard-to-change features rather than on appearance.

3

Where the idea came from

The modern system comes largely from Carl Linnaeus, a Swedish naturalist working in the 1700s. He inherited a mess: the "name" of an organism was often a descriptive Latin phrase, so a tomato might be labelled with a string of words describing its stem, leaves and fruit while a rival naturalist used a different string for the same plant. Linnaeus imposed order: nested ranks of groups, and a two-part name for every species.

It is important to be honest about how much of his sorting was wrong. Linnaeus classified plants mainly by counting stamens. It is easy to apply in the field, which is why he chose it, but it is a shallow feature: counting stamens throws unrelated plants into one group because their flowers share a number, and splits close relatives when their counts differ. Many of his plant groups have since been broken up entirely.

There is a darker part too, and it should not be skipped. Linnaeus also applied his sorting habit to human beings, dividing people into so-called races and attaching descriptions of temperament and character to each. Those descriptions were not observations; they were the prejudices of his time written down as though they were science. Later writers took his framework further, using it to argue that some groups of people were naturally superior to others. That argument was false, and the damage it did was enormous.

The lesson is not that Linnaeus was a fraud; his filing system was a real advance and we still use its shape. It is that a sorting scheme can be tidy, useful and widely adopted while built on the wrong features — and that when people are what is being sorted, wrong features cause harm rather than mere inconvenience.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is grouping by what an organism does or where it lives rather than what it is. "Things that fly" and "things that live in water" cut across the real groups: whales live in water and are mammals; penguins do not fly but are birds; bats fly but are not.

The second is treating groups as fixed. Classification is a claim about relationships, so it is revised when better evidence arrives.

Commonly missed

A pupil sorts four animals — a whale, a shark, a penguin and a bat — into "swimmers" and "fliers". Explain why a scientist would reject this classification, and suggest a better first split.

It is a trap because the sorting is consistent: every animal lands in one box, so it looks correct. The fault is not inconsistency but uselessness.

How to work it: Ask what the group lets you predict. "Swimmer" tells you an animal is streamlined and nothing more — the whale breathes air with lungs and feeds its young on milk, while the shark does neither, so the grouping predicts almost nothing. A better first split asks whether the animal is warm-blooded and feeds its young on milk. That puts whale and bat together as mammals, leaving shark and penguin outside, and each label carries reliable predictions.

5

Connected ideas

The biggest split in the animal kingdom is between vertebrates and invertebrates, classification's opening move. Once groups exist you need a way to place an unknown specimen into one, which a dichotomous key does, and a way to name species unambiguously, which is binomial naming. Underneath sits adaptation, because the features you sort on are shaped by the environment, and knowing which it shaped quickly stops you being fooled.

In one line

Classification sorts living things into nested groups so that naming the group predicts real facts about its members, which only works if you sort on deep features such as skeletons and reproduction rather than shape, colour or habitat.
