Concept

Classification

Classification is sorting living things into groups whose members share features, so that knowing which group something belongs to tells you something true about it.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

You classify things all day without noticing. The cutlery drawer has a slot for forks and a slot for spoons. A supermarket puts milk and cheese together and keeps them away from the shampoo. Nobody had to teach you why: if similar things sit together, you can find what you want, and you can guess what you have not seen. If someone hands you an unfamiliar object from the fork slot, you already expect it to have prongs.

Classification in science works on the same idea, but the groups have to be chosen far more carefully. The point is not tidiness. The point is prediction. A good group is one where the members share so much that a fact learned about one member is likely to be true of the others. If you know that an animal is a mammal, you can predict without checking that it has a backbone, that it is warm-blooded, that it feeds its young on milk, and that it breathes air with lungs. That is a lot of free information from one word.

A bad group gives you nothing. You could sort animals into "brown things" and "not brown things", and that grouping would be perfectly consistent — but knowing an animal is brown tells you nothing about how it breathes, eats or reproduces. So a scientific group is judged by how much it lets you predict, not by how easy it is to apply.

2

How it works

Living things are classified in a set of boxes inside boxes. The big boxes are called kingdoms — animals, plants, fungi and some groups of very small organisms. Inside a kingdom the boxes get smaller and their members get more similar, until you reach the smallest box, the species, roughly a group of organisms similar enough to breed together and produce young that can themselves breed.

Notice what happens as you move down. At the kingdom level you can only say very general things: an animal eats other living things and can usually move. Move down to the group containing cats and you can say much more: sharp teeth for cutting meat, claws that retract, eyes that face forwards. Wide groups cover a lot but say little; narrow groups cover little but say a lot, and that trade-off is the whole engine of classification.

The hard part is choosing which features to sort on, because organisms have hundreds of features and most of them are misleading. A dolphin and a shark are both smooth, grey, fast and shaped like torpedoes. Sorted on shape they belong together. But a dolphin breathes air, is warm-blooded and feeds its young on milk, while a shark does none of these. The shape is shared because both animals must push through water, not because they are related. Features like this, which look alike but arose separately, will wreck a classification if you trust them.

So scientists learned to prefer features that are hard to change and deeply built in — how the skeleton is arranged, how the young develop, how the organism reproduces — over features the environment can shape quickly, such as colour, size or overall shape. A bat's wing and a bird's wing both fly, but the bones inside the bat's wing are laid out like a mammal's hand. The bone plan gives away the true relationship; the flying does not.

The heart of it

A group is worth having only if belonging to it lets you predict things you have not checked, which is why scientists sort on deep, hard-to-change features rather than on appearance.

3

Where the idea came from

The modern system comes largely from Carl Linnaeus, a Swedish naturalist working in the 1700s. He inherited a genuine mess. Before him, the "name" of an organism was often a whole descriptive phrase in Latin. A tomato might be labelled with a string of Latin words describing its stem, its leaves and its fruit, and a rival naturalist might use a different string for the same plant. There was no short, fixed handle to grab. Linnaeus imposed order: nested ranks of groups, and a short two-part name for every species.

It is important to be honest about how much of his actual sorting was wrong. Linnaeus classified plants mainly by counting their reproductive parts — the number of stamens a flower had. This is beautifully easy to apply in the field, which is exactly why he chose it, but it is a shallow feature. Counting stamens throws completely unrelated plants into the same group simply because their flowers happen to share a number, and splits close relatives apart when their counts differ. Many of his plant groups have since been broken up entirely.

There is a darker part too, and it should not be skipped. Linnaeus also applied his sorting habit to human beings, dividing people into so-called races and attaching descriptions of temperament and character to each one. Those descriptions were not observations; they were the prejudices of his time and place written down as though they were science. Later writers took his framework and pushed it much further, using it to argue that some groups of people were naturally superior to others. That argument was false, and the damage it did was enormous.

The lesson is not that Linnaeus was a fraud. His filing system was a real advance and we still use its shape. The lesson is that a sorting scheme can be tidy, useful and widely adopted while still being built on the wrong features — and that when people are the things being sorted, wrong features cause harm rather than mere inconvenience.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is grouping by what an organism does or where it lives rather than what it is. "Things that fly" and "things that live in water" feel like sensible categories, but they cut straight across the real groups. Whales live in water and are mammals; penguins do not fly and are birds; bats fly and are not birds.

The second mistake is treating the groups as fixed forever. Classification is a claim about relationships, and claims get revised when better evidence arrives. Organisms have been moved between groups many times, and will be again.

Commonly missed

A pupil sorts four animals — a whale, a shark, a penguin and a bat — into "swimmers" and "fliers". Explain why a scientist would reject this classification, and suggest a better first split.

It is a trap because the sorting is perfectly consistent. Every animal lands cleanly in exactly one box, so it looks correct. The fault is not inconsistency but uselessness.

How to work it: Ask what the group would let you predict. Knowing an animal is a "swimmer" tells you it is streamlined, and nothing more — the whale breathes air with lungs and feeds its young on milk, while the shark does neither. So the grouping predicts almost nothing. A better first split uses a deep feature: whether the animal is warm-blooded and feeds its young on milk. That puts whale and bat together as mammals, leaving shark and penguin outside, and each of those labels now carries a whole list of reliable predictions.

5

Connected ideas

The first and biggest split in the animal kingdom is between vertebrates and invertebrates, so that idea is really classification's opening move. Once groups exist you need a practical way to place an unknown specimen into one, which is what a dichotomous key does, and a way to name each species unambiguously, which is binomial naming. Underneath it all sits adaptation, because the features you sort on are features shaped by the environment — and knowing which ones the environment shaped quickly is exactly what stops you being fooled.

In one line

Classification sorts living things into nested groups chosen so that naming the group predicts real facts about its members, which only works if you sort on deep features such as skeletons and reproduction rather than on shape, colour or habitat.
