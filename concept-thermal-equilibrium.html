Concept

Thermal Equilibrium

Thermal equilibrium is the state two objects reach when they arrive at the same temperature and heat stops flowing between them.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Leave a mug of hot tea on a table. It cools. Leave a glass of iced water beside it. It warms. Come back in three hours and both are at room temperature, and neither is changing any more. They have reached thermal equilibrium with the room and with each other.

The rule behind this is simple and has no exceptions: heat flows from hotter to colder, and it keeps flowing for exactly as long as there is a temperature difference to drive it. Remove the difference and the flow stops. Thermal equilibrium is that stopping point — the state where everything involved is at one temperature.

It is worth being clear that equilibrium does not mean nothing is happening. At the particle level, energy is still being exchanged constantly across every boundary in both directions. What has changed is that the two flows now balance exactly, so there is no net movement either way and no observable change. It is a busy stillness, not an empty one.

2

How it works

Put a hot block against a cold one. At the boundary, fast particles from the hot side collide with slow particles from the cold side. On average the fast ones lose energy and the slow ones gain it, so energy flows one way. As the hot side slows and the cold side speeds up, the difference in average speeds shrinks, and with it the rate of transfer. When the average speeds match, collisions still occur but a particle is now as likely to gain as to lose. The net flow is zero. That is equilibrium, seen from underneath.

Because the driving force shrinks as the gap closes, cooling is fastest at the start and slows as it goes. A cup of tea at 90 °C in a 20 °C room loses heat rapidly in the first minutes and then more and more sluggishly. This means objects approach equilibrium quickly at first and then linger near it, technically never quite arriving — although in practice the last fraction of a degree is unmeasurable.

The final temperature is not simply the average of the two starting temperatures, and this is where most careful thinking is required. Where the balance point lands depends on the mass of each object and on the material each is made from, through its specific heat capacity — the energy needed to raise one kilogram of it by one degree. Drop a small hot iron nail into a large tub of cool water and the water barely warms while the nail cools dramatically, because there is far more water to share the energy among and because water needs unusually much energy per degree. Reverse the sizes and the outcome reverses.

This idea is also what makes thermometers possible at all, and that is not a minor point. A thermometer reports the temperature of an object only because it has been left in contact long enough to reach equilibrium with it. Read a thermometer too soon and it tells you about the journey rather than the destination. Every temperature measurement ever made depends on this concept working.

The heart of it

Heat flows only while a temperature difference exists, so equilibrium is not the end of activity but the point where opposing flows cancel.

3

Where the idea came from

The observation is ancient. That hot things cool and cold things warm until everything matches its surroundings needs no instrument to notice. What is interesting is how late it was granted the status of a law.

The formal statement is this: if object A is in thermal equilibrium with object B, and B is in equilibrium with C, then A is in equilibrium with C. It sounds so obvious as to be hardly worth saying. But without it thermometry collapses — the whole practice of comparing two objects by touching each in turn with the same thermometer assumes it, and Joseph Black in Edinburgh in the 1760s had already relied on this assumption in distinguishing heat from temperature.

The name is the entertaining part. By the time physicists realised this principle needed stating explicitly, the first law of thermodynamics (energy is conserved) and the second law (heat flows from hot to cold on its own, never the reverse) had already been established and numbered in the mid-1800s. The new principle was more fundamental than either, since both quietly assumed it, so it could not sensibly be called the third. The solution was to number it zero. The term "zeroth law" is generally credited to the British physicist Ralph Fowler, in the 1930s, and it stuck.

It is a good reminder that the order in which ideas are discovered is rarely the order in which they logically belong. Sometimes the foundation is laid last, after the building is already standing.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking that objects at equilibrium have stopped exchanging energy. They have not. Particles keep colliding and energy keeps crossing in both directions; the flows are simply equal, so nothing changes overall.

The second error is assuming the final temperature is the midpoint between the two starting temperatures. It only is when the two objects have equal mass and are made of the same material. Otherwise the larger or more heat-hungry object pulls the result strongly towards its own starting temperature.

A third is expecting equilibrium to arrive at a definite moment. Objects approach it asymptotically, quickly at first and then ever more slowly, so there is no instant at which it snaps into place.

Commonly missed

A small metal spoon at 90 °C is placed into a large bowl of water at 20 °C. A pupil predicts the final temperature will be 55 °C, the average of the two. Why is this wrong, and what is a better estimate?

The trap is that averaging feels like the fair, symmetrical answer, and it is even correct in the special case where the two objects match in mass and material — which is not the case here.

How to work it: Ask which object has to change the most to accommodate the other. The spoon is small; the bowl of water is large and made of a substance that takes a great deal of energy per degree. The heat leaving the spoon is a fixed quantity, and it must be shared among a great many water particles, so it can only raise the water's temperature by a fraction of a degree. Meanwhile the spoon has given away nearly all its excess. The final temperature will be just above 20 °C — very close to the water's starting value. The general rule: the object with more mass and more heat capacity wins, and the equilibrium temperature sits nearer to its starting point.

5

Connected ideas

Thermal equilibrium is where heat flow ends, so it cannot be understood without heat and temperature themselves. Objects reach it by any of the three transfer routes — conduction, convection and radiation — usually all working at once, and the whole account rests on the particle model.

In one line
