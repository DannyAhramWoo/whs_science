Concept

Sound

Sound is a vibration that travels through a material as a wave of squeezed and stretched particles.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

Rest your fingers lightly on your throat and hum. You can feel it buzzing. Every sound that has ever existed began with something vibrating — vocal cords, a guitar string, a drum skin, the air rushing past a car. Nothing vibrating means no sound, without exception.

But the vibration has to get to your ear, and this is where the real content lies. When a drum skin moves outwards it shoves the air particles next to it together, making a region where they are squashed closer than usual, called a compression. When the skin springs back it leaves a region where particles are spread further apart than usual, called a rarefaction. Do this repeatedly and a train of compressions and rarefactions spreads outwards. That travelling pattern is the sound wave.

The essential point is that the air does not travel. Each particle jostles back and forth about its own position and passes the disturbance to its neighbour. When someone speaks to you from across a room, no air from their lungs arrives at your ear. Only the pattern moves — like a wave crossing a field of wheat while every stalk stays rooted where it grew.

Because sound needs particles to pass the disturbance along, it cannot travel through a vacuum. This is the single most important fact about it.

2

How it works

Two properties of the wave map onto the two things your ear notices. Frequency is how many vibrations happen each second, measured in hertz (Hz), and it determines pitch. Faster vibration means higher pitch. A young person can typically hear from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz, and the upper limit falls steadily with age, which is why children hear high-pitched sounds that adults nearby genuinely cannot. Amplitude is how far the particles are displaced, and it determines loudness. A harder strike moves the drum skin further, squeezes the air more strongly, and sounds louder — while the pitch stays the same.

Speed is the part that catches people out, because it runs opposite to intuition. Sound travels at about 340 metres per second in air, roughly 1,500 m/s in water, and about 5,000 m/s in steel. It goes fastest in solids and slowest in gases, which seems wrong until you see the reason: in a solid the particles are packed tightly and firmly bonded, so each one passes the disturbance to its neighbour almost immediately. In a gas the particles are far apart and must travel some distance before they collide, which takes time. Dense packing helps sound and hinders nothing.

That 340 m/s in air is slow enough to notice, and it explains thunder. Light from a lightning flash reaches you effectively instantly, while the sound crawls. Counting the seconds between flash and thunder and dividing by three gives roughly the distance in kilometres — a three-second gap means about one kilometre away. The same lag explains the delay when watching a distant firework, or someone hammering across a valley.

Sound also reflects. A hard flat surface bounces it back, and if the reflection returns late enough to be heard separately you get an echo. This is not merely a curiosity: bats and dolphins navigate and hunt by emitting pulses and timing the returns, ships measure ocean depth by sonar the same way, and ultrasound scans build pictures inside a body from reflections at the boundaries between tissues.

The heart of it

Sound is a pattern of compressions passed from particle to particle, so it needs a material to travel through and moves fastest where particles are packed most tightly.

3

Where the idea came from

That sound comes from vibration was known in antiquity — Pythagoras and his followers in the sixth century BC studied how the length of a plucked string relates to the note it produces, though the stories attached to him are heavily legendary and should not be taken literally.

Measuring sound properly began much later. Marin Mersenne, a French friar and mathematician, made the first serious measurements of the speed of sound in the 1630s, timing the delay between a distant gunshot's flash and its report. His figure was somewhat too high but in the right region, and he also worked out the laws relating a string's length, tension and thickness to its pitch — laws still taught today.

The decisive experiment on whether sound needs a medium was performed in Oxford in 1660 by Robert Boyle, using an air pump built for him by Robert Hooke. Boyle suspended a ticking watch, or in some versions a bell, inside a sealed glass jar and pumped the air out. As the air thinned, the sound faded until it could no longer be heard — while the mechanism remained plainly visible, still working. Sight passed through the vacuum; sound did not. It was a beautifully direct demonstration that light and sound are fundamentally different kinds of thing.

It is worth noting the experiment was harder than it sounds. Early pumps could not produce a true vacuum, and sound leaked out through the mountings holding the object, so the noise never vanished completely and critics could argue the point. The conclusion was correct, but the evidence was messier than the tidy version in textbooks, and this is closer to how experimental science usually goes.

Isaac Newton attempted a theoretical calculation of the speed of sound in 1687 and got a value about 15 per cent too low. The discrepancy went unexplained for over a century until Pierre-Simon Laplace realised in the early 1800s that Newton had neglected the heating that accompanies rapid compression of the air.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is imagining that air itself travels from the source to your ear. It does not. Individual particles oscillate about fixed positions and hand the disturbance on; only the pattern crosses the room.

The second is expecting sound to travel more easily through thin air than through dense solids, by analogy with pushing through a crowd. The analogy misleads. Sound needs particles close enough to bump into each other quickly, so it moves fastest in steel, slower in water, slowest in air, and not at all in nothing.

A third is confusing pitch with loudness. They are independent: you can have a high quiet note or a low loud one. Pitch comes from frequency, loudness from amplitude.

Commonly missed

An astronaut outside a spacecraft strikes the metal hull hard with a spanner. A second astronaut, floating nearby but not touching the craft, hears nothing. A third, whose gloved hand is resting on the hull, reports hearing a clear clang. Both are in the same vacuum. Explain.

The trap is a false either-or: once you know that space is a vacuum, "no sound in space" seems to settle it, and the third astronaut looks impossible.

How to work it: Do not ask whether sound is possible in space; ask what path of particles connects the source to each listener. For the floating astronaut, the path is vacuum — no particles, no way to pass a compression along, so nothing arrives. For the astronaut touching the hull, there is an unbroken chain of particles from the struck metal, through the hull, into the glove, and on through the suit material to the ear. Solids carry sound better than air does, so the clang is transmitted clearly. Both observations are correct. The rule is not that sound cannot exist in space but that it cannot cross a vacuum, and a physical contact path removes the vacuum from the journey.

Connected ideas

Sound bounces off hard surfaces by reflection, the same process that governs light in a mirror, though the two are otherwise very different — light crosses a vacuum and sound cannot. Sound carries energy from its source to your ear, its behaviour depends entirely on the particle model of matter, and it travels differently through each state of matter.

Sound is a vibration passed along as compressions and rarefactions between particles, so it needs a material, travels fastest through solids, and cannot cross empty space.
