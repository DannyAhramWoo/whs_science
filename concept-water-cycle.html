Concept

The Water Cycle

The water cycle is the continuous movement of the Earth's water between sea, air and land, driven by energy from the Sun and by gravity.

Introduced in Chapter 13: Cycles and the Environment

1

What it means

The Earth has a fixed amount of water — about 1.4 billion cubic kilometres — and has had for billions of years. Essentially none escapes to space and essentially none is created. What water does instead is move, and change state, endlessly. That circulation is the water cycle.

Four changes of state do the work. Evaporation is liquid water becoming water vapour, an invisible gas. Condensation is vapour becoming liquid again, which is what clouds and dew are. Freezing and melting move water between liquid and solid ice. Water is unusual in being the only substance found naturally in all three states across the ordinary range of temperatures on Earth, which is precisely why it can cycle so readily.

The cycle needs two things to keep going. The Sun supplies the energy to lift water into the air — evaporation requires energy, which is why a wet towel dries faster in sunshine. Gravity brings it back down, as rain and then as rivers running to the sea. Without the Sun the cycle would stop; without gravity the water would never return.

2

How it works

Sunlight warms the surface of oceans, lakes and rivers. Water molecules at the surface with enough energy break free and enter the air as vapour. About 86 per cent of all evaporation is from the oceans, which is unsurprising given that they cover 71 per cent of the planet. Plants add their share by transpiration — water drawn up by roots and released as vapour through tiny pores in the leaves called stomata. A large oak can release hundreds of litres on a hot day. Because evaporation and transpiration are hard to separate in practice, they are often lumped together as evapotranspiration.

Warm, moist air rises, and here the crucial mechanism appears. Air cools as it rises, at roughly 10 degrees Celsius per kilometre in dry air. Cooler air can hold less water vapour than warm air, so as the parcel rises it eventually reaches the point where it cannot hold what it is carrying, and the vapour condenses into tiny droplets. These droplets need something to condense onto — specks of dust, salt or smoke, called condensation nuclei — and the result is a cloud. So a cloud is not vapour. It is billions of liquid droplets small enough to remain suspended.

Droplets in a cloud collide and merge. Once one is heavy enough that the air can no longer keep it aloft, it falls: this is precipitation, as rain, or as snow or hail if it forms and stays below freezing.

On land, water divides. Some runs across the surface into streams and rivers — run-off — and returns to the sea. Some soaks in, which is infiltration, and becomes groundwater, moving slowly through the pores of rock. Some is taken up by plants. Some evaporates straight back.

The timescales differ enormously between routes. A water molecule spends on average about nine days in the atmosphere, but thousands of years in the deep ocean and up to hundreds of thousands of years locked in an ice sheet. Ninety-seven per cent of the Earth's water is salt water in the oceans; of the remaining fresh water, most is frozen in ice caps or held underground, leaving well under one per cent readily available in lakes and rivers.

One further point: evaporation leaves salt behind, because only the water molecules escape. That is why rain is fresh even though most of it evaporated from the sea, and it is the reason the whole cycle is a natural purification system.

The heart of it

The Sun lifts water into the air as vapour and gravity brings it back as rain — the same water, over and over.

3

Where the idea came from

The wrong version of this story lasted nearly two thousand years, and it is instructive because it was rejected for a bad reason: a calculation nobody actually did.

The Greeks knew about evaporation and rain, but most thinkers did not believe rainfall could account for rivers. It simply seemed obviously insufficient — rivers flow all year, including through dry spells, and the water has to come from somewhere. So the accepted explanation was that seawater travelled through underground channels into the mountains, where it was somehow purified of its salt and emerged as springs. Aristotle proposed a version in which air in caverns condensed underground into water. These ideas persisted through the Middle Ages and were still standard in the 1500s.

Bernard Palissy, a French potter with no university training, argued in a book of 1580 that rivers and springs come from rainfall and snowmelt soaking into the ground, and nothing else. He was reasoning from what he had actually watched happen on hillsides and in gardens. Being an artisan rather than a scholar, he was largely ignored by the learned, though he was right.

What finally settled it was measurement. Around 1674 Pierre Perrault, a French official, published the results of a straightforward investigation: he estimated the area of the upper Seine basin, measured rainfall there over several years, and compared the total volume of rain with the volume of water the river actually carried. The rainfall turned out to be roughly six times more than the river discharged. The old objection collapsed — there was not too little rain, there was far more than enough, with plenty to spare for evaporation and soaking in. Edme Mariotte and later Edmond Halley confirmed and extended the work. It is a good example of a long-running dispute ended not by a clever argument but by somebody finally taking the measurements.

4

What people get wrong

The most frequent error is calling clouds and steam "water vapour". Water vapour is a gas and is completely invisible. If you can see it, it has already condensed into liquid droplets. Look at a boiling kettle: there is a small clear gap just at the spout, which is the actual vapour, and the visible white plume beyond it is condensed droplets. Naming this correctly matters, because the whole cycle turns on the difference between the two states.

A second error is thinking the water in a river is new water. It is not. Every glass of water contains molecules that have been through the cycle countless times.

Commonly missed

Almost all the water that evaporates comes from the salty oceans, yet rain is fresh water. Explain.

The trap is assuming that whatever is in the sea must go up with it, so the rain ought to be at least slightly salty. Something must be separating the two.

How to work it: Think about what evaporation actually does. Seawater is a solution: water is the solvent, and salt is the solute dissolved in it. Evaporation happens when individual water molecules at the surface gain enough energy to escape into the air as gas. Salt has a very high boiling point and does not evaporate at ordinary temperatures, so only the water molecules leave; the salt stays behind in the sea. The vapour that rises is therefore pure water, and when it condenses into cloud droplets and falls as rain, it is still pure water. This is the same principle used deliberately in distillation, and it is why the sea grows very slightly saltier as rivers keep delivering dissolved minerals to it while only pure water leaves.

Connected ideas

The whole cycle is a sequence of changes of state, with evaporation and condensation at its centre, and it depends on water's ability to act as a solvent and then leave its solutes behind. The rising of warm moist air is convection, and the falling of rain and flowing of rivers is gravity at work.

In one line

The Sun evaporates water into the air, where it condenses into clouds and falls as rain, and gravity returns it through rivers and groundwater to the sea — the same fixed store of water, circulating for ever.
