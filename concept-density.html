Concept

Density

Density is how much mass is packed into a given volume of a material, worked out by dividing the mass by the volume.

Introduced in Chapter 5: Floating, Sinking and Density

1

What it means

People say that lead is heavy and feathers are light, but said plainly that is not true. A lorryload of feathers weighs far more than a lead pellet. What they mean is that lead is heavy for its size. Density is the careful version of that idea: it tells you how much mass you get in a fixed amount of space, so you can compare materials fairly regardless of how much of each you happen to have.

You calculate it by dividing mass by volume. Mass is how much matter there is, measured in grams or kilograms. Volume is how much space it takes up, measured in cubic centimetres or cubic metres. So density comes out in grams per cubic centimetre (g/cm³), which literally reads as: how many grams sit in each cubic centimetre.

Water has a density of 1 g/cm³, which is a very convenient number and worth memorising, because it becomes the yardstick for everything else. Iron is about 7.9 g/cm³, so a lump of iron has nearly eight times the mass of the same-sized lump of water. Cooking oil is about 0.9. Air, at ordinary conditions, is roughly 0.0012 — about eight hundred times less dense than water, which is why swimming feels so unlike walking.

2

How it works

The crucial feature of density is that it does not depend on how much of the substance you have. Cut a gold bar in half and each half has half the mass and half the volume, so the division gives the same answer. Density is a property of the material, not of the object. That is what makes it useful for identification: measure a mystery lump's mass and volume, divide, and compare with a table.

Try it with numbers. A block has a mass of 270 g and measures 10 cm × 5 cm × 2 cm. Its volume is 100 cm³. Its density is 270 ÷ 100 = 2.7 g/cm³. That figure matches aluminium closely, so the block is very likely aluminium — and notice you never had to cut it open or melt it.

For awkward shapes you cannot use a ruler, so you use displacement. Drop the object into a measuring cylinder with water in it and read the level before and after. The rise in level is the object's volume, because the object has pushed aside exactly its own volume of water. If the level goes from 50 ml to 74 ml, the object occupies 24 cm³, since 1 ml and 1 cm³ are the same amount of space.

Why do materials differ in density at all? Two reasons, and they work together. The particles that make up different materials have different masses — a gold atom is far heavier than an aluminium atom. And in different materials the particles are packed at different spacings. Gases are thousands of times less dense than solids almost entirely because of spacing: their particles are far apart with empty space between.

That spacing point explains why density changes with temperature. Heat a liquid and its particles move faster and spread slightly further apart. The mass has not changed but the volume has grown, so the density falls. This is exactly why hot air rises and why warm water sits on top of cold in a lake, and it is the engine behind convection.

The heart of it

Density is mass divided by volume, and because it is a ratio it stays the same however big or small a piece of the material you take.

3

Where the idea came from

The famous story is about Archimedes in Syracuse, in Sicily, in the third century BC. King Hiero had given a goldsmith gold to make a crown, and suspected the smith had kept some gold and mixed in cheaper silver. Archimedes was asked to find out without damaging the crown. He is said to have noticed, while lowering himself into a bath, that his body pushed the water up — realised this gave him a way to measure the volume of an irregular object — and run through the streets shouting "Eureka", meaning "I have found it".

It is a wonderful story and you should treat it with caution. It was written down by the Roman architect Vitruvius roughly two hundred years after Archimedes died. That is a long gap, and Archimedes never mentions it in any of his own surviving writing. It may be embroidered, and the running-through-the-streets detail has the flavour of a tale improved in the retelling.

There is also a technical objection. The displacement method as popularly described is probably too imprecise to have worked. The difference in water level between a pure gold crown and a slightly adulterated one would be a fraction of a millimetre, well below what could be read reliably with the equipment of the time. So even if Archimedes did have a bath and did have an idea, the method he actually used was very likely more subtle.

What is not in doubt is what Archimedes himself wrote. In On Floating Bodies he set out the principle about upthrust that still carries his name. A more accurate approach to the crown, and one consistent with his own work, would be to weigh the crown in air and again while it hung submerged in water: a crown with silver in it would be bulkier for its weight and so would lose more apparent weight in water. That comparison can be made sensitive enough to work. The bath may be legend; the physics is real.

4

What people get wrong

The main confusion is between density and mass. "Heavy" is about mass and depends on how much you have; density does not. A large polystyrene block can have more mass than a small steel bolt while being far less dense. Whenever a question uses the word "heavier", check whether it means more mass or more dense — they are different questions with different answers.

The second slip is forgetting that density can change for the same substance. Ice, water and steam are all the same substance at different particle spacings and their densities differ considerably. Ice is less dense than liquid water, which is unusual and is why ice floats.

Commonly missed

A solid cube of metal is cut exactly in half. What happens to its mass, its volume and its density?

The trap is treating all three as behaving the same way, and saying density halves along with the rest.

How to work it: Mass halves, because there is half as much matter. Volume halves, because it takes up half the space. Density is mass ÷ volume, and halving the top and the bottom of a fraction leaves it unchanged — for example 200 ÷ 25 = 8, and 100 ÷ 12.5 = 8. The density stays the same, because it is a property of the metal, not of the lump.

5

Connected ideas

Density is the key to floating: an object floats in a fluid less dense than itself only if it can displace enough of that fluid, which is the idea of upthrust. It rests on mass and on the distinction between mass and weight. It is also what drives convection, because heating a fluid lowers its density and the warmer, lighter fluid rises. And it makes sense at all only because of the particle model.
