Concept

Reproduction in Plants

Plants make new plants in two ways: sexually, through flowers, pollen and seeds, which mixes two parents' characteristics; and asexually, through runners, tubers, bulbs and cuttings, which copies one parent exactly.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

A flower is not decoration. It is the reproductive organ of the plant, and every part has a job in making seeds. The colour and scent are advertising, aimed at insects.

The male part is the stamen, in two pieces: the anther, a sac making pollen (fine grains carrying the male sex cells), and the filament, the stalk holding it up.

The female part is the carpel, in three pieces: the stigma at the top, a sticky or feathery surface that catches pollen; the style, a stalk below it; and at the base the ovary, a chamber holding one or more ovules, the structures containing the female sex cells, which become seeds.

Around these are the petals, and outside them the sepals, green flaps that protected the bud. In a longitudinal section — a diagram cutting the flower in half top to bottom — the anther sits on its slender filament near the outside, while the stigma is on the central stalk directly above the swollen ovary. Identifying the two is a standard question, and the ovary beneath is the reliable landmark for the stigma.

2

How it works

Two steps follow, and confusing them is the classic error, so keep them apart.

Pollination is the transfer of pollen from an anther to a stigma. It happens on the outside of the flower and involves no joining of cells. Self-pollination means pollen reaches a stigma on the same plant; cross-pollination means a different plant of the same species.

Fertilisation happens afterwards, inside. The pollen grain grows a pollen tube down through the style to the ovary, and the male sex cell travels down it and fuses with the female sex cell in an ovule. Only now have two cells joined. Pollination is delivery; fertilisation is joining. Pollination can happen without fertilisation following — most obviously when the pollen is the wrong species.

That point is examined directly. Move pollen from a hibiscus onto an orchid's stigma and you have genuinely pollinated it, but no offspring will form. They are different species, so their sex cells are not compatible and fertilisation cannot occur.

Flowers are built around whichever carrier they use. Insect-pollinated flowers have bright petals, scent and nectar. Anthers and stigmas sit inside so a visiting insect brushes against them, and the pollen is sticky or spiky to cling to a body, with little of it since delivery is targeted. Wind-pollinated flowers such as grasses are the opposite: dull petals or none, no scent, no nectar. Anthers dangle outside where the wind shakes them, stigmas are large and feathery to sieve pollen from the air, and the pollen is smooth, light and produced in enormous quantities because most grains are wasted.

After fertilisation each ovule becomes a seed, and the ovary wall thickens to become the fruit — so botanically a pea pod, a tomato and an acorn are all fruits.

Seeds must then get away from the parent, or they grow in its shade competing for light and water. This is seed dispersal, and the fruit's structure tells you the method. A feathery plume or papery wing means wind dispersal — dandelion, sycamore. A sweet fleshy fruit is animal-dispersed by being eaten, the seeds passing through undamaged; hooks and burrs also use animals, catching on fur. A light, waterproof fruit like a coconut is water-dispersed. Some pods dry, twist and split, flinging seeds out — explosive dispersal. Given a picture, read the structure and name the agent it suits.

Asexual reproduction uses no flowers, pollen or seeds. A strawberry sends out a horizontal stem called a runner that roots where it touches soil. A potato is a tuber, a swollen underground stem whose buds sprout. An onion is a bulb, a short stem with fleshy leaves that divides. Gardeners exploit this with cuttings: a piece of stem in soil grows roots and becomes a whole plant, and tissue culture does the same from a few cells. Both are artificial vegetative propagation, since a person does the propagating — while growing from seed is not, seeds being the sexual route.

The genetic difference matters. Asexual offspring are clones, identical to the one parent with no variation. That is useful if the parent is a good variety, but a field of identical plants shares identical weaknesses, so one disease can destroy all of them. Sexual reproduction combines two parents, producing offspring that vary, and that variation lets a population cope with change.

The heart of it

Pollination moves pollen to the stigma; fertilisation is the later fusing of sex cells inside the ovule — two separate steps, and the first can happen without the second.

3

Where the idea came from

That plants reproduce sexually was not obvious and was established surprisingly late. Farmers had known for millennia that date palms would not fruit unless certain trees were present, and hand-pollinated them without knowing why.

The demonstration is credited to Rudolf Jakob Camerarius, a German botanist at Tübingen, around 1694. His method was simple: he removed the anthers, or isolated female plants away from any male, and showed that no seeds formed. Restore the pollen and seeds appeared. He had proved pollen is necessary, and that anther and ovary are the male and female parts.

The idea met resistance, partly because assigning sexes to plants offended sensibilities of the period. Carl Linnaeus later built his plant classification on counting stamens and carpels, which spread the sexual interpretation widely.

What nobody could see was fertilisation itself. Camerarius knew pollen was needed but not what it did. The pollen tube growing down the style was observed only in the nineteenth century, and only then did pollination and fertilisation become clearly separate events.

4

What people get wrong

Treating pollination and fertilisation as the same thing is the error, and it is examined deliberately. Fix it by location: pollination happens outside, on the stigma; fertilisation inside, in the ovule. Fix it by action: pollination is transport, fertilisation is fusion.

A second confusion is between seed dispersal and pollination. Both carry something away, but pollination moves pollen before seeds exist, while dispersal moves finished seeds afterwards. If the question shows a fruit, it is dispersal.

Past IMSO papers have asked for anther and stigma to be identified on a cut-open flower, matched a plumed fruit to wind dispersal, and asked why cross-pollinating two species yields nothing.

Commonly missed

A gardener brushes pollen from a hibiscus onto the stigma of an orchid. No seeds form. Has pollination occurred? Explain what has and has not happened.

The trap is that "no seeds formed" makes people answer that pollination failed. It did not fail — the later step did.

How to work it: Pollination is the transfer of pollen to a stigma, and pollen was transferred to a stigma, so yes, pollination occurred, and the answer must say so. What did not occur is fertilisation. Hibiscus and orchid are different species, so the pollen grain cannot grow a tube and fuse its sex cell with the orchid ovule. Without fertilisation the ovules do not become seeds. The point tested is that the two are separate steps and only the second was blocked.

5

Connected ideas

Flower structure is used to classify plants, and the differences between insect- and wind-pollinated flowers are adaptations to different carriers. The seed grows into a plant that feeds itself by photosynthesis. Variation from sexual reproduction is the raw material natural selection works on, while clones supply almost none.

In one line

Pollen goes from anther to stigma — that is pollination — and only afterwards does a pollen tube carry the male cell down the style to fuse with an ovule, which is fertilisation, turning ovules into seeds and the ovary into a fruit, while runners, tubers, bulbs and cuttings skip all of it and produce identical clones.
