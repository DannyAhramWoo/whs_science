Concept

Reproduction in Plants

Plants make new plants in two ways: sexually, through flowers, pollen and seeds, which mixes two parents' characteristics; and asexually, through runners, tubers, bulbs and cuttings, which copies one parent exactly.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

A flower is not decoration. It is the reproductive organ of the plant, and every part of it has a job in making seeds. The colour and scent that people plant gardens for are advertising, aimed at insects.

The male part is the stamen, and it has two pieces: the anther, a small sac at the top that makes pollen (fine grains carrying the male sex cells), and the filament, the thin stalk holding the anther up. A flower usually has several stamens.

The female part is the carpel, sometimes called the pistil, and it has three pieces. At the top is the stigma, a sticky or feathery surface that catches pollen. Below it is the style, a stalk connecting stigma to base. At the base is the ovary, a chamber containing one or more ovules — the structures holding the female sex cells, which become seeds.

Around these are the petals, often coloured, and outside them the sepals, small green flaps that protected the flower when it was a bud. In a longitudinal section — a diagram cutting the flower in half from top to bottom — the anther sits on its slender filament near the outside, while the stigma is on the central stalk directly above the swollen ovary. Finding the anther and the stigma on such a diagram is a standard question, and the ovary beneath is the reliable landmark for locating the stigma.

2

How it works

Two steps follow, and confusing them is the classic error in this topic, so keep them firmly apart.

Pollination is the transfer of pollen from an anther to a stigma. It happens on the outside of the flower and involves no joining of cells whatsoever. Self-pollination means pollen reaches a stigma on the same plant; cross-pollination means it reaches a different plant of the same species.

Fertilisation is what happens afterwards, inside. The pollen grain grows a pollen tube down through the style to the ovary. The male sex cell travels down this tube and fuses with the female sex cell in an ovule. Only now have two cells joined. Pollination is a delivery; fertilisation is the joining. Pollination can happen without fertilisation following — most obviously when the pollen is the wrong species.

That last point is examined directly. Move pollen from a hibiscus onto the stigma of an orchid and you have genuinely pollinated it, but no offspring will ever form. The two are different species, and their sex cells are not compatible, so the pollen tube fails or fertilisation cannot occur. Pollination between species is possible; successful reproduction between species is not.

Flowers are built around whichever carrier they use, and comparing the two is a favourite question. Insect-pollinated flowers have large bright petals, scent and nectar (a sugary liquid) to attract insects. Their anthers and stigmas sit inside the flower so a visiting insect must brush against them. Their pollen is sticky or spiky, to cling to a body, and there is relatively little of it, because delivery is targeted. Their stigmas are small and sticky. Wind-pollinated flowers, such as grasses, are the opposite in every respect: small dull petals or none, no scent, no nectar. Anthers dangle outside on long filaments where the wind can shake them, and stigmas are large and feathery to sieve pollen out of moving air. The pollen is smooth, light and produced in enormous quantities, because the wind aims at nothing and most grains are wasted.

After fertilisation, each fertilised ovule becomes a seed, and the ovary wall thickens around them to become the fruit. That is the botanical definition, so a pea pod, a tomato and an acorn are all fruits. Petals and stamens, having done their work, wither and drop.

The seeds must then get away from the parent, or they will grow in its shade and compete with it for light, water and minerals. This is seed dispersal, and the fruit's structure tells you the method. A fruit with a feathery plume or papery wing is wind-dispersed — dandelion, sycamore — because those structures catch the air and slow the fall. A sweet fleshy fruit is animal-dispersed by being eaten, the seeds passing through undamaged; hooks and burrs are also animal dispersal, catching on fur. A fruit that is light, waterproof and air-filled, like a coconut, is water-dispersed. Some pods dry, twist and split, flinging seeds out — explosive dispersal. Given a picture of a fruit, read the structure and name the agent that structure suits.

Asexual reproduction uses no flowers, pollen or seeds. A strawberry sends out a horizontal stem called a runner that roots where it touches soil. A potato is a tuber, a swollen underground stem whose buds sprout into new plants. An onion is a bulb, a short stem with fleshy leaves that divides. Gardeners exploit this with cuttings: a piece of stem placed in soil or water grows roots and becomes a whole plant. Tissue culture does the same from a few cells in a laboratory. Cuttings and tissue culture are called artificial vegetative propagation, because a person, not the plant, does the propagating — and note that growing a plant from seed is not vegetative propagation at all, since seeds are the sexual route.

The genetic difference matters. Asexual offspring are clones: genetically identical to the single parent, with no variation. That is useful if the parent is a good variety, since every copy will be as good. But a whole field of identical plants shares identical weaknesses, so one disease can destroy all of them. Sexual reproduction combines two parents, producing offspring that vary, and that variation is what lets a population cope with change.

The heart of it

Pollination moves pollen to the stigma; fertilisation is the later fusing of sex cells inside the ovule — two separate steps, and the first can happen without the second.

3

Where the idea came from

That plants reproduce sexually at all was not obvious and was not established until surprisingly late. Farmers had known for millennia that date palms would not fruit unless certain trees were present, and had hand-pollinated them without knowing why.

The demonstration is credited to Rudolf Jakob Camerarius, a German botanist at Tübingen, around 1694. His method was elegantly simple: he removed the anthers from plants, or isolated female plants away from any male, and showed that no seeds formed. Restore the pollen and seeds appeared. He had proved that pollen is necessary, and that the anther and the ovary are male and female parts.

The idea met resistance, partly because assigning sexes to plants offended some sensibilities of the period. The Swedish naturalist Carl Linnaeus later built his plant classification system on counting stamens and carpels, which spread the sexual interpretation widely even though that particular classification scheme was eventually replaced.

What nobody could see was fertilisation itself. Camerarius knew pollen was needed but not what it did. The pollen tube growing down the style was observed in the nineteenth century, and only then did pollination and fertilisation become clearly separate events rather than one process.

4

What people get wrong

Using pollination and fertilisation as though they mean the same thing is the error, and it is examined deliberately. Fix it by location: pollination happens outside, on the stigma; fertilisation happens inside, in the ovule. Fix it by action: pollination is transport, fertilisation is fusion.

A second confusion is between seed dispersal and pollination. Both involve something being carried away, but pollination moves pollen before seeds exist, while dispersal moves finished seeds afterwards. If the question shows a fruit, it is dispersal.

A third is assuming self-pollination is impossible or abnormal. It is common and produces viable seeds, though with less variation than cross-pollination.

Past IMSO papers have tested this by asking for anther and stigma to be identified on a cut-open flower, by matching a plumed fruit to wind dispersal, and by asking why cross-pollinating two different species yields nothing.

Commonly missed

A gardener brushes pollen from a hibiscus onto the stigma of an orchid. No seeds form. Has pollination occurred? Explain what has and has not happened.

The trap is that "no seeds formed" makes people answer that pollination failed. It did not fail — the later step did.

How to work it: Pollination is defined as the transfer of pollen to a stigma, and pollen was transferred to a stigma. So yes, pollination occurred, and the answer must say so. What did not occur is fertilisation. Hibiscus and orchid are different species, so the pollen grain cannot successfully grow a tube and fuse its sex cell with the orchid ovule. Without fertilisation the ovules do not become seeds and the ovary does not become a fruit. The point being tested is that pollination and fertilisation are separate steps and only the second was blocked.

5

Connected ideas

Flower structure is one of the features used to classify plants, and the differences between insect- and wind-pollinated flowers are adaptations to different carriers. The seed that results grows into a plant that feeds itself by photosynthesis. Variation from sexual reproduction is the raw material natural selection works on, while clones from asexual reproduction supply almost none.
