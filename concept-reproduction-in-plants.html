Concept

Reproduction in Plants

Plants make new plants in two ways: sexually, through flowers, pollen and seeds, which mixes two parents' characteristics; and asexually, through runners, tubers, bulbs and cuttings, which copies one parent exactly.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

A flower is not decoration. It is the reproductive organ of the plant, and every part has a job in making seeds. The colour and scent are advertising.

The male part is the stamen: the anther, a sac making pollen (grains carrying the male sex cells), on the filament that holds it up.

The female part is the carpel: the stigma at the top, a sticky surface that catches pollen; the style, a stalk below it; and at the base the ovary, holding ovules, which contain the female sex cells and become seeds.

Around these are the petals and the sepals, green flaps that protected the bud. In a longitudinal section — the flower cut in half top to bottom — the anther sits on its filament near the outside, while the stigma tops the central stalk above the swollen ovary, the landmark for finding it.

2

How it works

Two steps follow, and confusing them is the classic error. Pollination is the transfer of pollen from an anther to a stigma, outside the flower, with no joining of cells. Self-pollination means pollen reaches a stigma on the same plant; cross-pollination a different plant of the same species.

Fertilisation happens afterwards, inside. The pollen grain grows a pollen tube down the style to the ovary, and the male sex cell travels down it and fuses with the female cell in an ovule. Pollination is delivery; fertilisation is joining, and the first can happen without the second — most obviously when the pollen is the wrong species. Move hibiscus pollen onto an orchid's stigma and you have pollinated it, but no offspring will form: different species, so the sex cells are not compatible.

Flowers are built around whichever carrier they use. Insect-pollinated flowers have bright petals, scent and nectar; anthers and stigmas sit inside so a visiting insect brushes against them, and the pollen is sticky. Wind-pollinated flowers such as grasses are the opposite: dull petals or none, no scent, anthers dangling outside where the wind shakes them, stigmas large and feathery to sieve pollen from the air, and smooth light pollen in enormous quantities because most grains are wasted.

After fertilisation each ovule becomes a seed and the ovary wall thickens into the fruit — so botanically a pea pod and an acorn are fruits.

Seeds must then get away from the parent, or they grow in its shade competing for light and water. This is seed dispersal, and the fruit's structure tells you the method. A feathery plume or papery wing means wind — dandelion, sycamore. A fleshy fruit means animals; hooks and burrs catch on fur. A light, waterproof fruit like a coconut means water. A few plants do the work themselves: a pea or gorse pod dries out until it splits and flings the seeds several metres, which is called explosive or self-dispersal. Read the structure and name the agent it suits.

Asexual reproduction uses no flowers, pollen or seeds. A strawberry sends out a runner, a horizontal stem that roots where it touches soil. A potato is a tuber, a swollen underground stem whose buds sprout. An onion is a bulb. Gardeners use cuttings: a piece of stem in soil grows roots into a whole plant, and tissue culture does the same from a few cells. Both are artificial vegetative propagation — growing from seed is not.

Asexual offspring are clones, identical to the one parent — useful if the parent is a good variety, but a field of identical plants shares identical weaknesses, so one disease can destroy all of them. Sexual reproduction produces offspring that vary, letting a population cope.

The heart of it

Pollination moves pollen to the stigma; fertilisation is the later fusing of sex cells inside the ovule — two separate steps, and the first can happen without the second.

3

Where the idea came from

That plants reproduce sexually was established surprisingly late. Farmers had long known that date palms would not fruit unless certain trees were present, and hand-pollinated them without knowing why.

The demonstration is credited to Rudolf Jakob Camerarius, a German botanist at Tübingen, around 1694. He removed the anthers, or isolated female plants away from any male, and no seeds formed; restore the pollen and seeds appeared. The idea met resistance, partly because assigning sexes to plants offended sensibilities of the period.

Fertilisation itself nobody could see. The pollen tube growing down the style was observed only in the nineteenth century, and only then did the two steps become clearly separate.

4

What people get wrong

Treating pollination and fertilisation as the same thing is the error. Fix it by location: pollination happens outside, on the stigma; fertilisation inside, in the ovule. Pollination is transport, fertilisation is fusion.

A second confusion is between dispersal and pollination. Pollination moves pollen before seeds exist; dispersal moves finished seeds afterwards. If the question shows a fruit, it is dispersal.

Past IMSO papers have asked for anther and stigma on a cut-open flower, and why cross-pollinating two species yields no seed.

Commonly missed

A gardener brushes pollen from a hibiscus onto the stigma of an orchid. No seeds form. Has pollination occurred?

The trap is that "no seeds formed" makes people answer that pollination failed. It did not.

How to work it: Pollination is the transfer of pollen to a stigma, and pollen was transferred, so yes, pollination occurred. What did not occur is fertilisation: the two are different species, so the pollen grain cannot grow a tube and fuse its sex cell with the orchid ovule, and without that the ovules do not become seeds. Only the second step was blocked.

5

Connected ideas

Flower structure is used to classify plants, and the insect- and wind-pollinated forms are adaptations to different carriers. Variation from sexual reproduction is the raw material natural selection works on, while clones supply almost none.

In one line

Pollen goes from anther to stigma — that is pollination — and only afterwards does a pollen tube carry the male cell down the style to fuse with an ovule, which is fertilisation, turning ovules into seeds and the ovary into a fruit, while runners, tubers and cuttings skip all of it to produce clones.
