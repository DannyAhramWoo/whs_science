Concept

How Blood Carries Gases

Oxygen travels bound to haemoglobin inside red blood cells, while most carbon dioxide travels as hydrogencarbonate ions dissolved in the plasma — the two gases are carried in quite different ways.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

It is easy to picture blood as a bucket of water with gases stirred in. That picture is wrong, and wrong in a different way for each gas.

Gases do not dissolve well in water. If your blood relied on dissolving oxygen, it could carry only a tiny fraction of what your body needs, and you could not run, or even walk far. So blood carries oxygen using a chemical that grabs it, and carries carbon dioxide mostly by turning it into something else.

This detail goes beyond what primary papers usually ask, but it is here because it does come up.

2

How it works

Oxygen. Red blood cells are packed with a red, iron-containing protein called haemoglobin. It picks up oxygen in the lungs, where oxygen is plentiful, and releases it in a working tissue, where oxygen is scarce; the surroundings decide, so it needs no instruction. A small amount simply dissolves in the plasma.

Your red blood cells have two features that suit this job. They have no nucleus, leaving more room for haemoglobin and giving the cell its dented disc shape. And they have no mitochondria, so they cannot respire aerobically or consume the oxygen they carry. A delivery van running on its own cargo would arrive with less than it set out with. This is a mammal arrangement rather than a rule for all animals: birds and reptiles keep the nucleus in their red blood cells, so it is mammals that are the odd ones out here.

Carbon dioxide. This travels three ways at once, and the order of importance is what matters. Most, roughly seven parts in ten, is converted into hydrogencarbonate ions (also called bicarbonate) and carried in the plasma. Carbon dioxide reacts with water to form these ions, helped by an enzyme inside red blood cells; in the lungs the reaction runs backwards, releasing the gas to be breathed out. A little over two parts in ten are carried bound to haemoglobin, at a different site from the one oxygen uses, so the two do not compete. Well under one part in ten is simply dissolved. The usual figures are about seventy, twenty-three and seven parts in a hundred. The ranking is what to remember: bicarbonate most, haemoglobin next, dissolved least by a wide margin.

Which blood goes where. The usual rule is that arteries carry oxygenated blood and veins deoxygenated blood, and it holds nearly everywhere. The real exception is at the lungs: the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs, and the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood back. That is not a trick of naming — arteries are vessels leading away from the heart, whatever they contain.

A second exception is at the liver. The hepatic portal vein brings blood from the gut, loaded with newly absorbed food but already through the gut's capillaries, so it is not fully oxygenated. The liver receives a mixture: fresh blood from its own artery, and this nutrient-rich but partly deoxygenated supply.

The heart of it

Oxygen rides on haemoglobin; most carbon dioxide travels as hydrogencarbonate ions in the plasma; and only the lungs and the liver receive blood that is not simply oxygenated.

3

Where the idea came from

None of this was obvious, because gases are invisible and blood is opaque. In the 1770s and 1780s Joseph Priestley in England and Antoine Lavoisier in France established that one gas in air supports burning and breathing. Lavoisier named it oxygen and argued that breathing is a slow kind of burning. The explanation before that was phlogiston, a supposed substance thought to leave things as they burned — a carefully argued idea that was simply wrong.

The carrier was found later. In the 1860s the German scientist Felix Hoppe-Seyler obtained the red pigment of blood in crystal form and named it haemoglobin. In 1904 the Danish physiologist Christian Bohr and colleagues showed that haemoglobin releases oxygen more readily where carbon dioxide is abundant — exactly where cells most need it.

The bicarbonate story was pieced together in the early twentieth century by several researchers rather than one person. A key piece arrived in 1932, when Norman Meldrum and Francis Roughton identified carbonic anhydrase, the enzyme that speeds the conversion of carbon dioxide into hydrogencarbonate. Without it the reaction would be far too slow to keep pace with the blood flowing past.

4

What people get wrong

The first common error is assuming carbon dioxide is mainly dissolved in the blood. It sounds sensible, since it dissolves in fizzy drinks. But dissolving is the smallest of the three routes; most travels as hydrogencarbonate ions in the plasma.

The second is answering "the lungs" when asked which organ receives only oxygenated blood. The lungs are where oxygen is picked up, so they feel like the most oxygen-rich place in the body. But blood arriving there has just returned from the body, through the pulmonary artery, low in oxygen.

Commonly missed

Which organ receives only oxygenated blood: the lungs, the liver, the spleen, or none of them?

The trap is that the lungs are the organ most associated with oxygen, so the answer feels obvious before any reasoning happens. The liver catches anyone who avoids that.

How to work it: Do not ask which organ is most associated with oxygen. Ask which vessels supply it. The lungs are fed by the pulmonary artery, carrying deoxygenated blood — that is the whole point of sending it there — so the lungs are out. The liver receives the hepatic portal vein as well as its own artery, so its supply is mixed. The spleen has no such special vessel; an ordinary systemic artery from the aorta feeds it, so everything reaching it is oxygenated. The spleen is the answer, and the same reasoning works for the brain or a kidney.

Connected ideas

Breathing loads oxygen into the blood and unloads carbon dioxide at the alveoli, both crossing those thin walls by diffusion. Circulation does the carrying, and its double loop is why the pulmonary vessels break the usual artery rule. Respiration produces the carbon dioxide, whose removal is the body's largest act of excretion.
