Concept

Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants use light energy to build sugar out of carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen as they do it.

Introduced in Chapter 16: Photosynthesis and Respiration

1

What it means

Ask most people where a tree's wood comes from and they will say the soil. It seems obvious. But dig a hole around an old tree and you will not find a tree-shaped pit of missing earth. The soil is more or less still there. So where did all that wood come from?

Almost all of it came out of the air. A plant takes in carbon dioxide gas through tiny holes in its leaves, takes in water through its roots, and uses the energy in sunlight to stick these together into sugar. From sugar it builds everything else: sugar joined into long chains makes cellulose, which is what wood and leaves are largely made of. A tree is, in a real sense, air and water assembled by light.

The word says what is happening. Photo means light and synthesis means building up. What the plant gets from the soil is water and small amounts of dissolved minerals, needed in traces rather like vitamins. The bulk of the plant does not come from there.

2

How it works

Four things are needed: carbon dioxide, water, light, and a green substance called chlorophyll that absorbs the light. Remove any one and the process stops. Two things come out: sugar, which the plant keeps, and oxygen, which it mostly releases.

Carbon dioxide enters through pores on the underside of a leaf called stomata, which the plant can open and close. Water arrives from the roots through narrow tubes in the stem. Light is caught by chlorophyll inside small green structures in the leaf cells. That light energy is what makes the reaction possible, because building sugar from carbon dioxide and water needs energy put in. This is the opposite direction to burning, where sugar and oxygen fall apart and give energy out. Photosynthesis stores the Sun's energy in chemical bonds; respiration and combustion release it again. Every meal you have eaten and every fire you have seen is sunlight being paid back out.

The rate of photosynthesis is not fixed. It rises with more light, then levels off because something else has become the bottleneck, usually the supply of carbon dioxide, which is only about 0.04% of air. It rises with warmth up to a point and then falls, because the reaction depends on delicate substances in the cell that are damaged by high temperatures. Whichever ingredient is in shortest supply sets the pace, and that ingredient is called the limiting factor. Growers exploit this: adding carbon dioxide to a greenhouse does nothing if the light is dim, but a great deal on a bright day.

One detail catches people out. A plant photosynthesises only in light, but respires day and night, because like every living thing it needs a constant supply of energy. In daylight photosynthesis usually runs faster, so the leaf takes in carbon dioxide overall and gives out oxygen. At night only respiration continues, and the flow reverses.

The heart of it

A plant builds its own food out of air and water using the energy in light, which is why plants do not need to eat and why everything else ultimately depends on them.

3

Where the idea came from

The first real crack in the soil explanation came from Jan van Helmont, working in the early 1600s. He planted a small willow in a pot of soil he had weighed, gave it nothing but water, and let it grow for about five years. Then he weighed everything again. The tree had gained roughly 74 kilograms. The soil had lost almost nothing.

Van Helmont concluded the tree was made of water, since water was the only thing he had added. That was wrong — he had no way of knowing an invisible gas was involved. But the experiment was a huge step, because he measured instead of arguing, and he had disproved the belief everyone held.

The gas came into view over a century later. In 1771 Joseph Priestley found that a candle burning in a sealed jar went out and a mouse in that used-up air died, but a sprig of mint left in the jar restored the air so a candle could burn again. Something in plants was renewing air that burning had spoiled.

Priestley's results were maddeningly unreliable, though: sometimes the mint restored the air and sometimes it did not. Jan Ingenhousz found why, showing in 1779 that light was essential — the plant restored the air only when illuminated, and in darkness did the opposite. Priestley had missed the one condition that mattered most, which is why his experiment was so hard to repeat. Ingenhousz also showed it was the green parts doing the work.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking plants get their food from the soil. They do not; they make it. Soil minerals are needed in small quantities, but the carbon making up the mass of a plant comes from the air.

The second is thinking plants respire only at night, as though the two processes take turns. Respiration never stops. What changes is which process is faster, and so which way the gases appear to flow.

Commonly missed

A potted plant is sealed in a clear box with a carbon dioxide sensor and kept in bright light. The reading falls steadily, then levels off well above zero. Why does it not reach zero?

It is a trap because it looks like a question about photosynthesis stopping, and people answer that the plant ran out of light or died.

How to work it: The plant is doing two things at once. Photosynthesis removes carbon dioxide; respiration adds it back. As the carbon dioxide level falls, photosynthesis slows, because carbon dioxide has become the limiting factor. Respiration continues at much the same rate. The reading levels off at the point where the two rates are equal, so carbon dioxide is being used up exactly as fast as it is produced. Nothing has stopped — the two processes have balanced.

5

Connected ideas

Photosynthesis is the reverse of respiration, and the two together move carbon around the living world, which is the carbon cycle. Because plants capture the Sun's energy and everything else eats plants or eats plant-eaters, photosynthesis is the first link in every food chain. It depends entirely on chlorophyll, and the oxygen it releases is what makes combustion and animal life possible.
