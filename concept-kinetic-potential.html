Concept

Kinetic and Potential Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy something has because it is moving; potential energy is the energy it has because of where it is or how it is arranged.

Introduced in Chapter 8: Energy and Its Changes

1

What it means

These two names describe one quantity in two situations. Kinetic energy is energy currently being expressed as motion. Potential energy is energy that has been put somewhere and is waiting.

The word "potential" is unfortunate, because in everyday English it suggests possibility, as though the energy might turn up if you are lucky. It means nothing of the sort. Potential energy has definitely been paid in: lifting a book onto a shelf costs real effort, and that effort now sits in the arrangement of the book and the Earth. A better word would be "positional", since gravitational potential energy depends entirely on position.

Both are measured in joules, like every other form of energy, because they are not different substances. They are one quantity counted in two different pockets.

2

How it works

Kinetic energy grows with mass and with speed, but these two do not matter equally. Doubling the mass doubles the kinetic energy. Doubling the speed multiplies it by four, because speed enters the calculation squared: kinetic energy is one half of mass times speed times speed.

Try it. A 2 kg ball at 3 m/s carries half of 2 times 9, which is 9 J. Speed it up to 6 m/s and it carries half of 2 times 36, which is 36 J — four times as much, from twice the speed. This is why a car at 60 km/h needs roughly four times the braking distance of the same car at 30 km/h, and why high-speed crashes are so much worse than the speed difference suggests.

Gravitational potential energy is gentler. It is weight multiplied by height gained, so doubling either the height or the mass doubles the store. Nothing is squared. Lift a 2 kg book 1.5 m and you have stored roughly 30 J, since on Earth each kilogram is pulled down with about 10 N.

Now put the two together, which is what most questions test. Drop that book from 1.5 m and the 30 J of potential energy becomes 30 J of kinetic energy just before it lands, a speed of about 5.5 m/s. Seeing the numbers match is what makes the trade believable rather than a slogan.

Gravitational potential energy is also not stored "in the book". It is stored in the relationship between the book and the Earth — in the gap between them. This is why you cannot state a potential energy without saying what height you measured from, and why a book on a table has one value relative to the table and a larger one relative to the floor. Both are correct, because only the change matters.

The heart of it

Kinetic energy depends on speed squared, so speed matters far more than mass; potential energy depends on height, and it is stored between the object and the Earth, not inside the object.

3

Where the idea came from

These ideas came out of a long argument, and the argument is more instructive than the conclusion. In the late 1600s and into the 1700s, European natural philosophers split into two camps over one question: when objects collide and bounce around, which quantity stays the same?

On one side stood Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, who argued for vis viva — living force — which he took to be mass multiplied by velocity squared. On the other stood the followers of Descartes and, later, of Newton, who backed "quantity of motion": mass multiplied by velocity, no squaring. Each side had experiments that appeared to support it. The dispute ran for decades and became bad-tempered.

The resolution is the interesting part. Neither side was wrong. Both quantities are conserved, separately, because they measure different things. Physics today keeps them both: mass times velocity is momentum, and mass times velocity squared is, with a factor added, kinetic energy. The argument lasted so long precisely because both parties were partly right and neither could produce an experiment refuting the other.

The missing factor of one half was supplied much later, in 1829, by the French engineer Gaspard-Gustave Coriolis. It is not a deep truth about nature; it is a bookkeeping choice that makes kinetic energy match work done exactly, with no conversion factor in between.

4

What people get wrong

The frequent error is assuming "not moving" means "no energy". A ball held at the top of a tall building is perfectly still, so it is easy to conclude it has nothing. It has a great deal, all of it potential, which becomes obvious the moment you let go.

The mirror-image error is assuming mass matters as much as speed for kinetic energy. It does not, because speed is squared and mass is not. Given a heavy slow object and a light fast one, do not assume the heavy one wins.

Commonly missed

Trolley P has a mass of 4 kg and moves at 2 m/s. Trolley Q has a mass of 2 kg and moves at 4 m/s. Which has more kinetic energy?

The trap is that the two look symmetrical, so the instinct is to call them equal. They are equal in momentum, which is mass times speed — exactly the intuition Leibniz argued against.

How to work it: square the speed first, then multiply. For P: 2 squared is 4, times mass 4 is 16, halved is 8 J. For Q: 4 squared is 16, times mass 2 is 32, halved is 16 J. Trolley Q has twice the kinetic energy despite being half as heavy. When mass and speed are traded against each other, the faster one wins.

5

Connected ideas

These are the two forms of energy that swap most visibly, and conservation of energy governs the swap. Work is how energy gets into the store, since lifting means pushing against gravity through a distance. Gravity is what makes height worth anything, and mass matters to both forms, which is why it turns up in every calculation here.

In one line

Kinetic energy is the energy of moving and grows with the square of speed, while potential energy is the energy of position and is stored between an object and the Earth until something lets it fall.
