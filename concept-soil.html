Concept

Soil

Soil is the thin loose layer covering most land, made of broken-down rock particles mixed with decayed plant and animal remains, along with water, air and enormous numbers of living things.

Introduced in Chapter 12: The Planet Earth

1

What it means

Dig a hole in a garden and you notice at once that soil is not one substance. Rub a handful between your fingers and you can feel grit. There are dark crumbly bits, threads of root, the occasional worm, and if it has rained the whole lot is damp. Soil is a mixture, and every part of it does a job.

There are five ingredients. First, mineral particles: fragments of rock broken up over long ages, which make up roughly half the volume of a typical soil. Second, humus — pronounced hew-mus — the dark, spongy material left when dead leaves, wood, and animal remains have been broken down by decay. Third, water, held in the gaps as a thin film round each particle. Fourth, air, filling the gaps the water does not, which roots and soil animals need in order to breathe. Fifth, living things: bacteria, fungi, worms, insects and roots, in numbers so large that a single spoonful of good soil contains more bacteria than there are people on Earth.

Soil is not the same as dirt or as rock. Rock is solid and unbroken. Soil is loose, layered, alive, and it took a very long time to make.

2

How it works

Sort the mineral particles by size and you get the three names you need. Sand particles are the largest, big enough to see and feel as grit. Silt particles are smaller, and silt feels smooth and floury when dry, rather like flour itself. Clay particles are the smallest by far, far too small to see individually, and clay feels sticky and can be rolled into a sausage when wet. That is the only difference between them: size. It is not what the particle is made of but how big it is.

Size decides everything else, because size decides the gaps. Large sand grains cannot pack tightly, so they leave big gaps between them and water pours straight through. A sandy soil therefore has excellent drainage — water passes down easily — and plenty of air, but it dries out quickly and the draining water washes away dissolved nutrients that plants need. Tiny clay particles pack very closely, leaving only minute gaps, so water moves through slowly and clay soil stays wet, sometimes waterlogged. Waterlogging is a real problem because water fills the gaps that should hold air, and roots without air die. Clay holds nutrients well, but when it dries it sets hard and roots struggle to push through it.

The best growing soil, called loam, is a mixture of all three sizes with a good deal of humus. It drains freely enough to keep air in the gaps, yet holds enough water and nutrients for plants between showers. Humus is what makes the difference: it behaves like a sponge, holding many times its own weight in water, and it also sticks small particles together into crumbs, which builds larger gaps into a soil that would otherwise pack tight.

Now dig deeper and cut a clean vertical face. You will see the soil is layered, and the layers are called horizons. At the very top is a layer of leaf litter, sometimes labelled the O horizon: fallen leaves and twigs that are recognisably still leaves and twigs. Below that is the topsoil, the A horizon, and this is the crucial one. It is dark, sometimes almost black, because it holds by far the most humus in the whole profile. It gets that humus because it is the layer receiving everything that falls and dies on the surface — leaves, wood, dead insects, animal droppings — which the bacteria, fungi and worms living there break down on the spot. Topsoil is where nearly all the nutrients are, so it is where most roots are, and it is typically only a few tens of centimetres deep.

Under it lies the subsoil or B horizon, usually paler and often reddish or yellowish, with much less humus because it is too far down to receive fresh remains. It does collect minerals washed down from above by rainwater. Below that, the C horizon is broken rock fragments, partly weathered but not yet soil. And at the bottom is bedrock: solid, unbroken rock, the parent material the whole soil above was originally made from. The pattern to remember is that organic material and darkness both decrease with depth, for the plain reason that dead things land on the top. Past competition papers, IMSO among them, have asked exactly this — which horizon holds the most organic material, and why.

How does soil get made? It starts with weathering, the breaking down of rock where it lies. Water seeps into cracks, freezes, expands and splits the rock apart. Plant roots widen the cracks. Chemicals in rainwater slowly dissolve some minerals. Once there are enough fragments, tough plants like lichens and mosses take hold, die, and add the first traces of humus. Better plants can then grow, adding more, and the soil slowly deepens. This is extremely slow. Forming a single centimetre of topsoil takes somewhere in the region of a hundred to a thousand years, depending on climate and rock.

Losing it is fast. Erosion is the carrying away of soil by wind or moving water, and it can strip a field in a single storm. Plant roots hold soil together and leaves shield it from the impact of rain, so bare ground erodes many times faster than covered ground. This is why clearing forests on slopes, over-grazing grassland or leaving fields bare through winter can cost centuries of soil-making in a few seasons. Farmers slow it with terraces cut across slopes, hedges and tree belts to break the wind, and cover crops planted purely to keep roots in the ground.

The heart of it

Topsoil is the darkest, richest layer because dead leaves and organisms land on the surface and decay there, and it takes centuries to build but can be washed away in a day.

3

Where the idea came from

Farmers have known for thousands of years that some ground grows better crops than other ground, and that adding manure helps. Ancient Roman writers on agriculture described different soils and how to treat them. But knowing what works is not the same as knowing why.

For a long time the reigning idea was that plants eat soil — that a growing plant consumes solid earth and turns it into wood and leaf. In the 1600s Jan Baptist van Helmont tested this. He planted a willow in a weighed pot of dried soil, watered it for five years, then weighed both again. The willow had gained many kilograms; the soil had lost only a few grams. He concluded, correctly, that the plant had not been made from soil, and, incorrectly, that it must have been made from water. He had no way of knowing most of the added mass came from carbon dioxide in the air. It was a good experiment that reached half the right answer.

Soil was still generally treated as inert ground rock. That changed with Vasily Dokuchaev, a Russian scientist working in the 1880s, regarded as the founder of modern soil science. Studying the deep black soils of the Russian steppe, he argued that soil is not simply crushed rock but a natural body in its own right, formed over time by the parent rock, the climate, the landscape and above all the living things in it. He mapped soil horizons systematically, and the idea that a soil profile records its own history has shaped the subject ever since.

Charles Darwin spent decades studying earthworms and published a book on them in 1881, showing that worms pass great quantities of soil through their bodies and gradually turn over the top layers of an entire field — among the first demonstrations that soil is actively made by living things rather than merely lying there.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking plants take their bulk from the soil. They do not. Most of a plant's dry mass is carbon, taken from carbon dioxide in the air during photosynthesis. Soil supplies water, mineral nutrients in small quantities, and something to grip.

The second is judging soil quality by how much water it holds. Wetter is not better. A waterlogged clay soil drowns roots by leaving no room for air, and roots need to take in oxygen just as leaves do. What plants want is a soil that holds some water and keeps some air.

The third is imagining that a lost soil can be quickly replaced. Centuries to build, a storm to remove: soil is best thought of as a resource that is renewable only on a timescale far longer than a human life.

Commonly missed

A soil profile is shown with four layers. Which layer contains the most organic material, and why?

The trap is reasoning that things sink, so the richest material must have settled at the bottom, or that the deepest layer has had the longest time to accumulate.

How to work it: Ask where organic material comes from. It comes from dead leaves, twigs, animals and microorganisms, and all of those arrive at or live near the surface. Decay happens where they land, so the humus forms there. Only a little is washed downwards by rainwater. The topsoil, the A horizon just below the leaf litter, therefore holds the most, and you can confirm it by colour: humus is dark, so the darkest layer near the top is the richest, and the layers grow paler with depth.

5

Connected ideas

Soil is where several cycles meet. Weathering and erosion explains both how rock becomes soil particles and how soil is lost, the rock cycle shows where the parent material comes from, and the carbon cycle runs through the decay that makes humus. Soil is also the foundation of terrestrial ecosystems and a store in the water cycle.

In one line

Soil is weathered rock mixed with humus, water, air and living things, arranged in horizons with the darkest and richest at the top because that is where dead material falls and decays, built over centuries and stripped away in hours.
