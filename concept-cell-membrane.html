Concept

The Cell Membrane

The cell membrane is a double sheet of fatty molecules that forms itself automatically in water, and it is what separates a living inside from a non-living outside.

Introduced in The Origin of Life — beyond the syllabus

Beyond the syllabus

This idea is not on the exam syllabus. It is here because it is worth knowing, not because it will be tested.

1

What it means

Every cell of every living thing is wrapped in a membrane. It is astonishingly thin — around five millionths of a millimetre, too thin to see with an ordinary light microscope and only pictured directly once electron microscopes existed. Yet without it there are no cells, and without cells there is nothing that is clearly alive.

The membrane is built from molecules called phospholipids. A phospholipid has a small head at one end and two long tails, and the two ends behave in opposite ways towards water. The head is hydrophilic, meaning water-loving — it carries electrical charge and mixes with water happily. The tails are hydrophobic, meaning water-avoiding — made of carbon and hydrogen, carrying no charge, and water will not mix with them, in the same way that oil will not mix with water in a salad dressing.

One molecule with both properties is in an awkward position in water. Its head wants to be surrounded by water and its tails do not, and the two are chemically joined and cannot separate. The only way to satisfy both halves is for many such molecules to arrange themselves together, and that requirement is what builds the membrane. Two sheets form back to back, tails pointing inwards towards each other and heads facing outwards into the water on both sides, so every head is in contact with water and every tail is hidden in an oily layer in the middle. This double sheet is called a lipid bilayer, and it is the basic structure of every membrane in every organism on Earth.

2

How it works

The most remarkable thing about the bilayer is that it needs no assembly instructions. Put purified phospholipids in water, shake, and bilayers appear on their own, because that arrangement is the one that satisfies water: pushing the tails together into a single hidden layer frees the greatest number of water molecules to behave normally. The membrane is therefore not really built by the phospholipids; it is built by water pushing them together.

A flat sheet still has edges, and at an edge the tails at the rim are exposed to water again. The tidy solution is for the sheet to curve round and close on itself into a hollow sphere, which has no edges at all — trapping a droplet of water inside. Such a sphere is called a vesicle, and it forms spontaneously too. A closed compartment with a distinct inside and outside, the basic architecture of every cell, appears by itself from one kind of molecule and the properties of water, requiring no genes, no enzymes and no plan.

Why does a boundary matter so much? First, it keeps useful molecules together: a molecule that catalyses a helpful reaction is worthless if it drifts away into the ocean. Second, it concentrates things, and since reaction speed depends on how often molecules bump into one another, confining them to a tiny volume speeds everything up. Third, and most importantly, it allows the inside to differ from the outside. Life is not in equilibrium with its surroundings — it maintains different concentrations, acidity and chemistry within than without, and every one of those differences is a store of energy that can be drawn on.

The barrier cannot be perfectly sealed, or nothing could get in or out. The oily interior lets small uncharged molecules such as oxygen and carbon dioxide slip straight through, while charged particles and larger molecules such as sugars cannot, which is why real membranes are studded with proteins acting as channels and pumps. A membrane is therefore selectively permeable, and that selectivity is what makes control possible. It is also not rigid like a wall. The phospholipids are not bonded to one another, only held in place by their dislike of water, so they slide about freely within the sheet, and the membrane behaves as a two-dimensional liquid. This is why cells can change shape, divide, and heal small punctures — a hole in a bilayer closes by itself, because the exposed edges are unstable.

The heart of it

Because a phospholipid has a water-loving head and water-avoiding tails, water itself forces the molecules into a closed bilayer — so the container every cell needs assembles without any instructions.

3

Where the idea came from

The structure had to be worked out indirectly, because nothing could see it. The key experiment was done in 1925 in the Netherlands by Evert Gorter and François Grendel. They took red blood cells, convenient because they have no internal membranes to confuse matters, extracted the lipids from a known number of them, and spread those lipids as a film one molecule thick on a water surface, measuring the area it covered. Comparing that with the calculated surface area of the cells gave a ratio of about two to one, and they drew the correct conclusion: the membrane must be two molecules thick, a bilayer.

There is an honest postscript. Later workers found two mistakes in their procedure: their extraction did not recover all of the lipid, so the measured film area was too small, and their estimate of the cells' surface area was also too low. The two errors ran in opposite directions and were of similar size, so they cancelled, leaving a ratio close to two. Gorter and Grendel reached the right answer partly by luck — a useful reminder that a correct conclusion does not by itself prove the reasoning was sound, and one reason results are repeated rather than accepted on first publication.

The full modern picture came much later. In 1972 Seymour Jonathan Singer and Garth Nicolson published the fluid mosaic model, still used today: a fluid bilayer in which proteins are embedded like tiles in a mosaic, some passing right through, all drifting rather than fixed. The word fluid captured what earlier models, which had pictured rigid protein layers coating the lipid, had missed.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest misunderstanding is picturing the membrane as a solid skin or wall, like a balloon. It is a liquid film, with the molecules constantly moving past one another, and picturing it as rigid makes cell division, shape change and self-repair puzzling instead of obvious.

A second is confusing the membrane with the cell wall found in plants, fungi and many bacteria. Both may be present. The wall lies outside the membrane, is rigid, is made of different material — cellulose in plants — and provides support. It does not control what enters and leaves; that is always the membrane's job. Animal cells have a membrane and no wall.

Commonly missed

Purified phospholipids are shaken in a test tube of water. Tiny hollow spheres form, each holding a droplet of water inside. Why does no instruction or enzyme need to be present for this to happen?

The trap is assuming that any organised structure must have been organised by something. Order here comes from the molecules and their surroundings, not from a builder.

How to work it: Begin with the molecule. Each phospholipid has a water-loving head and two water-avoiding tails, permanently joined, so in water no single position satisfies both ends. If many line up with tails facing tails and heads facing outwards, every head touches water and every tail is hidden from it — the arrangement that suits both halves at once. That is the bilayer, and it forms because it is stable, in the same way an oil droplet becomes round without anything rounding it. Then the sphere: a flat sheet leaves tails exposed along its edges, and curving it until it closes removes the edges entirely, with water inevitably trapped inside. The structure follows from the shape of the molecule and the behaviour of water, which is why the compartment problem is thought to be one of the easier steps in explaining the origin of life.

5

Connected ideas

The membrane supplies the compartment that the origin-of-life problem needs, and that most versions of the RNA world hypothesis assume. Its behaviour is best understood through the particle model, since it depends entirely on how molecules attract and repel one another, and the formation of a bilayer is a physical change rather than a chemical one.
