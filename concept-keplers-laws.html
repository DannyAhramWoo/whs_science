Concept

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Kepler's three laws describe how planets actually travel round the Sun: on slightly squashed orbits rather than circles, speeding up when near the Sun and slowing when far, with the more distant planets taking far longer to complete a lap.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Swing a conker on a string around your head and it traces a circle at a steady speed, because the string is always the same length. It is tempting to imagine planets doing the same, held on invisible strings of gravity. They very nearly do — but not quite, and that small difference is what these three laws are about.

A planet's path round the Sun is called its orbit. One complete trip round is one revolution, and the time it takes is that planet's year. Do not confuse this with rotation, which is a planet spinning on its own axis, giving it a day. The two are separate motions, and that separation is where most mistakes are made.

Johannes Kepler, working in the early 1600s, discovered three rules every planet obeys. They are descriptions, not explanations: Kepler found the pattern, and Isaac Newton later explained why it exists.

2

How it works

The first law: orbits are ellipses, with the Sun at one focus. An ellipse is a squashed circle — an oval. You can draw one by pressing two pins into card, looping string loosely round both, and pulling the loop tight with a pencil as you go round. Each pin sits at a point called a focus (two of them are called foci). A circle is the special case where both foci sit at the same spot in the middle. Kepler's first law says the Sun sits at one focus of a planet's ellipse; nothing at all sits at the other, which is just an empty point in space. Because the Sun is off to one side, a planet is genuinely closer to it during part of its year and further away during the rest. The nearest point is perihelion, the furthest aphelion. For most planets, including Earth, the ellipse is only slightly squashed, so a scale drawing of Earth's orbit looks like a circle to the eye.

The second law: a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This is the one worth slowing down for. Picture a straight line drawn from the Sun to the planet. As the planet moves along its orbit, that line sweeps across a slice of space, like the hand of a fan opening. Kepler's second law says that in any thirty days, the slice swept out has the same area — no matter where in the orbit those thirty days fall.

Now think about what that forces the planet to do. Near the Sun, the line from Sun to planet is short. A short line sweeping a slice can only cover a decent area if it swings round quickly, so the planet must race along. Far from the Sun, the line is long, and a long line covers a large area even with a small swing, so the planet can dawdle. The slices are the same area but different shapes: short and fat near the Sun, long and thin far away. So a planet moves fastest at perihelion and slowest at aphelion.

Why should nature behave this way? Because gravity — the pull every mass exerts on every other mass — gets stronger the closer two objects are. As a planet falls inward towards perihelion, the Sun's pull on it is strengthening and is partly pulling it forwards along its path, so the planet accelerates, exactly as a ball rolling down a slope picks up speed. Having swung round the Sun, the planet climbs back outwards, now with gravity partly pulling backwards against its motion, and it slows again, like a ball rolling up the far side of a valley. Earth's orbital speed varies between roughly 29 and 30 kilometres per second across the year for exactly this reason.

The third law: the further out a planet is, the longer its year. Mercury takes 88 Earth days. Earth takes 365. Jupiter, five times further from the Sun than Earth, takes about 12 Earth years. Neptune takes about 165. Two separate effects stack up here. First, a wider orbit is simply a longer path to walk. Second, and less obviously, a distant planet also travels more slowly at every moment, because the Sun's grip out there is weaker and a slower speed is enough to keep it in orbit. Earth moves at about 30 kilometres per second; Neptune plods along at about 5. A longer road travelled at a lower speed means a very long year indeed. Kepler also found the exact numerical relationship between orbit size and year length, though here the direction of the effect is what counts.

One check worth carrying: Earth reaches perihelion in early January and aphelion in early July. Yes — Earth is closest to the Sun during the northern winter. That surprises people, and it is useful precisely because it proves distance from the Sun does not cause the seasons. Seasons come from the tilt of Earth's axis, which changes how directly sunlight strikes each hemisphere. If distance caused seasons, the whole planet would have winter together, and it plainly does not.

The heart of it

Because the Sun sits at one focus of an ellipse rather than at the centre, a planet is nearer the Sun for part of its year, is pulled harder there, and so sweeps round faster — covering equal areas in equal times.

3

Where the idea came from

In 1543 Nicolaus Copernicus published his argument that the Sun, not the Earth, sits at the centre. It was a huge step, but he kept the ancient assumption that heavenly motion must be built from perfect circles, so his predictions were still not accurate.

The Danish astronomer Tycho Brahe spent decades from the 1570s measuring planetary positions from his observatory, night after night, entirely by eye — the telescope had not yet been turned on the sky. His measurements were far more precise than anyone else's. Tycho died in 1601, and his assistant Johannes Kepler inherited the observations, above all the long record of Mars.

What happened next is the best part of the story, because it is a story of failure. Kepler spent years trying to fit circles to the Mars data, testing combination after combination. He got very close. His best circular model missed the observations by about eight minutes of arc — a tiny angle, roughly a quarter the width of the full Moon. A less careful person would have shrugged that off as measurement error. Kepler trusted Tycho's precision enough to believe the eight minutes were real, threw out the circles, and eventually found that an ellipse fitted perfectly. He published the first two laws in 1609 in a book on Mars, and the third in 1619, ten years later.

Kepler had the rules but not the reason. In 1687 Isaac Newton published his law of universal gravitation and showed mathematically that all three of Kepler's laws follow from a single idea: that every mass attracts every other mass, with a pull that weakens as the distance grows. Kepler had found the pattern in the data; Newton showed where the pattern comes from.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is picturing orbits as strongly stretched ovals. Most planetary orbits are so close to circular that you could not tell by eye; the Sun's offset from the centre is small. The ellipse matters because it produces real speed changes, not because the shape looks dramatic.

The second error is assuming distance from the Sun drives the seasons. Earth is nearest the Sun in January, so it cannot. Tilt does.

The third is the trap that past papers, IMSO ones among them, actually set: sliding from orbital speed to spin. Kepler's laws describe how a planet travels along its orbit. They say nothing whatever about how fast that planet turns on its own axis. Venus creeps round its axis once in about 243 Earth days while orbiting in 225; Jupiter, far out and orbiting slowly, spins right round in under ten hours. Orbit and spin are independent.

Commonly missed

A diagram shows a planet sweeping two equal-area slices, one near the Sun and one far away. Which conclusion is false? (a) The planet moves faster when nearer the Sun. (b) Planets closer to the Sun have shorter years. (c) The planet spins faster on its axis when nearer the Sun.

It is a trap because all three statements sound like the same kind of claim, and the first two really are true. Under time pressure, the third slips past.

How to work it: Ask what each statement is about. (a) is about speed along the orbit — true, and it is precisely what equal areas in equal times forces. (b) is about year length — true, by the third law, since a wider orbit is both longer and travelled more slowly. (c) is about rotation, a completely separate motion that the diagram gives no information about at all. A diagram of orbital sweeping cannot support any claim about spin, so (c) is the false one.

5

Connected ideas

Gravity is the cause behind all three laws. Rotation and revolution keeps the two motions apart, which is the discrimination the laws are usually tested on. The direction planets travel round these ellipses is prograde or retrograde motion, and the Moon's own ellipse is what makes some eclipses annular.
