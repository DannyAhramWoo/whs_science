Concept

Excretion

Excretion is the removal of waste that the body's own cells have made, such as carbon dioxide, urea and excess water and salts.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Everything your body does chemically leaves something over. Cells burning sugar for energy make carbon dioxide. Cells handling protein make a waste called urea. None of this is useful, and some of it is harmful if it builds up, so the body must keep removing it. That removal is excretion.

There is a second kind of thing that leaves your body, and it is not the same. Some of what you eat is never absorbed at all. Fibre and other indigestible bits travel the whole length of the gut without ever entering your blood or your cells, and leave as faeces. That is called egestion. Most of that material never joined you, so getting rid of it is not getting rid of your waste.

One honest complication is worth knowing, because a careful reader will spot it. Faeces is not purely egested food. The brown colour comes from bile pigments, which your liver makes when it breaks down worn-out red blood cells, and those pigments genuinely are your own waste leaving in the faeces. So the tidy rule has a real exception. For answering a question the rule still holds, because the question is about the bulk of what leaves, and that is unabsorbed food.

The test is simple. Ask where the substance was made. If your cells made it, removing it is excretion. If it merely passed through a tube inside you, removing it is egestion.

2

How it works

Three organs do the excreting, and each removes something different.

The lungs remove carbon dioxide. Every cell produces it during respiration, the blood collects it, and it leaves across the thin walls of the alveoli each time you breathe out. By sheer quantity this is the largest excretion the body performs, which surprises people because we usually think of waste as liquid or solid.

The kidneys remove urea, together with excess water and excess salts. They do this by filtering the blood and sending the leftovers out as urine. Note the word excess: water and salt are not waste, and the kidneys only remove what is over and above what the body needs at that moment. Urea, however, is always removed, because it is always waste.

The skin removes a little water and salt in sweat. This is the odd one out. Sweating exists to cool you, not to clean you: the water takes heat with it as it evaporates. Salt and a trace of urea happen to be dissolved in that water, so they go too. The skin is therefore only incidentally an excretory organ, and it would be wrong to say the body sweats in order to get rid of waste.

Where does urea come from in the first place? Protein in your food is broken into amino acids, and your body cannot store the ones it does not need. The liver strips them apart, releasing ammonia, which is extremely toxic even in small amounts. The liver immediately converts it into urea, far less toxic and safe to carry in the blood until the kidneys remove it. Urea is not a mistake, then, but a deliberately made safer version of something dangerous.

The heart of it

Excretion removes waste your own cells made; faeces is mainly egestion, because the bulk of that material was never part of you.

3

Where the idea came from

Urea was known as a substance in urine long before anyone understood why the body bothers to make it. What made it famous happened in a laboratory rather than in a body.

In 1828 the German chemist Friedrich Wohler was working with ammonium cyanate, a plainly non-living chemical, and found that it turned into urea — a compound found in living things. At the time many chemists divided the world in two: substances made by living things, and substances made otherwise, with a boundary that could not be crossed. Wohler had crossed it by accident.

The story is often told as the moment that destroyed that belief in a single stroke. Historians now dispute this, and it is fairer to say it was one step among several. The old view faded gradually as more such substances were made in laboratories over the following decades. Scientific ideas usually die slowly rather than all at once.

Understanding why the liver makes urea came much later. The chemical steps that turn ammonia into urea were worked out in the 1930s, chiefly by Hans Krebs and Kurt Henseleit.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error by far is calling faeces excretion. It feels like waste, it leaves the body, and it is unpleasant, so the label seems obvious. But excretion has a precise meaning, and the deciding question is not how the substance feels or looks. It is whether your cells produced it.

A second error is thinking sweating is mainly a way of removing waste. It removes a small amount of salt and water, but its purpose is cooling. Purpose and side effect are different things, and examiners like to test whether you can separate them.

Commonly missed

Which of these is not an example of excretion: breathing out carbon dioxide, passing urine, sweating, or passing faeces?

The trap is that faeces looks like the most obviously wasteful of the four, so it is tempting to think it must be the strongest example rather than the exception.

How to work it: Take each one and ask where it was made. Carbon dioxide was made by cells during respiration, so that is excretion. Urea in urine was made by the liver, so that is excretion. Sweat is water and salt from your blood, so that counts too. Faeces is mostly food that was never absorbed into the blood at all, so it was never part of your body's chemistry. It is egestion, and it is the answer.

Connected ideas

Excretion is the far end of several other systems. Respiration makes the carbon dioxide, digestion supplies the protein whose leftovers become urea, and circulation carries both wastes to the organ that removes them. The kidneys deserve a page of their own, and carbon dioxide travels to the lungs in a less obvious way than most people assume.
