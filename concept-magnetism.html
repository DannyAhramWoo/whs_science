Concept

Magnetism

Magnetism is the force by which some materials attract or repel each other without touching.

Introduced in Chapter 4: Magnetism

1

What it means

Hold two magnets close and you can feel something push back through empty air. Nothing is touching, yet a force is plainly there. That is magnetism, and it is one of the few forces a child can hold in two hands and experiment with directly.

Every magnet has two ends, called poles, and they are not interchangeable. One is the north-seeking pole, one is the south-seeking pole, and the rule is that like poles repel while unlike poles attract. North pushes north away; north pulls south closer. There is no such thing as a magnet with only one pole. Snap a bar magnet in half and you do not get a north piece and a south piece — you get two smaller magnets, each with a north and a south of its own.

Magnetism is also fussy about materials. It attracts iron, nickel, cobalt, and steel, which is mostly iron. These are called magnetic materials. It does not attract copper, aluminium, gold, plastic or wood, even though copper and gold are metals. So "metal" and "magnetic" are not the same word, and a magnet is a quick test that separates one metal from another.

2

How it works

The explanation lies inside the material. Every atom has electrons moving within it, and a moving charge behaves like a very small magnet. In most substances these atomic magnets point in every direction at random, so their effects cancel and the material shows no magnetism at all.

In iron, nickel and cobalt something different happens. Neighbouring atoms fall into step with one another, forming small regions called domains — patches, far too small to see, in which millions of atoms all point the same way. An ordinary iron nail is full of domains pointing in scattered directions, so overall it is not a magnet. Bring a strong magnet near, and the domains rotate to line up. Now they add together instead of cancelling, and the nail is temporarily magnetised, which is why it can pick up a paper clip of its own.

This single picture explains a great deal. It explains why a magnet attracts an unmagnetised nail but the nail alone attracts nothing: the magnet has to line the domains up first. It explains why steel makes a permanent magnet and soft iron does not — steel's structure holds its domains in place after the magnet is removed, while iron's relax back to disorder. And it explains why dropping or heating a magnet weakens it: banging or shaking the atoms knocks the domains out of alignment. Heat every magnet past a certain temperature, called the Curie point, and it loses its magnetism entirely.

Magnetism also passes through materials that are not themselves magnetic. A magnet will hold a note to a fridge through a sheet of paper, and will attract a paper clip through a hand or a glass of water, because the force acts through the intervening material rather than needing contact. The strength falls off very quickly with distance, however — move a magnet twice as far from a clip and the pull becomes far weaker than half.

The heart of it

A magnet is magnetic because countless tiny atomic magnets inside it point the same way instead of cancelling out.

3

Where the idea came from

Magnetism has no single discoverer. It was found, not invented, and found more than once. Naturally magnetic rock — lodestone, a form of iron ore — was described in ancient Greece, and the Greek philosopher Thales of Miletus is said to have written about its attraction for iron in around 600 BC. The name may come from the region of Magnesia in Asia Minor, though that origin is traditional rather than certain. Chinese writers described the same stones independently.

The practical breakthrough was Chinese. Lodestone spoons were used for divination well before they were used for travel, and by roughly the 11th century Chinese texts describe magnetised needles being used to find direction, including at sea. The compass reached Europe about a century later. Nobody at the time could say why it worked. A common belief was that the needle was drawn towards the pole star, or towards a great magnetic mountain somewhere in the far north.

That guess was overturned in 1600 by William Gilbert, physician to Queen Elizabeth I, in his book De Magnete. Gilbert carved a sphere of lodestone, which he called a terrella or little earth, and moved a small compass needle over its surface. The needle behaved exactly as compasses do across the real world, dipping and turning in the same pattern. He concluded that the Earth is itself a giant magnet, and that compasses point north because of the planet beneath them rather than any star above. It was a genuinely new kind of argument: build a small model, show it reproduces the large behaviour, and infer the cause.

Gilbert was not right about everything. He also believed magnetism was connected to the soul of the Earth, an idea no one holds today, and the source of the Earth's field — flowing molten iron in the outer core — was not understood until the twentieth century.

4

What people get wrong

The most common error is assuming all metals are magnetic. Copper coins, aluminium foil and gold rings are all metal and none is attracted. Only iron, nickel, cobalt and alloys such as steel respond appreciably.

The second is expecting that breaking a magnet separates its poles. It never does, because the poles are not objects stored at the ends. They are simply where the aligned domains meet the outside world, and every fragment has ends of its own.

A third worth noting is the naming muddle at the poles of the Earth. Since unlike poles attract, and a compass needle's north-seeking pole points towards the Arctic, the magnetic pole physically located near the geographic North Pole must actually be a magnetic south pole. The names were fixed by usage long before anyone worked this out.

Commonly missed

Two identical iron bars are given. One is a magnet and one is not, and no other equipment is allowed. How can you tell which is which?

The trap is trying to use attraction, since each bar will attract the other whichever way round you hold them — the magnet simply magnetises the plain bar.

How to work it: Use repulsion instead, because only a magnet can repel. Touch one end of bar A against the middle of bar B, then against the middle of the other end, and watch carefully. A cleaner version: bring an end of A near an end of B, then flip B round and try again. If at some orientation you feel a definite push apart, the pair includes a magnet, and testing both ends of each bar identifies it. Attraction proves only that a magnetic material is present; repulsion is the only sure test of a magnet.

5

Connected ideas

The region in which a magnet exerts its force is its magnetic field, and an electric current produces a magnetic field of its own, which is how an electromagnet works. Magnetism is a force that acts without contact, in the same family as gravity, and like all forces it can act in balance with others.

In one line

Magnetism is a non-contact force from aligned atomic magnets inside iron-like materials, always with two poles, where like repels like and unlike attracts.
