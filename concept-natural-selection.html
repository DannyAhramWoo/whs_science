Concept

Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process by which individuals that happen to carry a helpful variation survive and breed more than the rest, so that variation becomes commoner in the population over generations.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Look at any group of the same animal — a litter of puppies, a field of rabbits, a jar of beetles — and no two are identical. Some are larger, some faster, some a slightly different colour. That everyday fact has a name: variation, the small differences between individuals of the same species. Most of it is inherited, passed from parent to offspring.

Now add a second everyday fact. Living things produce far more offspring than can possibly survive. A single frog lays thousands of eggs; a pair of frogs need only two to reach adulthood for the population to stay the same. Almost all of them die — eaten, starved, diseased, unlucky.

Put those two facts together and something follows automatically. If most die and individuals differ, then which ones die is not entirely random. An individual whose particular variation makes it slightly harder to catch, or slightly better at finding food, is a little more likely to be among the survivors. Survivors breed, and their offspring inherit that variation. Do this for generation after generation and the helpful variation spreads through the population.

That is natural selection. Nothing chooses. The environment simply kills off some individuals more readily than others, and the surviving individuals pass on what they had. Over long enough, the accumulated changes can produce a new species, which is evolution.

2

How it works

Set out as four steps you can recite:

1. Variation exists. Within any population, individuals differ, and much of that difference is inherited. 2. Overproduction. More offspring are produced than the food, space and safety available can support, so most die before breeding. 3. Selection. Individuals with variations that suit the current conditions are more likely to survive long enough to reproduce, and so leave more offspring. 4. Inheritance and change. Those offspring inherit the helpful variation, so it makes up a larger share of the next generation. Repeat, and the whole population shifts.

The clearest documented case is warfarin resistance in rats. Warfarin is a poison introduced for rat control in the 1950s; it stops blood clotting, so poisoned rats bleed internally. It worked extremely well at first. Within about a decade, populations in parts of Britain were barely affected by it. What happened is the whole of natural selection in miniature. In the original rat population, a very small number of individuals already carried a gene variant that let them cope with warfarin — by chance, before any poison was laid. When warfarin was spread, ordinary rats died and those rare resistant rats survived. They bred. Their offspring inherited resistance. Within a few years resistant rats were the majority, because the ones without resistance had been removed.

Notice what did not happen. No rat sensed the poison and toughened itself up. No rat's body changed during its life. The resistant rats were resistant from birth, and would have been resistant if warfarin had never existed — they simply had no advantage until it did. Warfarin did not create resistance; it made resistance worth having.

Antibiotic resistance in bacteria works identically, and faster because bacteria divide in minutes. Treat an infection of Mycobacterium tuberculosis with an antibiotic, and the vast majority die. But among billions of bacterial cells, a few carry a chance variation that lets them survive the drug. Those few are left with no competition and multiply into a whole population the drug no longer touches. This is why doctors insist on finishing a course of antibiotics: stopping early leaves the hardiest cells alive to rebuild.

The peppered moth is the classic textbook case. This British moth exists in a pale speckled form and a dark form. Before industrialisation the pale form dominated, camouflaged against lichen-covered bark. As soot from coal-burning blackened tree trunks in industrial areas through the nineteenth century, the dark form became far commoner there, while pale moths remained common in unpolluted countryside. Birds could see pale moths against dark bark and ate them. After clean-air legislation reduced the soot, the pale form recovered. The frequency of a variation tracked the environment, in both directions.

The heart of it

The helpful variation is already there by chance before the environment changes; the environment does not create it, it only decides which individuals live to pass it on.

3

Where the idea came from

Charles Darwin worked out natural selection in the late 1830s, after his voyage on HMS Beagle, and then sat on it for roughly twenty years, accumulating evidence and, by most accounts, dreading the reaction.

What forced his hand is the better story, and it is true. In 1858 Darwin received an essay from Alfred Russel Wallace, a naturalist collecting specimens in the Malay Archipelago, who had arrived at essentially the same mechanism independently and was asking Darwin's opinion of it. Rather than one man being scooped, their friends arranged for papers by both to be read jointly to the Linnean Society of London in July 1858. Darwin published On the Origin of Species the following year, in 1859. Two people reaching the same explanation separately is strong evidence that the explanation was sitting in the available facts, waiting to be seen.

What Darwin got wrong was inheritance. He had no idea how traits passed to offspring, and the accepted view — that parental characteristics blended in the offspring — was a serious problem for his theory, since blending would dilute any new helpful variation away within a few generations. Gregor Mendel's work on inheritance in units, published in 1866, would have solved it, but the two bodies of work were not brought together until the twentieth century.

The peppered moth field experiments are associated with Bernard Kettlewell in the 1950s. His methods were later criticised in detail — how he released and recaptured the moths, and where moths actually rest on trees. It is worth being honest about that. The criticisms were about experimental technique, and repeated studies with improved methods have supported his overall conclusion. The example is sound; the original experiments were imperfect.

4

What people get wrong

One error dominates everything else here. People write that the rats "developed resistance", that bacteria "became resistant", or that moths "changed colour to match the trees". All three describe individuals adapting during their own lifetimes, and all three are wrong.

An individual does not adapt. A rat born without warfarin resistance never acquires it; it dies. A pale moth never darkens. What changes is the population, and only across generations, as the proportion carrying each variation shifts. This mistaken idea — that a creature changes itself in response to need and passes the change on — is associated with Jean-Baptiste Lamarck, and questions are often built around it as the tempting wrong answer.

Fix it in your wording. Never "they developed"; always "some already had it, and those survived". Never "adapted to"; always "became commoner in the population because the others died". Graph questions about resistance over time in past IMSO papers are designed almost entirely around this distinction.

Commonly missed

A graph shows the percentage of warfarin-resistant rats in a town rising from 2 per cent to 70 per cent over four years of poison use. Which statement best explains the rise: the rats developed resistance to survive, or resistant rats were already present?

The trap is that "developed resistance to survive" sounds like a sensible cause-and-effect sentence, and it matches how people talk about getting stronger through effort.

How to work it: Start with the graph itself — it reads 2 per cent at the beginning, not zero. Resistant rats existed before the poisoning campaign, which alone rules out the idea that the poison created the trait. Then reason through the mechanism: warfarin killed the non-resistant rats, leaving the resistant ones to breed. Their offspring inherited resistance, so the resistant share of the population climbed each generation. The poison did not change any individual rat; it changed which rats were left. Choose "resistant rats were already present and were selected for".

5

Connected ideas

Natural selection is the process that produces adaptations, and the pressure doing the selecting is often a predator. It acts on populations, and it is fastest in microorganisms, which is why antibiotic resistance appears within years. The features it produces are what classification groups organisms by.

In one line

Individuals vary, more are born than can survive, and those whose inherited variations happen to suit the conditions leave more offspring — so the population changes across generations, while no individual ever adapts within its own lifetime.
