Concept

Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process by which individuals that happen to carry a helpful variation survive and breed more than the rest, so that variation becomes commoner in the population over generations.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

In any litter of puppies, no two are identical. That fact has a name: variation, the differences between individuals of one species. Much of it is inherited.

Add a second fact. Living things produce far more offspring than can survive. A frog lays thousands of eggs, yet only two need reach adulthood for the population to hold steady.

Put those together and something follows automatically. If most die and individuals differ, which ones die is not random. An individual whose variation makes it harder to catch is likelier to survive, and survivors breed, so over generations that variation spreads.

That is natural selection. Nothing chooses; the environment kills off some individuals more readily than others, and the survivors pass on what they had. Given long enough, the accumulated changes can produce a new species, which is evolution.

2

How it works

Four steps you can recite. 1. Variation exists within any population, and much of it is inherited. 2. Overproduction: more offspring are produced than the food and space can support. 3. Selection: individuals whose variations suit current conditions are likelier to survive to reproduce. 4. Inheritance: their offspring inherit the helpful variation, so it makes up a larger share of the next generation.

The clearest case is warfarin resistance in rats. Warfarin, introduced for rat control in the 1950s, stops blood clotting so poisoned rats bleed internally. It worked well at first, yet within a decade populations in parts of Britain were barely affected. A few rats already carried a gene variant letting them cope with it — by chance, before any poison was laid. Ordinary rats died, those rare resistant ones survived and bred, and within a few years resistant rats were the majority.

Notice what did not happen. No rat sensed the poison and toughened itself up. The resistant rats were resistant from birth and would have been so had warfarin never existed — they simply had no advantage until it did. Warfarin did not create resistance; it made it worth having.

Antibiotic resistance works identically but faster, since bacteria divide in minutes: treat an infection of Mycobacterium tuberculosis and most cells die, but a few carry a chance variation letting them survive and multiply into a population the drug no longer touches. The peppered moth shows the same process running both ways. Before industrialisation its pale speckled form dominated, camouflaged on lichen-covered bark; as soot blackened trunks, birds spotted pale moths and ate them, so the dark form became commoner. After clean-air laws cut the soot, the pale form recovered.

The heart of it

The helpful variation is already there by chance before the environment changes; the environment does not create it, it only decides which individuals live to pass it on.

3

Where the idea came from

Charles Darwin worked out natural selection in the late 1830s, after his voyage on HMS Beagle, then sat on it for twenty years gathering evidence and dreading the reaction.

What forced his hand is the better story, and it is true. In 1858 Darwin received an essay from Alfred Russel Wallace, a naturalist collecting in the Malay Archipelago, who had reached the same mechanism independently. Rather than either man being scooped, their friends had papers by both read jointly to the Linnean Society of London in July 1858, and Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859. Two people arriving separately is strong evidence the explanation was sitting in the facts.

What Darwin got wrong was inheritance. The accepted view that parental characteristics blended was a serious problem, since blending would dilute any new variation within a few generations. Gregor Mendel's work, published in 1866, would have solved it, but the two were not brought together until the twentieth century.

The peppered moth field experiments are associated with Bernard Kettlewell in the 1950s. His methods were later criticised over how he released and recaptured moths and where moths actually rest on trees. The criticisms concerned technique, and repeated studies with better methods have supported his conclusion. The example is sound; the original experiments were imperfect.

4

What people get wrong

One error dominates here. People write that the rats "developed resistance" or that moths "changed colour to match the trees". Both describe individuals adapting during their own lifetimes, and both are wrong.

An individual does not adapt. A rat born without warfarin resistance never acquires it; a pale moth never darkens. What changes is the population, and only across generations, as the proportion carrying each variation shifts. This mistaken idea — that a creature changes itself in response to need and passes the change on — is associated with Jean-Baptiste Lamarck, and is often the tempting wrong answer.

Fix it in your wording: never "they developed", always "some already had it, and those survived". Resistance graph questions in past IMSO papers turn on this.

Commonly missed

A graph shows warfarin-resistant rats in a town rising from 2 per cent to 70 per cent over four years of poison use. Which explains the rise: the rats developed resistance to survive, or resistant rats were already present?

The trap is that "developed resistance to survive" sounds like sensible cause and effect, and matches how people talk about getting stronger through effort.

How to work it: Start with the graph — it reads 2 per cent at the beginning, not zero. Resistant rats existed before the campaign, which alone rules out the poison creating the trait. Then the mechanism: warfarin killed the non-resistant rats, leaving the resistant ones to breed, so the resistant share climbed each generation. The poison did not change any individual rat; it changed which rats were left.

5

Connected ideas

Natural selection produces adaptations, and the pressure doing the selecting is often a predator. It acts on populations, and is fastest in microorganisms.

In one line

Individuals vary, more are born than can survive, and those whose inherited variations suit the conditions leave more offspring — so the population changes across generations, while no individual ever adapts within its own lifetime.
