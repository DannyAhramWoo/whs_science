Concept

Chromatography

A way of separating a mixture by letting a liquid carry its parts through a material, so that the parts which travel fastest end up furthest along.

Introduced in Chapter 20: How Scientists Work

1

What it means

Spill a drop of water on a page of felt-tip writing and the black ink runs. Look closely and the black is not black any more: there is a blue edge, or a pink one, or a smear of yellow ahead of the rest. That accident is chromatography, and the method is just a way of doing it on purpose and reading the result.

Chromatography is a separation method for mixtures whose parts dissolve to different extents and stick to a surface to different extents. A mixture is two or more substances that are together but not chemically joined, so each keeps its own properties and can be got back. The parts of the mixture are called components. In paper chromatography the surface is a strip of absorbent paper, and the liquid that does the carrying is called the solvent — usually water or alcohol.

The result is a piece of paper with coloured marks up it, called a chromatogram. Every mark is one component. Read properly, a chromatogram tells you two things at once: how many substances were in your sample, and — if you run known substances alongside it — which substances they were.

2

How it works

Here is the method, step by step, with the reason for each step attached.

One. Draw a line across the paper about two centimetres from the bottom. Draw it in pencil, not pen, because pencil is graphite and does not dissolve, so it will not run and add fake spots of its own. This line is called the baseline or start line.

Two. Put a small concentrated spot of the sample on the baseline. Small matters. A wide blob spreads sideways as well as upwards and its components overlap into a smudge you cannot read.

Three. Stand the paper in a shallow layer of solvent so that the solvent level is below the baseline. This is the step people get wrong, and it is the classic trap. If the solvent covers the spot, the sample simply dissolves off the paper into the liquid in the beaker, and there is nothing left to separate. The solvent must reach the spot by climbing up through the paper, not by drowning it.

Four. Wait. The solvent soaks upward through the paper fibres, reaches the spot, and carries the components along with it. Different components travel at different speeds, so they spread out into separate spots. When the solvent has nearly reached the top, take the paper out and immediately mark where the wet edge got to. That mark is the solvent front, and once the paper dries you cannot find it again.

Why do components travel at different speeds? Two forces compete for each one: the solvent carries it upward, and the paper holds it back. A component that dissolves well and clings weakly to the paper is carried a long way. One that dissolves poorly, or clings tightly, is left near the bottom. So the height a spot reaches is a fact about that substance in that solvent, which is what makes it useful for identifying things.

To turn the height into a number, we use the Rf value. Rf stands for retention factor, and it is:

Rf = distance moved by the spot ÷ distance moved by the solvent front

Both distances are measured from the baseline, and the spot distance is measured to the centre of the spot. A worked example: a green spot has travelled 3.6 cm from the baseline, and the solvent front has travelled 8.0 cm. Then Rf = 3.6 ÷ 8.0 = 0.45. A second spot on the same paper, yellow, has travelled 6.4 cm, so its Rf = 6.4 ÷ 8.0 = 0.80. The yellow one is more soluble in this solvent, or less attracted to the paper, or both.

Notice that Rf has no units, because it is a length divided by a length. Notice also that Rf is always less than 1. The spot is carried by the solvent, so it can never overtake the solvent that is carrying it. If you calculate an Rf above 1, you have divided the wrong way round or misread the solvent front.

Because the same substance in the same solvent gives the same Rf every time, Rf identifies substances. Run leaf pigments from an oak leaf and from a spinach leaf, and the chlorophyll spot lands at the same Rf in both, because Rf depends on the substance and the solvent, not on where the substance came from. With green leaves, the usual order from slowest to fastest is xanthophyll, then chlorophyll, then anthocyanin. Leaf pigments also separate far better in alcohol than in water, because they are much more soluble in alcohol — a choice of solvent is part of designing the experiment.

Chromatography is used to check which dyes are in a food colouring, to compare the ink on a suspect document with ink from a particular pen in forensic work, to test athletes and patients for drugs in blood or urine, and to separate the pigments in plants. Past papers set this as a whole practical, most notably in an IMSO 2009 experimental paper, where candidates separated inks, food colourings and leaf pigments and then computed Rf values from their own measurements.

The heart of it

Components separate because the solvent pulls them forward while the paper holds them back, and the balance of those two pulls is different for every substance.

3

Where the idea came from

Chromatography was invented by Mikhail Tsvet, a botanist of Russian and Italian parentage, working around 1900. He was trying to solve a plant problem, not a chemistry problem: he wanted to know whether the green of a leaf was one substance or several. Chemists at the time largely assumed chlorophyll was a single thing.

Tsvet poured a solution of leaf pigments through a glass tube packed with powdered chalk. As the liquid trickled down, the pigments stopped at different heights and spread into coloured bands laid one above another. He named the method from the Greek words for colour and writing, so chromatography means roughly colour writing.

What happened next is the instructive part. His work was largely ignored for decades, because some senior chemists suspected the coloured bands were substances damaged by the chalk rather than components that had genuinely been there all along. Tsvet died in 1919 with the method still little used. Only in the 1930s was it rediscovered, and from the 1940s it became one of the most important separation tools in science. A correct method can sit unused for a generation if nobody is persuaded.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is the solvent depth. It feels natural to stand the paper in plenty of liquid so it works quickly, but if the solvent starts above the baseline the sample washes off. Always check that the spot sits in dry paper at the start.

The second is forgetting to mark the solvent front before the paper dries. Without that measurement no Rf can be calculated, and the experiment cannot be redone from a dried strip.

The third is reading a chromatogram backwards. A spot that travelled furthest is the most soluble in that solvent, not the least. And a substance that stays on the baseline has not failed to be tested — it has given you the real answer that it barely dissolves in this solvent at all.

Commonly missed

A pupil sets up paper chromatography of a black ink. She fills the beaker with water to a depth of 3 cm and stands the paper in it, with her ink spot 2 cm from the bottom of the paper. After ten minutes the paper is wet but there are no coloured spots on it, and the water in the beaker has turned grey. Explain what went wrong.

It is a trap because everything about the setup looks right, and the paper really did absorb the solvent, so it is tempting to blame the ink or the paper.

How to work it: Compare the two depths. The water is 3 cm deep and the spot is only 2 cm up, so the spot started underneath the water surface. The ink therefore dissolved straight off the paper into the beaker, which is exactly why the water went grey. Nothing was carried up the paper because nothing was left on it. The fix is to lower the water level, or raise the baseline, so the solvent starts below the spot and has to climb to reach it.

5

Connected ideas

Chromatography is one of the separation methods, chosen when the parts of a mixture differ in solubility rather than in size or magnetism, so it sits naturally beside filtration and the general question of how to pull a mixture apart. It only works on mixtures, never on compounds, which is why the distinction between elements, compounds and mixtures matters here. Solubility explains why one component runs further than another and why alcohol beats water for leaf pigments, and the pigments themselves are worth meeting in their own right.

In one line

Chromatography separates a mixture because each component is pulled forward by the solvent and held back by the paper to a different degree, and dividing how far a spot moved by how far the solvent moved gives an Rf value that is always less than 1 and always the same for that substance in that solvent.
