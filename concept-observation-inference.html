Concept

Observation and Inference

An observation is what your senses or your instruments actually record; an inference is the explanation you add on top of it.

Introduced in Chapter 20: How Scientists Work

1

What it means

Walk into the kitchen and find a broken glass on the floor with the cat sitting beside it. You say, without pausing, "the cat knocked the glass over". But look carefully at what you actually saw. You saw a broken glass. You saw a cat. Everything else — the knocking, the cat's involvement, the order of events — you supplied yourself. You did not see it happen.

That split is the whole idea. An observation is information that came in through a sense or an instrument: the glass is in pieces, the pieces are near the fridge, the cat is present, the floor is dry. An inference is a conclusion you reached by reasoning from observations and from things you already believe: cats knock things over, therefore this cat knocked this thing over.

Inferences are not bad. Science would be useless without them, because observations on their own are just a list. The point is not to avoid inferring but to know at every moment which of the two you are doing, because they carry very different weight. An observation can be checked by someone else looking. An inference can only be argued about.

A quick test that almost always works: could a person who disagreed with you completely still accept this statement? "The liquid turned blue" — yes, they would have to accept it, they can see it too. "The liquid turned blue because it is alkaline" — no, they might have a different explanation. The first is an observation, the second an inference.

2

How it works

The reason this matters so much in practice is that inferences are invisible to the person making them. Nobody feels themselves inferring. It arrives in your head feeling exactly like something you saw. When you read "the plant died because it had no light", the "because" slipped in without announcing itself, and unless you are in the habit of hunting for it you will carry it forward as though it were data.

Consider a real example. You put a glass of iced water on a table and after ten minutes the outside is wet. The observation is: there is water on the outside of the glass. The inference most people make first is: the glass is leaking, or the water has soaked through. That is a perfectly reasonable inference and it is wrong. The water came out of the air, which cooled against the cold glass until the water vapour in it condensed. Notice that the observation was fine. Only the explanation went wrong, and it went wrong quietly.

The same observation can support several different inferences, and this is the crucial skill. Suppose you observe that a seedling on a windowsill is bent towards the window. Possible inferences: the plant grows towards light; the plant grows away from the cold wall; the plant was planted at an angle; someone bent it. All four explain the observation. The observation alone cannot choose between them — only a further test can, which is exactly why experiments exist.

There is also a trap in the other direction. Some things that feel like inferences are really observations made with an instrument. A thermometer reading of 23 degrees is an observation even though no sense organ measured it, because the instrument recorded it and anyone can go and look. What makes something an observation is not that it came from your eyes; it is that it was recorded rather than reasoned.

In written answers this distinction is worth marks directly. If a question asks what you observe, write only what a camera would have captured. If it asks what you conclude or what this suggests, that is where the reasoning belongs. Mixing them loses marks in both directions.

The heart of it

An observation is what you recorded; an inference is what you added — and the second one always feels like the first.

3

Where the idea came from

The most instructive story here is not a triumph but a tragedy. In 1847 a Hungarian doctor named Ignaz Semmelweis was working at the Vienna General Hospital, which had two maternity wards. One was staffed by doctors and medical students, the other by midwives. Semmelweis observed something that anyone could have checked: mothers in the doctors' ward were dying of childbed fever at a far higher rate than mothers in the midwives' ward. The women knew it too, and begged to be admitted to the midwives' side.

That difference was the observation. Semmelweis then looked for what was different between the two wards and inferred that the doctors, who came to the maternity ward straight from dissecting corpses, were carrying something on their hands. He told them to wash in a chlorine solution before examining patients, and the death rate in the doctors' ward fell sharply.

His inference was correct. But he could not say what the something was, because germ theory did not yet exist, and without a mechanism his colleagues found the claim insulting rather than persuasive — he was, after all, telling respected physicians that their own hands were killing their patients. His work was rejected, he grew increasingly bitter, and he died in an asylum in 1865 at the age of forty-seven. Within a couple of decades, the work of Louis Pasteur and Joseph Lister on germs showed he had been right all along.

The lesson is double-edged. Semmelweis shows that a careful inference from a solid observation can be true long before anyone can explain it. It also shows why science demands a mechanism: his opponents were not simply being stupid, they were asking a fair question that he could not answer.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is treating an inference as though checking it were unnecessary because it "obviously" follows. The stronger an inference feels, the less likely you are to notice you made one. Semmelweis's opponents inferred from their own social standing that clean-looking hands were clean hands, and never thought of that as an inference at all.

The second mistake is the opposite: thinking that because something is "only an inference" it does not count. Inferences are how science moves. No one has ever observed the centre of the Earth, and yet we know a great deal about it, all of it inferred from earthquake waves. What matters is that an inference is labelled as one, so that it can be tested rather than assumed.

Commonly missed

A student leaves a piece of bread in a sealed box for a week. On opening it she writes: "The bread has gone mouldy because the box was damp." Which part of this is an observation and which is an inference?

The trap is that the sentence reads as one continuous piece of evidence. The word "because" does an enormous amount of hidden work in the middle of it, and readers skate straight over it.

How to work it: split the sentence at the word "because". Everything before it — the bread has gone mouldy — is an observation, because anyone opening the box could confirm it. Everything after it is an inference: she did not measure the dampness, and even if she had, damp being present is not the same as damp being the cause. The mould spores could have been on the bread before it went in. To turn her inference into something testable she would need a second, identical box kept dry, so that dampness is the only difference between them.

5

Connected ideas

An inference that you write down carefully enough to test becomes a hypothesis, and testing it fairly is what variables and controls are for. Accuracy and precision decide how much you can trust the observations you are reasoning from, since an inference built on a sloppy measurement inherits that sloppiness. Almost every other concept in this book began as somebody's inference from an observation they could not otherwise explain — the way sound needs a medium, or the way a magnetic field reveals itself only through what it does to iron filings.
