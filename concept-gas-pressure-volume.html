Concept

Gases Under Pressure

Gas pressure is the combined push of countless particles hitting the walls of their container, so squeezing a gas into less space or heating it makes the pressure rise.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Put your finger over the end of a bicycle pump and push the handle in. It goes some way and then fights back, and the harder you push the harder it pushes back. Nothing has been added and nothing has escaped. The same air is being made to occupy less room, and it objects.

To see why, picture what a gas actually is. In a gas the particles are far apart and moving fast in all directions, bouncing off each other and off the walls. Each time a particle strikes a wall it gives that wall a tiny push. One push is far too small to feel, but there are enormous numbers of particles striking constantly, and the total of all those tiny pushes is what we call gas pressure.

Pressure is defined as force divided by the area it acts on, so pressure = force ÷ area. It is measured in pascals, written Pa, where one pascal is one newton spread over one square metre. Everyday atmospheric pressure is about 100 000 Pa, which is roughly the weight of a kilogram pressing on every square centimetre of you — you do not notice it because it pushes equally from all sides and there is matching pressure inside you.

2

How it works

Two things change gas pressure in a sealed container, and it is worth keeping them apart.

First, the volume. Halve the space and you have the same number of particles in half the room. Each particle now has a shorter distance to travel before it meets a wall, so it strikes more often — twice as often. Twice as many hits per second on each patch of wall means twice the pressure. So at a fixed temperature, pressure and volume are inversely related: when one is multiplied by a number, the other is divided by it. Squash to one third the volume and the pressure trebles.

Written as a relationship, pressure × volume stays constant, so P1 × V1 = P2 × V2. A worked example: 300 cm3 of gas at a pressure of 100 kPa is squeezed into 100 cm3 at the same temperature. Then 100 × 300 = P2 × 100, so P2 = 30 000 ÷ 100 = 300 kPa. The volume fell to a third, and the pressure rose to three times — which is the check to make on any answer of this kind. On a graph of pressure against volume this shows as a curve that falls steeply and then flattens, never touching either axis, because you can never squeeze a gas to no volume at all.

Second, the temperature. Heating a gas makes its particles move faster. Faster particles hit the walls more often, and each hit is harder. In a sealed rigid container, where the volume cannot change, both effects raise the pressure. This is why aerosol cans carry a warning not to heat them or leave them in a fire — the gas inside cannot expand, so the pressure climbs until the can bursts. If the container can change size instead, as a balloon can, the gas expands rather than raising its pressure much, which is why a balloon put in a fridge shrinks and puffs back out on the table.

Everyday examples repay the reasoning. A bicycle pump forces a lot of air into a small tyre, so the tyre pressure is high; the tyre is stiff because the air inside pushes outward harder than the atmosphere pushes inward. Your ears pop in a plane or a lift because the air pressure outside changes while the air trapped behind your eardrum does not, so the eardrum bulges until a swallow lets air through and equalises it. A syringe with its nozzle blocked can be pushed part way and no further, and it springs back when released.

There is a further consequence that papers like, and it is worth following slowly. Raising the pressure on a liquid raises its boiling point. Boiling happens when particles escaping the liquid can push back the pressure above it; if that pressure is greater, they need more energy to escape, so a higher temperature is needed. This is why a pressure cooker cooks faster — the water inside reaches perhaps 120 degrees C without boiling away. It is also why water deep underground can stay liquid at 300 degrees C: the weight of rock and water above it holds it at enormous pressure, so it never reaches its boiling point at all. IMSO papers have used both directions of this reasoning, in 2019 and in 2014, one reading pressure from a graph and one applying it to hot water underground.

The heart of it

Pressure is the total of countless particle collisions with the walls, so anything that makes collisions more frequent or harder — less space, or more heat — raises it.

3

Where the idea came from

Robert Boyle, working in Oxford with his assistant Robert Hooke, published the pressure-volume relationship in 1662. His apparatus was a J-shaped glass tube sealed at the short end, trapping air. Pouring mercury into the long open arm squeezed the trapped air, and the height of mercury told him the pressure. He measured the length of the trapped air column as he added mercury, and found that doubling the pressure halved the volume. It is one of the cleanest early experiments in physical science, and it still carries his name.

What Boyle did not have was an explanation. He knew the numbers behaved, and he offered a picture of air as being made of tiny springs or coiled particles that resisted being compressed. That picture is wrong. Gas particles are not springy; the resistance comes from their motion and their collisions, which is a quite different idea. The correct account came with the kinetic theory in the nineteenth century, developed by James Clerk Maxwell and Ludwig Boltzmann among others, more than two hundred years after the measurement.

There is also a fairness point about the credit. Edme Mariotte, working in France, published the same relationship independently around 1679, adding the important condition that the temperature must be held constant. For that reason it is still called Mariotte's law in France. This is common in science: a result found by careful measurement tends to be found more than once, and which name sticks often depends on who published where.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking that compressing a gas adds particles or makes the gas heavier. Nothing enters or leaves a sealed syringe. The mass is unchanged; only the spacing is.

The second is getting the direction backwards on a graph. Pressure and volume move in opposite directions at fixed temperature, so a curve sloping downwards is what you expect, and a straight rising line would be a different experiment entirely.

The third is forgetting that the relationship needs the temperature held constant. If you compress a gas quickly it warms up, and the pressure rises by more than the volume change alone would predict, which is why a bicycle pump gets hot.

Commonly missed

Deep underground, water in the rocks can reach a temperature of 300 degrees C and yet remain a liquid rather than turning to steam. Explain how this is possible.

It is a trap because water boils at 100 degrees C is learned so early that 300 degrees C seems flatly impossible for liquid water, and pupils often answer that the water must be a different substance or that the rock somehow cools it.

How to work it: Notice that 100 degrees C is the boiling point at normal atmospheric pressure, and that condition is not met underground. Boiling requires escaping particles to push back the pressure above the liquid. Deep in the rock, the weight of everything above presses down enormously, so the pressure is many times atmospheric. At that pressure the particles need far more energy to escape, so the boiling point is raised well above 300 degrees C. The water is below its boiling point for its actual pressure, and therefore stays liquid. Bring the same water to the surface and the pressure drops, so it flashes into steam — which is exactly what a geyser does.

5

Connected ideas

All of this rests on the particle model and on the fact that a gas has particles far apart and free to move, which is what makes gases compressible while liquids and solids are not. Pressure as force over area is the general idea behind it, heating a gas is a case of thermal expansion, and the raised boiling point links directly to how boiling works. Dissolved gases behave under pressure in the same spirit, which is why a bottled fizzy drink is kept under pressure to hold its carbon dioxide.
