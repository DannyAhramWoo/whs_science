Concept

Arthropods and Crustaceans

Arthropods are animals with jointed legs, a hard external skeleton and a body built in segments — the largest group in the animal kingdom — and crustaceans are the mainly water-living branch that includes crabs, prawns and lobsters.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

A crab, a spider, a beetle and a centipede look nothing alike, yet all four belong to one group. The name explains why: arthropod comes from Greek words meaning "jointed foot", and the jointed limb is the defining feature.

Arthropods are invertebrates — animals with no backbone — and are the largest animal group on Earth by a wide margin. Well over three quarters of all known animal species are arthropods, most of them insects.

Four features define them. Jointed appendages: every limb is rigid sections joined by flexible joints, the only way a creature encased in armour can bend. An exoskeleton: the skeleton is outside, a hard casing largely of a tough substance called chitin, protecting the body and giving muscles something to pull against. A segmented body, built from repeated sections usually grouped into larger regions. Bilateral symmetry: the left side mirrors the right.

Because the exoskeleton is rigid it cannot stretch as the animal grows, so arthropods must moult: they split the old casing, climb out, and expand while the new soft covering hardens. For a while afterwards the animal is soft and defenceless, which is why crabs hide after moulting. Moulting is the answer whenever a question asks how an animal in a hard case grows.

2

How it works

Papers test whether you can tell the four main arthropod groups apart, and the reliable method is counting legs and body divisions.

Insects. Six legs — three pairs. Body in three parts: head, thorax, abdomen. One pair of antennae. Usually two pairs of wings. Ants, beetles, butterflies, flies.

Arachnids. Eight legs — four pairs. Body in two parts: cephalothorax (head and thorax fused) and abdomen. No antennae, the quickest way to separate them from every other group. Spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites.

Crustaceans. Usually ten walking legs, though the number varies. Body in two parts: cephalothorax and abdomen. Two pairs of antennae, which no other group has. Almost all live in water. Crabs, prawns, lobsters, barnacles, and woodlice on damp land.

Myriapods. Many legs, the body a long chain of similar segments rather than two or three regions. One pair of antennae. Centipedes have one pair of legs per segment; millipedes two pairs, so far more legs.

Two counts settle most identification questions. Eight legs and no antennae means arachnid; six legs and three body parts means insect; two pairs of antennae means crustacean.

Now look at a crustacean closely, since this is where the detailed questions come from. The cephalothorax is the front region, covered by a shield of exoskeleton called the carapace, carrying the eyes (often on stalks), both pairs of antennae, the mouthparts and the walking legs. The abdomen is behind: in a prawn it is the long muscular tail, and in a crab it is folded flat underneath, which is why a crab looks tailless.

The limbs are called appendages, covering any jointed structure attached to the body — legs, antennae, mouthparts and claws all count. They are strikingly varied because they are modified for different jobs from one basic segmented design. The front pair is often enlarged into the great claw, the cheliped, used for gripping food, fighting and display.

Counting segments in an appendage is examined. A crustacean walking leg typically has seven segments. A cheliped follows the same plan, but the pincer at the tip has a fixed part and a movable finger, adding a division — so a cheliped typically counts eight against the walking leg's seven. The claw adds one. The lesson is that a claw is not a different kind of structure but a modified walking leg, which is why the counts are so close.

Crustaceans breathe with gills, feathery structures with a large surface area that take dissolved oxygen from water. In a crab they sit in chambers under the carapace, which explains a favourite question: a crab survives long out of water, unlike a fish, because the gills are enclosed in a moist chamber holding water against them. Gills only work wet, since oxygen must dissolve before crossing into the blood. Let them dry and they collapse, losing surface area, and the crab suffocates.

The hermit crab breaks the pattern usefully. Its abdomen is soft and unprotected, so it lives in an abandoned sea snail shell. The shell does not grow, so as the crab moults it must find a bigger one. The shell is borrowed, not part of the animal.

The heart of it

Jointed appendages plus a hard exoskeleton is what defines an arthropod, which is why a shrimp and a beetle belong in the same group despite living entirely different lives.

3

Where the idea came from

Aristotle, writing in the fourth century BC, was the first we know of to attempt a serious classification of animals, and he did well here, grouping insects, crustaceans and others together on the basis of their divided bodies. His system stood, more or less, for around two thousand years.

Carl Linnaeus placed most of these animals in a class he called Insecta in Systema Naturae in 1758. This was too broad, lumping crabs and spiders in with beetles. Jean-Baptiste Lamarck — better remembered for being wrong about inheritance — did valuable work here, separating crustaceans and arachnids from insects in the early 1800s. The name Arthropoda came from the German zoologist Karl von Siebold around 1848.

The difficult question was whether the group is real. Arthropods are so varied that scientists argued for over a century about whether jointed legs and an exoskeleton evolved once or several times independently — in which case the group would be a convenience rather than a family. Molecular evidence from the 1990s onwards indicates a single common ancestor. Aristotle's instinct was right, though he had no way of knowing why.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is calling every small many-legged creature an insect. Spiders are not insects, and neither are woodlice, centipedes or crabs. Count first: six legs and three body parts, or it is not an insect.

The second is expecting arthropods to be small or land-dwelling. Crabs and lobsters are arthropods, and the group includes animals over three metres across.

The third is thinking the exoskeleton grows with the animal. It cannot — it is dead, hard material, so growth requires moulting, which is why arthropod growth happens in jumps.

Past IMSO papers have tested concrete morphology: counting segments in a cheliped against a walking leg, asking which listed animals moult, and why a crab kept moist survives out of water.

Commonly missed

A diagram shows a prawn's walking leg with seven segments and its front claw. How many segments does the claw-bearing appendage have, and what does the answer tell you about how the claw evolved?

The trap is treating the claw as a completely different kind of structure and guessing a number unrelated to seven.

How to work it: Start from the walking leg's seven segments. The cheliped follows the same plan, but the tip is divided into a pincer — a fixed part and a movable finger closing against it. That division adds one, giving eight. The reasoning matters more than the number: a claw is a modified walking leg, not a new invention, which is why the counts differ by exactly one. The general rule is to find the ordinary version of the appendage, count that, then add for the modification.

5

Connected ideas

Arthropods are the largest branch of the invertebrates, and separating the four groups by leg count is the kind of question a dichotomous key is built from. The variety of appendages within one body plan is a set of adaptations. Insects also pollinate the flowers that plants reproduce through.

In one line

Arthropods are jointed-legged invertebrates in a hard exoskeleton they must moult to grow out of, split into insects with six legs and three body parts, arachnids with eight legs and no antennae, myriapods with many segments, and crustaceans with a cephalothorax, an abdomen, two pairs of antennae, gills and appendages including the claw-bearing cheliped.
