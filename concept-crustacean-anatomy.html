Concept

Arthropods and Crustaceans

Arthropods are animals with jointed legs, a hard external skeleton and a body built in segments — the largest group in the animal kingdom — and crustaceans are the mainly water-living branch that includes crabs, prawns and lobsters.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

A crab, a spider, a beetle and a centipede look nothing alike, yet all belong to one group. Arthropod comes from Greek words meaning "jointed foot", and the jointed limb is the defining feature.

Arthropods are invertebrates — animals with no backbone — and the largest animal group on Earth: well over three quarters of all known animal species, most of them insects.

Three features define them. Jointed appendages: every limb is rigid sections joined by flexible joints, the only way a creature encased in armour can bend. An exoskeleton: a hard casing outside the body, largely of a tough substance called chitin. A segmented body, built from repeated sections grouped into larger regions.

Because the exoskeleton cannot stretch as the animal grows, arthropods must moult: they split the old casing, climb out, and expand while the new soft covering hardens. Moulting is the answer whenever a question asks how an animal in a hard case grows.

2

How it works

Papers test whether you can tell the four groups apart, and the method is counting legs and body divisions.

Insects. Six legs. Body in three parts: head, thorax, abdomen. One pair of antennae, usually two pairs of wings. Ants, beetles, butterflies, flies.

Arachnids. Eight legs. Body in two parts: cephalothorax (head and thorax fused) and abdomen. No antennae, the quickest way to separate them from every other group. Spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites.

Crustaceans. Usually ten walking legs, though the number varies. Body in two parts: cephalothorax and abdomen. Two pairs of antennae, which no other group has. Almost all live in water. Crabs, prawns, lobsters, and woodlice on damp land.

Myriapods. Many legs, the body a long chain of similar segments rather than two or three regions. Centipedes have one pair of legs per segment; millipedes two.

Two counts settle most identification questions. Eight legs and no antennae means arachnid; six legs and three body parts, insect; two pairs of antennae, crustacean.

Now look at a crustacean closely. The cephalothorax is the front region, covered by a shield called the carapace, carrying the eyes, both pairs of antennae, the mouthparts and the walking legs. The abdomen is behind: in a prawn the long muscular tail, in a crab folded flat underneath, which is why a crab looks tailless.

The limbs are called appendages — legs, antennae, mouthparts and claws all count. They are varied because they are modified for different jobs from one segmented design. The front pair is often enlarged into the great claw, the cheliped.

Counting segments in an appendage is examined. A crustacean walking leg typically has seven segments. A cheliped follows the same plan, but the pincer at the tip has a fixed part and a movable finger, adding a division — so a cheliped counts eight against the walking leg's seven. A claw is not a different kind of structure but a modified walking leg, which is why the counts are so close.

Crustaceans breathe with gills, feathery structures with a large surface area that take dissolved oxygen from water. In a crab they sit in chambers under the carapace, which explains a favourite question: a crab survives out of water because those chambers hold water against the gills, and gills only work wet.

The heart of it

Jointed appendages plus a hard exoskeleton is what defines an arthropod, which is why a shrimp and a beetle belong in the same group despite living entirely different lives.

3

Where the idea came from

Aristotle, writing in the fourth century BC, was the first we know of to classify animals seriously, grouping insects and crustaceans together by their divided bodies. His system stood for around two thousand years.

Carl Linnaeus placed most of these animals in a class he called Insecta in Systema Naturae in 1758, which was too broad, lumping crabs and spiders in with beetles. Jean-Baptiste Lamarck separated crustaceans and arachnids from insects in the early 1800s, and the name Arthropoda came from Karl von Siebold around 1848.

The difficult question was whether the group is real. Arthropods are so varied that scientists argued for a century about whether jointed legs and an exoskeleton evolved once or several times independently — in which case the group would be a convenience rather than a family. Molecular evidence points to a single ancestor.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is calling every small many-legged creature an insect. Spiders are not insects, and neither are woodlice, centipedes or crabs. Count first: six legs and three body parts, or it is not an insect.

The second is thinking the exoskeleton grows with the animal. It cannot — it is dead material, so growth requires moulting, which is why arthropod growth happens in jumps.

Past IMSO papers have tested concrete morphology: counting segments in a cheliped against a walking leg, which listed animals moult, and why a crab kept moist survives out of water.

Commonly missed

A diagram shows a prawn's walking leg with seven segments and its front claw. How many segments does the claw-bearing appendage have, and what does that tell you?

The trap is treating the claw as a different kind of structure and guessing a number unrelated to seven.

How to work it: Start from the walking leg's seven segments. The cheliped follows the same plan, but the tip is divided into a pincer — a fixed part and a movable finger closing against it — and that adds one, giving eight. A claw is a modified walking leg, not a new invention, which is why the counts differ by exactly one.

5

Connected ideas

Arthropods are the largest branch of the invertebrates, and separating the four groups by leg count is the kind of question a dichotomous key is built from. Insects also pollinate the flowers that plants reproduce through.

In one line

Arthropods are jointed-legged invertebrates in a hard exoskeleton they must moult to grow out of: insects with six legs and three body parts, arachnids with eight legs and no antennae, myriapods with many segments, and crustaceans with a cephalothorax, an abdomen, two pairs of antennae, gills and a claw-bearing cheliped.
