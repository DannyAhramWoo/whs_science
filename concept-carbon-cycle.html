Concept

The Carbon Cycle

The carbon cycle is the continual movement of carbon between the air, living things, the oceans and the rocks.

Introduced in Chapter 13: Cycles and the Environment

1

What it means

Carbon is the element that living things are built from. Sugars, starches, fats, proteins and DNA are all built on frameworks of carbon atoms, which is why life on Earth is described as carbon-based. Carbon atoms can bond to four other atoms at once, including other carbon atoms, so they can form long chains and rings — the reason such an enormous variety of molecules is possible.

Like water, carbon is neither created nor destroyed on Earth in any meaningful quantity. The same atoms are used again and again. A carbon atom in your body was once in the air, before that perhaps in a plant, and before that possibly in a rock or a dinosaur. What the carbon cycle describes is the set of routes those atoms take between the places carbon is stored.

Those stores are called reservoirs, and they differ hugely in size. Rocks hold by far the most, mostly as limestone. The deep oceans hold a great deal dissolved. Fossil fuels, soil and living things hold moderate amounts. The atmosphere holds the least of all — and it is the smallest reservoir, being the one everything passes through, that changes fastest when the flows are disturbed.

2

How it works

Only one process moves carbon out of the air and into living things in any quantity, and that is photosynthesis. Plants, algae and some bacteria take carbon dioxide from the air, and using energy from sunlight combine it with water to make glucose, releasing oxygen. The carbon that was a gas is now part of a solid sugar, and from there it becomes cellulose, starch and everything else the plant builds. This is the point where carbon enters the living world, which is why plants are called producers.

Several processes send it back. Respiration is the reverse reaction: every living thing, plants included, breaks down glucose with oxygen to release energy, giving off carbon dioxide. Decomposition is respiration carried out by bacteria and fungi feeding on dead material, which is why a compost heap is warm and why fallen leaves disappear. Combustion — burning wood, coal, oil or gas — combines carbon with oxygen rapidly and releases carbon dioxide in one go. Carbon also moves along food chains: an animal eating a plant takes in the plant's carbon, uses some for growth and returns some by breathing.

There is a much slower branch worth knowing. Carbon dioxide dissolves in seawater, and marine creatures use the dissolved carbon to build shells of calcium carbonate. When they die, the shells sink and over millions of years compress into limestone. Chalk cliffs are essentially compacted shells — carbon that has been out of circulation for tens of millions of years. It returns only through volcanic eruptions, or when limestone is weathered by acidic rain. This slow cycle takes hundreds of millions of years, while the fast biological cycle turns over in years to decades.

In a natural balance the flows roughly cancel: photosynthesis removes about as much as respiration, decomposition and volcanism return. The difficulty now is that burning fossil fuels adds a large one-way flow. Fossil fuels are carbon that photosynthesis removed from the air hundreds of millions of years ago and that was buried before decomposers could return it — a reservoir taken out of circulation. Burning it releases that carbon far faster than any process can put it back into long-term storage, and since the atmosphere is the smallest reservoir, the effect on it is large. Atmospheric carbon dioxide has risen from about 280 parts per million before industrialisation to over 420 today.

The heart of it

Photosynthesis takes carbon out of the air; respiration, decomposition and burning put it back — and the cycle stays balanced only while those flows match.

3

Where the idea came from

There is no single discoverer of the carbon cycle. It was assembled over more than a century from separate discoveries about gases, plants and animals, by people who mostly did not realise they were describing one connected system.

An important early step came from Joseph Priestley, an English clergyman and chemist, in 1771. He knew that a candle burning in a sealed jar goes out, and that a mouse in a sealed jar dies — the air becomes, in his phrase, "injured". He then put a sprig of mint into a jar in which a candle had burned out, left it for some days in the light, and found the air would once again support a flame. He had shown that plants can restore air that burning has spoiled. It was a genuinely important result and he was awarded a medal for it. It is also honest to record that his results were difficult for others to reproduce, and Priestley himself sometimes failed to repeat them — the reason being that he did not realise light was essential, so experiments done in poor light did not work. He also interpreted his findings using the phlogiston theory, a framework we now know to be wrong.

Jan Ingenhousz, a Dutch physician, sorted this out in 1779 by establishing that the restoring effect happens only in sunlight and only in the green parts of the plant, and that in darkness plants spoil the air as animals do. Antoine Lavoisier in France identified the gases properly, naming oxygen and showing that respiration and combustion are the same kind of process. Joseph Black had already identified carbon dioxide, which he called "fixed air", in the 1750s.

Only in the nineteenth century, as decomposition and the ocean's role were understood, did these pieces come together into the closed loop we now draw. The idea that human activity could shift the balance came later still — Svante Arrhenius calculated the effect of burning coal in 1896.

4

What people get wrong

The most persistent error is believing that plants only photosynthesise and animals only respire — often expressed as "plants breathe in carbon dioxide and breathe out oxygen". Plants respire constantly, day and night, because they need energy at all times, just as animals do. During the day photosynthesis runs faster than respiration, so the net movement of carbon dioxide is into the plant. At night photosynthesis stops entirely while respiration continues, so a plant at night is a net producer of carbon dioxide.

A second error is thinking trees remove carbon permanently. They store it for as long as they live, but when a tree dies and decays, or burns, its carbon returns to the air. Planting trees stores carbon; it does not delete it.

Commonly missed

A sealed transparent box contains a healthy green plant. The carbon dioxide inside is measured over 24 hours. It falls during the day and rises during the night. Explain both.

The trap is the belief that plants are the opposite of animals, which predicts carbon dioxide falling all the time. The rise at night then looks like an error in the experiment.

How to work it: Recognise that two processes run in the plant, not one. Respiration happens continuously, day and night, because the plant needs energy for growth and repair at all times, and it releases carbon dioxide. Photosynthesis needs light, so it runs only during the day, and it takes carbon dioxide in. During the day photosynthesis proceeds much faster than respiration, so more carbon dioxide is absorbed than released and the level falls. At night photosynthesis stops completely while respiration carries on, so carbon dioxide is only being released and the level rises. The measurement is not an error — it is evidence that both processes are happening.

5

Connected ideas

The cycle is driven by photosynthesis on one side and respiration, decomposition and combustion on the other, with carbon passing between organisms along food chains. Burning fossil fuels adds carbon faster than the cycle can remove it, which strengthens the greenhouse effect.

Photosynthesis Respiration Combustion Fossil Fuels The Greenhouse Effect

In one line

Carbon moves endlessly between air, living things, oceans and rocks — pulled out of the air by photosynthesis and returned by respiration, decay and burning — and burning buried fossil carbon adds it back faster than it can be removed.
