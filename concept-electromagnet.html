Concept

Electromagnet

An electromagnet is a magnet made by passing an electric current through a coil of wire, usually wrapped around an iron core.

Introduced in Chapter 4: Magnetism

1

What it means

A bar magnet is always a magnet. You cannot switch it off, you cannot make it stronger, and you cannot swap its poles. An electromagnet can do all three, and that is the entire reason it matters.

The starting fact is that every electric current produces a magnetic field around itself. A single straight wire carrying a current is already surrounded by a weak field, in circles around the wire. Wind that wire into a coil, called a solenoid, and the fields from each turn add together, giving a much stronger field down the middle. Slide a piece of iron inside the coil and the field becomes stronger again, often by hundreds of times.

Switch the current off and the whole thing stops being a magnet. That is the useful part: an electromagnet is a magnet you control with a switch.

2

How it works

Each of the three parts contributes something, and it is worth being clear which does what.

The coil concentrates the field. A single loop of current produces a modest field through its centre; a hundred loops stacked together each contribute their own, and because they all point the same way down the axis they add up. Doubling the number of turns roughly doubles the strength, which makes the coil the cheapest way to gain power — more turns cost nothing but wire.

The current sets the strength directly. More current means a stronger field. This is the other adjustment available, and the one with a limit: push too much current through and the wire heats up, since it has resistance, and eventually the insulation fails.

The iron core multiplies everything. Iron is full of magnetic domains — small regions in which the atoms all point the same way. The coil's field turns those domains into line, so the iron becomes a magnet too, and its field adds to the coil's. This is why an electromagnet with a core is far stronger than the same coil around nothing. Crucially the core must be soft iron, not steel. Soft iron's domains fall back into disorder the moment the current stops, so the magnet switches cleanly off. Steel keeps its alignment and would stay magnetised, ruining the whole point.

Reversing the current reverses the poles, because the field direction follows the direction of current flow. Nothing else about the device changes. This ability to flip a magnet's poles thousands of times a second is what makes electric motors and loudspeakers possible: in a motor, a coil is made to flip its poles at just the right instant so that it is always being pushed round rather than pulled back. Scrapyard cranes use the switching, lifting a car and dropping it by cutting the current, and the doorbell and the electric relay work the same way.

The heart of it

Moving charge makes a magnetic field, so a coil of wire is a magnet you can turn on, strengthen, and reverse at will.

3

Where the idea came from

The link between electricity and magnetism was found by accident, in front of an audience. In April 1820 the Danish scientist Hans Christian Ørsted was giving a lecture in Copenhagen when he noticed that a compass needle lying near his apparatus twitched as he switched a current on and off in a nearby wire. Accounts differ on how deliberate this was, and the tidy story of pure serendipity may well be embroidered — Ørsted had suspected some connection between electricity and magnetism for years and had probably been looking for one. What is certain is that he saw the effect, spent three months confirming it carefully, and published in July 1820.

The result astonished Europe, because electricity and magnetism had been assumed to be entirely separate phenomena that merely happened to be studied by the same people. The needle also behaved oddly: it did not point towards the wire or away from it, but settled at right angles, which made no sense under any existing theory of forces acting along a line between two objects. Within months André-Marie Ampère in Paris had worked out much of the mathematics.

The practical device followed in 1825, when William Sturgeon, a former soldier working in England, varnished a bent iron bar to insulate it, wound bare copper wire around it, and connected a single cell. His electromagnet weighed around 200 grams and lifted about 4 kilograms — roughly twenty times its own weight. It was the first demonstration that this was not just a curiosity but a machine.

The American Joseph Henry improved it a few years later by insulating the wire rather than the core, which allowed him to wind many tightly packed layers of turns rather than a single sparse one. His electromagnets lifted hundreds of kilograms. The step from Sturgeon to Henry was not a new principle but a better understanding of which variable to push.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is thinking the iron core is the source of the magnetism. It is not. The current makes the field; the core amplifies it. A coil with no core is still an electromagnet, just a weaker one, whereas an iron bar with no current is nothing at all.

The second is forgetting that the core material matters. Swapping soft iron for steel gives a magnet that will not switch off, which for most uses is a fault rather than a feature.

A third is assuming more turns always means more strength without limit. In practice, more turns means more wire, more resistance, and therefore less current for a given battery, so past some point adding turns stops helping.

Commonly missed

Two electromagnets are compared. Magnet P has 100 turns and a steel core; magnet Q has 100 turns and a soft iron core. Both are connected to identical batteries, switched on, and used to pick up paper clips. Both pick up about the same number. The current is then switched off. What is the difference?

The trap is that the question tests them only while switched on, where the two behave almost identically, so it looks as though the core material makes no difference.

How to work it: The difference between soft iron and steel is not how easily domains line up but how well they stay lined up. Ask what happens when the aligning field is removed. Q's iron core loses its alignment immediately, so Q drops all its clips. P's steel core keeps much of its alignment and becomes a permanent magnet, so P keeps holding clips even with no current. For a crane, Q is the right design; for making a permanent magnet, P is.

Connected ideas

An electromagnet is the bridge between two chapters: it needs an electric current, and it produces ordinary magnetism and a magnetic field of exactly the same kind as a bar magnet's. The current that drives it is limited by the resistance of the coil, and the whole device turns electrical energy into a magnetic force and, unavoidably, some heat.

In one line

An electromagnet is a coil whose current creates a magnetic field, amplified by a soft iron core, and it can be switched off, strengthened or reversed in a way a permanent magnet never can.
