Concept

Accuracy and Precision

Accuracy is how close a measurement is to the true value; precision is how closely repeated measurements agree with each other, and a measurement can easily have one without the other.

Introduced in Chapter 20: How Scientists Work

1

What it means

In everyday speech accurate and precise mean roughly the same thing. In science they mean two quite different things, and the difference is genuinely useful.

Accuracy is about truth. A measurement is accurate if it is close to the real value of whatever you were measuring. Precision is about agreement. A set of measurements is precise if they all cluster tightly together, whether or not they are anywhere near the truth.

The classic picture is a dartboard. Three darts scattered widely but centred on the bullseye: accurate on average, but not precise. Three darts clustered tightly together in the top corner: very precise, and completely inaccurate. Three darts tight in the bullseye: both. Three scattered around the top corner: neither.

The corner case is the important one, because it is the dangerous one. Tightly clustered wrong answers look convincing. If you weigh the same object five times on a kitchen scale and get 152 g every time, the repeatability feels like proof. It is not. If the scale was never zeroed and reads 2 g high, you have five beautifully consistent wrong answers, and no amount of repeating will reveal it.

2

How it works

The two ideas come apart because they have different causes. Poor precision comes from random error: small unpredictable variations that push a reading up one time and down the next. Your reaction time when starting a stopwatch, a draught nudging a balance, the fact that you read the scale from a slightly different angle each time. Random errors scatter your results around a centre.

Poor accuracy usually comes from systematic error: something wrong with the method or the instrument that shifts every reading the same way. A ruler with a worn end so that measuring starts a millimetre in. A thermometer that reads two degrees high. A balance not zeroed. Systematic errors move the whole cluster off target without spreading it out.

This tells you what each problem responds to. Repeating and averaging fixes random error, because the high readings and low readings cancel. Repeating does absolutely nothing for systematic error, because every repeat is wrong in the same direction — averaging five readings that are all 2 g high gives you an answer 2 g high. Systematic error is only cured by checking the instrument against something you trust, or by fixing the method.

There is a third idea often muddled with these two: resolution, the smallest difference an instrument can show. A ruler marked in millimetres has a resolution of 1 mm. A digital balance showing two decimal places has a resolution of 0.01 g. High resolution encourages precision but does not guarantee accuracy — a balance can display 47.38 g and still be systematically 2 g out. Digits are not the same as truth, and the extra decimal places are what make people believe otherwise.

A practical consequence: never write down more digits than your instrument can justify. If you timed something with a stopwatch by hand, your reaction time is worth a couple of tenths of a second, so writing 12.34 s claims a certainty you do not have. Writing 12.3 s is more honest and, in an experiment, more correct.

The heart of it

Repeating a measurement improves precision but can never reveal a fault that shifts every reading the same way.

3

Where the idea came from

Measurement only becomes a shared problem when people need to compare results across distances, and the history of standard units is the history of trying to make a metre in one country the same length as a metre in another.

After the French Revolution, the metre was defined as a fraction of the distance from the North Pole to the equator, which sounds admirably universal but was hopeless in practice, since nobody could go and check. So physical objects were made instead: a metal bar whose length was the metre and a metal cylinder whose mass was the kilogram, kept in a vault at Sèvres near Paris from 1889. Copies went out to other countries, and every measurement in the world traced back, eventually, to those objects.

The flaw took decades to show. The standard kilogram and its official copies were compared occasionally over the following century, and they drifted apart — by tens of micrograms, a tiny amount but a real one. Nobody is certain why; contamination on the surfaces and losses from cleaning are the usual suspects. The awkward part is that because the original object defined the kilogram, it could not be said to be wrong. If it lost mass, then by definition the kilogram itself had changed, and everything measured against it changed with it. That is a systematic error nobody could correct, because there was nothing more trustworthy to check it against.

This is a large part of why the units were redefined in 2019 in terms of constants of nature rather than objects. The kilogram is now fixed through the Planck constant, a quantity that cannot be scratched, cleaned or lost. The metre had already been redefined in 1983 in terms of the speed of light. The reasoning is simple: a standard that can change is not a standard.

4

What people get wrong

The dominant mistake is treating consistency as proof of correctness. Consistent results feel authoritative, and a table of readings that agree to two decimal places looks like careful work. It may be careful work aimed slightly off target.

The second mistake is thinking more repeats always make a result better. They make it more precise and reveal random scatter, which is worth doing, but they cannot detect a bias. Before repeating, check the zero.

A third is treating an anomalous result as a nuisance to be quietly dropped. An odd reading is sometimes a mistake, but sometimes it is the most interesting thing in the table. The right response is to repeat that measurement, not to delete it.

Commonly missed

Two students each measure the boiling point of pure water at sea level. Student A records 97.1, 97.2 and 97.1 degrees Celsius. Student B records 98.5, 101.3 and 100.2 degrees Celsius. Whose measurements are more accurate, and whose more precise?

The trap is that Student A's readings look far more professional. They agree closely, they suggest a steady hand, and most people award them both labels at once.

How to work it: handle the two words separately. Precision is about agreement among the readings, so look only at the spread. A's readings span 0.1 degrees; B's span 2.8. A is clearly the more precise. Accuracy is about closeness to the true value, which for pure water at sea level is 100 degrees. A's average is about 97.1, nearly three degrees low, and every single reading is low — a consistent one-way offset, the signature of a systematic error, most likely a thermometer reading about three degrees under. B's average is about 100.0, which is right, though the individual readings scatter widely, the signature of random error. So B is the more accurate and A the more precise. The useful lesson is what each student should do next: A must check the thermometer against a known standard, since repeating will not help; B should simply take more readings and average them.

5

Connected ideas

Accuracy and precision set the limits on everything else in working scientifically. A hypothesis can only be tested if your measurements are sharp enough to detect the difference it predicts, and a fair test is wasted if the instrument is biased, since a systematic error affects both setups but a badly made one may affect only one. Every observation you record inherits the quality of the instrument that produced it, and every inference you build on top inherits it too. The same care applies whenever this book asks you to measure — temperature, mass, density or the current in a circuit.
