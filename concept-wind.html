Concept

Wind

Wind is air moving from where the pressure is higher to where it is lower, caused by the Sun heating the Earth unevenly.

Introduced in Chapter 11: Seasons, Climate and Wind

1

What it means

Wind is simply air on the move. Air is a real substance made of molecules with mass, even though we cannot see it, and like any fluid it flows when something pushes it.

What pushes it is a difference in air pressure. Pressure is the force that air exerts on each square metre of whatever it touches, produced by the weight of all the air above and by molecules constantly colliding with surfaces. Where air is packed more densely, the pressure is higher. Air always flows from a region of higher pressure towards a region of lower pressure, in the same way that water runs downhill or air rushes out of a punctured tyre.

So every wind is the answer to one question: why is the pressure higher over there than over here? And the answer, nearly always, traces back to the Sun heating one place more than another. Wind is uneven solar heating turned into motion.

Note the naming convention, because it catches people out: winds are named for where they come from. A northerly wind blows from the north towards the south. A sea breeze blows from the sea onto the land.

2

How it works

Follow the chain from sunlight to breeze. The Sun warms a patch of ground. The ground warms the air just above it. Warm air expands, so the same mass now occupies more space, making it less dense than the surrounding air. Being less dense, it rises — this is convection. As that warm air lifts away, it leaves fewer molecules pressing down near the surface, so surface pressure there falls. Meanwhile somewhere cooler, air is sinking and piling up, raising the pressure there. Air then flows along the ground from the high-pressure place to the low-pressure place, and that flow is the wind.

The sea breeze is the clearest example. Land and water do not warm at the same rate. Water has a high heat capacity — it takes roughly five times as much energy to raise the temperature of a kilogram of water by one degree as it does a kilogram of dry soil — and the sea also mixes its heat down through a great depth, while sunlight only heats the top few centimetres of land. So on a sunny day the land becomes much hotter than the sea. Air above the land warms, rises, and lowers the pressure there; the cooler, denser air over the sea has higher pressure and flows inland to replace it. That is why beaches are breezy on hot afternoons, and why the breeze blows off the sea rather than towards it.

At night it reverses. Land loses its heat quickly once the Sun is gone, while the sea, having stored a great deal of energy, stays warm. Now the air over the sea is warmer and rising, pressure over the sea is lower, and the wind blows from land to sea: a land breeze. Fishermen have used this pair of breezes for thousands of years, sailing out at dawn and returning in the afternoon.

The same process at a global scale gives the great wind belts. Air rises strongly at the equator where heating is most direct, spreads towards the poles high up, cools and sinks around 30 degrees latitude, and flows back along the surface towards the equator. Because the Earth is rotating beneath this moving air, the flow is deflected sideways — an effect named after Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis — which is why the trade winds blow from the north-east in the Northern Hemisphere rather than straight from the north.

The heart of it

Uneven heating makes some air rise and some sink, that creates pressure differences, and wind is air flowing to even them out.

3

Where the idea came from

Practical knowledge of wind is far older than any explanation of it. Sailors and fishermen around the Mediterranean, the Indian Ocean and the coasts of Asia knew the daily rhythm of sea and land breezes, and knew the seasonal monsoon winds well enough to time entire trading voyages around them, centuries before anyone wrote down why air moves. Greek and Roman writers named the winds and mapped their directions; the Tower of the Winds in Athens, built in the first or second century BC, carries a carved figure for each of eight winds. This was reliable, useful knowledge without a mechanism — a reminder that knowing what happens and knowing why are separate.

Explanation needed the discovery that air has weight and pressure, which came with Torricelli's barometer in 1643. Once air could be understood as a fluid with varying density and pressure, wind became a problem that could be reasoned about.

The first good account of the global pattern came from George Hadley, an English lawyer and amateur scientist, in 1735. Sailors had long known that the trade winds blow steadily from the east near the equator, and this was a puzzle: if warm equatorial air simply drew air in from north and south, the winds should blow straight from the poles. Hadley argued that air moving towards the equator keeps the slower eastward motion it had at higher latitudes, so it lags behind the fast-spinning ground beneath it and appears to blow from the east. The large circulation cells in the tropics are still called Hadley cells. His reasoning was not entirely right — he used the wrong physical quantity, and Coriolis and later William Ferrel supplied a better treatment in the 1800s — but the essential insight, that the Earth's rotation bends the winds, was correct and original.

4

What people get wrong

A frequent error is thinking that low pressure sucks air in, as though it were pulling. Nothing pulls. The higher-pressure air pushes harder than the lower-pressure air pushes back, so the air moves. The distinction matters because it keeps you looking for the place doing the pushing.

The other common slip concerns the sea breeze: people reason that the sea is cooler, so cool air must be rising from it, or they get the direction backwards. Keep hold of the rule that warm air rises, so the warm place is where pressure drops and where the wind is heading.

Commonly missed

On a hot afternoon at the coast the breeze blows from the sea towards the land. By late evening it has reversed and blows from the land towards the sea. Explain both.

The trap is remembering the afternoon case as a fact to recite, without the reasoning — so when the question asks for the reversal there is nothing to work with.

How to work it: Both halves come from the same rule, applied to whichever surface is warmer at the time. In the afternoon, land heats faster than the sea because water has a much higher heat capacity. The air over the land is therefore warmer, expands, becomes less dense and rises, lowering the pressure over the land. The higher-pressure air over the sea flows in to replace it, giving a sea breeze. At night the land loses heat quickly by radiation while the sea, holding a large store of energy, stays warmer. Now the air over the sea is the warmer and rising air, so the low pressure is over the sea, and air flows out from the land: a land breeze. The rule never changes — air flows from high pressure to low, and low pressure forms where air is warm and rising.

5

Connected ideas

Wind is convection working horizontally, driven by density differences in air that the Sun creates by heating land and sea unequally. Winds are a central part of the weather and of the atmosphere's behaviour, and moving air carries enough kinetic energy to be captured as a renewable source.
