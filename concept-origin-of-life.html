Concept

The Origin of Life

The origin of life is the unsolved question of how ordinary chemistry on the early Earth became the first thing that could feed, grow and copy itself.

Introduced in The Origin of Life

1

What it means

Every living thing you have ever seen came from a parent. A kitten comes from a cat, an oak from an acorn, a bacterium from the bacterium that divided in two. Follow that chain backwards far enough and it must reach a point where there was no parent — because the Earth is about 4.5 billion years old, and for its first few hundred million years it was far too hot and too battered by collisions for anything to live on. Somewhere between then and about 3.5 billion years ago, when the first convincing traces of life appear in the rocks, chemistry became biology.

The word abiogenesis is used for this: a- meaning not, bio- meaning life, -genesis meaning beginning. It is not the same question as evolution. Evolution explains how living things change once they already exist and can reproduce with variation; it takes the existence of reproducing things as its starting point. The origin of life asks how that starting point was reached — and it is far less settled.

The honest position, and the one scientists actually hold, is that nobody knows. Several serious ideas are supported by real experiments, and there is no agreement about which is right, or whether any is. That is not a gap a more advanced textbook would fill. It is one of the genuinely open questions in biology, and being able to say "we do not yet know" about it is more accurate understanding than any confident answer would be.

2

How it works

Any explanation has to account for what all life shares, because those shared features are the strongest evidence that everything alive descends from a single ancestral population, which biologists call LUCA, the last universal common ancestor. Every organism on Earth — a whale, a mushroom, a bacterium in a hot spring — stores its instructions in DNA, uses very nearly the same genetic code to read it, builds its proteins from the same twenty amino acids, and is wrapped in a membrane of fatty molecules. A polar bear and pond scum agreeing on all of that is not coincidence. It is inheritance.

So the problem splits into parts. You need building blocks: amino acids for proteins, and the components of DNA and RNA. You need those blocks joined into long chains. You need a chain that can copy itself, so that variation and selection can begin. And you need a container, or the useful molecules simply drift apart into the ocean. Several parts have been achieved in laboratories. Nobody has joined them into one unbroken sequence.

Two families of ideas compete over where it happened. The first is the warm surface pool: a shallow pond or lagoon, dried and rewetted by sun and tides, with lightning and ultraviolet light supplying energy. Drying is useful, because joining small molecules into chains means removing water, which is difficult while surrounded by it — an evaporating pool solves that. The weakness is that with no ozone layer the surface was harshly irradiated, so molecules that took a long time to build could be destroyed quickly.

The second is hydrothermal vents on the deep ocean floor, where water heated by the rock below streams out through porous chimneys. Alkaline vents produce structures full of tiny mineral compartments, with a natural chemical difference between the fluid inside and the seawater outside — a source of energy strikingly like the one every living cell still uses. Those compartments also solve the container problem without needing a membrane first. The weakness is the opposite of the pool's: everything is permanently diluted, which makes building long chains harder.

The heart of it

Everything alive shares the same chemistry, so everything alive is related — and the origin of life is the question of how that shared chemistry began, which nobody has yet answered.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of history the question did not exist, because the answer seemed obvious. Spontaneous generation — the belief that living things arise readily from non-living matter — was accepted from Aristotle onwards. Maggots appeared in meat and mice in stored grain, and it was taken as plain observation that the meat and grain produced them. The belief survived longest for microbes, which seemed to appear in broth from nothing at all.

It was Louis Pasteur, in France in 1859 and the years around it, who settled the matter. He boiled broth in flasks whose necks he had drawn out into long S-shaped curves. Air could pass freely down the neck, so the usual objection — that boiling had ruined some vital quality of the air — did not apply. But dust and microbes settled in the bend and never reached the broth, which stayed clear for months. Tilt the flask so the broth touched the bend, and it clouded within days.

That result created the puzzle we now have, and Pasteur understood as much. If life does not appear spontaneously now, how did it ever start? Charles Darwin was thinking about exactly this. In a private letter to his friend Joseph Hooker in 1871 he wondered whether life might have begun in "some warm little pond" with the right chemicals and a source of energy, adding that any such compound formed today "would be instantly devoured or absorbed, which would not have been the case before living creatures were formed" — a sharp observation, since a world without life has no bacteria waiting to eat the first molecules. He kept the thought out of his published work.

Serious theory arrived in the 1920s, when Alexander Oparin in Russia and J. B. S. Haldane in Britain independently proposed that the early atmosphere had no oxygen and that ultraviolet light could have built up organic molecules in the sea. Haldane's phrase, a "hot dilute soup", is the origin of the "primordial soup" people still say. Their prediction about oxygen has since been confirmed by the rocks; their guess about which gases the air did contain was less accurate.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest confusion is treating the origin of life and evolution as one theory, so that an unanswered question about the first cell is taken as a weakness in evolution. They are separate. The evidence for common descent would stand unchanged whatever turns out to be true about the first cell.

The second error is expecting a single moment when non-life became life. Chemistry probably became gradually more lifelike over a long stretch of time, and the boundary we draw is a matter of our definitions rather than an event that happened.

Commonly missed

A student says: "Pasteur proved life cannot come from non-living matter, so life on Earth cannot have started by itself." What is wrong with this reasoning?

The trap is treating an experiment's result as a universal rule, when the experiment was done under one particular set of conditions.

How to work it: Ask what Pasteur actually tested. He tested nutrient broth, on the modern Earth, over weeks or months, in a world already full of bacteria. What he showed is that under those conditions life does not arise from non-life. The early Earth differed in every one of those respects: no oxygen, no ozone layer and so far more ultraviolet light, intense volcanic and lightning activity, hundreds of millions of years rather than months, and — most importantly — no existing organisms to consume any new molecule that formed, which was Darwin's own point. So Pasteur's result does not extend to those conditions. Note carefully that this does not show life did begin by itself; it only shows his experiment cannot be used to argue it did not.

5

Connected ideas

The Urey-Miller experiment tested whether building blocks could form at all; the RNA world hypothesis is one attempt at the copying problem; the cell membrane supplies the container; and stromatolites are the physical evidence that life had already succeeded by 3.5 billion years ago. The composition of the early atmosphere sets the conditions all of these ideas must work within.

In one line

All living things share one chemistry and so share one ancestor, but how that chemistry first arose from non-living matter — in a warm pool, at a deep-sea vent, or somewhere else entirely — is still genuinely unknown.
