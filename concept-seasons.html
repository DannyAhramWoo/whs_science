Concept

Seasons

Seasons happen because the Earth's axis is tilted, so as the Earth orbits the Sun each hemisphere leans towards the Sun for part of the year and away from it for the other part.

Introduced in Chapter 11: Seasons, Climate and Wind

1

What it means

In many parts of the world the year has a rhythm: warm months with long light evenings, cold months when it is dark by teatime. Those are the seasons. Near the equator the pattern is different — temperature barely changes, and the year is divided into wetter and drier months instead — but the cause is the same underlying geometry.

The cause is the tilt of the Earth's axis. The axis is the imaginary line the Earth spins about, and it does not stand upright relative to the Earth's orbit. It leans over by about 23.5 degrees. Crucially, it keeps pointing in the same direction in space all year — towards the star Polaris — rather like a spinning top that leans but holds its lean steady.

So as the Earth travels round the Sun, the direction of the lean stays fixed while the Earth's position changes. In June the northern half of the Earth is leaning towards the Sun, and the southern half away from it. In December it is the other way round. That single fact produces every seasonal effect there is.

2

How it works

Leaning towards the Sun does two things at once, and both make it warmer.

First, the Sun climbs higher in the sky, so its rays strike the ground more steeply. This matters more than people expect. Imagine shining a torch straight down onto a table: you get a small, bright circle. Tilt the torch and the same light spreads into a long, dim oval. The torch is putting out exactly the same energy, but it is now spread over more surface, so each patch of table receives less. Sunlight in winter arrives at a shallow angle and spreads thinly over the ground; in summer it arrives steeply and concentrates. Slanting light also has more atmosphere to pass through, which absorbs and scatters a little more of it.

Second, the days are longer. When your hemisphere leans towards the Sun, your part of the Earth spends more of each rotation inside the lit half. In London in June the Sun is up for about 16 and a half hours and in December for about 8. More hours of heating and fewer hours of cooling at night compound the effect.

Four dates mark the cycle. Around 21 June the North Pole leans most towards the Sun: the northern summer solstice, the longest northern day. Around 21 December the reverse: the northern winter solstice. Around 20 March and 22 September come the equinoxes, when the tilt is sideways to the Sun, neither hemisphere favoured, and day and night are close to equal everywhere.

Near the equator the Sun is high all year and day length hardly varies, so temperature stays fairly constant. What moves instead is a band of heavy rainfall that follows the region of most direct sunlight north and south through the year, giving wet and dry seasons rather than hot and cold ones.

There is also a lag worth noticing. The longest day is in June but the hottest weather usually comes in July or August, because land and oceans take weeks to warm up — the same reason a pan stays hot after the heat is turned down.

The heart of it

Seasons come from the tilt, not the distance: leaning towards the Sun means steeper rays and longer days, and both mean more warmth.

3

Where the idea came from

The connection between the Sun's height in the sky and the seasons is very old. Ancient peoples tracked the solstices closely because farming depended on it, and monuments such as Stonehenge in England, built from around 3000 BC, and Newgrange in Ireland are aligned to sunrise or sunset at a solstice. By the time of the Greeks the tilt was being measured: Eratosthenes, working in Alexandria around 240 BC, obtained a value close to the modern figure of 23.5 degrees. So the explanation has been available for well over two thousand years.

What makes the seasons interesting is not the difficulty of the science but the stubbornness of the wrong answer. In 1987 a documentary called A Private Universe, made by Matthew Schneps and Philip Sadler at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics, filmed Harvard graduates in their gowns on graduation day and asked them what causes the seasons. Most answered confidently that the Earth is closer to the Sun in summer. These were well-educated people who had been taught the correct explanation and had replaced it with a more intuitive wrong one. The film became widely used in teacher training, because it showed that an appealing misconception can survive being taught against.

Part of the reason the distance idea will not die is that the Earth's orbit really is slightly oval, so the distance really does change — by about 3 per cent. The trap is that this genuine fact does not do what people assume. The Earth is actually nearest the Sun in early January, during the northern winter.

4

What people get wrong

The misconception is the distance explanation: summer because we are nearer the Sun, winter because we are further away. It is worth seeing clearly why this fails rather than just being told it is wrong.

The decisive objection is the Southern Hemisphere. Australia has summer in December and winter in July — the opposite of Europe. But the Earth as a whole is a single object at a single distance from the Sun on any given day. If distance were the cause, the entire planet would have summer together and winter together. It does not, so distance cannot be the cause. Something must differ between the two halves of the Earth, and what differs is which one is leaning towards the Sun.

Commonly missed

In January the Earth is at its closest to the Sun for the whole year, yet January is one of the coldest months in Britain. Explain.

The trap is that the fact given seems to contradict the answer, so it is tempting to doubt the fact. The fact is true, and it is a hint rather than a contradiction.

How to work it: First recognise that distance is not what drives the seasons; the change in distance over a year is only about 3 per cent, far too small to matter much. Then apply the real cause: in January the Northern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun. So the Sun stays low in the British sky, its rays strike the ground at a shallow angle and spread thinly, and the days are short. Little heat arrives and there are long nights in which to lose it. The tilt effect is much stronger than the small distance effect, and it is working in the opposite direction.

5

Connected ideas

Seasons depend on the Earth's revolution around the Sun combined with its fixed axial tilt, and on how much radiation each patch of ground receives. The seasonal pattern of temperature is a major part of what defines a place's climate, and uneven heating of the Earth drives the winds.
