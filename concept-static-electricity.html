Concept

Static Electricity

Static electricity is electric charge that has built up and stayed put on an object, usually because rubbing moved electrons from one material to another.

Introduced in Chapter 3: Electric Circuits

1

What it means

Rub a balloon on your hair for a few seconds. Your hair stands up and follows the balloon. Press the balloon against a wall and it hangs there, with nothing holding it. Pull a jumper off over your head in a dry room and you hear tiny crackles. Touch a metal door handle after walking on a carpet and you get a small sharp shock. All of these come from the same cause.

Everything is made of atoms, and atoms contain two kinds of charge. In the centre sits a nucleus carrying positive charge. Around it move electrons, which are tiny and carry negative charge. Normally an object has equal numbers of each, so the charges cancel and the object is neutral — it behaves as though it has no charge at all.

Electrons are on the outside and are far lighter than the nucleus, so they are the only part that can be moved easily. When two materials are rubbed together, some electrons are scraped from one surface onto the other. The material that gains electrons now has more negative than positive charge, so it is negatively charged. The material that lost them is left with more positive than negative, so it is positively charged. Nothing was created. Charge was only moved.

The charge is called static — meaning still — because it stays where it was put. On materials like plastic, rubber, glass and hair, charge cannot travel, so it sits on the surface until something lets it escape.

2

How it works

The governing rule is short: like charges repel, unlike charges attract. Two negatives push apart, two positives push apart, and a positive and a negative pull together. This is why rubbing a balloon on hair makes hair stand up. The balloon takes electrons and becomes negative; your hair, having lost them, is left positive. Each hair is now positive like every other hair, so the hairs repel one another and spread apart, while all of them are attracted to the negative balloon.

Attraction to a wall is stranger, because the wall is neutral. Here a second mechanism operates, called induction. Bring the negative balloon close to the wall. The balloon's negative charge pushes the wall's own electrons deeper into the wall, leaving the surface slightly positive. The balloon's negative and the surface's positive now attract, and the balloon sticks. Nothing was transferred; the wall's charges merely shifted position within it. The same thing lets a charged plastic comb pick up scraps of paper.

Induction can also be used to charge something permanently, and this is the version that examination papers set out step by step. Take a metal sphere on an insulating stand. Bring a positively charged rod near, without touching. The sphere's free electrons are pulled towards the rod, so the near side becomes negative and the far side positive — but the sphere overall is still neutral. Now earth the sphere, meaning connect it to the ground with a wire or simply a finger. Electrons flood up from the earth to the near side, attracted by the rod. Break the earth connection while the rod is still in place. Only then take the rod away. The extra electrons have nowhere to go, so they spread out evenly, and the sphere is left negative all over. The result is worth noticing: the sphere ends up with the charge opposite to the rod, and the rod lost nothing. A 2017 IMSO paper tested exactly this ordering.

Charge also shares out between conductors that touch. Connect a sphere carrying 10 units of charge to an identical sphere carrying 30. Like charges repel, so the charge spreads until it is even, and each sphere is left with 20. The total is unchanged; only the distribution moved. If the spheres are the same size, the split is equal.

When too much charge builds, it eventually escapes in one rush. Air normally does not conduct, but a large enough charge tears electrons off air molecules and makes a brief conducting path. Charge pours across it, heating the air so it glows and bangs — a spark. That crackle from a jumper is a shower of small sparks. Lightning is the same event enormously scaled up: ice and water churning inside a storm cloud rub against each other and separate charge, until the built-up charge discharges to the ground or to another cloud.

The heart of it

Rubbing moves electrons, not charge from nothing, and afterwards like charges repel while unlike charges attract.

3

Where the idea came from

The oldest record belongs to Thales of Miletus, a Greek thinker in what is now Turkey, around 600 BC. He noticed that amber — fossilised tree resin — attracted light straws and feathers after being rubbed with fur. We only have later authors reporting this, so the details are uncertain, but the observation is genuine and easily repeated. Thales is said to have thought the amber was in some way alive, since moving things without touching them looked like a property of living creatures. That guess was wrong, and it stood for around two thousand years with nothing better offered.

William Gilbert, physician to Queen Elizabeth I in England, made the first real progress around 1600 in his book De Magnete. He showed that this rubbing effect was not confined to amber, and, importantly, that it was a different thing from magnetism, which people had muddled with it. He coined a word for the effect from the Greek for amber, elektron — the origin of "electric" and of every related word since.

Stephen Gray, an English dyer and amateur experimenter, found in the 1720s that the effect could be sent along a distance. He transmitted it down damp thread over many metres, and found some materials carried it while others stopped it. That split is what we now call conductors and insulators. Gray's work was ignored for years because a more prominent figure at the Royal Society had blocked his publications.

Benjamin Franklin in America proposed in the 1740s and 1750s that there was a single electric fluid, present in excess or deficit, and it was he who chose the labels positive and negative. His choice was a fifty-fifty guess and it went the unlucky way: the particles that actually move in wires are electrons, which we now call negative. We have kept his labels regardless.

4

What people get wrong

The main confusion is between static charge and current electricity, and this is what papers test hardest. Static electricity is charge sitting still on a surface, built up by rubbing, with no complete path for it to travel around. Current electricity is charge flowing continuously around a closed circuit, pushed by a cell or battery. The charge is the same stuff. The difference is whether it is parked or moving in a loop. A charged balloon holds a huge voltage but almost no charge, so its shock is startling and harmless. A torch battery has a small voltage but keeps charge moving for hours.

The second error is thinking rubbing creates charge. It never does. Charge is only transferred, and the two objects always end up with equal and opposite amounts. If the cloth ends up positive, the rod is negative by the same amount.

The third is assuming attraction proves the other object is charged. It does not. A charged object attracts neutral objects too, by induction. Only repulsion is proof that something carries charge, because a neutral object is never repelled.

Commonly missed

A plastic rod is rubbed with a cloth and gains a negative charge. It is held near a small piece of neutral paper, and the paper jumps to the rod. What does this show about the paper's charge?

The trap is concluding the paper must be positively charged, since unlike charges attract. It is a fair-sounding piece of reasoning, and the question says the paper is neutral only once, quietly.

How to work it: the paper is neutral and stays neutral. The rod's negative charge repels electrons in the paper away from the near side, leaving that face slightly positive and the far face slightly negative. Attraction is stronger for the closer charges, so the paper is pulled in. That is induction, and it needs no charge on the paper at all. Remember the test: attraction can happen between a charged object and a neutral one, so only repulsion proves both objects are charged.

5

Connected ideas

Static electricity and electric current are the parked and flowing versions of the same charge, so the two belong together. Whether charge can drain away or stay put is decided by conductors and insulators, which is why static builds on plastic and never on bare metal you are holding. Once charge does flow, resistance controls how easily it moves, and the wire's own shape changes that resistance. Series and parallel circuits describe the paths available. Electrolytes conduct because charged particles in a liquid can move.
