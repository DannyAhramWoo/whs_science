Beyond the syllabus

How Chemical Reactions Work

A chemical reaction is how one substance turns into a different substance. Rust, cooking, fireworks, a fizzing tablet and the food in your own cells are all the same kind of event, running at different speeds.

Read this first

This chapter is not on the exam syllabus. Nothing here will be tested, so do not revise it the way you revise the numbered chapters.

It is here because a chemical reaction is how one substance becomes another, and once you can spot one you start seeing them everywhere — a nail going rusty, a cake rising, a firework, a match, the meal you ate this morning being taken apart inside you.

Parts of this chapter use the shorthand that chemists write down among themselves — formulae, equations, and a way of counting atoms. None of it is hard, and all of it is explained here from the beginning. Think of it the way you would think of learning to read music: the symbols are not the difficulty, they are the door.

1

When something becomes something else

Put an ice cube on a warm plate and it turns into water. Stir sugar into tea and the sugar seems to vanish. Neither of those is a chemical reaction. The ice is still water, just arranged differently. The sugar is still sugar — taste the tea and it is sweet, and if you boiled the water away you would get the sugar back. Changes like these are called physical changes. The same substance is still there.

Now burn a piece of wood. What is left is ash and smoke and warm air, and no amount of cooling will turn any of it back into wood. Something genuinely new has been made. That is a chemical change, also called a chemical reaction: a new substance forms, and it is usually very hard or impossible to reverse.

The signs to look for

You cannot see the particles rearranging, so you have to judge from clues. Any of these suggests a chemical reaction has happened:

  • A colour change that is not just mixing — a shiny nail turning orange-brown, a cut apple going brown.
  • A gas given off, seen as bubbles or fizzing when no boiling is going on.
  • Heat or light released, as in a flame, or heat absorbed, so the container feels suddenly cold.
  • A solid appearing when two clear liquids are mixed. Chemists call that solid a precipitate.
  • A new smell, as when bread turns to toast.

None of these is a guarantee on its own. Boiling water bubbles and gives off a gas, and it is still only water. That is why the real test is the honest one: is there a new substance, and can I get the old one back easily?

The one sentence to keep

A physical change rearranges the same substance. A chemical change makes a different substance. Everything else in this chapter is about how that happens, how fast, and what it produces.

2

Nothing is lost

All matter is made of tiny particles — that is the particle model. In a chemical reaction those particles are pulled apart from their old partners and joined to new ones. They are not made, and they are not destroyed. They are only rearranged.

The consequence is one of the most useful rules in all of chemistry, and it is called conservation of mass: if you weigh everything you started with, and weigh everything you finish with, the two totals are the same. Not roughly the same. The same.

People find this hard to believe, and the reason is always the same — something has escaped or arrived without being weighed.

The candle that seems to lose mass

Burn a candle and it visibly shrinks. Put it on a balance and the reading falls. Has mass disappeared? No. The wax has reacted with oxygen from the air and turned into hot gases, and those gases have drifted off the balance and into the room. They still exist and they still have mass. You are simply not weighing them any more.

The nail that seems to gain mass

Now the opposite puzzle. Leave a clean iron nail out in damp air for a few weeks and it goes rusty — and a rusty nail weighs more than the clean one did. Nothing was added by hand. What happened is that oxygen from the air joined the iron to make rust, and the oxygen brought its mass with it. The air around the nail is now very slightly lighter.

Here is the framing that resolves both cases at once. If you weighed the air as well, the total would never change. Seal the candle and its air inside a strong closed jar on a balance, let it burn, and the needle does not move. Seal the nail and its air in and let it rust, and again the needle does not move. Mass only appears to change when the container is open and part of what is reacting can walk in or out unnoticed.

How this was worked out

In the 1770s and 1780s a French chemist, Antoine Lavoisier, decided to settle arguments about burning by weighing things extremely carefully. At the time most chemists believed in phlogiston: a substance supposedly stored inside anything that burns and released into the air as it burned. If that were true, things should get lighter when they burn.

Lavoisier showed that metals get heavier when they burn or corrode. That single measurement was decisive, because phlogiston had no honest way to explain it. Something was being added from the air, not lost to it — and the thing being added was oxygen. Lavoisier gave oxygen its name and built the modern account of burning around it, but he did not discover it alone: Joseph Priestley in England and Carl Wilhelm Scheele in Sweden both have strong independent claims to isolating oxygen first, Scheele probably earliest and Priestley publishing first.

Rather earlier, around 1754 to 1756, the Scottish chemist Joseph Black had studied a gas he called "fixed air", which we now call carbon dioxide. He showed it was released by heating chalk and by breathing, and that it turns limewater milky — a test still used today, and one you will meet again in section 7.

Lavoisier was executed by guillotine in 1794, during the French Revolution, at the age of fifty. He had been a tax collector as well as a chemist, and it was the tax collecting that condemned him.

3

How chemists write it down

Everything so far has been in ordinary words, and ordinary words work. But chemists have a shorthand, and it is worth learning for the same reason musicians learn to read music: once you can read it, a page tells you in one glance what a paragraph takes a minute to say.

Symbols for elements

An element is a substance made of only one kind of atom — oxygen, iron, carbon, hydrogen. Each element has a symbol of one or two letters. The first letter is always a capital, the second always lower case, which is how you can tell where one symbol ends and the next begins.

  • H hydrogen, O oxygen, C carbon, N nitrogen
  • Na sodium, Cl chlorine, Ca calcium, Fe iron, Mg magnesium, Cu copper, Zn zinc

Some symbols look wrong until you know where they came from. Na for sodium comes from its Latin name natrium, and Fe for iron from ferrum. The symbols were fixed when Latin was still the language chemists wrote in, and nobody has ever bothered to change them.

Formulae for compounds

A compound is made when atoms of different elements join together. Its formula lists which atoms are in it and how many of each. The small number after a symbol tells you how many of that atom there are, and if there is no number it means one.

  • H2O — water. Two hydrogen atoms joined to one oxygen atom.
  • CO2 — carbon dioxide. One carbon atom, two oxygen atoms. The "di" in dioxide and the 2 are saying the same thing.
  • NaCl — sodium chloride, ordinary table salt. One sodium, one chlorine.
  • HCl — hydrochloric acid, the acid in your stomach.
  • NaOH — sodium hydroxide, a strong alkali used in oven cleaner and drain cleaner.
  • CaCO3 — calcium carbonate: chalk, limestone, marble and eggshell are all mostly this. One calcium, one carbon, three oxygens.

Two elements are worth a special note. Oxygen gas in the air is written O2 and hydrogen gas is written H2, because in both cases the atoms go about in pairs rather than singly. Nitrogen is N2 for the same reason.

Reading a formula

Capital letter starts a new element. A lower-case letter belongs to the element before it. A number belongs to the symbol in front of it. That is the whole of the code — CaCO3 is Ca, then C, then O with a 3, and nothing else it could be.

Equations

An equation shows a reaction. The starting substances, called reactants, go on the left. The substances made, called products, go on the right. An arrow between them means "turns into". The arrow points one way because, as section 1 said, chemical changes are usually hard to reverse.

Always write it in words first. Burning carbon in oxygen:

carbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide

Now the same thing in symbols:

C + O2 → CO2

Read it slowly. One carbon atom meets one pair of oxygen atoms and they join to make one carbon dioxide molecule. The word version and the symbol version say exactly the same thing; the symbol version also tells you how many of each atom is involved.

Why equations must balance

This is where section 2 comes back, and it is the reason the whole system works.

Atoms are not created in a reaction and they are not destroyed. They are only rearranged. So every atom on the left must appear on the right. If you count three oxygen atoms before, you must be able to count three oxygen atoms after. An equation where the counts match is called balanced, and an equation that does not balance is simply wrong — it is claiming atoms appeared from nowhere or vanished.

Take the burning of hydrogen to make water. In words:

hydrogen + oxygen → water

A first attempt in symbols looks reasonable:

H2 + O2 → H2O   (not balanced)

Count the oxygen. There are two oxygen atoms on the left, in the O2, but only one on the right. One oxygen atom has gone missing, which cannot happen. To fix it we put numbers in front of whole formulae — these are called coefficients, and a number in front multiplies everything in that formula.

2H2 + O2 → 2H2O   (balanced)

Now count again. Left: 2 × H2 gives four hydrogen atoms, and O2 gives two oxygen atoms. Right: 2 × H2O gives four hydrogen atoms and two oxygen atoms. Four and four, two and two. Nothing has been gained or lost, and the equation is now telling the truth.

One rule that matters: you may change the big numbers in front, never the small numbers inside a formula. Changing H2O to H2O2 would balance the count but would turn water into hydrogen peroxide, which is a different substance entirely. The formula says what a thing is. The coefficient says how much of it there is.

Four kinds of reaction

Chemists group reactions into families by what happens to the pieces. Four names cover a great deal of what you will meet, and you now have everything needed to read the examples.

  • Synthesis — two or more things join to make one. iron + sulphur → iron sulphide, written Fe + S → FeS. Already balanced: one of each atom on both sides.
  • Decomposition — one thing splits into two or more, usually with heating. Heating limestone: calcium carbonate → calcium oxide + carbon dioxide, written CaCO3 → CaO + CO2. Count the oxygen: three on the left, and one plus two on the right. Balanced.
  • Displacement — a more reactive element pushes a less reactive one out of its compound. Put zinc into copper sulphate solution and the blue fades as copper appears: zinc + copper sulphate → zinc sulphate + copper, written Zn + CuSO4 → ZnSO4 + Cu. The sulphate group has simply changed partners.
  • Neutralisation — an acid and a base cancel out to give a salt and water. This one has a whole section to itself, section 7.

These are useful labels rather than a list to memorise. Their value is that when you recognise a reaction as a decomposition, you already know roughly what to expect from it.

4

Counting atoms you cannot see

An equation tells you how many atoms react. But you cannot count out four hydrogen atoms in a laboratory — they are far too small to see, let alone pick up. What you can do is weigh things. So chemists needed a way to get from a mass on a balance to a number of atoms, and the invention that does it is called the mole.

The mole is just a counting word

You already use counting words. A pair means 2. A dozen means 12. Those exist because it is convenient to talk about eggs in twelves. Atoms are so tiny that a convenient counting word for them has to be enormous.

A mole means 602,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 of something. That is usually written 6.02 x 10^23, which is shorthand for 6.02 with the decimal point moved 23 places to the right. It is called Avogadro's number, after Amedeo Avogadro, an Italian scientist whose work in the early 1800s led to it, though the number itself was measured long after he died.

The size is hard to take in. A mole of grains of sand would bury the whole of Britain kilometres deep. A mole of water is about a tablespoonful. That gap is the point: it tells you how staggeringly small an atom must be.

Molar mass

Here is the useful trick. Every element has a number attached to it, found on the periodic table, called its relative atomic mass. And this is the part worth pausing on: that number, in grams, is the mass of one mole of that element. It is called the molar mass.

  • Hydrogen is 1, so one mole of hydrogen atoms weighs 1 g.
  • Carbon is 12, so one mole of carbon atoms weighs 12 g.
  • Oxygen is 16, so one mole of oxygen atoms weighs 16 g.
  • Calcium is 40. Sodium is 23. Chlorine is 35.5. Iron is 56.

For a compound, add up the masses of all the atoms in its formula.

Worked example 1 — the molar mass of water

Water is H2O: two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom.

Hydrogen: 2 × 1 = 2
Oxygen: 1 × 16 = 16
Total: 2 + 16 = 18

So the molar mass of water is 18 g per mole. Weigh out 18 g of water — about a tablespoon — and you are holding 6.02 x 10^23 water molecules. You have counted them without counting.

Worked example 2 — the molar mass of calcium carbonate

Calcium carbonate is CaCO3: one calcium, one carbon, three oxygens.

Calcium: 1 × 40 = 40
Carbon: 1 × 12 = 12
Oxygen: 3 × 16 = 48

Total: 40 + 12 + 48 = 100 g per mole. A round number, which is one reason chalk turns up so often in worked examples.

To go from a mass to a number of moles, divide the mass by the molar mass. To go the other way, multiply. That single pair of moves is nearly all the arithmetic there is.

Working out how much you will get

Now the pay-off, and the reason anyone bothered inventing the mole. A balanced equation says how many moles react. Molar mass turns moles into grams. Put the two together and you can predict, before doing anything, how much product a reaction will make. This is called stoichiometry, which is a forbidding word for a simple three-step routine.

Worked example 3 — heating 50 g of chalk

Question: if you heat 50 g of calcium carbonate until it decomposes, what mass of carbon dioxide is given off?

The balanced equation: CaCO3 → CaO + CO2
One mole of calcium carbonate gives one mole of carbon dioxide. A one-to-one swap, which keeps this simple.

Step 1 — turn the mass into moles.
Molar mass of CaCO3 = 100 g per mole (worked out above).
moles = 50 ÷ 100 = 0.5 moles

Step 2 — use the equation.
One mole in gives one mole out, so 0.5 moles of CaCO3 gives 0.5 moles of CO2.

Step 3 — turn moles back into a mass.
Molar mass of CO2 = 12 + (2 × 16) = 44 g per mole.
mass = 0.5 × 44 = 22 g of carbon dioxide

Check it against section 2. The 50 g of chalk became 22 g of carbon dioxide plus some calcium oxide. If the calcium oxide is 50 − 22 = 28 g, mass has been conserved. And it is: the molar mass of CaO is 40 + 16 = 56, and 0.5 × 56 = 28 g. The books balance, exactly as Lavoisier said they must.

That last check is worth more than the answer. Two entirely separate calculations agreed, and they agreed because of a rule discovered by weighing things two hundred and fifty years ago. This is what it feels like when a piece of science holds together.

5

Fast and slow

Some reactions are over before you can blink. A firework explodes. Others take years — a nail rusts, a statue slowly weathers. The speed of a reaction is called its rate, and four things change it.

Before the list, the underlying picture. For particles to react they have to meet, and they have to meet hard enough. Anything that makes meetings more frequent, or harder, speeds the reaction up. That single idea explains all four.

Temperature

Heat something and its particles move faster. They collide more often and hit harder, so the reaction speeds up. This is why a fridge keeps food fresh: cold does not stop the reactions that spoil food, it just slows them down enough that a week passes before you notice. A freezer slows them further still.

Surface area

Only the outside of a solid can be reached. A single lump has most of its material buried inside where nothing can get at it. Grind the same mass into powder and you expose far more surface, so the reaction runs much faster. This is why a fire is lit with thin kindling or paper before logs go on — a log has the fuel but almost no exposed surface, while shavings have the same wood spread over a huge area. It is also why flour or custard powder dust in the air can burn explosively, while a bag of flour will barely catch light.

Concentration

A concentrated solution is crowded — more of the reacting particles are packed into the same space, so they meet more often. Dilute it with water and the reaction slows. For gases, squeezing them into a smaller space does the same job.

Catalysts

A catalyst is a substance that speeds a reaction up without being used up by it. At the end you still have all of it, ready to go again. Catalysts matter enormously in industry, where a small amount of the right one can turn a reaction that would take a month into one that takes an hour.

Your body is full of natural catalysts called enzymes. The reactions of digestion would take far too long at body temperature to keep you alive — you cannot heat your stomach to two hundred degrees, so enzymes do the job instead. The enzymes in your saliva start breaking down starch before you have swallowed.

One idea, four levers

Particles must meet, and meet hard. Heat makes them faster. Powdering exposes more of them. Concentrating packs them closer. A catalyst makes each meeting more likely to work. Every rate question is one of those four in disguise.

6

Acids and bases

Safety first, and this one is not optional

You never identify a chemical by tasting it or by sniffing it. Not in a laboratory, not in a kitchen cupboard, not ever. Some acids and some bases cause severe burns to skin, eyes, mouth and throat within seconds, and a few are deadly. There is no situation in which tasting an unknown substance is a sensible way to find out what it is.

When this page says acids taste sour, it is describing foods that people already eat safely — lemon juice, vinegar, sour sweets. That is a fact about food, not a test you carry out. Chemists identify acids and bases with indicators, which is what the rest of this section is about.

With that settled: acids and bases are two opposite families of substances, and almost every liquid you meet belongs to one or the other or sits between them.

Acids are found in a great many foods. Lemons and limes contain citric acid, vinegar contains ethanoic acid, and it is those acids that make them taste sour. Your own stomach contains hydrochloric acid, HCl, which helps break down food and kills many of the microorganisms you swallow. Notice what every acid formula has in common: they start with H. Acids are the substances that release hydrogen into a solution, and that is what makes them behave as they do.

Bases are the opposite family. Soap, washing powder, baking soda, toothpaste and oven cleaner are all basic. Sodium hydroxide, NaOH, is a strong one. Bases tend to carry the OH pair — a hydroxide group — and that is the opposite half of the story. Soapy water feels slippery between your fingers, and that slipperiness is characteristic of bases, though oven cleaner is dangerous to touch, so this is another property to know about rather than to test.

One word needs care. A base that dissolves in water is called an alkali. So every alkali is a base, but not every base is an alkali — some bases barely dissolve at all. The two words are close relatives, not the same word.

Indicators

An indicator is a substance that changes colour depending on whether it is in an acid or a base. It answers the question that tasting must never be used to answer.

  • Litmus is the simplest. Litmus paper turns red in an acid and blue in a base. It gives a yes-or-no answer and nothing more.
  • Universal indicator is a mixture that gives a whole range of colours instead — red and orange for strong acids, through yellow and green in the middle, to blue and purple for strong alkalis. It tells you not just which family, but roughly how strong.

Red cabbage juice works as a home-made indicator for much the same reason, and turns a striking range of pinks, purples and greens.

The pH scale

Universal indicator colours are matched to a numbered scale called pH, running from 0 to 14.

  • 7 is neutral — neither acid nor base. Pure water is 7.
  • Below 7 is acidic, and the lower the number the stronger the acid.
  • Above 7 is alkaline, and the higher the number the stronger the alkali.

Now the part that surprises people, and it is the most important thing about the scale. Each step of 1 is a tenfold change. The numbers are not spaced out evenly like a ruler.

  • pH 4 is ten times more acidic than pH 5.
  • pH 3 is a hundred times more acidic than pH 5, because that is two steps: ten times ten.
  • pH 2 is a thousand times more acidic than pH 5.

So the gap between lemon juice at about pH 2 and black coffee at about pH 5 is not a small one, even though the numbers look close together. Lemon juice is roughly a thousand times more acidic.

A scale built this way, where each step multiplies rather than adds, is called a logarithmic scale. The word sounds difficult and the idea is not: a logarithmic scale counts how many times you have multiplied by ten. It is used whenever the real quantities range from the tiny to the enormous and an ordinary ruler-style scale would be useless. Earthquake magnitude and the decibel scale for loudness work in exactly the same way, for exactly the same reason.

Treat pH as a numbered scale for comparing things — but remember that a difference of two or three is much larger than it looks.

7

When acid meets base

Mix an acid and a base together and each cancels the other out. This is called neutralisation, and if you get the amounts right you end up at pH 7, with neither an acid nor a base left. What you have instead is a salt dissolved in water.

The word "salt" is broader here than the white grains on a dinner table. To a chemist, a salt is any substance made when an acid is neutralised by a base. Table salt is one member of a very large family.

In words, every neutralisation follows the same pattern:

acid + base → salt + water

Here is the clearest example, and it happens to be the one that makes table salt. Hydrochloric acid meets sodium hydroxide:

hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide → sodium chloride + water
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O

Check the balancing by counting each element. Hydrogen: one in the HCl and one in the NaOH makes two on the left; the H2O on the right has two. Chlorine: one and one. Sodium: one and one. Oxygen: one in the NaOH, one in the water. Every atom is accounted for, so no coefficients are needed — this equation balances as it stands.

You can see what happened physically. The acid's H and the base's OH found each other and became water, leaving the sodium and the chlorine to pair up as the salt. That is neutralisation in one sentence.

Where this gets used

  • Indigestion tablets. Your stomach makes acid on purpose, but too much of it hurts. Indigestion tablets contain a mild base that neutralises the excess. They are mild deliberately — a strong base would do far more harm than the acid.
  • Lime on fields. Soil that has turned too acidic grows crops badly. Farmers spread lime, which is basic, to bring the soil back towards neutral. The same is sometimes done to lakes damaged by acid rain.
  • Toothpaste. Bacteria living on your teeth turn sugar into acid, and that acid dissolves tooth enamel. Toothpaste is mildly basic and neutralises it, which is why brushing after eating helps more than brushing before.
  • Stings. A bee sting is acidic and a wasp sting is alkaline, so in principle they need opposite treatments. In practice, for anything worse than a mild sting, get proper first aid rather than reaching for the kitchen cupboard.

Acid plus carbonate: the fizz

There is a second reaction worth knowing, because you can see it happening. When an acid meets a carbonate — a family that includes chalk, limestone, marble, eggshell and baking soda — it fizzes. The bubbles are carbon dioxide gas, and you also get a salt and water.

The pattern in words is:

acid + carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide

Using hydrochloric acid on chalk, which is calcium carbonate:

hydrochloric acid + calcium carbonate → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide
2HCl + CaCO3 → CaCl2 + H2O + CO2

Why the 2 in front of the HCl? Because the calcium chloride made on the right is CaCl2, needing two chlorine atoms, and one HCl can only supply one. Two are needed. Check the rest: hydrogen, two on the left and two in the water. Calcium, one and one. Carbon, one and one. Oxygen, three on the left in the carbonate, and one in the water plus two in the carbon dioxide makes three. Balanced.

Drip vinegar onto a piece of chalk and it foams. Do it to an eggshell and the shell slowly disappears, leaving the soft membrane behind. Marble statues and limestone buildings are eaten away by acid rain over decades for exactly this reason.

The same reaction makes a cake rise. Baking soda is a carbonate. Mixed into batter with something acidic — buttermilk, yoghurt, lemon juice — it releases carbon dioxide bubbles throughout the mixture, and the heat of the oven sets the cake around them. The holes in a slice of cake are where those bubbles were.

Three gas tests, and they must not be muddled

Three gases turn up constantly in this chapter, and each has its own separate test. These get confused more often than almost anything else in chemistry, so they are worth learning as three distinct facts.

  • Hydrogen (H2) — hold a lighted splint at the mouth of the tube and you hear a squeaky pop. The pop test is for hydrogen, and for nothing else. The pop is the hydrogen burning all at once: 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O, the same balanced equation from section 3.
  • Oxygen (O2) — take a splint that has been blown out so it is only glowing, put it in, and it relights. No pop, no limewater involved.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO2) — bubble the gas through limewater and the clear liquid turns milky or cloudy. Carbon dioxide also puts out a lighted splint. It does not pop and it does not relight anything.

The limewater test is itself a neat reaction. Limewater is calcium hydroxide dissolved in water, and the carbon dioxide turns it back into solid chalk — the tiny grains of chalk are what make it look milky:

calcium hydroxide + carbon dioxide → calcium carbonate + water
Ca(OH)2 + CO2 → CaCO3 + H2O

The brackets in Ca(OH)2 mean that everything inside is doubled: two oxygen atoms and two hydrogen atoms, not two hydrogen atoms alone. This is the same test Joseph Black was using in the 1750s.

One related point, because it is easy to get backwards: carbon dioxide is denser than air, not lighter. That is why it can be poured down over a small flame to smother it, and why it collects in low places such as cellars and the bottoms of wells, where it can be dangerous.

8

Burning and breathing are the same kind of change

Combustion is the proper name for burning, and it needs three things at once: a fuel, a supply of oxygen, and enough heat to get it started. Remove any one of the three and the fire goes out. That is the whole principle behind putting fires out — a blanket cuts off oxygen, water removes heat, turning off a gas tap removes the fuel.

What burning actually produces

When a clean fuel burns completely in plenty of oxygen, the products are carbon dioxide and water, and a good deal of energy is released as heat and light. That is complete combustion, and a well-adjusted gas flame is close to it — it burns blue and leaves nothing behind.

Take the gas in a cooker hob, which is methane, CH4. In words:

methane + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water

In symbols, balanced:

CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O

Work through it. Carbon: one on the left in the CH4, one on the right in the CO2. Hydrogen: four on the left, and 2 × H2O gives four on the right. Oxygen: 2 × O2 gives four on the left, and on the right there are two in the CO2 plus two in the two waters, which is four. Everything matches, and the two coefficients are there for no other reason than to make it match.

Smoke is not the product of burning. Smoke is unburnt bits of fuel — tiny solid particles that did not get enough oxygen to burn properly — carried away in the hot gases. A smoky yellow flame is a sign of incomplete combustion, meaning oxygen was in short supply. Incomplete burning also produces carbon monoxide, a gas with no colour and no smell that is extremely poisonous, which is why fuel-burning heaters must always be properly ventilated.

The same reaction, running slowly

Now the connection that gives this section its title. Respiration is the process in every one of your cells that releases energy from food. It uses oxygen. It produces carbon dioxide and water. It releases energy.

Written out, using glucose — the sugar C6H12O6 that your cells actually burn:

glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water
C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O

The sixes look intimidating but they are doing the ordinary job. Carbon: six on the left in the glucose, six on the right in the six carbon dioxides. Hydrogen: twelve on the left, and 6 × H2O gives twelve. Oxygen: six in the glucose plus 6 × O2 giving twelve, so eighteen on the left; on the right, 6 × CO2 gives twelve and 6 × H2O gives six, which is eighteen. Balanced, as it has to be.

Read that list again and compare it with combustion. They are the same list. A fire and a living cell are doing chemically the same thing to a fuel. The difference is not what happens but how: burning does it all at once in a flame, while respiration does it slowly, in many small controlled steps, each helped along by its own enzyme, so the energy comes out gradually instead of as a burst. If your cells did it the fast way you would not survive it.

Lavoisier saw this in the 1780s and described respiration as a slow form of combustion. Given what was known at the time, that was a remarkable piece of insight, and it has held up: we would say it slightly differently today, but he was essentially right.

One last link. Burning fossil fuels — coal, oil and gas — takes carbon that was locked underground for hundreds of millions of years and releases it as carbon dioxide into the air. That is a real, measurable addition to the carbon cycle, and it is the reason a chapter about reactions ends up connected to a chapter about climate.

The point of the whole chapter

A candle flame and a cell in your finger are running the same kind of chemical change — fuel plus oxygen, giving carbon dioxide, water and energy. Once you notice that, chemistry stops being a list of separate topics and starts being one subject.

9

Questions to think about

These have no marks attached and no single correct wording. Try answering out loud, in full sentences, before opening anything.

1

A candle burning on a balance gets lighter. A nail rusting on a balance gets heavier. Explain how both of these are consistent with the rule that mass is conserved.

Show answer

In both cases the balance is only weighing part of what is involved. The candle turns into hot gases that float away into the room, so their mass leaves the balance even though it has not left the universe. The nail takes oxygen out of the air and locks it into rust, so mass arrives on the balance from somewhere that was never being weighed.

The test is to seal each one, with its air, in a closed container. Do that and the reading does not change at all, in either case.

The general lesson is worth more than the two examples: when a measurement seems to break a rule, check first whether you are measuring everything the rule is about.

2

Why is a fire lit with thin kindling rather than logs, when the logs contain far more fuel?

Show answer

Because rate depends on surface area, not on how much material is present. A log has almost all of its wood buried inside where oxygen cannot reach it, so only the thin outer skin can burn — and that skin cannot get hot enough, fast enough, to keep going on its own.

Thin kindling has the same wood spread over a much larger surface, so a great deal of it is in contact with air at once. It catches easily and produces enough heat to raise the log to the temperature at which the log's own surface will sustain a flame.

The same reasoning explains why powdered flour suspended in air can explode while a solid bag of flour will hardly burn.

3

You are handed two unlabelled beakers of clear liquid and told one is an acid and one is an alkali. Describe exactly how you would find out which is which, and say what you would refuse to do.

Show answer

Use an indicator. Dip a piece of litmus paper into each: the one that turns litmus red is the acid, the one that turns it blue is the alkali. Universal indicator would be better still, because the colour also tells you roughly how strong each one is.

What you refuse to do is taste either of them, or lean over and sniff them. Sourness and slipperiness are real properties, but they are things you know about safe foods, not tests to carry out on unknown chemicals. A clear liquid can be strong enough to burn your mouth, throat or eyes within seconds, and looking harmless is not evidence of anything.

A good general habit: an unlabelled container is treated as dangerous until an instrument, not a sense, tells you otherwise.

4

Lavoisier claimed respiration is a slow form of combustion. What evidence supports him, and in what way is the comparison not exact?

Show answer

For: both take a fuel and combine it with oxygen. Both produce carbon dioxide and water. Both release energy. Starting materials, products and outcome all match, which is a strong basis for saying they are the same kind of chemical change.

Against: burning happens in one uncontrolled rush at a very high temperature, with a flame. Respiration happens at body temperature, in a long series of small steps, each one controlled by its own enzyme, so the energy is released gradually and can be captured and used rather than lost as a burst of heat.

The fair conclusion is that Lavoisier was right about the chemistry and could not have known about the machinery. The comparison identifies the reaction correctly while saying nothing about how a cell manages to run it safely.

5

Someone tries to balance the burning of hydrogen by changing H2O into H2O2 instead of putting a 2 in front. Explain why this is not allowed.

Show answer

Because the small numbers inside a formula say what the substance is, and you are not allowed to change the substance in order to make your sums work. H2O is water. H2O2 is hydrogen peroxide, which is a bleach and an antiseptic and would burn your mouth. They are different substances with different properties.

The big number in front is different. It only says how many of that molecule are involved, so changing it changes the quantity and not the identity. That is why coefficients may be adjusted freely and subscripts may not.

The correct balance is 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O: four hydrogen atoms and two oxygen atoms on each side, and water still water.

6

Work out the mass of carbon dioxide given off when 25 g of calcium carbonate is heated until it fully decomposes. Show your steps.

Show answer

Equation: CaCO3 → CaO + CO2. One mole in, one mole of carbon dioxide out.

Step 1. Molar mass of CaCO3 = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 100 g per mole.
moles = 25 ÷ 100 = 0.25 moles.

Step 2. One to one, so 0.25 moles of CO2 is produced.

Step 3. Molar mass of CO2 = 12 + 32 = 44 g per mole.
mass = 0.25 × 44 = 11 g of carbon dioxide.

A sensible check: this is half the mass of chalk used in the worked example in section 4, and the answer is half of the 22 g found there. Halving the input halves the output, which is what a one-to-one reaction should do.

7

Why does toothpaste being mildly basic help your teeth, and why would a strongly basic toothpaste be a bad idea?

Show answer

Bacteria on your teeth turn sugar from food into acid, and that acid dissolves the hard enamel coating. A mild base neutralises the acid, bringing the mouth back towards neutral before much damage is done.

A strong base would be worse than the problem. Strong bases attack living tissue — gums, tongue, the inside of the cheek — and can cause serious burns. What is wanted is just enough base to cancel a weak acid, and no more.

That balance is the general principle behind neutralisation in medicine and farming alike: match the strength to the problem rather than reaching for the strongest thing available.

Why this is worth knowing

None of this is on your syllabus, and none of it will be tested. It is here because it joins things up. Rust on a gate, a cake rising, a match striking, an indigestion tablet fizzing and the quiet chemistry going on in your own cells all turn out to be versions of one idea: particles let go of old partners and take new ones, nothing is created and nothing is destroyed, and the speed depends on how often they meet.

Two things from this chapter are worth carrying away above the rest. The first is conservation of mass, because it is the habit of asking where everything went — and that habit catches mistakes far beyond chemistry. The second is that burning and breathing are the same reaction at different speeds, which is the sort of connection that makes a whole subject feel smaller and more manageable.

You also picked up the notation chemists really use. A formula, a balanced equation, a molar mass, a calculation that predicts how much you will get before you have made any of it — these are not decorations on top of the science, they are the tools the work is done with. Being able to read an equation is like being able to read music: nobody reads it to admire the symbols, they read it because it lets them hear what is going on. Every formula in this chapter was explained before it was used, and there was nothing behind any of them but atoms being counted. That is a door standing open, not a pile of things to memorise.

And one rule that is not about understanding at all, but about staying unharmed: chemicals are never identified by taste or by smell. Lemons are sour and vinegar is sharp, and those are facts about food you already eat. They are not permission. Some acids and bases burn skin, eyes and throat within seconds, and a clear liquid gives no warning of which one it is. Use an indicator. That is what indicators are for.
