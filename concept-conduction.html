Concept

Conduction

Conduction is the transfer of heat through a material by direct contact between neighbouring particles.

Introduced in Chapter 6: Heat and Temperature

1

What it means

Stand a metal teaspoon in a mug of hot tea and wait. Within a minute the handle, which is nowhere near the liquid, has become warm. No tea has climbed the spoon. Heat has travelled up through the metal itself, particle by particle. That is conduction.

Conduction is the transfer of heat without any transfer of material. The spoon does not move; nothing flows along it. Only energy travels, handed from each particle to the next like a message passed down a line of people who all stay in their places.

Because it relies on particles being close enough to touch and jostle, conduction works best in solids, where particles are packed tightly and fixed in position. It works poorly in liquids, badly in gases, where particles are far apart and rarely meet, and not at all in a vacuum, where there are no particles to pass anything on.

2

How it works

In a solid, particles vibrate about fixed positions. Heat one end and those particles vibrate harder. Since each particle is bonded to its neighbours, a violently vibrating particle shakes the ones beside it, which begin vibrating harder in turn, and shake theirs. The vibration spreads along the material, and energy travels with it, always from the busier region to the quieter one.

Metals do this far better than anything else, and for a second reason on top of the vibrations. As we saw with electrical conductors, a metal contains loose electrons that are free to move through the whole block rather than being tied to one atom. These electrons pick up energy at the hot end, travel bodily to the cold end, and deliver it there. Because they move rather than merely jiggle in place, this route is enormously faster. It is why metals conduct heat roughly a thousand times better than plastic, and why the same materials tend to be good conductors of both heat and electricity — one mechanism, two effects.

Materials that conduct badly are called insulators, and most of the good ones work by a trick: they trap air. Wool, feathers, fur, polystyrene, fibreglass loft insulation and a duvet are all mostly air held in small pockets. Air is a poor conductor because its particles are far apart, and dividing it into small pockets stops it circulating and carrying heat by convection instead. The fibres are just scaffolding for the air.

This explains several everyday things at once. A saucepan has a metal base to conduct heat quickly into the food and a plastic handle to conduct it slowly into your hand. Birds fluff up their feathers in cold weather to trap a thicker layer of air. Two thin jumpers keep you warmer than one thick one because of the extra air layer between them. And a wetsuit works by trapping a thin film of water that your body warms and then holds in place.

The rate of conduction also depends on the temperature difference. A hot pan on a cold worktop loses heat quickly at first and more slowly as the two approach each other, because a bigger difference drives a faster flow.

The heart of it

In conduction the energy moves but the material stays put, which is why it needs particles in contact and fails completely in a vacuum.

3

Where the idea came from

Conduction has no single discoverer. That heat spreads through solids has been obvious to anyone who has held a stick in a fire since long before there was any such thing as science. What took time was separating conduction from the other two ways heat travels, and treating it as one distinct mechanism rather than as the general behaviour of heat.

That separation happened gradually across the 1800s, alongside the collapse of caloric theory. While heat was thought to be an invisible fluid, conduction seemed straightforward — the fluid seeped through the material — and there was little reason to distinguish it from anything else.

The mathematical treatment came first, ahead of the physical explanation. Joseph Fourier, a French mathematician who had accompanied Napoleon's expedition to Egypt, published Théorie analytique de la chaleur in 1822, giving equations that describe how heat spreads through a solid over time. His equations were correct and are still used today, and remarkably they did not depend on knowing what heat actually was — Fourier wrote them while the caloric fluid was still widely believed in. It is a useful example of how a good description of behaviour can be right even when the underlying explanation is wrong.

The particle explanation followed later in the century with kinetic theory, and the special role of free electrons in metals was only understood after the electron was identified around 1897 — which is why the striking match between good heat conductors and good electrical conductors, noticed by Gustav Wiedemann and Rudolph Franz in 1853, sat unexplained as a curious empirical rule for half a century.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is imagining that something material travels along the spoon. Nothing does. If you marked a particle at the hot end it would still be at the hot end afterwards, merely vibrating harder. Conduction moves energy through matter that stays where it is — this is exactly what distinguishes it from convection, where the matter itself moves.

The second is calling an insulator a blocker of heat. Insulators only slow conduction down; given enough time, heat gets through anyway. A vacuum flask keeps coffee hot for hours, not for ever.

Commonly missed

A pupil takes a metal tray and a book from the same cupboard, which has been at a steady 22 °C all week. The tray feels distinctly colder. He concludes the metal tray is at a lower temperature. What has gone wrong?

The trap is trusting touch as a thermometer. The sensation is so vivid that the conclusion feels like an observation rather than an inference.

How to work it: Start with what you know for certain — both objects have sat in the same cupboard for a week, so both must be at 22 °C. Something else must explain the sensation. Your skin does not detect temperature; it detects how fast heat leaves it. Metal is an excellent conductor, so it draws heat from your hand quickly and your nerves report cold. The book conducts poorly and takes heat slowly, so it feels neutral. Both are at 22 °C; only the rate of conduction differs. Note also that the effect reverses above body temperature, where metal feels far hotter than wood for the same reason.

5

Connected ideas

Conduction is one of the three ways heat travels, and it is the one that requires touching particles — convection carries the material itself, and radiation needs no material at all. Whether a substance conducts heat well is closely tied to whether it conducts electricity, since both depend on free electrons, and the whole mechanism only makes sense through the particle model.
