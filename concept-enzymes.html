Concept

Enzymes

An enzyme is a substance made by a living thing that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up itself.

Introduced in Chapter 16: Photosynthesis and Respiration

1

What it means

Chew a slice of bread for a minute and it starts to taste faintly sweet. Nothing sweet was added. The starch in the bread is a long chain of sugar units joined together, and something in your saliva has been snipping that chain into short pieces you can taste. Left in a glass of warm water, that same bread would take years to break down. In your mouth it takes seconds.

The something is an enzyme. Enzymes are biological catalysts — a catalyst is anything that makes a reaction go faster while coming out of it unchanged at the end. That last part matters. An enzyme is not an ingredient that gets consumed. One enzyme molecule can work on a starch chain, let go, and start on the next. It may handle thousands of reactions a second and still be there afterwards, ready to go again.

The substance an enzyme works on is its substrate. The enzyme in your saliva is amylase, and its substrate is starch. Most enzyme names end in -ase, and the front of the name usually gives the substrate: amylase works on amylum, an old word for starch; protease works on protein; lipase works on lipids, the scientific word for fats and oils.

Enzymes are not only in digestion. Every reaction inside every cell is run by one — releasing energy in respiration, building sugar in photosynthesis, copying a cell's instructions before it divides. Without enzymes, life's chemistry would still happen, but so slowly that nothing would be alive to see it.

2

How it works

The best simple picture is a lock and key. Somewhere on the enzyme's surface is a dent of a very particular shape, called the active site. The substrate fits into that dent the way a key fits a lock. Held in place, it is broken apart, or joined to another molecule, far more easily than if it were floating about loose. Then the products fall away and the active site is empty again.

This picture explains the most important fact about enzymes: they are specific. Each enzyme works on one substrate, or one narrow group of similar substrates, and nothing else. Amylase digests starch and will not touch protein, because protein is the wrong shape to sit in amylase's active site. Your body therefore needs a whole toolkit of different enzymes, one shaped for each job, rather than one general-purpose one.

Because the whole thing depends on shape, anything that changes the enzyme's shape wrecks it. Two conditions do this, and both are examined by drawing a graph.

Temperature. Warm things up a little and the reaction goes faster, because the enzyme and substrate are moving about more quickly and bump into each other more often. So the graph of activity against temperature climbs steadily from cold. It reaches a peak — for most human enzymes at about 37 degrees Celsius, which is body temperature — and then something different happens. Past the peak the line does not level off or drift down gently. It plunges, and by roughly 60 degrees it has hit the floor. The heat has shaken the enzyme so hard that the active site has lost its shape. The key no longer fits the lock. We say the enzyme has been denatured. The curve is therefore lopsided: a gentle rise on the left, a cliff on the right.

pH. pH measures how acidic or alkaline a liquid is, on a scale where 1 is strongly acidic, 7 is neutral and 14 is strongly alkaline. Acids and alkalis pull on the enzyme's shape too. Each enzyme has an optimum pH where it works fastest, and the graph is a hump: activity rises to the optimum and falls away on both sides. The crucial point is that the hump does not sit at pH 7 for every enzyme. It sits wherever that enzyme normally does its job. Amylase works in the mouth, which is roughly neutral, so its peak is at about pH 7. Protease in your stomach works surrounded by hydrochloric acid, so its peak is at about pH 2, far over on the left of the graph. Move stomach protease into neutral liquid and it slows to almost nothing — not because neutral is harsh, but because it is the wrong condition for that particular enzyme.

Once you can read those two curve shapes, most questions on this topic answer themselves. Ask first: which enzyme, and where in the body does it live? The answer tells you where the peak belongs.

The heart of it

An enzyme works because of its shape, so it is specific to one substrate, and anything that changes that shape — too much heat, the wrong pH — stops it working.

3

Where the idea came from

People used enzymes for thousands of years before knowing they existed. Bread rose, beer fermented and cheese set, and nobody could say why. The first step came in 1833, when the French chemist Anselme Payen extracted a substance from sprouting barley that turned starch into sugar. He called it diastase. It was the first enzyme ever isolated, though the word did not yet exist.

For decades afterwards there was a genuine argument about whether such things could work outside a living body. Many chemists thought fermentation needed a living cell present — some "vital force" that could not be bottled. In 1877 the German scientist Wilhelm Kuhne coined the word enzyme, from Greek words meaning roughly "in yeast", for the parts that seemed able to act alone.

The argument was settled in 1897. Eduard Buchner ground up yeast cells, filtered out every trace of living material, and found the leftover juice still fermented sugar into alcohol. No living cell was needed. Chemistry, not a vital force, was doing the work. He was given the Nobel Prize for Chemistry in 1907 for it.

The lock-and-key picture came from Emil Fischer, who proposed it in 1894 to explain why enzymes are so fussy about their substrates. It is worth being honest about that model. It is an approximation. Later work showed the active site is not a rigid slot but flexible: it closes around the substrate rather like a hand around a ball, which is called the induced-fit model. Lock-and-key is still the right first picture, because it gets the essential idea — shape decides everything — across correctly. It is simply not the whole story.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is thinking a denatured enzyme has been killed. An enzyme was never alive. It is a substance, not an organism, so it cannot die. What has happened is that its shape has been permanently changed. Think of frying an egg: the clear part turns solid white, and no amount of cooling will turn it clear again. Denaturing works the same way — it is not reversible. Cooling a denatured enzyme back to 37 degrees does not restore it. This is exactly why the temperature graph never recovers on the right-hand side.

The second error is assuming every enzyme prefers neutral conditions, so every pH graph should peak at 7. That assumption is what these questions are built to catch, and it recurs in IMSO papers — a 2017 paper asked candidates to pick the correct activity-against-pH curve for a stomach enzyme, where the tempting symmetrical peak at neutral is the wrong answer.

Commonly missed

Four graphs show the activity of protease from the stomach plotted against pH from 1 to 14. Which graph is correct?

It is a trap because one option shows a neat symmetrical hump peaking at pH 7, and neutral feels like the natural, healthy, sensible answer. Most people pick it without asking where the enzyme actually works.

How to work it: First, name the enzyme and locate it. Protease digests protein, and protein digestion begins in the stomach. Second, ask what the stomach is like inside. It is full of hydrochloric acid, so strongly acidic — around pH 2. Third, apply the rule that an enzyme's optimum matches its normal working conditions. So the peak must sit at about pH 2, right over at the left-hand end, with activity falling away and reaching nearly zero by pH 7. The correct graph is a hump squashed against the left edge, not a symmetrical one in the middle. The same reasoning run in reverse tells you amylase from the mouth peaks near pH 7, and that a protein-rich food such as meat is the one chemically digested in the stomach.

5

Connected ideas

Enzymes are the reason digestion works at all: each region of the gut supplies a different enzyme at the pH that suits it. They also drive respiration inside every cell and the sugar-building reactions of photosynthesis, which is why both processes have a temperature at which they run fastest and fall off sharply above it. The products of digestion are small enough to pass through a cell membrane by diffusion, which is the point of breaking food down in the first place.

In one line

Enzymes are shape-specific biological catalysts that speed up reactions without being used up, and each has a temperature and a pH at which it works best — for stomach protease that pH is about 2, not 7.
