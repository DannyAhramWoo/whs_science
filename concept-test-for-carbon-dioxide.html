Concept

The Test for Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide is identified by bubbling it through limewater, which turns from clear to milky white.

Introduced in Chapter 15: Changes in Matter

1

What it means

Gases are awkward to identify. Most met at this level are colourless, so you cannot tell them apart by looking, and in a laboratory chemicals are never identified by taste or smell. Instead each gas has a test: a small procedure giving a clear, unmistakable result for that gas and not for others.

The test for carbon dioxide uses limewater, a clear colourless solution made by dissolving a small amount of calcium hydroxide in water. Calcium hydroxide barely dissolves, so limewater is very dilute and looks like plain water. It is a laboratory chemical, handled with care, kept off the skin and out of the eyes, and never drunk.

The result is easy to see. Bubble carbon dioxide through clear limewater and within seconds it turns milky white. If nothing happens and it stays clear, the gas was not carbon dioxide.

2

How it works

Why does a clear liquid go cloudy? Because a new solid is being made inside it. When carbon dioxide meets the dissolved calcium hydroxide, the two react to form a substance that does not dissolve. It cannot spread out among the water particles as a dissolved substance does, so it stays as enormous numbers of tiny specks suspended through the liquid.

Those specks are what you see. Light hits them and scatters in all directions instead of passing cleanly through, and a liquid that scatters light everywhere looks white rather than clear — the same reason milk looks white and water does not. Left standing, the specks settle into a white layer.

To carry the test out, bubble the gas through a little limewater using a delivery tube, noting the appearance before and after. Breathe out gently through a straw and it turns milky, because exhaled air contains far more carbon dioxide than the air you breathed in. The gas from an open fizzy drink does the same, and so does the gas from vinegar dripped onto crushed eggshell or chalk.

This test must be kept apart from the tests for the other two common gases. For oxygen, a glowing splint pushed into the gas relights into flame. For hydrogen, a lit splint at the mouth of the tube makes the gas burn with a squeaky pop. For carbon dioxide, a lit splint is put out, and limewater turns milky. Note carefully that the squeaky pop test is the test for hydrogen, never for carbon dioxide. Carbon dioxide does not burn and does not pop; it does the opposite, extinguishing flames, which is why it is used in some fire extinguishers.

The heart of it

Limewater goes milky because carbon dioxide makes an insoluble solid inside it, and the countless tiny suspended specks scatter light.

3

Where the idea came from

Until the middle of the 1700s, most scientists thought there was only one gas — air — which might be more or less pure but was a single kind of thing. The idea that there are many different gases had to be established, and carbon dioxide was one of the first to be pinned down.

The key work was done by the Scottish chemist Joseph Black, in research usually dated to roughly 1754 to 1756. Studying heated limestone, he showed it gave off a gas he called fixed air, so named because it had been fixed inside the solid. He demonstrated this gas differed from ordinary air: it did not support burning or life, and it turned limewater milky. He also showed it was present in air breathed out by animals. Fixed air is what we now call carbon dioxide, and the limewater test used today is essentially his.

Black's use of the balance mattered as much as the discovery. By weighing limestone before and after heating, he could account for where the missing mass had gone, making it hard to argue nothing real had been released. That approach helped overturn older ideas about air and burning.

4

What people get wrong

The single most damaging error is attaching the squeaky pop to the wrong gas. The pop belongs to hydrogen and nothing else. It is a small explosion as hydrogen burns in air, and carbon dioxide cannot do it because it does not burn at all. Its behaviour with a flame is the reverse: it puts flames out.

A second error is describing limewater as changing colour the way a dye would. Nothing is dyed; a solid forms and hangs suspended, so "turns milky" is the accurate description.

Commonly missed

A gas is collected in a test tube. A lit splint is held in the mouth of the tube and it goes out. The gas is then bubbled through limewater, which turns milky. A student concludes the gas is hydrogen because a lit splint was used. What is the gas, and how do you know?

It is a trap because the three gas tests all involve a tube and a splint, so they blur together, and "lit splint" gets attached to hydrogen whatever the result was.

How to work it: Take the observations one at a time. First, the lit splint went out. Hydrogen would have burned with a squeaky pop and oxygen would have made the splint burn more brightly, so it is neither; extinguishing a flame points to carbon dioxide. Second, the limewater turned milky, the specific test for carbon dioxide. The two results agree, so the gas is carbon dioxide. The mistake was matching the equipment rather than the result: what identifies a gas is what happened to the splint, not that a splint was used.

5

Connected ideas

Carbon dioxide is produced by combustion and respiration and used up by photosynthesis, so the limewater test lets you show those processes are happening. The milkiness is itself a chemical change producing a new insoluble substance. The gas is also a greenhouse gas and part of the carbon cycle.
