Concept

Waves

A wave is a travelling disturbance that carries energy from one place to another without carrying any matter with it.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

Drop a stone into a still pond. Rings spread outwards. Now watch a leaf floating on the surface. As each ring passes, the leaf bobs up and down — and stays exactly where it was. The rings crossed the pond. The leaf did not travel with them.

That is the whole idea. A wave carries energy, not matter. Something at the far side of the pond receives energy from the stone, but no water has moved across. Each bit of water rises and falls in place and passes the movement to its neighbour, which is why the pattern travels while the substance stays put. Every wave you meet works this way.

Sound does it with air, light does it with electric and magnetic fields, and a shaken rope does it with rope. The disturbance moves; the stuff does not.

To describe a wave precisely we need four measurements. Amplitude is how far the disturbance reaches from the undisturbed middle line — the height of a crest above the resting level, not the full distance from crest to trough. Wavelength is the distance from any point on the wave to the matching point on the next repeat, most easily measured crest to crest. Frequency is the number of complete waves passing a fixed point each second, in hertz (Hz). Period is the time for one complete wave to pass, in seconds.

2

How it works

Frequency and period are two ways of saying the same thing, and each is one divided by the other. If 5 waves pass every second the frequency is 5 Hz, and each one takes 1 divided by 5, which is 0.2 seconds. That is the period. Double the frequency and you halve the period.

Wave speed follows from wavelength and frequency together. In one second, a number of waves equal to the frequency go past, and each is one wavelength long, so speed = frequency x wavelength. A sound of 500 Hz travelling in air at 340 metres per second must have a wavelength of 340 divided by 500, which is 0.68 metres. Note what this means: in a fixed material the speed is fixed, so raising the frequency shortens the wavelength. The two run opposite to each other.

Waves come in two shapes. In a transverse wave the disturbance is at right angles to the direction of travel — shake a rope side to side and the wave runs along the rope while the rope moves across it. Light is transverse. In a longitudinal wave the disturbance is along the direction of travel — push and pull the end of a long spring and regions of squeezed coils race down its length. Sound is longitudinal: air is squeezed into compressions and stretched into rarefactions that travel forwards.

This difference explains the single biggest split between light and sound. Sound is a disturbance of particles, so it needs a material to travel through, called a medium. Remove the air and there is nothing to squeeze, so sound stops. Light is a disturbance of electric and magnetic fields, which need no material at all, so light crosses the emptiness between the Sun and the Earth while sound cannot cross a sealed vacuum jar.

For sound, the two measurements map neatly onto what you notice. Frequency gives pitch: faster vibration, higher note. Amplitude gives loudness: a bigger disturbance carries more energy, so it sounds louder. These are independent, which is why a note can be high and quiet or low and loud. For light, frequency gives colour and amplitude gives brightness.

One practical trick recurs. Echo problems are about a round trip. If a cliff is 60 m away and sound travels at 300 metres per second, the sound covers 60 m out and 60 m back — 120 m in total — so the echo returns after 120 divided by 300, which is 0.4 seconds. Forgetting to double the distance is the standard slip, and past IMSO papers have used exactly this setup.

The heart of it

A wave transfers energy without transferring matter, and its speed equals its frequency multiplied by its wavelength.

3

Where the idea came from

Whether light is a wave was argued over for more than a century, and both sides had good evidence. In the 1670s Robert Hooke in London and Christiaan Huygens in the Netherlands argued that light spreads as a wave. Huygens set out the case carefully, showing how a wave picture accounts for reflection and refraction, and published it in 1690.

Isaac Newton disagreed. He held that light was a stream of tiny particles, and he had a serious objection: waves bend around corners, as sound plainly does when you hear someone through a doorway, yet light casts sharp shadows. That was a real problem, not stubbornness, and Newton's reputation meant the particle view dominated for over a hundred years.

The deadlock broke with Thomas Young in England, who lectured on his double-slit experiment in 1802 and published it in 1807. He sent light through two narrow slits close together and found alternating bright and dark bands on a screen beyond. Particles arriving from two openings could not produce dark stripes where light plus light gave darkness, but overlapping waves cancelling each other could. Young was attacked bitterly for contradicting Newton, and it took two more decades, notably work by Augustin-Jean Fresnel in France, before the wave picture was generally accepted.

The honest ending is that Newton was not simply wrong. In the early 1900s light turned out to behave as particles in some experiments and waves in others, and both seventeenth-century camps had hold of something real.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is thinking the medium travels with the wave. It does not. Water does not cross the pond, and air does not fly from a speaker to your ear. Only the disturbance moves.

The second is measuring amplitude from crest to trough. Amplitude is measured from the resting middle line to a crest, so the full crest-to-trough height is twice the amplitude. On a drawn wave, find the centre line first.

The third is confusing wavelength with the visible width of one hump. A full wavelength is one crest and one trough — the distance to where the pattern repeats. Measuring crest to the next crest is the safe method.

Commonly missed

A wave is drawn on a grid where each square is 1 cm. The crests reach 3 squares above the centre line and the troughs 3 squares below, and successive crests are 8 squares apart. A pupil writes: amplitude 6 cm, wavelength 4 cm. Both answers are wrong. Why?

The trap is that both errors feel like reasonable readings, and each is exactly a factor of two out — one too big, one too small.

How to work it: Take amplitude first. It is measured from the undisturbed centre line to a crest, not from trough to crest. The crest is 3 squares above the line, so the amplitude is 3 cm, and the pupil doubled it by measuring the full swing. Now wavelength. It is the distance over which the pattern repeats, which is crest to next crest, so the wavelength is 8 cm. The pupil halved it, probably by measuring just the hump above the line and forgetting that a full wave needs a trough as well. The check to remember: amplitude is a half-measurement, wavelength is a whole one.

5

Connected ideas

Sound is the longitudinal wave you meet most often, and the audible range is a statement about which frequencies your ears can detect. Colour is the same idea for light, with wavelength setting the colour, and scattering, reflection and refraction are all things waves do when they meet something in their path. Underneath all of it, a wave is a way of moving energy.
