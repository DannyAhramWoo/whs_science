Concept

Work

Work is done when a force moves an object along the direction in which that force is pushing or pulling, and it measures how much energy the force transferred.

Introduced in Chapter 8: Energy and Its Changes

1

What it means

Work means something narrower in science than at home. At home, work is anything that takes effort: revising, standing guard, holding a door for a long queue. In science, none of those count unless something moved.

The definition has two conditions and both must hold. There must be a force, and the object must move. A third condition hides inside the second: the movement must be along the direction the force points.

The reason for such a fussy definition is that work is not a measure of effort at all. It measures energy transferred — it is the accounting entry recording energy moving from one thing to another by means of a force. That is why work and energy share the same unit, the joule. Doing 60 J of work on a trolley gives it 60 J of energy; the number is the same because it is one transaction described from two sides.

2

How it works

Work done equals force multiplied by the distance moved in the direction of the force. Push a box with 20 N and it slides 3 m, and you have done 60 J of work, so 60 J of energy left you and went into the box and the floor.

Once you see work as a transfer, the strange cases stop being strange. Push a brick wall as hard as you can and the wall does not move, so no energy enters it and no work is done on it. Your muscles burn chemical energy, but it heats you, not the wall.

The sideways case is the same argument. Carry a bag along a level corridor and your hand pushes up while the bag goes forward. The bag never rises or falls, so its store of potential energy is untouched, and no work is done against gravity.

A useful consequence: since work depends only on force and distance, the route can be as awkward as you like. Lifting a 10 kg suitcase 3 m straight up takes about 300 J, and carrying it up a long gentle ramp to that height also takes about 300 J against gravity, ignoring friction. The ramp saves not energy but force, spreading the same total over a longer distance. This is the principle behind ramps, levers and pulleys, and why no machine gives you more energy than you put in.

Work can also be negative. When friction acts on a sliding box, the force points backwards while the box moves forwards, so friction takes energy out rather than putting it in. That is why a box eventually stops.

The heart of it

Work is not a measure of how hard something feels; it is the exact amount of energy a force transferred, which is why no movement means no work at all.

3

Where the idea came from

The precise scientific meaning of work was fixed by the French mathematician and engineer Gaspard-Gustave Coriolis, in an 1829 book with the plain title Du calcul de l'effet des machines — On the Calculation of the Effect of Machines.

His choice of word was deliberate and practical. He took the ordinary French word travail, meaning work or labour, because he was writing for people building and running machines rather than for philosophers. Engineers already talked about how much labour a water wheel or a steam engine could replace, and Coriolis wanted a quantity they would recognise instantly.

It also created the confusion that has troubled learners ever since. Because the word was borrowed from ordinary speech, people expect it to keep its ordinary meaning, and it does not. Coriolis needed force times distance, because that is what matters when working out what a machine can deliver. Holding a heavy load still delivers nothing to the load, so it had to count as zero, however exhausting it is for a person or a horse.

The same book is where the factor of one half in the kinetic energy formula comes from. Coriolis chose it so that work done on an object would equal exactly the kinetic energy it gained. The two ideas were designed to fit together, which is why they still do.

4

What people get wrong

Almost every mistake here comes from letting the everyday meaning leak in. Learners see a person straining and conclude work is being done. The strain is real and the energy is real, but the question is always whether energy reached the object named in the question.

The cure is a two-step check, in this order. Is there a force on the object? Did the object move in the direction of that force? If either answer is no, the work is zero joules — say zero, not "a little".

Commonly missed

A waiter holds a tray of drinks steady at shoulder height and walks 10 m across a level floor. How much work does he do on the tray?

The trap is that everything in the question suggests effort: the tray is heavy, the distance is stated, and multiplying force by distance is tempting because both numbers seem to be there. The distance given is the wrong distance.

How to work it: apply the two steps. There is a force — the waiter pushes the tray upwards to support its weight. Did the tray move that way? No. It moved horizontally and its height never changed, so the distance moved in the direction of the force is 0 m. Work done is 0 J. His arm aches because his muscles convert chemical energy into heat inside his own body, but none of it reached the tray.

5

Connected ideas

Work is the bridge between forces and energy. Lifting something does work against gravity and stores it as potential energy; pushing something along does work that becomes kinetic energy. Friction does negative work, taking energy back out as heat, which is why conservation of energy is needed to track the total.

In one line

Work is force multiplied by the distance moved in the direction of that force, so it measures energy transferred rather than effort felt, and it is zero whenever nothing moves the right way.
