Concept

Electrolytes and Conducting Solutions

A liquid conducts electricity only if it contains charged particles free to move through it, which is why salt water conducts and pure water and sugar water barely do.

Introduced in Chapter 3: Electric Circuits

1

What it means

Everyone is told not to touch a switch with wet hands, which suggests water conducts electricity. Yet truly pure water is a poor conductor, near enough an insulator. Both statements are true, and the gap between them is the whole idea on this page.

An electric current is a flow of charged particles. In a metal wire these are electrons, tiny negatively charged particles drifting freely between the metal atoms. A liquid has no free electrons, so if it conducts, something else must carry the charge.

That something is an ion: an atom, or small group of atoms, that has gained or lost electrons and so carries an electric charge. Lose an electron and the particle is left positive; gain one and it becomes negative. A liquid that conducts because it contains free ions is called an electrolyte. Salt solution is one, and so are acids, including lemon juice.

2

How it works

Common salt is sodium chloride, NaCl. In the solid crystal it is already built from charged particles: positive sodium ions and negative chloride ions, locked in a rigid arrangement by their attraction to one another. Solid salt does not conduct, because although the ions are charged they cannot move.

Drop the crystal into water and the arrangement comes apart. Water particles surround each ion and pull it away, so the sodium and chloride ions separate and spread through the liquid, now charged and free to move. Connect a battery and the negative chloride ions drift towards the positive electrode while the positive sodium ions drift towards the negative one, and that two-way traffic of charge is the current.

Sugar behaves quite differently, and this is the comparison papers keep returning to. Sugar dissolves perfectly well, so the liquid looks identical to salt solution. But a sugar molecule is not built from ions. It comes apart into whole neutral molecules, and a particle with no charge cannot carry a current however freely it moves. Dissolving and conducting are separate questions.

How much salt is dissolved matters too. More dissolved salt means more ions in each spoonful of water, so more charge carriers and a larger current for the same voltage. Adding salt in steps and recording the meter reading each time gives a rising line on a graph, and experimental sections of past competition papers, including IMSO ones, have been built around plotting that relationship.

Distilled water — water boiled and its steam collected, leaving dissolved substances behind — contains almost no ions and so conducts very poorly. Tap water is not distilled water: it carries dissolved minerals from rock and pipes, all supplying ions, so it conducts noticeably better. This is why wet hands are dangerous around electricity. Not because water itself conducts well, but because tap water, sweat and seawater are salty solutions full of ions, and wet skin offers current a far easier path into the body.

Hold on to the difference in mechanism, because it is the point most often examined. A metal conducts by free electrons moving while the atoms stay put, and the metal itself is unchanged. A solution conducts by whole ions moving bodily through the liquid, so matter travels as well as charge, and the substances at the electrodes can actually change — this is why passing current through salt water gives off gas.

The heart of it

Metals conduct because electrons move through a fixed lattice; solutions conduct only if dissolved substances split into ions, so salt water and acids conduct while sugar water and distilled water do not.

3

Where the idea came from

In the 1830s Michael Faraday, working at the Royal Institution in London, studied what happened when current was passed through liquids. He found the amounts of substance released at the electrodes followed strict numerical rules, which pointed to charge being carried in definite parcels. He introduced the words still used: ion for the travelling particle, electrolyte for the conducting liquid, and anode and cathode for the two electrodes. He believed, though, that the current itself created the ions as it flowed.

That belief was overturned by Svante Arrhenius, a Swedish student who argued in his 1884 doctoral thesis that salt splits into ions the moment it dissolves, before any current is applied. His examiners found this hard to accept — free charged atoms floating loose in water seemed absurd — and passed the thesis with a grade so low it nearly ended his career. The idea proved right, and in 1903 the same work won him the Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

The picture was completed once J. J. Thomson identified the electron in 1897, since an ion could then be described as an atom with electrons missing or added.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is believing water conducts electricity by itself. What conducts is whatever is dissolved in it. Given a question about distilled or pure water, the expected answer is that it conducts very poorly, because there are almost no ions to carry charge.

The second is assuming anything which dissolves will conduct. Sugar disproves it: it dissolves completely and conducts not at all, separating into neutral molecules rather than ions. The test is never "does it dissolve" but "does it form ions when it dissolves". The third is explaining a solution's conduction with electrons, which are the carriers in metals; in solutions the carriers are ions moving as whole particles, and naming the wrong one loses the mark even when the prediction is right.

Commonly missed

Four beakers are tested with a bulb and battery: distilled water, salt solution, sugar solution and lemon juice. Which light the bulb?

The trap is sorting the beakers by whether something is dissolved in them. Three of the four contain a dissolved substance, so that sorting gives the wrong answer.

How to work it: Ask of each liquid whether it holds free ions. Distilled water has had its dissolved substances removed, so almost none — the bulb stays dark. Salt solution is full of sodium and chloride ions, so it lights. Sugar solution has dissolved molecules but no ions, so it stays dark. Lemon juice contains an acid, and acids release ions in water, so it lights. Salt solution and lemon juice light the bulb; the other two do not.

5

Connected ideas

This joins two topics that are usually taught apart: what makes a material a conductor, and what happens when a substance dissolves. A solution's ion content also affects how much it resists current.
