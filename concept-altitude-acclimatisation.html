Concept

How the Body Adjusts to Thin Air

High up, each breath brings in less oxygen, so the body first breathes faster and beats its heart harder, and then over weeks makes far more red blood cells to carry what oxygen it can get.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

Air is held near the Earth by gravity, so it is squashed thickest at the bottom. Climb a mountain and there is less air above you pressing down, so the air around you is less compressed — it is thinner, meaning the same volume contains fewer molecules of everything, including oxygen.

One point surprises people. The proportion of oxygen in air does not change with height. At sea level air is about 21 parts oxygen in every 100, and on the summit of the highest mountains it is still about 21 parts in 100. What changes is how tightly packed the air is. So a lungful at 5000 metres has the same recipe as a lungful at sea level, but roughly half as much of it. Fewer molecules in, less oxygen delivered.

The consequence is that the amount of oxygen crossing from your lungs into your blood falls. Every cell in your body needs oxygen for respiration, the reaction that releases energy from food, so a shortage is felt everywhere at once — first in the brain and the muscles, which use the most.

The body's answer comes in two speeds. There is an immediate response, within seconds and minutes, and a long-term one taking days and weeks. The slow one is called acclimatisation: gradually adjusting to conditions so that what was once punishing becomes bearable.

2

How it works

Immediately. Sensors in the arteries detect that oxygen in the blood has fallen and signal the brain. Two things follow within moments. Breathing becomes faster and deeper, moving more air through the lungs each minute so that more oxygen crosses into the blood despite each breath being poorer. And the heart beats faster, so the blood you have circulates more often, passing through the lungs and the tissues more times per minute. Neither response makes more oxygen carriers; both simply work the existing system harder. That is why they arrive quickly and also why they are not enough on their own — you cannot pant and race your heart for a month.

Over days and weeks. The real adjustment is to increase the blood's carrying capacity, and it works like this. The kidneys detect the low oxygen and release a hormone called erythropoietin, often shortened to EPO. It travels in the blood to the bone marrow, the soft tissue inside bones where blood cells are made, and instructs it to produce more red blood cells. Since every red cell is packed with haemoglobin, the iron-containing protein that carries oxygen, more red cells means more haemoglobin, and more haemoglobin means more oxygen carried per unit of blood.

This is the change that matters most and the one to remember. After several weeks at altitude, the red blood cell count rises and the haemoglobin count rises with it. The proportion of blood made up of cells — the haematocrit — climbs from something like 45 parts in 100 towards 50 or higher. Notice what does not change in any comparable way: white blood cells have nothing to do with oxygen, they fight infection; platelets deal with clotting; and plasma volume actually tends to fall slightly at first as the body loses water, which is part of why the cell proportion rises so quickly at the start. If you are asked which blood measurement rises most after weeks at altitude, the answer is the red cell count, and the reason is that only red cells carry oxygen.

Two smaller changes accompany it. The body builds more capillaries, the tiny vessels that deliver blood to tissues, shortening the distance oxygen must travel to reach a cell. And the chemistry inside red cells shifts slightly so that haemoglobin gives up its oxygen a little more readily where the tissues need it.

Altitude sickness. Go up faster than the body can adjust and you get acute mountain sickness — headache, nausea, dizziness, poor sleep, breathlessness. It usually begins above roughly 2500 metres and comes on within hours. In its severe forms, fluid gathers in the lungs or around the brain, and both can kill. The treatment that always works is descent.

This explains a practice that looks illogical from outside. Climbers on a high mountain often walk up to a higher camp and then come back down to sleep — "climb high, sleep low" — and may spend weeks covering ground they could cross in days. They are not being slow out of caution alone. They are buying the time the bone marrow needs, because making red blood cells simply cannot be hurried. Fitness does not substitute for it; a very fit climber who ascends too fast gets ill just the same.

The same physiology explains altitude training for athletes. Train for several weeks at altitude, then compete at sea level, and for a while you have extra red cells breathing rich air — more oxygen to the muscles, so more energy released aerobically before fatigue sets in. Many endurance athletes use it. The evidence that it reliably improves race results is mixed rather than settled, partly because training hard is harder when oxygen is short, which is why some athletes follow a "live high, train low" pattern instead. It is a real effect, not a guaranteed advantage.

The heart of it

The quick response to thin air is to move air and blood faster, but the lasting one is to make more red blood cells — so after weeks at altitude it is the red cell count and haemoglobin that have risen, not the white cells or platelets.

3

Where the idea came from

People knew for centuries that high places made travellers ill, and blamed it on the mountain air being poisonous. In 1590 the Jesuit missionary José de Acosta described a violent sickness crossing the high Andes and argued that the air itself was too thin and delicate to sustain a person. He had no notion of oxygen — it would not be identified for nearly two hundred years — but his account of thinness was closer to the truth than the poison explanations around him.

The physics arrived first. In 1648 Blaise Pascal in France arranged for a barometer to be carried up the Puy de Dôme, and the mercury column stood measurably lower at the summit than at the base, proving air pressure falls with height. The link to breathing came later, chiefly through the French physiologist Paul Bert, who showed in the 1870s that the harm is caused by the reduced pressure of oxygen specifically, not by low pressure in general — a distinction that made high-altitude flight and diving medicine possible.

That extra red cells appear at altitude was reported in the 1890s by the Italian physiologist Angelo Mosso and by the Swiss researcher François-Gilbert Viault, who found his own red cell count sharply raised after time in the Andes. The mechanism took much longer. The hormone erythropoietin was proposed in the early twentieth century, but it was only isolated and confirmed decades afterwards, and the detailed account of how cells sense low oxygen was worked out towards the end of the twentieth century.

There is also a genuinely uncertain part, and it is worth stating honestly. Populations who have lived at high altitude for many generations — in Tibet, in the Andes and in the Ethiopian highlands — show inherited differences from lowland populations, and researchers have identified genetic variants associated with them. What is striking is that the patterns are not the same in each place. Andean highlanders tend to show notably high haemoglobin, whereas Tibetan highlanders generally do not, breathing more instead and appearing to manage blood flow differently. This is an active area of research rather than a closed question, and the details are still debated, so it is better to say that different populations appear to have arrived at different solutions than to give a single tidy story. It does mean that inherited adaptation and the acclimatisation an individual visitor undergoes are two different things, working on completely different timescales.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking there is a smaller percentage of oxygen in mountain air. There is not — the recipe is unchanged. The air is simply less dense, so each breath contains fewer molecules of everything.

The second is expecting lung size to change. Lungs do not grow larger in an adult who moves to altitude. Breathing gets faster and deeper, which is a change in how the lungs are used, not in what they are.

The third is assuming fitness protects you. It does not. Altitude sickness depends on how fast you ascended, and a strong climber who goes up quickly can fare worse than a weaker one who took a week over it, partly because being capable of going fast is exactly what tempts people to.

Commonly missed

A person moves from the lowlands to a village above 2000 metres and stays for several weeks. Which of these rises most noticeably: platelet count, white blood cell count, red blood cell count, or plasma volume?

The trap is that all four are genuine blood measurements, and each sounds like something a stressed body might increase. White cells feel plausible because people associate altitude with feeling unwell, and platelets because the body seems to be under strain.

How to work it: Start from the problem, not the options. The problem at altitude is one thing only — not enough oxygen reaching the tissues. Now ask of each option whether it addresses that. Platelets clot blood; clotting has nothing to do with oxygen, so no. White cells fight infection; thin air is not an infection, so no. Plasma volume is the liquid part, and while a little oxygen dissolves in plasma it is a tiny amount, and in fact plasma volume tends to drop at first, so no. Red cells carry haemoglobin, and haemoglobin is what carries oxygen — so increasing red cells directly fixes the actual problem. That is the answer. The general method is worth keeping: identify what the body is short of, then pick the component whose job matches.

This angle — asking which blood index rises after weeks at altitude, with platelets, white cells and plasma volume offered as distractors — has appeared in IMSO papers, including 2019.

Connected ideas

Gas transport explains how haemoglobin picks up and releases oxygen, which is the whole point of making more red cells, and the page on what blood is made of gives the normal proportions that altitude shifts. Breathing covers the faster, deeper ventilation, and circulation the faster heartbeat. Respiration is why the oxygen is wanted at all, and diffusion is how it crosses into the blood. The inherited differences between highland populations connect to genetics and to adaptation, while acclimatisation itself is a slow relative of the homeostasis behind temperature control.

In one line

High air is thinner but not poorer in oxygen by proportion, so each breath delivers less — the body responds at once by breathing faster and deeper and beating its heart harder, and over weeks by making more red blood cells so the haemoglobin count rises, which is why climbers ascend slowly and why acclimatisation cannot be rushed.
