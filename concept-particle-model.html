Concept

The Particle Model

The particle model is the idea that all matter is made of tiny particles which are always moving, and that the behaviour of materials comes from how those particles are arranged and how fast they move.

Introduced in Chapter 14: Properties of Matter

1

What it means

Water looks continuous. Pour it, and it seems to be smooth all the way through, with no grains or gaps. You could keep dividing a drop in half and imagine going on forever. The particle model says you could not. Keep dividing and eventually you reach single particles of water, and dividing one of those would leave you with something that is no longer water at all.

The particles are far too small to see. A single drop of water contains more of them than there are grains of sand on a large beach. This is why the model took so long to be accepted: nobody could point to one.

The model has two claims, and the second matters as much as the first. Matter is made of particles — and those particles are never still. In a solid they vibrate on the spot. In a liquid they slide past one another. In a gas they fly about at speeds of hundreds of metres per second. Even a stone sitting motionless on a table has particles jiggling inside it. Motion is the normal state of matter, not a special condition.

It is called a model rather than a fact because it is a mental picture used to explain and predict. It is an extremely good model — it explains dissolving, melting, pressure, diffusion and much else — but the real particles are stranger than the little spheres we draw.

2

How it works

The power of the model is that a small number of assumptions explain a large number of unrelated-looking observations. Take three.

First, why can you compress a gas but not a liquid? Push a syringe with the end blocked and full of air, and the plunger moves a long way. Fill it with water and the plunger barely moves at all. The model says gas particles are far apart with empty space between them, so squeezing simply pushes them closer. In a liquid the particles are already touching, so there is nothing left to squeeze out.

Second, why does a smell spread across a room? Because gas particles are constantly moving in all directions and collide with air particles, bouncing off in new directions as they go. They gradually wander from where they are concentrated to where they are not. This also explains why the spreading is slow despite the particles being fast: they travel a long, zigzagging route.

Third, why does a sealed can of gas burst when heated? Heating makes particles move faster. Faster particles hit the walls harder and more often. Pressure is the total effect of all those collisions, so the pressure rises until the can gives way.

The model also gives dissolving a sensible mechanism. Sugar does not vanish into water. Its particles are separated from one another by water particles and spread out among them, ending up in the gaps between water particles. This predicts something you can check: the mass afterwards equals the mass of the sugar plus the mass of the water, because no particles were destroyed. It also predicts that the volume increase is less than you might expect, because some sugar particles fit into gaps that were already there.

Notice what is happening across all these examples. We cannot see the particles, so we test the model by whether its predictions come true. When a model keeps predicting correctly across situations it was not designed for, that is the strongest evidence we can have for something invisible.

The heart of it

Everything is made of tiny particles in constant motion, and how a material behaves depends on how closely those particles are packed and how fast they are moving.

3

Where the idea came from

Around 400 BC the Greek philosopher Democritus argued that matter could not be divided endlessly, and that you would eventually reach indivisible pieces. He called them atomos, meaning "uncuttable". It sounds like a brilliant anticipation, and in a sense it was, but it is important to be honest about what it was not. Democritus had no evidence whatsoever. It was philosophy — an argument from reasoning about what division must mean — not an experimental result. Other thinkers argued the opposite with equal confidence, and Aristotle's view that matter was continuous was more influential for the next two thousand years. Democritus turned out to be roughly right, but he was not right for reasons anyone could check.

Real evidence took until the nineteenth century. Around 1803 John Dalton, in Manchester, noticed that when elements combine to form compounds they do so in fixed, simple ratios by mass. That is very hard to explain if matter is continuous, and very easy to explain if each element is made of particles of a characteristic mass which combine in whole numbers. Dalton's evidence was chemical bookkeeping rather than any sighting of a particle, and plenty of chemists used his ratios as a useful rule while doubting the particles were real.

The clinching evidence came from an unlikely direction. In 1827 the Scottish botanist Robert Brown, looking through a microscope at pollen grains suspended in water, saw that tiny particles released from them jiggled about ceaselessly in random directions. He tested whether it was something to do with life by trying particles of ground-up rock and dust, and found they did it too. He described the motion carefully and could not explain it. It sat unexplained for nearly eighty years.

In 1905 Albert Einstein worked out the explanation mathematically. The jiggling particles were being bombarded from all sides by invisible water particles, and because the bombardment is uneven from moment to moment the visible particle gets knocked one way and then another. Crucially, Einstein's treatment made numerical predictions about how far the particles should wander over time. Jean Perrin tested these in the following years, the numbers matched, and the remaining serious doubt about the existence of atoms collapsed. It had taken about 2,300 years to get from a guess to a demonstration.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest mistake is giving particles the properties of the material they make up. Particles of ice are not cold, particles of copper are not orange, particles of a gas do not expand when heated. Temperature, colour and expansion are properties of large collections of particles. A single particle does not have a temperature; temperature is the average energy of motion of many.

A closely related error is thinking that when something expands, its particles get bigger. They do not. They move faster and so spread out further apart, which makes the whole object bigger while each particle stays exactly the same size.

The third error is imagining something in the gaps between particles in a gas. There is nothing there — not air, not anything. Air is the particles.

Commonly missed

A sealed flask contains air. It is warmed gently. Explain what happens to the particles, and state what happens to the mass of the gas inside.

The trap is answering that the particles expand or that the gas becomes heavier as it gets hotter.

How to work it: Warming gives the particles more energy, so they move faster. They therefore hit the walls harder and more often, so the pressure rises. Because the flask is sealed and rigid, the volume cannot change, so the particles cannot actually spread out. The particles themselves do not change size and none are created or destroyed — so the mass stays exactly the same. Faster is not bigger, and hotter is not heavier.

5

Connected ideas

The particle model is the foundation the rest of the chemistry sits on. States of matter are three different particle arrangements; changes of state are rearrangements; diffusion is particle motion made visible; dissolving is particles mixing at the smallest scale. It also underpins physics ideas such as temperature, which is the average energy of particle motion, and density, which is about how tightly particles are packed.

In one line

All matter is built from tiny particles that never stop moving, and almost everything a material does can be explained by how those particles are spaced and how fast they travel.
