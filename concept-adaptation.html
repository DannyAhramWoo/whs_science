Concept

Adaptation

An adaptation is a feature of a living thing that helps it survive and breed where it lives.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

A polar bear has thick fur and a layer of fat. A cactus has spines instead of leaves and a fat green stem. A duck has webbed feet. Each feature fits the place: fur for cold, water storage for drought, paddles for swimming. That fit is adaptation.

Adaptations come in three sorts, and separating them makes questions easier. Structural adaptations are parts of the body — a beak shape, a thick coat, a long root. Behavioural ones are things the animal does — migrating before winter, hunting at night, huddling for warmth. Physiological ones are chemical processes inside the body, such as producing concentrated urine so little water is wasted.

There is one thing an adaptation is not, and this is the point most often missed. An animal does not grow an adaptation because it needs one; a polar bear born in a warm zoo does not shed its fur to suit. Adaptations belong to the species, built up across generations, not to the individual reacting to its own life.

2

How it works

The mechanism is natural selection, which runs on three plain facts. First, individuals vary: no two rabbits are identical, some having longer legs, thicker fur or better eyesight. Second, more offspring are produced than can survive, and most die before breeding. Third, some of that variation is inherited.

Put those together and something follows automatically. If more are born than can survive, and they differ, which ones survive will not be random. A rabbit that runs faster is more likely to escape a fox and breed, and its offspring inherit that tendency, so fast runners make up a larger share of the next generation. Repeat for hundreds of generations and the population becomes fast, without any rabbit having tried to change.

Notice what does the work. Nothing designs the rabbit. The environment removes some individuals and not others, and because survivors pass on what they had, the population drifts towards whatever helped them survive — a filter, applied again and again.

This also explains why adaptations are never perfect. A filter works only with the variation it is handed: if no rabbit is born with better night vision, selection cannot produce it, however useful. Adaptations are also compromises — thick fur that keeps a bear alive in winter is a burden in a warm spell. An adaptation is good enough to survive with, not the best imaginable.

The heart of it

Adaptation happens to populations across generations, not to individuals during their lives, because the mechanism is which individuals survive to breed, not any effort they make.

3

Where the idea came from

The most famous example in biology is a group of birds on the Galápagos Islands, usually called Darwin's finches. The standard story is that Charles Darwin visited in 1835, noticed birds on different islands had beaks suited to different foods, and worked out natural selection on the spot. That is tidier than the history.

Darwin did not label his specimens carefully. He did not record which island many birds came from, and later had to reconstruct this from other collections. The beaks became famous through much later work, particularly by the British ornithologist David Lack in the 1940s, who studied the birds properly and set out how beak shapes matched different foods. The neat version is therefore partly retrospective — not a criticism of Darwin so much as a reminder that discoveries rarely happen in a single flash, and that we cannot be sure exactly what he thought at the time.

The finches then did better than confirm an old story: they gave evidence for adaptation fast enough to watch. From the 1970s, Peter and Rosemary Grant studied Daphne Major, a small island in the same group, measuring individual birds year after year. When drought killed the small soft seeds and left large hard ones, birds with deeper, stronger beaks survived and bred, and average beak depth measurably increased within a few years. Then wetter conditions returned and the change partly reversed. Adaptation had been treated as too slow to observe; the Grants measured it in a human lifetime.

4

What people get wrong

The big error is talking as though animals adapt on purpose. "The giraffe stretched its neck to reach the leaves, so its babies had longer necks" sounds reasonable and is wrong, because stretching your neck does not change what you pass on. What happened is that giraffes with longer necks reached more food, survived better, and left more offspring who inherited longer necks.

The second error is treating every feature as an adaptation. Some are side effects of others, or leftovers from an ancestor that no longer do anything useful. Asking "what is this for" always produces an answer, but not always a true one, and without evidence it is better to say we cannot be sure.

Commonly missed

A type of bacteria is treated with a medicine. At first almost all die, but after several months the medicine no longer works on them at all. Explain why, using the idea of adaptation.

It is a trap because the obvious answer — that the bacteria got used to the medicine, or learned to resist it — is the wrong mechanism.

How to work it: The bacteria varied from the start, and by chance a few already had a feature letting them survive. The medicine killed the others, leaving those few with space and food and no competition, so they multiplied. Because the resistance was inherited, their offspring had it too. Over months the whole population descended from those survivors, so the medicine works on none of them. No individual bacterium changed; the population was filtered.

5

Connected ideas

Adaptation decides which populations survive in a habitat, and is sharpest where predators and prey push each other, since every improvement in hunting selects for better escaping. It also decides where an organism feeds within a food chain. Classification groups organisms by shared features, though similar adaptations in unrelated animals can mislead you.

In one line

An adaptation is a feature that helps a living thing survive where it lives, and appears because individuals vary, more are born than survive, and those that survive pass their features on.
