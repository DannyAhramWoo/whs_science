Concept

Adaptation

An adaptation is a feature of a living thing that helps it survive and breed in the particular place it lives.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

A polar bear has thick fur and a layer of fat. A cactus has spines instead of leaves and a fat green stem. A duck has webbed feet. In each case the feature fits the place: fur for cold, water storage for drought, paddles for swimming. That fit is what we mean by adaptation.

Adaptations come in three sorts, and separating them makes questions much easier to answer. Structural adaptations are parts of the body — a beak shape, a thick coat, a long root. Behavioural adaptations are things the animal does — migrating before winter, hunting at night, huddling together for warmth. Physiological adaptations are chemical processes inside the body — producing very concentrated urine so that little water is wasted, or making a venom.

There is one thing an adaptation is not, and this is the point most often missed. An animal does not grow an adaptation because it needs one. A polar bear born in a warm zoo does not shed its thick fur to suit. Adaptations belong to the species, built up across many generations, not to the individual reacting to its own life.

2

How it works

The mechanism is natural selection, and it runs on three plain facts.

First, individuals vary. No two rabbits in a wood are identical: some have slightly longer legs, slightly thicker fur, slightly better eyesight. Second, more offspring are produced than can survive. A pair of rabbits does not produce two rabbits; it produces many, and most of them die before breeding. Third, some of that variation is inherited — passed from parent to offspring.

Put those three together and something follows automatically. If more are born than can survive, and they differ, then which ones survive will not be random. A rabbit that runs slightly faster is slightly more likely to escape a fox and live long enough to breed. Its offspring inherit the tendency to run fast. In the next generation, fast runners make up a slightly larger share of the population. Repeat this for hundreds of generations and the whole population becomes fast, without any individual rabbit ever having tried to change.

Notice what does the work here. Nothing designs the rabbit. The environment simply removes some individuals and not others, and because the survivors pass on what they had, the population drifts towards whatever helped them survive. It is a filter, applied again and again.

This also explains something puzzling: why adaptations are never perfect. A filter can only work with the variation it is handed. If no rabbit in the wood happens to be born with better night vision, natural selection cannot produce it, no matter how useful it would be. Adaptations are also compromises. Thick fur that keeps a bear alive in winter is a burden in a warm spell. An adaptation is good enough to survive with, not the best imaginable.

Consider camouflage as a worked example. Suppose a moth population resting on pale bark contains a few darker individuals. Birds spot the dark ones more easily, so fewer of them survive to breed, and the population stays pale. Now suppose soot darkens the bark. The same birds now spot the pale ones more easily. Nothing about the moths changed; the filter reversed, and within some generations the population shifts dark. The moths did not adapt. The population was reshaped by which moths got eaten.

The heart of it

Adaptation happens to populations across generations, not to individuals during their lives, because the mechanism is which individuals survive to breed — not any effort they make.

3

Where the idea came from

The most famous example in the whole of biology is a group of birds on the Galápagos Islands, usually called Darwin's finches. The standard story is that Charles Darwin visited in 1835, noticed that the birds on different islands had beaks suited to different foods, and worked out natural selection on the spot. That story is tidier than the history.

Darwin did not label his specimens carefully at the time. He did not record which island many of the birds came from, and later had to try to reconstruct it from other people's collections. The beaks became famous largely through much later work, particularly by the British ornithologist David Lack in the 1940s, who studied the birds properly and set out how different beak shapes matched different foods. The neat version of the story is therefore partly retrospective — assembled afterwards, and made to look more immediate than it was. This is not a criticism of Darwin so much as a reminder that discoveries rarely happen in a single flash, and that we cannot be sure exactly what he thought at the time.

The finches then did something better than confirming an old story: they gave evidence for adaptation happening fast enough to watch. From the 1970s, Peter and Rosemary Grant ran a long-term study on Daphne Major, a small island in the same group, measuring the beaks of individual birds year after year. When drought killed off the small soft seeds and left mostly large hard ones, the birds with deeper, stronger beaks were the ones that survived and bred, and the average beak depth in the population measurably increased within a few years. Then wetter conditions returned and the change partly reversed. Adaptation had been treated as something too slow to observe. The Grants showed it could be measured in a human lifetime.

4

What people get wrong

The big error is talking as though animals adapt on purpose. Sentences like "the giraffe stretched its neck to reach the leaves, so its babies had longer necks" sound reasonable and are wrong. Stretching your own neck does not change what you pass on. What happened is that giraffes with longer necks reached more food, survived better, and left more offspring who inherited longer necks.

The second error is treating every feature as an adaptation. Some features are side effects of others, or leftovers from an ancestor that no longer do anything useful. Asking "what is this for" always produces an answer, but the answer is not always true, and where there is no evidence it is better to say we cannot be sure.

Commonly missed

A type of bacteria is treated with a medicine. At first almost all of them die, but after several months the medicine no longer works on that bacteria at all. Explain why, using the idea of adaptation.

It is a trap because the obvious answer — that the bacteria got used to the medicine, or learned to resist it — is exactly the wrong mechanism.

How to work it: The bacteria varied from the start, and by chance a very small number already had a feature that let them survive the medicine. When the medicine was applied, it killed the others but not those few. That left the survivors with plenty of space and food and no competition, so they multiplied. Because the resistance was inherited, their offspring had it too. Over months the whole population descended from those few survivors, so the medicine now works on none of them. No individual bacterium changed; the population was filtered.

5

Connected ideas

Adaptation is what decides which populations can survive in a habitat, and it is sharpest where predators and prey push each other — every improvement in hunting selects for better escaping. Adaptations decide where an organism can feed within a food chain, and grouping organisms by their shared features is what classification does, though similar-looking adaptations can appear in unrelated animals and mislead you.

In one line

An adaptation is a feature that helps a living thing survive where it lives, and it appears because individuals vary, more are born than survive, and the ones that happen to survive pass their features on.
