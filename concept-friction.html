Concept

Friction

Friction is the force that appears wherever two touching surfaces try to slide across each other, and it always acts to oppose that sliding.

Introduced in Chapter 1: Forces and Friction

1

What it means

Drag your hand across a table and you feel something resisting. Do it on ice and the resistance almost vanishes. That resistance is friction, and the difference tells you the essential fact: friction depends on the two surfaces involved, not on one alone.

Two words are worth pinning down. To oppose means to act in the opposite direction: friction never helps something slide, it always points against the sliding. And a surface here means the boundary where two materials meet — rubber against tarmac, a shoe against wet tiles. Change either material and you change the friction.

Two situations are worth telling apart. When something is already sliding, friction acts backwards and slows it. When it is not sliding but you are trying to make it, friction acts backwards on your push and holds it still. The second kind is often stronger, which is why a heavy box takes a big shove to get going and then feels easier once moving.

2

How it works

Why should touching surfaces resist at all? No surface is truly smooth. Even a polished metal block is, under a microscope, a landscape of ridges and hollows. Press two such landscapes together and they touch only at the tips of their high points, so the real contact area is a tiny fraction of what your eye sees. At those points the materials are squeezed hard together and stick slightly. To slide, those junctions must be broken and remade over and over, and that constant breaking is what you feel as friction.

This picture explains several things at once. Press harder and the ridges flatten, more tips touch, more junctions form, and friction rises — which is why a loaded box is harder to slide than an empty one. It also explains heat. Every junction that snaps sets nearby atoms jiggling, and jiggling atoms are exactly what heat is. Rub your palms together and warmth arrives within a second, because your movement is converted into that jiggling.

Wear follows from the same picture. If junctions are torn apart continuously, small pieces of material go with them, which is why shoe soles thin and tyres go bald. Oil puts a thin layer of liquid between the surfaces so the high points never quite meet.

Finally, friction is not one force with one verdict. Under a shoe it is why you can walk: your foot pushes backwards on the ground, friction stops it slipping, and the ground pushes you forwards. Under a heavy crate it is a nuisance wasting your effort as heat. The same force, judged by what you were trying to do.

The heart of it

Friction exists because no surface is really smooth — it is the cost of tearing apart the countless tiny points where two rough surfaces are stuck together.

3

Where the idea came from

Friction has one of the odder histories in physics, because the man who worked it out first got no credit for nearly five hundred years.

Between roughly 1493 and 1500, Leonardo da Vinci experimented with blocks, sledges and pulleys, and wrote the basic rules of friction in his notebooks: the resisting force depends on the load pressing the surfaces together, and not on the apparent area of contact. Those are essentially the rules still taught today. But his notebooks were private, written in mirror-reversed handwriting, and unpublished for centuries. The knowledge simply did not circulate.

So in 1699 the French engineer Guillaume Amontons, working independently with no access to Leonardo's pages, discovered the same rules over again. They have been known as Amontons' laws ever since. In 1781 Charles-Augustin de Coulomb tested them across many materials and confirmed they held, adding that friction is a little lower once sliding has begun.

Leonardo's priority was only established firmly by modern scholarship. In 2016 Professor Ian Hutchings of the University of Cambridge worked through the surviving notebooks and traced exactly when and how Leonardo had done the work. It is a good reminder that being first is not the same as being remembered, and that publishing is part of doing science, not an afterthought.

4

What people get wrong

Two errors dominate. The first is treating friction as a property of one object: "sandpaper has high friction". Friction is always a property of a pair. Sandpaper on rubber and sandpaper on ice behave completely differently, so the question is never how rough one thing is but how the two behave together.

The second is assuming friction is bad and should always be reduced. Without friction you could not walk, hold a pencil, tie a knot, or stop a car. Any statement containing "friction is always unhelpful" is safely wrong.

A subtler one: friction acts on stationary objects too. A book on a sloping desk is held there by friction. Nothing is sliding, but friction is doing real work in keeping it that way.

Commonly missed

A block is pulled across a bench. It is then turned so it rests on a face with half the area, and pulled again in the same way. What happens to the friction?

The trap is that "half the area" looks as though it must mean half the friction. Surfaces feel as though they grip in proportion to how much is touching, and the question is designed to make you reach for that.

How to work it: friction depends on how hard the surfaces are pressed together, and the block weighs the same in both positions, so the pressing force is unchanged. Real contact happens only at microscopic high points, and halving the area concentrates the same weight onto fewer points, pressing each one harder. The friction stays about the same.

5

Connected ideas

Friction is one particular kind of force, so it obeys everything true of forces generally. It makes balanced and unbalanced forces make sense on a real floor, and it is why inertia is so hard to notice in everyday life — objects do stop, but only because friction stops them. Because friction turns movement into warmth, it is also a clear everyday link between motion and heat, and a standard example of energy changing form.

In one line

Friction is the force that opposes sliding wherever two surfaces touch, it comes from the countless microscopic points where those surfaces stick together, and it is as often useful as it is a nuisance.
