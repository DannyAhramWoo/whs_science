Concept

Inside a Leaf

A leaf is a thin, flat, layered food factory, and every one of its layers is placed where it is for a reason.

Introduced in Chapter 16: Photosynthesis and Respiration

1

What it means

Hold a leaf up to a window. It is thin enough for light to glow through, broad enough to catch a lot of it, and the green is not evenly spread — the top surface is a deeper, glossier green than the underside. That is not decoration. Slice the leaf across and look at the cut edge under a microscope, and you find it is built in layers, like a sandwich, with the green material concentrated near the top.

A cross-section means a slice cut straight through something so you can see its internal layers. Almost every question about leaf structure is really about a cross-section drawing, with the layers labelled 1, 2, 3, 4 and so on from top to bottom. What is tested is not whether you recognise a word but whether you can walk down through the layers in order and say what each one does.

So learn the leaf as a journey rather than a list. Start at the top, where light arrives, and travel downwards to where air comes in. Every layer you pass exists to solve one problem: catch light, let gases in, keep water in, or move materials about.

2

How it works

Here is the tour, top to bottom.

1. The waxy cuticle. The outermost coat, a thin transparent layer of wax the leaf secretes over its upper surface. Wax is waterproof, so it stops the leaf drying out in sun and wind. It is transparent because it has to be — anything opaque on top would block the light the leaf is trying to collect. It is not made of cells and contains no chloroplasts.

2. The upper epidermis. A single layer of flat, tightly packed, see-through cells forming the leaf's skin, just under the cuticle. Its job is protection: a continuous barrier keeping out dust and disease. These cells have almost no chloroplasts — the tiny green structures where photosynthesis happens — and that is deliberate. Chloroplasts here would absorb light before it reached the layer that does the real work below.

3. The palisade mesophyll. Now the factory floor. Mesophyll just means "middle leaf". These cells are tall and column-shaped, standing upright side by side like books on a shelf, and they are crammed with chloroplasts — more than any other cells in the plant. Their position is the whole point: they are near the top, so they meet sunlight first, while the transparent layers above let it straight through. Their tall shape means light passing down through one cell travels through a long column of chloroplasts, so little is wasted. Most of the leaf's photosynthesis happens here.

4. The spongy mesophyll. Below the palisade layer, rounder cells packed loosely with large air spaces between them. This layer is where gases move. Carbon dioxide entering from underneath spreads through the air spaces to reach every cell, and the oxygen made by photosynthesis diffuses out the same way. The air spaces also give a huge moist surface for gases to dissolve into. These cells do contain chloroplasts and do photosynthesise, but fewer, since less light reaches this depth.

5. The lower epidermis. The skin on the underside, again a protective layer of flat cells — but this one is pierced with holes.

6. Stomata and guard cells. A stoma (plural stomata) is a small pore through the lower epidermis. Around each one sit two curved guard cells, and unlike the rest of the epidermis these do have chloroplasts. By swelling with water or losing it, the guard cells bend and open the pore or straighten and close it, so the plant controls its own gas exchange. Stomata are mostly on the underside, and the reason is water. An open pore lets water vapour escape as well as letting carbon dioxide in. The underside is shaded and cooler, so less water is lost there than through a sunlit upper surface. The plant gets its gases while giving away as little water as it can.

7. The veins. Running through the middle of the leaf, and visible as the pattern you can feel on the back of a leaf, are bundles of two kinds of tube. Xylem brings water and dissolved minerals up from the roots to the photosynthesising cells. Phloem carries the dissolved sugar that has just been made out of the leaf and away to wherever the plant needs it. Veins also stiffen the leaf and hold it flat and open to the light.

The heart of it

Chloroplasts are concentrated in the palisade layer near the top because that is where light arrives first, and the layers above it are transparent for exactly that reason.

3

Where the idea came from

None of this could be known before the microscope. In 1665 Robert Hooke published drawings of thin slices of cork and named the little boxes he saw cells, after the small rooms in a monastery. He was looking at empty walls of dead cork, so he did not realise living cells hold anything inside. At around the same time Nehemiah Grew in England and Marcello Malpighi in Italy were separately cutting up plants and drawing what they found, and between them worked out that plant tissue is layered and that leaves contain distinct kinds of cell. Grew described leaf pores in the 1680s, though what they were for stayed guesswork for a long while.

The green grains inside the cells were seen much later, in the 1800s, once microscopes improved. Hugo von Mohl described them in the 1830s and 1840s and pointed out that the green material sits in separate bodies rather than filling the cell. The name chloroplast came later still.

Working out what stomata were for took experiment, not just looking. Early guesses included sweating and taking in food. It became clear only once two findings were combined: leaves lose enormous amounts of water as vapour, and they take in carbon dioxide from the air. The pores had to be the way in and out for both, which is why they must be openable — a permanently open leaf would dry out and a permanently shut one would starve. Smearing petroleum jelly over one leaf surface and comparing water loss with an untreated leaf shows most pores are underneath.

4

What people get wrong

The big mistake with a numbered diagram is answering from the picture rather than from the logic. People see green in the drawing, or see a layer near the top, and guess. The reliable method is to identify the layer by its position and shape first, then state its job, then answer the question asked.

The specific confusion these questions exploit, and one that recurs in IMSO papers such as a 2019 leaf cross-section question, is which layers contain chloroplasts. The answer is the palisade mesophyll and the spongy mesophyll — and, in a small way, the guard cells. Not the cuticle, which is wax and not even made of cells, and not the ordinary epidermis cells, which are transparent on purpose.

Commonly missed

A leaf cross-section is labelled 1 to 6 from top to bottom. Which labelled part carries dissolved sugar away from the leaf to a storage organ such as a tuber?

It is a trap for two reasons. People pick the vein without saying which tube inside it, and people who do choose a tube often pick xylem, because xylem is the one they remember by name.

How to work it: Read the question for two clues: the material moving, and the direction. The material is dissolved sugar — food the leaf has just made. The direction is away from the leaf, downwards to a storage organ. Xylem is the water pipe and it only ever carries water and minerals upwards from the roots, so it cannot be the answer. Phloem is the food pipe, it carries dissolved sugar, and it can travel down as well as up. So the answer is the phloem inside the vein. Say phloem, not just vein — naming the tissue is what earns the mark.

5

Connected ideas

A leaf's structure only makes sense once you know what it is for, which is photosynthesis, powered by the chlorophyll inside its chloroplasts. The gases move through the air spaces by diffusion, and the two kinds of vein tube are the plant's transport system. The shape of a leaf is also a good example of adaptation — thin and broad to catch light, waxy on top to hold water.

In one line

A leaf is layered from a waxy transparent top through chloroplast-packed palisade cells and airy spongy cells to a pierced underside, so that light reaches the green cells and gases reach them too, while as little water as possible is lost.
