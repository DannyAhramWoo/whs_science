Study skills

How Marks Are Actually Given

Most study material shows you correct answers. This page shows you something more useful: why a completely true answer can score zero. Everything here comes from the official mark schemes markers were given — including the notes they wrote in the margins.

Read this first

There is no new science on this page. Every fact used here you already know.

What changes is what you do with it. A student who understands the gap between an answer that is true and an answer that earns the mark will score noticeably better without learning a single new topic.

1

The same answer, three different scores

Start with the clearest example in the whole set of mark schemes. It comes from IMSO 2017 Theory 2, from a question asking why a plant in dim light gives out more carbon dioxide than it takes in.

Three students wrote three answers. Here is what the marker actually wrote beside them.

What the student wroteMarker's noteScore
More carbon dioxide comes out than goes in"(No mention of either process)"0
The plant gives out more than it uses in photosynthesis"(Only mention of one process)"0.5
Names both processes and compares their ratesfull credit1

Now look hard at the first row. That answer is true. More carbon dioxide really does come out than goes in. It is not wrong science. It is not a misunderstanding. A scientist would nod at it.

It scored nothing.

It scored nothing because the question asked why, and the student described the result instead. Two processes were competing inside that plant — photosynthesis taking carbon dioxide in, respiration giving it out — and the answer named neither of them. Naming one gets you half. Naming both, and saying which one is winning, gets you the mark.

The idea this whole page rests on

Marks are not given for being right. They are given for doing the specific thing the question asked. Those are two different targets, and most lost marks are the difference between them.

Once you see this, a lot of frustrating results start to make sense. You were not being marked harshly. You were answering a slightly different question from the one on the paper.

2

What each command word demands

The first word of a question is an instruction. It tells you the shape of the answer that will be paid for. Get the shape wrong and the content barely matters.

State, name, write down

One precise term. Nothing more is credited, and a near-miss earns nothing at all.

In one question about the structure that increases absorbing surface in the intestine, the correct answer was villi. A student wrote "Epithelial cells" and the marker wrote: "Epithelial cells [0]". That is a real biology word, from the right organ, at the wrong level of detail. Villi are made of epithelial cells — but the villi are the named structure doing the job.

Weak: "the lining of the intestine". Fixed: "villi".

There is good news attached. Word forms are forgiven. One scheme reads "Sublimation (accept sublime, subliming) [1m]". Your grammar is not being tested. Your choice of concept is.

Explain

A causal chain with at least two links, ending at the thing the question named. One link is half an answer.

The most useful marker note in the whole collection is this one, from a question about why a candle in a sealed container goes out. A student wrote that the oxygen was used up. The marker wrote:

Verbatim from the mark scheme

"Note: Oxygen is used up. [0]"

Full credit needed two clauses: the oxygen falls to a very low amount and oxygen is required for burning. The second clause is the one nearly everybody leaves out, because it feels obvious. It is exactly the part being bought.

The other trap is naming a rule instead of using it. On a refraction question the scheme states plainly: "No point if writing just 'law of refraction' or 'Snell's law'". Quoting the name of a law is not applying it. Say what the law makes happen here.

Weak: "Because of the law of refraction." Fixed: "The light from the fish bends away from the normal as it leaves the water, so the fish looks shallower than it is."

Describe

The right direction and the right size of effect. Overstating is treated as a wrong answer, not a rough one.

A question about a partly blocked blood vessel produced this run of notes: "Heartbeat slower [0] / Pulse slower [0] / Flow of blood will stop [0]". The credited answer was that the flow becomes slower, reduced or decreased. Two separate errors in that line: the first two answers are about the wrong thing entirely, and the third has the right thing at the wrong magnitude. A partial blockage slows blood. It does not stop it.

The same trap, one question later, in three words: "Cannot instead of decreased [0]".

Weak: "The person cannot digest food." Fixed: "The ability to digest food decreases."

Compare

Both sides named, joined by a comparative word: more than, less than, faster than, higher than. A true fact about one side alone earns nothing.

This is the carbon dioxide question from section 1, and the schemes are consistent about it elsewhere too. On a question comparing two kinds of cloud, every accepted answer was a comparison — "White cloud less dense than dark cloud" — and none described only one cloud.

Weak: "Copper is a good conductor." Fixed: "Copper conducts heat faster than plastic does."

Calculate

Four separately payable parts: formula, substitution, answer, unit. One scheme lays the ladder out in full:

What was writtenScore
"PE = mgh"0.25
"PE = mgh = 100,000 /100"0.5
"PE = mgh = 100,000 J /100 kJ"1

Read the bottom two rows again. The number is identical. The unit alone doubles the score. Another scheme says it outright: "No unit deduce 0.5 point".

And method is protected. On one calculation the scheme awards "only formula but wrong answer: 1.0" out of 2 — a correct formula with a wrong number still banks half. Another says: "(write the correct formula & wrong answer, score 1; without formula & correct answer, score 2)".

So: never leave a calculation blank, and never rub out your working. Working is money.

Draw and sketch

Axis titles, units, scale, arrowheads and labels are each marked separately from the shape. Section 5 deals with this properly, because it is where the easiest marks on the whole paper live.

Suggest, or give a reason

One specific named mechanism. Vague verbs cap you at half.

On a question about insects laying eggs under leaves, a student wrote that the leaf protects the eggs. The marker note: "Note: The leaf protects the eggs. [0.5]". Protects from what? The credited answers named the threat — hidden from predators, or sheltered from sunlight, wind and rain.

Watch for protects, helps, affects, is better. Every one of them is a half-mark word until you name the specific thing.

Also: these questions want one reason, well expressed. The schemes head their answers "Any of the three [1]". Writing all three earns no extra, and risks the penalty in section 6.

Predict

A definite value with its unit, following the pattern already in the data. Not a range, not a hedge. Markers often pre-declare a tolerance — one scheme accepts "around 66 +/- 2 °C (score 1)" — so a sensible near-miss is usually safe. A vague answer is not.

3

Why "true but zero" happens

Collect the zero-scoring answers together and they fall into six repeating shapes. Almost none of them are caused by not knowing the science.

1. You gave the observation instead of the reason

"Note: Oxygen is used up. [0]"

You wrote what changed. The mark was for why that change matters. Test: if your sentence could be followed by someone saying "so what?", you have stopped one clause early.

2. You named a law instead of describing the physics

"No point if writing just 'law of refraction' or 'Snell's law'"

A label is not an explanation. Interestingly, the same scheme accepted three completely different levels of answer — the direction the light bends, a comparison of two angles, or a comparison of the two materials — all worth the same. Markers do not want sophistication. They want the physical situation described rather than named.

3. You got the magnitude wrong

"Flow of blood will stop [0]"

Right thing, wrong size. Reduced is not stopped.

4. You used an absolute word

"Cannot instead of decreased [0]"

Be suspicious of cannot, stops, never, always, completely. Science answers at this level are almost always more, less, slower, reduced — not zero and not infinite.

5. You gave half of a two-part answer

On a question about why a plant container is transparent, the model answer reads: "Allows light to pass through (this part alone no marks) + plants need light to photosynthesise / make food [1m]".

The scheme states it outright — that first half alone is worth nothing. A property is only half an answer. The other half is always so that ___ can happen.

There is a beautiful detail beside it. In the "accepted" column sits allows light energy to enter, which passes. In the "not accepted" column sits allows light to pass through, which fails. One word — energy, hinting the light gets used — decides it.

6. You answered about the wrong thing

"Heartbeat slower [0] / Pulse slower [0]"

Both are sensible statements about hearts. The question was about blood flow. Before you write, underline the noun the question is actually about, and check your answer is about that noun.

Commonly missed

One phrase, two questions, two different scores.

In one paper the words "get heart attack" appear twice. On the first part the marker wrote "Get heart attack [0]". On the very next part, the same words earned [partial ½m].

How to work it: nothing changed about the answer. The question changed. The first part asked for the mechanism — the muscle is starved of oxygen, so muscle cells die — and naming the disease skips all of that. The second part was closer to asking for the outcome, so the name earned something. The lesson is uncomfortable but freeing: there is no such thing as a good answer in general, only an answer that fits the question in front of you.

4

The mark allocation tells you what to write

The little [2] printed beside a question is not just a total. It is an instruction, and it is telling you how many separate things to say.

One scheme splits a two-mark question like this, and it labels the columns itself:

Verbatim from the mark scheme

"Partial credit: 0.5 point for correct drawing / 0.5 point for correct answers about the changes on heating curve (what) / 1.0 point for explanation (why)"

The scheme uses the words what and why. And the why is worth double the what. Observation is cheap. Mechanism is what is being bought.

That pattern repeats. A question about vitamin C splits [0.5] for the property (it dissolves in water) and [1] for the consequence (so the excess leaves in urine). Again two to one, again in favour of the consequence.

But be careful, because this is not a universal law and you should not learn it as one. A question about the cooling feeling of alcohol on skin splits the other way: [1] for naming the process, evaporation, and only [0.5] for the mechanism, that it absorbs heat from the skin. Here the naming carries more weight.

So the honest rule is narrower, and more useful:

What the marks really tell you

The printed mark tells you how many separate things to say. The wording of the question tells you how to balance them. A [2] means find two points, not write two sentences about one point.

Some other allocations worth recognising on sight:

Name plus justify. "Layer A (score 1) because it is originated from decomposition of leaves, woods, tree branches, microorgranisms, etc. (score +1)". Getting the right letter with no reason halves your score. Whenever a question says explain your answer, the reason is worth as much as the answer.

One mark per step. A separation-method question is marked "0.5 points for each step", added up. Every step you are sure of banks credit on its own, so writing the three steps you know always beats writing nothing.

One mark per distinct idea, capped. One question banks a mark for each separate valid reason up to "(Max 3 points)". Distinct ideas are rewarded — but only genuinely distinct ones, not the same idea reworded.

Bonus marks exist. On a water cycle question the scheme gives the mark for evaporation and then a bonus mark for also mentioning transpiration. Going beyond the obvious process is sometimes actively paid for — as long as what you add is correct, which is the difference between this and guessing.

5

Drawings and graphs are marked line by line

This is where the free marks are, and where most people throw them away. A drawn answer is not judged as a picture. It is taken apart and each feature is paid for separately.

One paper contains this standing rule, printed twice in the same paper with identical wording, which tells you it is a policy and not a one-off:

Verbatim from the mark scheme

"Without axis title : 0.5 reduced / Prefect except no units: 0.5 reduced / axis only: 1.5 reduced"

("Prefect" is the original's typo for "perfect", left as written.)

Read what that costs. A graph that is otherwise flawless loses half a mark for missing units. Missing axis titles cost the same again. These are marks for writing four words next to two lines, and they take about six seconds.

Now the one that surprises everybody. On a solubility graph the scheme awards:

FeatureWorth
Correct points, plotted without lines0.5
Correct straight line0.5
Scale1.0

The scale is worth more than the points and the line combined. Most students treat choosing a scale as the boring bit before the real work. The marker treats it as the largest single component. Another paper agrees: a depth profile scores "Same pattern with scale (2 point) / Same pattern without pattern (1 point)" — correct shape without a proper scale is worth exactly half.

Arrowheads are half a mark

On a ray diagram: "[1] for both rays correct with arrows / Partial [½] for correct lines but without arrows". A geometrically perfect diagram with no arrowheads loses fifty per cent, because without direction it does not show light travelling.

Note also that the same scheme includes an explicit "No penalty" clause for a different feature. Markers pre-declare what they will forgive. Arrowheads are not on that list.

The working counts as much as the answer

On a question about where a fish appears to be when seen from above the water, the instruction to markers reads:

Verbatim from the mark scheme

"Student must draw 2 paths of ray that bend away from the normal line (0.5 point) and extrapolate those lines backward to make intersection for the apparent position (0.5 point)"

Two rays bending the right way: half. Extending them backwards as construction lines until they cross: the other half. Those backward dashed lines are the working, and they are worth as much as the physics.

The same principle appears as a blunt note elsewhere: "Note: Point only without distance [0]". A dot in roughly the right place is a guess. The rule that fixes the position is the mark.

Draw and describe

A correct diagram often caps at half until you write down what it shows. One scheme reads "Note: Ray diagram only [0.5]", with the other half going to the written statement that the image is inverted. If you have drawn something, add a sentence saying what it means.

Checklist for any drawn answer

Axes titled, with units. A scale that uses most of the grid and goes up in sensible steps. Arrowheads on anything that travels. Every arrow and region labelled. Construction lines left in, not rubbed out. The right chart type — bars for separate categories, a line for a continuous measurement. Then one sentence saying what the drawing shows.

6

Marks lost for no reason at all

These cost marks without costing you any knowledge. They are pure waste.

Shotgunning

Listing extra answers to improve your chances does not improve your chances. It destroys the answers you had right. Three separate papers say so, in the markers' own words:

"( 0. 5 for each correct answer); more than 2 no marks"

"0.5 for each; zero if one answer is wrong"

"one correct, 0.5; one answer wrong / choose all, zero"

That last one names the strategy — choose all — and gives it a zero on sight. And it is not only about ticking boxes. Where a question wanted two facts for one mark, the note reads: "[1m for both correct, 0 for both or one wrong]". One right and one wrong scores the same as two wrong. Your correct answer is deleted by the guess sitting next to it.

The rule is simple. Only write what you can justify. Over-answering is punished harder than under-answering.

Leaving off units

Covered in section 2, repeated here because it is the single most common free mark on the paper. "No unit: 0.5". It is half a mark for two letters.

Hedging

Something happens to it. It gets affected. It changes a bit. These sentences cannot be marked, so they are not. If you are unsure, commit to the most specific answer you believe, and say it in the direction and magnitude you mean.

Writing a paragraph where a word was wanted

If the question says name, a paragraph does not earn more than the word, and it gives you more chances to contradict yourself. One scheme even notes that where the task was simply to write something down, extra detail is neither required nor penalised — so all that writing bought exactly nothing, at the cost of time you needed elsewhere.

Reassurance, from the schemes themselves

Phrases like "or similar meaning" and "(the same meaning)" appear again and again. Markers mark for meaning, not wording. One scheme accepts "change water from liquid to gaseous state" in place of the technical word evaporate. If you cannot remember a term, describe what happens. You will usually be paid in full.

7

Practice: find the missing mark

Each one below gives a question and a student answer that is not wrong. Say why it still loses marks, then open the answer.

1

Question: A cup of hot soup left on a table gets cold. Explain why. [2]

Student answer: "Heat leaves the soup."

Show answer

The failure: observation instead of reason. This is the "Oxygen is used up" shape exactly. The answer restates what happens and stops before saying why it happens.

The [2] is also telling you something: two separate points are wanted, and one short clause cannot be two points.

Model answer: "The soup is hotter than the surrounding air, so heat is transferred from the soup to the air and to the cup. This continues until the soup reaches the same temperature as its surroundings, so the soup cools down."

Two links in the chain: a temperature difference exists, therefore heat flows. Plus the endpoint the question was heading towards.

2

Question: Water flows through a garden hose. Someone stands on part of the hose, squashing it partly flat. Describe what happens to the water coming out of the end. [1]

Student answer: "The water cannot come out."

Show answer

The failure: an absolute word. This is "Cannot instead of decreased [0]" and "Flow of blood will stop [0]" in a different costume.

The hose is squashed partly. Partly means the flow is reduced, not ended. By writing "cannot", the student turns a correct direction into a false claim, and a false claim earns nothing even though the underlying idea was right.

Model answer: "The water comes out more slowly, because the squashed part is narrower so less water can pass through each second."

Habit to build: whenever you are about to write cannot, stops or never, check whether the question said partly, some or a little. If it did, downgrade your word.

3

Question: A greenhouse for growing seedlings has a fan fitted in the roof. Suggest why. [1]

Student answer: "The fan helps the plants."

Show answer

The failure: a vague verb. This is "The leaf protects the eggs. [0.5]". Helps, like protects, names no mechanism, so there is nothing for the marker to credit.

Helps how? That is the whole question, and the answer skips it.

Model answer: "The fan moves hot air out of the greenhouse, which stops the temperature rising so high that the seedlings dry out or are damaged."

Note that only one reason is needed. You could equally have written about circulating fresh air containing carbon dioxide for photosynthesis. Pick the one you can complete properly rather than listing both half-finished.

4

Question: Two identical mugs of tea are made. One is left uncovered, one has a lid. After ten minutes the uncovered one is cooler. Compare the rate of heat loss from the two mugs and explain the difference. [2]

Student answer: "The uncovered mug loses heat by evaporation."

Show answer

The failure: only one side named. This is the carbon dioxide question from section 1. The command word is compare, and a compare answer must contain both things and a comparative word joining them.

The science here is correct. It is also unmarkable as a comparison, because the mug with the lid never appears in the sentence.

Model answer: "The uncovered mug loses heat faster than the covered one. Water evaporates freely from the open surface and carries heat away, while the lid traps the water vapour and blocks that escape route, so less heat is lost from the covered mug."

The test to apply: circle the two things being compared. Are both in your answer? Is there a more/less/faster/higher than between them?

5

Question: A trolley of mass 4 kg is pushed with a force of 12 N. Calculate its acceleration. Show your working. [1]

Student answer: "3"

Show answer

The failure: no unit, no working. The number is right. That is worth about half of what was available.

Compare with the real ladder from the schemes: formula alone 0.25, formula and substitution 0.5, formula and substitution and answer with unit 1.0. A bare number sits low on that ladder no matter how correct it is. And one scheme is explicit — "only 72 : 1.0 / only 72 kg: 1.5" — the unit alone was worth half a mark.

Model answer: "a = F ÷ m = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 m/s2"

The bonus you get for free: because method is protected, if you had written the formula and then made an arithmetic slip, you would still have scored. A bare wrong number scores nothing at all. Working is insurance.

6

Question: Name two materials from this list that are good conductors of electricity: copper, wood, aluminium, plastic, rubber, glass. [1]

Student answer: "copper, aluminium, glass, wood"

Show answer

The failure: shotgunning. The student knew the answer. Copper and aluminium are both there and both correct.

Then they added two wrong ones as insurance, and the insurance is what kills them. The schemes are blunt about this: "more than 2 no marks", and "[1m for both correct, 0 for both or one wrong]". Four answers to a question asking for two is an automatic zero in several papers, even though two of the four were right.

Model answer: "copper, aluminium"

Count the number asked for. Write exactly that many. If you can only justify one, write one — partial credit is often available for one, and is never available after a wrong extra.

8

The last two minutes

Six checks. None needs any thinking about science, and together they are worth more than most people's last revision session.

Run these before you hand the paper in

1. Re-read the command word. Did an explain get a because? Did a compare get both sides and a comparative word?

2. Count your points against the marks. A [2] wants two separate things. Count them on your fingers.

3. Check every unit. Every number on the page. This is the cheapest half-mark there is.

4. Check the noun. Did you answer about the exact thing named, or about the thing next to it?

5. Hunt your absolute words. Any cannot, stops, never, always. Should it be less, slower or reduced?

6. Delete your extras. Asked for two, wrote four? Cross out the two you are least sure of. You are not losing an answer. You are rescuing one.
