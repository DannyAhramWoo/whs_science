Concept

A Balanced Diet and the Vitamins

A balanced diet supplies seven things — carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, fibre and water — and the vitamins matter out of all proportion to the tiny amounts needed, because without them specific diseases follow.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

You could eat a great deal of food every day and still become seriously ill. That fact took a long time to accept. If food is fuel, surely enough fuel is enough. But food is not only fuel, and a diet can be full of energy while missing something the body cannot make for itself.

A balanced diet contains all seven kinds of nutrient in the right proportions. Carbohydrates — sugars and starches from rice, bread, pasta, potatoes and fruit — are the main energy supply, broken down to glucose and used in respiration. Proteins, from meat, fish, eggs, milk, beans and nuts, are broken into amino acids and rebuilt into the body's own materials for growth and repair; enzymes and antibodies are proteins too. Fats, from oils, butter, nuts and oily fish, store energy densely, insulate against heat loss, cushion organs such as the kidneys and form part of every cell membrane. Vitamins are needed in tiny amounts to make particular chemical processes work. Minerals such as iron and calcium, also needed in small amounts, become parts of the body's structures and chemicals. Fibre is plant material we cannot digest; it gives the gut muscles something to push against, keeping food moving and preventing constipation. Water makes up most of the body, is the liquid in which every reaction happens, and is the main part of blood plasma.

Balance also means quantity. Food energy is measured in kilojoules or kilocalories, and body mass follows a simple rule: eat more energy than you use and the surplus is stored, mostly as fat, so mass rises; use more than you eat and stored fat is broken down, so mass falls. That is why losing mass requires eating less energy or using more, and why a food being labelled healthy does not stop it counting.

2

How it works

Vitamins do not supply energy and are not building materials. They are helpers — most of them assist enzymes in carrying out reactions, and a few do structural jobs. The body cannot make them, or cannot make enough, so they must come from food. Each vitamin has its own job, and each has its own deficiency disease, meaning the illness caused by not getting enough.

Vitamin A is needed for vision. It forms part of a light-sensitive pigment in the retina at the back of the eye, so a shortage first shows up as night blindness — difficulty seeing in dim light, while daytime vision seems fine. Severe deficiency damages the surface of the eye and remains a major cause of childhood blindness worldwide. It is found in liver, eggs, dairy and oily fish, and the body can also make it from the orange pigment in carrots and sweet potatoes. The carrot story is real, though the wartime claim that eating carrots gives outstanding night vision was pushed further than the evidence supported.

The B group is not one vitamin but a family, all helping release energy from food. Vitamin B1, also called thiamine, is the one most often examined. Without it a disease called beriberi develops, damaging the nerves and the heart, causing weakness in the legs and sometimes heart failure. B1 sits mostly in the outer layers of grains, so it is stripped away when brown rice is polished into white rice — which is why beriberi appeared in populations that had eaten rice safely for centuries and then changed how they processed it.

Vitamin C, ascorbic acid, is needed to make collagen, the tough protein that holds skin, blood vessels, gums and healing wounds together. Without it collagen is made badly, and the result is scurvy: bleeding gums, teeth loosening, old wounds reopening, exhaustion and eventually death. It comes from citrus fruits, berries, peppers, tomatoes and fresh green vegetables. Vitamin C is destroyed by heat and dissolves out into cooking water, so heavily boiled vegetables lose much of it.

Vitamin D lets the gut absorb calcium from food. This is worth stating precisely: you can eat plenty of calcium and still have weak bones if vitamin D is missing, because the calcium is never taken in. In children the result is rickets, where growing bones stay soft and bend under the body's weight, giving bowed legs. In adults the equivalent condition softens bones and causes pain. Vitamin D is unusual because your skin makes it when sunlight falls on it, so it is partly not a dietary vitamin at all. It also occurs in oily fish and eggs. Rickets became common in the smoky industrial cities of nineteenth-century Britain, where children indoors and thick air blocked the sunlight.

Vitamin K is needed to make several of the clotting factors, the plasma proteins that seal a wound. Without it, bleeding does not stop properly. It is found in green leafy vegetables, and helpfully some is made by the bacteria that live in the human large intestine.

Vitamin E protects cell membranes from chemical damage and comes from vegetable oils, nuts and seeds. Deficiency is rare in people eating an ordinary diet.

Fat-soluble and water-soluble. This split explains a great deal, so learn it as a pair. Vitamins A, D, E and K dissolve in fat. Vitamins B and C dissolve in water. The consequence is about storage. Water-soluble vitamins are not stored: any excess dissolves in the blood plasma and is filtered out by the kidneys into the urine, which is why very high doses of vitamin C mostly produce expensive urine, and why you must eat these vitamins regularly — a shortage shows within weeks. Fat-soluble vitamins are stored in the liver and in body fat, so you can go longer without them, but they can also build up to harmful levels if taken in large doses. Overdosing on vitamin A or D is genuinely possible; overdosing on vitamin C is very hard. A useful memory hook: the fat-soluble four spell A, D, E, K, and everything left over is water-soluble.

Minerals. Three are examined most. Iron is part of haemoglobin, the oxygen carrier in red blood cells; without enough, the blood carries less oxygen and the person becomes tired and pale, a condition called anaemia. Iron comes from red meat, liver, beans and dark green leaves. Calcium makes bones and teeth hard and is also needed for muscles to contract and nerves to work; it comes from milk, cheese, yoghurt and green vegetables, and remember it needs vitamin D to be absorbed. Iodine is needed by the thyroid, a gland in the neck, to make the hormone that controls metabolic rate. Without it the thyroid swells, producing a visible lump in the neck called a goitre. Iodine comes from seafood and from iodised salt, and adding iodine to table salt is one of the cheapest public health measures ever adopted.

The heart of it

Vitamins are needed in tiny amounts but each has one job and one deficiency disease — and the A, D, E, K group dissolves in fat and can be stored and overdosed, while B and C dissolve in water and must be eaten regularly because the body cannot keep them.

3

Where the idea came from

Scurvy killed more sailors on long voyages than storms or fighting. In 1747, aboard HMS Salisbury, the Scottish naval surgeon James Lind took twelve sailors already ill with scurvy, kept them on the same food, and divided them into six pairs, each given a different supposed remedy: cider, sulphuric acid, vinegar, seawater, a paste of spices, and two oranges with a lemon. The citrus pair recovered so quickly that one returned to duty within about a week. This is one of the earliest controlled clinical trials we have on record — the whole design turns on changing one thing at a time and holding everything else steady.

What happened next is the honest and uncomfortable part. The Royal Navy did not act on Lind's result for roughly forty years. Lind himself did not present it as the clear finding we now see; he still believed scurvy had several causes, he buried the trial in a long book, and he later boiled lemon juice into a concentrate for storage, which destroyed much of the vitamin C and made the remedy look unreliable. Having the right evidence is not the same as recognising it or persuading anyone of it.

The second thread began in the 1890s in the Dutch East Indies, where the physician Christiaan Eijkman was investigating beriberi. He noticed that chickens fed on polished white rice developed a beriberi-like illness, and that feeding them unpolished rice cured it. His first interpretation was wrong: he thought a toxin in the starch of polished rice was causing the disease and that something in the husk acted as an antidote. The truth was the reverse — nothing was being added, something was being removed. His colleague Gerrit Grijns argued for the missing-nutrient explanation, and it held.

In 1912 the Polish biochemist Casimir Funk proposed that several diseases including scurvy, beriberi and rickets were each caused by a missing trace substance, and he coined the word vitamine, from "vital amine", believing these substances were all chemicals of a type called amines. Most turned out not to be, so the final "e" was dropped and we write vitamin. The name is a memorial to a mistake.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is thinking vitamins give you energy. They do not. Only carbohydrates, fats and proteins supply energy. Several B vitamins help release energy from food, which is where the confusion starts, but a helper in a process is not a fuel.

The second is assuming more must be better. For water-soluble vitamins extra is simply lost in urine, so large doses waste money. For fat-soluble ones extra is stored and can accumulate to genuinely harmful levels. Neither outcome is an improvement.

The third is muddling calcium with vitamin D, or iron with vitamin C. Calcium builds bone but needs vitamin D to be absorbed. Iron builds haemoglobin, and vitamin C helps the gut absorb it. In each pair the mineral is the material and the vitamin is what makes it usable.

Commonly missed

From the vitamins A, B1, C, D, E and K, which are water-soluble?

The trap is that vitamin C is so strongly associated with oranges and fruit juice that people classify it correctly by luck, then guess the rest. Vitamin A also feels watery because of carrots and vegetables, and vitamin K feels watery because of green leaves — but where a vitamin is found tells you nothing about what it dissolves in.

How to work it: Do not reason from foods. Learn the fat-soluble set as a single block of four letters, A, D, E and K, and take everything else as water-soluble. Run the list against that block: A is in it, so fat-soluble. B1 is not, so water-soluble. C is not, so water-soluble. D is in it, fat-soluble. E is in it, fat-soluble. K is in it, fat-soluble. Only B1 and C are water-soluble. Then sanity-check with the storage rule: those two are the ones you must eat regularly, which fits scurvy and beriberi both appearing within weeks or months of a diet losing them.

This angle — sorting a list of vitamins into water-soluble and fat-soluble, and the energy-balance argument about gaining or losing mass — has appeared in IMSO papers, including 2009, 2017 and 2019.

5

Connected ideas

Digestion is how these nutrients are broken down and absorbed, and fibre matters because of how the gut physically moves food along. Respiration is what carbohydrates and fats are ultimately for. Iron connects directly to what blood is made of, since haemoglobin is built from it, and vitamin K to the clotting factors in plasma. Calcium and vitamin D connect to the skeleton. Many vitamins work as helpers to enzymes, and the kidneys are what remove the water-soluble surplus.

In one line

A balanced diet supplies carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, fibre and water, and each vitamin has one job and one deficiency disease — A for vision and night blindness, B1 for beriberi, C for collagen and scurvy, D for calcium absorption and rickets, K for clotting — with A, D, E and K stored in fat while B and C wash out and must be eaten often.
