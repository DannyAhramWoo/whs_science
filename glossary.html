Glossary

abiotic
The non-living parts of an ecosystem, such as sunlight, temperature, water and soil. Not the plants and animals — those are the biotic parts. Chapter 19
absorb
To take something in, so it does not pass through or bounce off. A black surface absorbs most of the light that lands on it, which is why it warms up in the sun. Chapter 7
accuracy
How close a measurement is to the true value. Not the same as precision: a clock that is always eleven minutes fast gives precise readings that are not accurate. Chapter 20
acid rain
Rain made acidic by waste gases from burning fuels. It damages leaves, kills fish in lakes and slowly eats away stone buildings. Chapter 13 Chapter 9
adaptation
A feature of a living thing that helps it survive where it lives. A cactus stores water in a thick stem and has spines instead of leaves to lose less water. Chapter 19
air resistance
The friction-like force of air pushing back on anything moving through it. It is a contact force, because the object is touching the air. Chapter 1
alveoli
The tiny air sacs at the ends of the tubes in your lungs, where oxygen passes into the blood. Their huge total surface makes the swap fast. Chapter 17
ammeter
An instrument that measures current, in amperes. It is always connected in series, in the circuit itself, because all the current must flow through it. Chapter 3
amphibian
A vertebrate with moist skin that usually lays jelly-covered eggs in water, such as a frog. Young ones live in water and adults on land. Chapter 18
amplitude
How big a vibration is. A larger amplitude means a louder sound — it changes volume, not pitch. Chapter 7
angle of incidence
The angle between a ray hitting a mirror and the normal line drawn at right angles to the surface. It always equals the angle of reflection. Chapter 7
artery
A blood vessel carrying blood away from the heart. Both words begin with A, which is the easiest way to remember it. Chapter 17
asteroid
A lump of rock orbiting the Sun, most of them in a belt between Mars and Jupiter. Smaller than a planet and not round. Chapter 10
atmosphere
The layer of gases held around the Earth by gravity. It is mostly nitrogen, with about a fifth oxygen and only a trace of carbon dioxide. Chapter 12
axis
The imaginary line through the Earth from pole to pole that it spins around. The Earth is tilted on its axis, and that tilt is what gives us seasons. Chapter 10 Chapter 11

balanced forces
Forces that cancel out completely, so nothing is left over. They do not mean the object is still — a car at a steady speed has balanced forces on it. Chapter 1
battery
Two or more cells joined together, used to push current round a circuit. Strictly, one on its own is a cell, not a battery. Chapter 3
biotic
The living parts of an ecosystem — every plant, animal, fungus and microbe in it. The opposite of abiotic. Chapter 19
boiling
A liquid turning to gas throughout the whole liquid, with bubbles forming inside it. Not the same as evaporation, which happens only at the surface and at any temperature. Chapter 15
boiling point
The temperature at which a liquid boils. Pure water boils at 100 degrees Celsius, and the temperature stops rising while the boiling happens. Chapter 15 Chapter 6
bulb
A component that turns electrical energy into light and heat. It only lights if the circuit is complete, with no gaps. Chapter 3

C

carbon cycle
The way carbon moves between the air, living things and the ground. Photosynthesis takes carbon dioxide out of the air, and respiration and burning put it back. Chapter 13 Chapter 16
carnivore
An animal that eats only other animals. A lion and a frog are both carnivores. Chapter 19
cell (electrical)
A single unit that pushes current round a circuit using stored chemical energy. Several cells joined together make a battery. Chapter 3
chemical change
A change that makes a new substance and is very hard to reverse. Burning wood, rusting iron and cooking an egg are all chemical changes. Chapter 15
chlorophyll
The green substance in leaves that traps light energy for photosynthesis. Without it a plant cannot make food, which is why white patches on a leaf make no starch. Chapter 16
chloroplast
The tiny part inside a leaf cell that holds the chlorophyll and where photosynthesis happens. Root cells have none, because there is no light underground. Chapter 16
chromatography
A way of separating coloured dyes by letting a solvent creep up paper. Each dye travels a different distance and appears as its own band. Chapter 14
circuit
A complete loop that current can flow round. A single gap anywhere stops the flow everywhere in that loop. Chapter 3
climate
The usual pattern of weather in a place over many years. Not the same as weather, which is what is happening outside today. Chapter 11
cold-blooded
An animal whose body temperature changes with its surroundings, such as a lizard or a fish. It does not mean the blood is cold — a lizard in the sun is very warm. Chapter 18
combustion
The scientific word for burning: a fuel joins with oxygen and releases heat and light. It is a chemical change, so the ash cannot be turned back into wood. Chapter 15 Chapter 9
community
All the living things of all the different species in one place. A pond community includes the fish, the plants, the insects and the microbes. Chapter 19
compass
A small magnet free to turn, which lines up with the Earth's magnetic field. The end that points north is the north pole of the magnet. Chapter 4
compressible
Able to be squashed into a smaller volume. Gases are compressible because of the large gaps between their particles; liquids and solids are not. Chapter 14
condensation
A gas turning into a liquid, usually when it is cooled. The water on the outside of a cold glass came from the air, not through the glass. Chapter 13 Chapter 15
conduction
Heat moving through a material by particles passing energy to their neighbours, without the material itself travelling. This is how a metal spoon in hot soup warms up. Chapter 6
conductor
A material that lets electricity or heat pass through easily. Most metals are good conductors of both. Chapter 3 Chapter 6
consumer
A living thing that cannot make its own food and must eat other living things. Every animal is a consumer. Chapter 19
contact force
A force that only works when two things are touching, such as friction, air resistance or a push. Everything except magnetic, gravitational and electrostatic force. Chapter 1
convection
Heat moving because a warmed fluid rises and cooler fluid sinks to take its place. It happens in liquids and gases only, never in a solid. Chapter 6 Chapter 11
core
The innermost layer of the Earth, made mostly of iron and nickel, and the hottest part. The outer core is liquid, the inner core solid. Chapter 12
crust
The thin, solid, rocky outer layer of the Earth that we live on. Very thin compared with the layers beneath it. Chapter 12
current
The flow of electricity round a circuit, measured in amperes with an ammeter. In a series circuit the current is the same at every point. Chapter 3

D

decomposer
A living thing that breaks down dead plants and animals and returns their nutrients to the soil. Fungi and many bacteria are decomposers. Chapter 19 Chapter 13
deforestation
Cutting down large areas of forest. It removes the trees that take carbon dioxide out of the air, and leaves the bare soil to be washed away. Chapter 13
density
How much mass is packed into a given volume. Something floats if its density is lower than the liquid's, whatever its size. Chapter 5 Chapter 14
dichotomous key
A set of paired either-or questions that leads step by step to the name of a living thing. Each step has exactly two choices. Chapter 18
diffusion
The spreading of particles from where they are crowded to where they are not, simply because particles never stop moving. It needs no wind and no stirring. Chapter 14
digestion
Breaking large food molecules into small ones that can pass into the blood. It happens in a long tube from mouth to rectum. Chapter 17
displacement
The volume of liquid pushed out of the way by an object put into it. Measuring that liquid is how you find the volume of an odd-shaped stone. Chapter 5
dissolve
When a solid seems to vanish into a liquid because its particles spread out among the liquid's particles. The solid is not destroyed — boil the liquid away and it reappears. Chapter 14
distillation
Separating a liquid from a solution by boiling it off and then cooling the vapour back into a liquid. It keeps the liquid, whereas evaporation throws it away. Chapter 15 Chapter 14
E

E

earthquake
A sudden shaking of the ground caused by rock moving deep below the surface. It starts in the crust, not at the centre of the Earth. Chapter 12
eclipse
When one body moves into another's shadow. In a solar eclipse the Moon blocks the Sun; in a lunar eclipse the Earth's shadow falls on the Moon. Chapter 10
ecosystem
All the living things in an area together with the non-living surroundings they depend on. A pond, a rock pool and a forest are each an ecosystem. Chapter 19
electromagnet
A magnet made by wrapping a coil of wire round an iron core and passing current through it. Its great advantage is that it can be switched off. Chapter 4
electrostatic force
The force between electric charges, which pulls or pushes without touching. A rubbed balloon sticking to a wall is showing electrostatic force. Chapter 1
energy
The ability to make something happen — to move, heat, light or grow. It is never made or destroyed, only changed from one form into another. Chapter 8
enzyme
A chemical that speeds up a reaction in a living thing without being used up. Saliva contains one that starts breaking down starch. Chapter 17
equator
The imaginary line round the middle of the Earth, halfway between the poles. It is the hottest region because sunlight strikes it most directly all year. Chapter 11 Chapter 10
erosion
The carrying away of broken rock and soil by water, wind or ice. Weathering breaks the rock; erosion moves the pieces. Chapter 12
eutrophication
When fertiliser washes into a lake, algae grow thickly, then die and rot, and the rotting uses up the oxygen so the fish suffocate. The water looks green long before the fish die. Chapter 13
evaporation
A liquid turning into a gas at its surface, at any temperature. Wet clothes dry on a cold day, so it does not need boiling. Chapter 13 Chapter 15
expansion
Getting bigger when heated, because the particles vibrate more and take up more room. Bridges are built with gaps so the metal has somewhere to expand into. Chapter 6

F

fair test
An investigation where only one thing is changed and everything else is kept the same. Otherwise you cannot tell which change caused the result. Chapter 20
filter (light)
A coloured sheet that lets its own colour through and absorbs the rest. A red filter does not add red — it removes everything else. Chapter 7
filtration
Separating an undissolved solid from a liquid by pouring it through filter paper. Useless against a dissolved solute, whose particles slip straight through. Chapter 14
food chain
A line showing who eats whom, always starting with a producer. The arrows point in the direction the energy travels, from the eaten to the eater. Chapter 19
food web
Several food chains joined together, showing that most animals eat more than one thing. Removing one species affects everything connected to it. Chapter 19
force
A push or a pull, measured in newtons. You cannot see a force, only what it does: start, stop, turn or change the shape of something. Chapter 1
fossil
The preserved remains or trace of a living thing from long ago, usually found in sedimentary rock. Igneous rock is far too hot when it forms to keep one. Chapter 12
fossil fuel
A fuel formed from the remains of living things over millions of years — coal, oil and natural gas. Non-renewable, because it forms far more slowly than we burn it. Chapter 9
freezing
A liquid turning into a solid as it cools. It happens at the same temperature as melting, just in the opposite direction. Chapter 15
frequency
How many vibrations happen each second. A higher frequency gives a higher pitch, not a louder sound. Chapter 7
friction
The force that resists sliding wherever two surfaces touch. It always acts against the direction of motion, and it produces heat. Chapter 1
fulcrum
The fixed point that a lever turns about. On a seesaw it is the bar in the middle. Chapter 8
fungus
A living thing that feeds on dead or living matter and reproduces using spores. A mushroom is not a plant — it has no chlorophyll and makes no food. Chapter 18

galaxy
A vast group of billions of stars held together by gravity. Our Sun is one star in the Milky Way galaxy. Chapter 10
gas
A state of matter with no fixed shape and no fixed volume, whose particles are far apart and move fast. It spreads out to fill its whole container. Chapter 14
genus
A group of closely related species. In a scientific name it comes first and takes a capital letter. Chapter 18
germination
When a seed starts to grow. It needs water, warmth and air — but not light, which surprises most people. Chapter 16
gravity
The non-contact force that pulls every object towards every other one. The Earth's gravity is what gives objects their weight. Chapter 2 Chapter 1
greenhouse effect
The way gases such as carbon dioxide trap heat in the atmosphere and keep the Earth warm. Some is essential for life; too much causes warming. Chapter 13 Chapter 11

habitat
The particular place where a living thing lives. A rock pool is a habitat; the animals living in it are its community. Chapter 19
haemoglobin
The substance in red blood cells that grabs onto oxygen and carries it round the body. It is also what makes blood red. Chapter 17
heat
The energy that flows from a hotter object to a cooler one. Not the same as temperature: a bath is cooler than a spark but contains far more heat energy. Chapter 6
hemisphere
One half of the Earth, either north or south of the equator. When one hemisphere is tilted towards the Sun it has summer; the other has winter. Chapter 11 Chapter 10
herbivore
An animal that eats only plants. A cow, a rabbit and a caterpillar are herbivores. Chapter 19
hypothesis
A testable idea that predicts what will happen and why. A guess with no reason behind it is not a hypothesis. Chapter 20

igneous
Describing rock formed when melted rock cooled and hardened. Granite and basalt are igneous, and they never contain fossils. Chapter 12
inertia
The tendency of an object to keep doing what it is already doing. The more mass an object has, the more inertia it has and the harder it is to start or stop. Chapter 2 Chapter 1
inference
A conclusion worked out from what you observed. Seeing wet ground is an observation; deciding it rained is an inference. Chapter 20
insoluble
Not able to dissolve in a particular liquid. Sand is insoluble in water, so it can be filtered out. Chapter 14
insulator
A material that does not let electricity or heat pass through easily. Plastic, wood and air are good insulators. Chapter 3 Chapter 6
invertebrate
An animal with no backbone, such as an insect, a worm or a snail. Most animal species are invertebrates. Chapter 18
involuntary
A movement you cannot control by choosing. Your heartbeat and the squeezing of your gut are involuntary. Chapter 17

joule
The unit of energy and of work, written J. One joule is a small amount — lifting an apple one metre takes about one. Chapter 8

kinetic energy

L

lever
A bar that turns about a fulcrum, letting a small force move a large load. A crowbar and a pair of scissors are levers. Chapter 8
light year
The distance light travels in one year. It measures distance, not time, despite the word year in it. Chapter 10
liquid
A state of matter with a fixed volume but no fixed shape, whose particles are close together but can slide past each other. Poured into any container, the amount stays the same. Chapter 14
luminous
Making its own light. The Sun is luminous; the Moon is not, because it only reflects sunlight. Chapter 7

magnet
An object that attracts magnetic materials and has a north and a south pole. Its force works across a gap, with nothing touching. Chapter 4
magnetic field
The region round a magnet where its force can be felt. Field lines always run from the north pole round to the south pole. Chapter 4
magnetic material
A material a magnet attracts: iron, steel, nickel and cobalt. Not all metals are magnetic — copper, aluminium and gold are not. Chapter 4
mantle
The thick layer of hot, slowly flowing rock between the Earth's crust and its core. It is by far the largest layer by volume. Chapter 12
mass
The amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms. Not the same as weight, which is a force — your mass is the same on the Moon as on Earth. Chapter 2
melting
A solid turning into a liquid as it is heated. Not the same as dissolving, which needs a liquid to dissolve into. Chapter 15

The temperature at which a solid melts. Ice melts at 0 degrees Celsius, and the temperature stays there until all the ice has gone. Chapter 15 Chapter 6
metamorphic
Describing rock changed by great heat and pressure while staying solid. Marble was once limestone, and slate was once mud. Chapter 12
mixture
Two or more substances jumbled together but not joined. The parts keep their own properties and can be separated by physical means. Chapter 14
moon
A natural object that orbits a planet. Our Moon takes about 28 days to go round the Earth once. Chapter 10

newton
The unit of force, written N. Weight is measured in newtons; mass is measured in kilograms. Chapter 2 Chapter 1
non-contact force
A force that works across a gap with nothing touching. There are only three to know: magnetic, gravitational and electrostatic. Chapter 1
non-renewable
An energy source that will run out because it cannot be replaced within a human lifetime. Coal, oil, gas and nuclear fuel are all non-renewable. Chapter 9
normal
The line drawn at right angles to a surface where a ray hits it. Angles of incidence and reflection are always measured from the normal, never from the mirror. Chapter 7
nutrient
A substance a living thing needs to grow and stay healthy. Plants take mineral nutrients from the soil; animals get theirs from food. Chapter 16 Chapter 13

omnivore
An animal that eats both plants and animals. Humans, bears and many birds are omnivores. Chapter 19
opaque
Letting no light through at all. A brick wall and a sheet of card are opaque, so both cast a sharp shadow. Chapter 7
orbit
The curved path one object takes round another because of gravity. The Earth orbits the Sun once a year. Chapter 10
oxidation
When a substance joins with oxygen. Burning is fast oxidation; rusting is very slow oxidation. Chapter 15

A gas high in the atmosphere that blocks harmful ultraviolet rays from the Sun. It is not the same as the greenhouse effect — that is a separate problem. Chapter 12 Chapter 13

parallel
An arrangement with more than one path for the current, so each component has its own branch. Remove one bulb and the others stay lit. Chapter 3
parallel circuit
A circuit with more than one path for the current. If one bulb is removed the others stay lit, because their own paths are still complete. Chapter 3
particle
One of the extremely small pieces that all matter is made of. How they are packed and how much they move explains almost every property of a material. Chapter 14
penumbra
The lighter, fuzzy outer part of a shadow, where only some of the light is blocked. The dark middle is the umbra. Chapter 10 Chapter 7
phase
The shape of the lit part of the Moon that we can see, from new moon to full moon and back. The Moon does not change shape — we see different amounts of the lit half. Chapter 10
photosynthesis
How a green plant makes its own food, using light energy to turn carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. It only happens in the light. Chapter 16
physical change
A change where no new substance is made and which can usually be reversed. Melting, freezing, dissolving and boiling are all physical changes. Chapter 15
pitch
How high or low a sound is. It depends on frequency, not on how hard you hit something. Chapter 7
planet
A large round object that orbits a star and has cleared its own path. There are eight in our Solar System. Chapter 10
pole
One of the two ends of a magnet where its force is strongest. Like poles repel, unlike poles attract — and repulsion is the only sure test that both objects are magnets. Chapter 4
pollination
The moving of pollen from one flower to another, often by insects or wind. It comes before fertilisation, not after it. Chapter 16
population
All the members of one species living in one place. All the frogs in a pond are a population; all the living things there are a community. Chapter 19
potential energy
Stored energy, ready to be released. A book on a high shelf, a stretched elastic band and a lump of coal all hold potential energy. Chapter 8
precipitation
Water falling from clouds as rain, snow, sleet or hail. It is the step in the water cycle that returns water to the ground. Chapter 13 Chapter 11
precision
How close repeated measurements are to each other. Precise readings can still all be wrong together, which is why precision is not accuracy. Chapter 20
predator
An animal that hunts and eats other animals. Its numbers usually fall when its prey becomes scarce. Chapter 19
prey
An animal that is hunted and eaten by another. If the predators are removed, the prey numbers usually rise sharply at first. Chapter 19
producer
A living thing that makes its own food using light, which is every green plant. Every food chain starts with one. Chapter 19 Chapter 16
pulley
A wheel with a rope over it, used to change the direction of a force or to lift a load with less effort. It does not reduce the work needed, only the force. Chapter 8

radiation
Heat travelling as invisible waves, needing no material at all to pass through. It is the only way the Sun's heat can cross empty space to reach us. Chapter 6
reflection
Light bouncing off a surface. The angle it arrives at always equals the angle it leaves at, both measured from the normal. Chapter 7
refraction
Light bending as it passes from one material into another, because it changes speed. It is why a straw in a glass of water looks broken. Chapter 7

An energy source that will not run out, because it is replaced as fast as it is used. Sunlight, wind, moving water and biomass are renewable. Chapter 9

To push away. Two north poles repel each other, and repulsion is the only test that proves both objects are magnets. Chapter 4
reptile
A vertebrate with dry scaly skin that lays leathery eggs on land, such as a snake or a lizard. Not the same as an amphibian, whose skin is moist. Chapter 18
resistance
How much a component slows the current down. Adding more bulbs in series raises the resistance, so the current falls and every bulb dims. Chapter 3
respiration
How every living cell releases energy from glucose using oxygen, giving off carbon dioxide and water. It happens day and night, in plants as well as animals. Chapter 16
revolution
One complete trip of one object round another. The Earth's revolution round the Sun takes a year — not to be confused with rotation, which takes a day. Chapter 10
rotation
One complete spin of an object on its own axis. The Earth's rotation takes 24 hours and is what gives us day and night. Chapter 10

A solution holding as much dissolved solute as it can at that temperature. Adding more just leaves it sitting at the bottom, though heating lets more dissolve. Chapter 14

Describing rock formed from layers of settled sediment squeezed together over a long time. It is the only kind that commonly contains fossils. Chapter 12

A circuit with only one path for the current. Remove one bulb and every bulb goes out, because the single loop is broken. Chapter 3

The loose top layer of ground made of broken rock, rotted plant matter, water and air. It takes hundreds of years to form and can be washed away in one storm. Chapter 12 Chapter 13

A device that turns sunlight straight into electricity. Not the same as a solar panel that heats water — that one produces heat, not electricity. Chapter 9

The Sun and everything held in orbit around it: eight planets, their moons, asteroids and comets. Chapter 10

A state of matter with a fixed shape and a fixed volume, whose particles are packed in a fixed pattern. They still vibrate, but they cannot swap places. Chapter 14
soluble
Able to dissolve in a particular liquid. Salt is soluble in water; chalk is not. Chapter 14

The substance that dissolves into a liquid. In salt water, the salt is the solute. Chapter 14

The clear mixture formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent. It stays clear and never settles, unlike a suspension. Chapter 14

The liquid that a solute dissolves into. Water is the most common solvent, but it is not the only one. Chapter 14

A group of living things so alike that they can breed together and produce young that can also breed. It is the smallest group in classification. Chapter 18

A tiny cell that fungi, ferns and mosses use to reproduce instead of seeds. Spores need damp conditions to grow. Chapter 18

The food a plant stores after photosynthesis. Iodine solution turns blue-black on starch, which is how you test whether a leaf has been making food. Chapter 16 Chapter 17

Electric charge that builds up on an object and stays there instead of flowing. Rubbing a balloon on your hair charges it up. Chapter 3

The tiny holes on the underside of a leaf that let gases in and out. They also let water vapour escape, which is transpiration. Chapter 16

Shaped so that air or water flows past smoothly, reducing resistance. Fish, birds and racing cars are all streamlined. Chapter 1

A solid turning straight into a gas without becoming a liquid first. Dry ice does this, which is where stage fog comes from. Chapter 15

A cloudy mixture where undissolved bits float in a liquid and settle if left alone. Sand stirred into water is a suspension, not a solution. Chapter 14

An animal that keeps its body at a steady temperature whatever the surroundings, such as a bird or a mammal. It uses energy from food to do this. Chapter 18

The endless journey of water: evaporation, condensation into clouds, precipitation as rain, then collection and flow back to the sea. No new water is made or lost. Chapter 13

How to use this list

Reading a definition is not the same as knowing a word. The test is whether you can put the term into a sentence of your own, about something real — not the sentence printed here. Try it out loud: say what upthrust is, then say why a heavy steel ship floats. If the second sentence will not come, the first one was only copied, not understood.

Some entries here are deliberately short, because a definition in one line can only take you so far. If one does not make sense, follow the chapter link rather than reading the words again. The diagram in the chapter usually does the real explaining, and the commonly-missed box next to it will show you the exact trap the term is used to set.
