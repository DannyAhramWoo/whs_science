Concept

Habitat and Environment

A habitat is the place an organism lives; its environment is everything around it, living and non-living, that affects how it lives there.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

If somebody asks where an earthworm lives, you say: in the soil. That is its habitat — the kind of place where it is normally found. A rock pool is the habitat of a limpet, a hedgerow of a wren, the open ocean of a tuna. A habitat is a place you could point at.

Now ask a different question: what is the earthworm actually dealing with down there? How damp the soil is, how much the sun warms it, how much oxygen lies between the grains, how acidic it is, how many dead leaves there are to eat, and whether a blackbird is digging above. That bundle of conditions is its environment.

So the two words are not the same, and this is the distinction most people slide past. A habitat is a place. An environment is a set of conditions. The habitat is the address; the environment is what it is like to live there. Two earthworms in the same habitat, one under a shady hedge and one in an open field, have different environments. And the environment includes living things too: other worms competing for the same leaves, the blackbird, the fungus rotting the leaf soft enough to swallow.

2

How it works

Because conditions vary from spot to spot, a single habitat contains many smaller worlds. These are microhabitats — small patches where conditions differ sharply from those a short distance away.

The underside of a stone is a good one: on top it is bright, dry and hot at midday, underneath dark, damp and several degrees cooler because the stone shields it. Rock pools are another. A high pool is left by the tide for hours, so it heats up, evaporates and turns saltier, while a low pool is refreshed constantly, and different animals live in each on one beach.

Once you can describe the conditions, adaptation starts to make sense. An adaptation is a feature helping an organism survive where it lives, and every one answers a problem set by the environment.

A polar bear's habitat is Arctic ice, and its problem is losing heat. So it has dense fur trapping a layer of still air, a poor conductor of heat; thick fat that insulates and stores energy; and a compact body with small ears, less surface for its size to lose heat through. A cactus faces the reverse: water is scarce and the sun fierce. Its leaves are reduced to spines, because a broad leaf loses much water while a spine loses almost none, and the thick green stem takes over photosynthesis and stores water.

You will also meet the word niche. It is not a place but the role an organism plays: what it eats, what eats it, when it is active. Two birds can share a habitat and have different niches if one takes insects from bark and the other seeds from the ground. You meet this properly later, so for now hold the contrast: habitat is where it lives, niche is what it does for a living.

The heart of it

The habitat is the address, the environment is the conditions at that address, and every adaptation is an answer to one of those conditions.

3

Where the idea came from

Habitat comes straight from Latin, where it means "it dwells". Early natural history books used it to head the line describing where a specimen had been found, and it kept that plain meaning as it passed into English.

The wider study these words belong to was named in 1866, when the German biologist Ernst Haeckel coined ecology from the Greek word for household. Naming a subject does not create it, and people had described where creatures lived for centuries. What the word did was mark out those relations as worth studying in themselves rather than as background to the animal.

The separation of role from place came later. Joseph Grinnell used niche in 1917 in a way still tied to the kind of place a species occupied. Charles Elton, in 1927, pushed the meaning towards what an animal does — its job in the community, particularly what it feeds on. The two senses sat awkwardly together until G. E. Hutchinson gave the term a more exact definition in 1957. The muddle between place and role is one the subject itself had to work through.

4

What people get wrong

The usual error is using the two words interchangeably. Asked how the environment affects an organism, replying "it lives in the desert" names the habitat and describes no condition at all. The fix is a habit: when you write environment, name at least one actual condition — how hot, how wet, how bright, who else is there. A second error is treating the environment as only physical: predators, competitors and food are part of it as surely as the rainfall.

Commonly missed

Woodlice are found under stones and rotting logs, not on open ground. Describe the habitat and the environment of a woodlouse, and explain why the difference between them matters here.

The trap is answering "under a stone" twice, which names the place two ways and explains nothing about why the woodlouse is there.

How to work it: Give the habitat as a place: woodland floor, and within it the microhabitat under a stone or log. Then give the environment as conditions: dark, damp, cool, dead wood to feed on, birds hunting nearby. A woodlouse loses water through its body surface and breathes through structures that must stay moist, so it would dry out in the sun; the conditions under the stone prevent that, and the stone hides it from birds. The habitat tells you where to look; only the environment tells you why.

Connected ideas

Habitats sit inside ecosystems, and the conditions making up an environment are sorted into biotic and abiotic factors. The features fitting an organism to those conditions are its adaptations, the living parts include the things that eat it, and the numbers of each kind present are studied as populations.

In one line

A habitat is the place an organism lives and its environment is the set of living and non-living conditions it meets there, which is why naming the habitat tells you where to look but only the environment explains how it survives.
