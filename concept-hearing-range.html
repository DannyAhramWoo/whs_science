Concept

The Range of Hearing

A healthy young human ear can hear sounds from about 20 Hz to about 20 000 Hz, and everything outside that band is silent to us even when it is loud.

Introduced in Chapter 7: Light, Sound and Colour

1

What it means

Pluck a rubber band stretched between your fingers. Pull it tight and the note goes higher; let it slacken and the note drops. Nothing about the band has changed except how fast it is shaking. That speed of shaking has a name.

Frequency is the number of complete vibrations something makes in one second. It is measured in hertz, written Hz, and one hertz means one vibration per second. A guitar string shaking 440 times a second has a frequency of 440 Hz, and that is the note orchestras tune to. Higher frequency means higher pitch — how high or low a note sounds.

Here is the part that matters most. Your ear does not respond to every frequency. It has a floor and a ceiling. Below roughly 20 Hz and above roughly 20 000 Hz, the vibration is still there, still travelling through the air, still carrying energy — but your ear produces no sensation at all. The sound exists. You simply are not equipped to receive it.

Those two numbers are worth memorising exactly: 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz. Twenty and twenty thousand. A thousandfold span, and a neat pair to remember.

2

How it works

Sound below 20 Hz is called infrasound. The prefix "infra" means below. Elephants rumble to each other at these frequencies across several kilometres of savannah, because low frequencies bend around obstacles and lose energy slowly. Whales do something similar through ocean water. Earthquakes and volcanic eruptions generate powerful infrasound, and some animals appear to react to it before humans notice anything is wrong. You can occasionally feel very low frequencies in your chest — a passing lorry, a large organ pipe — without hearing a note, which is exactly what you would expect at the edge of the range.

Sound above 20 000 Hz is ultrasound. "Ultra" means beyond. A dog whistle is built to produce a frequency of roughly 23 000 to 35 000 Hz. To the person blowing it, the whistle is nearly silent, because that frequency sits above the human ceiling. To the dog it is a clear loud note, because a dog's hearing extends to about 45 000 Hz. Nothing mysterious is happening. The whistle is working perfectly; the human ear simply cannot pick up that band. Bats hunt by emitting ultrasonic pulses and timing the echoes. Hospitals use ultrasound to build images inside a body, since the waves reflect at the boundaries between different tissues. Jewellers and dentists clean delicate objects in ultrasonic baths, where rapid vibration shakes dirt loose without scrubbing.

The ceiling is not fixed for life. The upper limit falls steadily with age, as the tiny hair cells in the inner ear that respond to the highest frequencies wear out and are never replaced. A newborn may reach beyond 20 000 Hz. A teenager might manage 17 000 Hz. Many adults over fifty cannot hear above 12 000 Hz. This is why a room full of children can hear a shrill whine from an old television that the adults nearby honestly cannot detect. Loud noise accelerates the loss, which is the real reason to keep headphone volume down.

Frequency is only half the story. Whether you actually hear a sound depends on its loudness as well, measured in decibels (dB). The ear is far more sensitive in the middle of its range, around 1000 Hz to 4000 Hz — roughly where speech sits — than at the edges. A sound at 50 Hz has to be considerably louder than a sound at 1000 Hz before you notice it at all. So a quiet 40 dB tone at 50 Hz may be genuinely inaudible, while the same 40 dB at 1000 Hz is easy to hear. This exact reasoning has been examined in past IMSO papers, using a graph of loudness against frequency, and it is why the honest answer is "roughly 20 Hz" rather than a hard line.

The heart of it

Human hearing runs from about 20 Hz to about 20 000 Hz; below that is infrasound and above it is ultrasound, and both are real sounds we are simply not built to detect.

3

Where the idea came from

The link between how fast something vibrates and the note it makes was studied long before anyone could measure it. Marin Mersenne, a French friar working in the 1630s, worked out the laws relating a string's length, tension and thickness to its pitch, and made an early estimate of vibration rates.

Measuring frequency directly needed better apparatus. Félix Savart in France and August Seebeck in Germany, working in the first half of the 1800s, used toothed wheels and rotating discs to produce tones of known frequency, then asked listeners where the sound began and ended. Their answers varied a good deal. This was not carelessness: at very low frequencies people confuse feeling a vibration with hearing it, and at very high frequencies a tone may be too faint to notice rather than genuinely inaudible.

Hermann von Helmholtz brought the field together in the 1860s with a detailed account of how the inner ear separates arriving frequencies into distinct notes. The familiar 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz figures settled into textbooks over the following decades, once electrical equipment could generate a steady tone at any chosen frequency. They are a rounded summary of many people's measurements, not one person's discovery.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating an inaudible sound as no sound. A dog whistle is not silent. It is loud. It is simply pitched above the human ceiling. Saying "the whistle makes no sound" is wrong; saying "the whistle makes a sound humans cannot hear" is right, and examiners look for exactly that distinction.

The second error is confusing pitch with loudness. Turning a dog whistle up does not help a human hear it, because the problem is frequency, not volume. Equally, a very quiet sound at a perfectly audible frequency may still go unnoticed — inaudibility can come from either cause, and a good answer says which one applies.

The third is quoting 20 Hz and 20 000 Hz as if they were exact walls. They are approximate, they differ between people, and the upper one drops with age.

Commonly missed

A trainer blows a whistle rated at 30 000 Hz. The dog comes running; the trainer hears nothing. A pupil concludes that the whistle produces sound only for dogs. What is wrong with that conclusion, and what is the correct explanation?

The trap is treating hearing as a property of the sound rather than of the listener. It invites the idea that the whistle somehow aims its sound at dogs, which no whistle can do.

How to work it: First, state what the whistle does: it vibrates the air at 30 000 Hz, producing a genuine sound wave that spreads out to everyone nearby. Second, compare that frequency against each listener's range. The human range tops out at about 20 000 Hz, and 30 000 Hz is above it, so the human ear gives no response. A dog's range extends to about 45 000 Hz, and 30 000 Hz falls inside it, so the dog hears a clear note. The sound is identical for both. The difference lies entirely in the two sets of ears. A sound above 20 000 Hz is called ultrasound, and this is the standard use of the term.

5

Connected ideas

Frequency is a property of any wave, not just sound, so the general picture of amplitude, wavelength and frequency underlies everything here. Sound itself explains why a vibration needs particles to travel through, and reflection explains how bats and ultrasound scanners turn returning echoes into information. Light has its own version of a detectable range, which is what we call colour.
