Concept

Dichotomous Keys

A dichotomous key is a chain of either/or questions that identifies an unknown organism by halving the possibilities at every step.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

The word dichotomous comes from Greek and means "cut in two", which is exactly what the tool does. At each stage two statements are offered, only one of which can be true of the creature before you. You choose the true one, and it either names the organism or sends you on to another pair, until you reach a name.

You have probably played a guessing game on the same principle. A key is that game written down in advance by someone who knows all the answers, so the questions come in the best order and you cannot get lost.

The crucial word is either/or. Every pair must divide the remaining possibilities cleanly, with no organism fitting both statements and none fitting neither. "Has six legs" and "has eight legs" looks fair until a snail arrives and the key jams, because neither statement is true. "Has six legs" against "does not have six legs" leaves nothing out.

2

How it works

The power of the method is in the halving. Suppose you have sixty-four possible organisms. Each good question halves the field — thirty-two, sixteen, eight, four, two, one — so six questions pick out one creature from sixty-four. Flipping through a picture book takes sixty-four comparisons instead of six, and the gap widens sharply as the number grows. This is why keys exist.

But the halving only happens if the questions are chosen well, and this is where most of the skill lies. A good key question must be three things at once. It must be observable: "does it have wings" is answerable, "does it migrate in winter" is not. It must be unambiguous, so that two people examining the same creature give the same answer — "is it large" fails, because large compared with what, while "is it longer than five centimetres" has a fixed line anyone can apply. And it must split the group roughly in half, because a question that separates off one organism and leaves fifty-nine to sort has wasted most of its power.

Order matters too. Broad, reliable questions belong at the top. A key opening with the pattern on a wing has assumed the creature has wings, so it collapses when a worm turns up. "Does it have a backbone" is safe, because every animal gives a definite answer.

One last honest limitation. A key does not explain anything and does not prove a relationship; it is a look-up device. Two organisms can end up side by side in a key while being distantly related, simply because the chosen questions ran them together.

Running a key down to a species

Knowing how a key is built is one thing; being handed a specimen and made to run five steps down to a species name is another. The discipline is simple. Always start at step 1, however obvious the creature seems. Answer the either/or using only the specimen in front of you. Go exactly where that answer sends you and nowhere else. Never look ahead at the names waiting at the bottom.

Here is an invented key to some freshwater animals.

1. Body has a hard outer shell — go to 2. / Body has no hard outer shell — go to 4.
2. Shell is a single coiled piece — Gyrella fontana. / Shell is in two hinged halves — go to 3.
3. Shell longer than 3 cm — Anodonta riveria. / Shell shorter than 3 cm — Pisidium minimum.
4. Body has jointed legs — go to 5. / Body has no jointed legs — Tubifex palustris.
5. Three pairs of legs — Baetis clarus. / More than three pairs of legs — Gammarus lucidus.

Now key out a specimen: a small animal with a soft body, no shell at all, and seven pairs of jointed legs. Step 1: does it have a hard outer shell? No, so go to 4 — and notice that steps 2 and 3 are now sealed off entirely, so the two shell measurements never need thinking about. Step 4: jointed legs? Yes, so go to 5. Step 5: three pairs, or more than three? Seven pairs is more than three, so the specimen is Gammarus lucidus.

You have almost certainly never heard that name, and that is normal rather than a sign you went wrong. A key's whole purpose is to deliver names you do not know. The name is the output, not something you were meant to recognise on the way in. If you were sure at step 1 that the answer would be the shrimp-like one and jumped straight to step 5, you would have got the same name by luck and learnt nothing about whether it was right.

The heart of it

Each question must be visible, unambiguous and cut the remaining group roughly in half, because it is the repeated halving — not the questions themselves — that does the work.

3

Where the idea came from

There is no single discoverer of the dichotomous key, and it would be wrong to name one. The method grew gradually out of the way naturalists worked in the century or so after Linnaeus, whose nested groups had already trained people to think in repeated splits. If your system is built by dividing a large group into smaller ones again and again, writing a chain of either/or questions to walk someone down through those divisions is a natural next step rather than a flash of invention. Naturalists in different countries arrived at much the same layout, and no one of them can fairly be credited.

That is a common pattern in science: some ideas have a discoverer and a date, others are practices many people drift into because the problem pushes everyone the same way. The key belongs in the second class, and we cannot be sure who wrote the first one worth the name.

Why the method has lasted is more interesting. Two hundred years on, with genetic sequencing available, field biologists still carry printed keys, because a key needs no equipment: it runs on eyes, a page and the specimen, so it works in a wet forest with no power or signal.

4

What people get wrong

The most damaging habit is jumping ahead: deciding you already know what a specimen is and skipping to the part of the key that confirms it. This destroys the whole point, because the key's reliability comes from every branch being ruled out honestly on the way down. Skip steps and you are not being told the answer, you are checking your own guess.

The second error is writing pairs that overlap or leave gaps. Writing the second half as a plain negative of the first — "has X" against "does not have X" — makes both faults impossible.

Commonly missed

A key begins: 1. Animal has legs — go to 2. / Animal has wings — go to 3. Explain why this key will fail, and rewrite step 1.

It is a trap because the two statements sound like a sensible opposition, and most animals you picture match exactly one. The fault shows up only at the edges.

How to work it: Test the pair against awkward cases. A bee has both legs and wings, so it matches both routes and the user cannot choose; an earthworm has neither, so it is stranded. A pair must be exhaustive and exclusive, and this one is neither. Rewrite it as a true split on one feature: "1. Animal has legs — go to 2. / Animal has no legs — go to 3." Now every animal has exactly one path.

5

Connected ideas

A key is the practical counterpart of classification: classification decides what the groups are, while the key places an unknown specimen into one. The first question in an animal key is nearly always the vertebrate split, because it is quick and certain, and the name a key delivers is a binomial. Writing a key is also an exercise in observation and inference, because it forces you to separate what you see from what you assume.

In one line

A dichotomous key identifies an organism through a chain of either/or questions, each observable, unambiguous and able to halve the remaining possibilities, so a handful of steps singles out one creature from hundreds.
