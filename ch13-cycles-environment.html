Chapter 13

Cycles and the Environment

Water and carbon go round and round the Earth, and human activity has started to change how fast. The one mistake to fix before anything else: the hole in the ozone layer and global warming are two completely different problems.

13.1

The water cycle

There is no tap filling up the Earth. The water in your glass today is the same water that has been on this planet for millions of years. It just keeps moving round in a loop called the water cycle.

The Sun drives the whole thing. Heat from the Sun turns liquid water at the surface of seas, lakes and puddles into an invisible gas. That is evaporation. Plants add to this: water travels up through a plant and escapes as water vapour from tiny holes in the leaves. That is transpiration. Both are liquid turning into gas.

Higher up, the air is colder. The water vapour cools and turns back into tiny droplets of liquid — condensation. Millions of those droplets together are what a cloud is made of. When the droplets grow heavy enough they fall as precipitation: rain, or snow if it is cold enough.

What falls then has to go somewhere. Some flows over the ground into rivers and back to the sea — that is run-off. Some soaks down through the soil and is stored underground as groundwater. Either way it ends up where it can evaporate again, and the loop closes.

Which way does the change go

Evaporation and transpiration are liquid to gas. Condensation and precipitation are gas back to liquid. Questions often just ask you to sort the steps into those two piles, so learn them as two pairs rather than four separate words.

13.2

The carbon cycle

Carbon moves in a loop too, but it is easier to think of it as one question: what takes carbon dioxide out of the air, and what puts it back?

Only one process takes it out in a big way. Photosynthesis: green plants absorb carbon dioxide and use light energy to build sugars and their own bodies. The carbon becomes wood, leaves and roots.

Three processes put it back. Respiration — every living thing, plants included, releases carbon dioxide as it uses food for energy. Decay — when dead things rot, decomposers break them down and release the carbon. Burning — any fuel that came from living things gives off carbon dioxide when it burns.

Carbon is not only in living things. It is stored in the ocean, in rocks such as limestone, and in fossil fuels — coal, oil and gas, made from plants and animals buried millions of years ago. That store had been locked away and out of the cycle. When we dig it up and burn it, we push all that old carbon back into the air in a couple of centuries. The taking-out and putting-back no longer balance, and the amount of carbon dioxide in the air climbs.

Commonly missed

A plant is kept in the dark for two days. Does it add carbon dioxide to the air, remove it, or neither?

The trap is thinking plants only photosynthesise and never respire. In fact a plant respires all the time, day and night, exactly like you do. Photosynthesis is the extra thing it can do, and only when there is light.

How to work it: in the dark there is no photosynthesis, so nothing removes carbon dioxide. Respiration carries on. So the plant adds carbon dioxide to the air. In bright light photosynthesis is much faster than respiration, so the plant is a net remover — but that is a different situation, and the question tells you which one you are in.

13.3

The greenhouse effect and global warming

Sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms the ground. The warm ground then gives off heat, which tries to escape back out to space. Some gases in the air absorb that escaping heat and send part of it back down. These are greenhouse gases: carbon dioxide, methane and water vapour are the main ones.

This is not a fault. Without any greenhouse effect the Earth's average temperature would be roughly 30 degrees Celsius colder — frozen, and almost impossible to live on. The natural greenhouse effect is what keeps the planet comfortable.

The problem is the extra amount. Burning fossil fuels and clearing forests have raised the level of carbon dioxide, so more heat is trapped than before and the average temperature creeps up. That is global warming. The consequences follow from the warmth: ice sheets and glaciers melt, sea level rises and low-lying land floods, and weather patterns shift so some places get more storms and others get droughts.

Natural is not the problem

Never write that the greenhouse effect is bad. The greenhouse effect is what makes Earth liveable. What is causing trouble is the enhanced greenhouse effect — the extra gases we have added on top of the natural amount.

13.4

Ozone is a different story

High above the weather, in a layer of the atmosphere called the stratosphere, there is a thin band of a gas called ozone. Ozone absorbs ultraviolet light from the Sun — the invisible light that burns skin and damages eyes. The ozone layer acts like sunblock for the whole planet.

Chemicals called CFCs, once used in fridges and spray cans, drifted up and broke ozone apart. The layer thinned, worst of all over Antarctica, and this is what people call the ozone hole. Most countries have now banned CFCs and the layer is slowly recovering.

Notice that nothing in that paragraph was about heat. Ozone depletion lets more ultraviolet light in. Global warming is about heat being stopped from getting out. Different gases, different part of the atmosphere, different harm.

Commonly missed

Which of these is a direct result of damage to the ozone layer: rising sea levels, more skin cancer, or melting glaciers?

This is the single most confused pair in the whole topic. Both problems involve the atmosphere and both are caused by gases we released, so people blur them into one thing and pick whichever consequence sounds most serious.

How to work it: ask what each problem lets through. Ozone damage lets ultraviolet light reach the ground, and ultraviolet light harms skin and eyes — so the answer is more skin cancer. Rising seas and melting glaciers are heat, and heat means greenhouse gases, not ozone. A quick check that works every time: if the answer mentions temperature, it belongs to global warming. If it mentions sunburn, skin or eyes, it belongs to ozone.

13.5

Pollution, deforestation and looking after resources

Pollution is any harmful substance added to the environment, and it comes in three kinds: air, water and land. Two examples turn up in questions again and again.

Acid rain. Burning fossil fuels releases sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides. These dissolve in the water droplets in clouds and make the rain acidic. Acid rain eats into stone buildings and statues, damages the leaves of trees, and makes lakes so acidic that fish cannot survive.

Eutrophication. This one is a chain, and you must be able to say the chain in order. Fertiliser from farm fields washes into a river or lake. The extra nutrients make algae grow enormously — an algal bloom. The thick layer of algae blocks light from reaching the plants below, so they die. Then the algae themselves die and decomposers break them down, and those decomposers use up the oxygen dissolved in the water. With no oxygen left, the fish suffocate.

Deforestation is cutting down forests. Fewer trees means less photosynthesis, so less carbon dioxide is taken out of the air — which makes global warming worse. Tree roots also hold soil together, so without them rain washes the soil away, and that is soil erosion. Every animal that lived in the forest loses its habitat.

Against all this, we manage what we have. Renewable resources can be replaced or do not run out — sunlight, wind, water power, trees if they are replanted. Non-renewable resources cannot be replaced in any useful time: coal, oil, gas and metal ores. The three rules are in order of usefulness: reduce first (use less in the first place), then reuse (use the same object again), then recycle (break it down into material for something new). Recycling is last because it still costs energy.

Say the middle of the chain

If a question asks why fish die after fertiliser enters a lake, "the water was polluted" earns nothing. The marks are in the middle steps: algae bloom, light is blocked, algae decay, decomposers use up the oxygen. Name the oxygen.

13.6

Section B: the shortcut worth memorising

In this question format you get three statements and must decide which are correct. Only these four options are ever offered:

OptionMeans
A1, 2 and 3 are correct
B1 and 2 only
C2 and 3 only
D1 only

Notice what is missing: there is no "2 only", no "3 only", no "1 and 3". So statement 1 does most of the work for you.

If statement 1 is…Then…
wrongThe answer is C. You can stop reading.
rightCheck statement 3 → correct means A, wrong means B or D

These questions are worth 2 marks each, so they carry far more weight than their length suggests. There is no penalty for a wrong answer — never leave one blank.

13.7

Think and Explain

Answer these out loud, in full sentences, before opening the answer.

1

Wet clothes are hung outside on a sunny day and are dry two hours later. Name the process, say which change of state it is, and explain where the water went.

Show answer

The process is evaporation, a change from liquid to gas. Heat energy from the Sun gives the water particles at the surface of the cloth enough energy to escape into the air as water vapour.

The water has not disappeared. It is now an invisible gas mixed into the air around the clothes. Later, when that air rises and cools, the vapour will condense into cloud droplets, so the water is still in the water cycle.

2

Coal has been underground for millions of years. Explain why burning it changes the carbon cycle more than burning wood from a tree planted last year.

Show answer

The carbon in last year's tree was taken out of the air by photosynthesis very recently. Burning the wood puts back roughly the same carbon that the tree had just removed, so the air's total does not change much.

The carbon in coal was taken out of the air millions of years ago and then buried, which removed it from the cycle completely. Burning coal adds carbon that the modern air has never held.

So burning fossil fuels increases the total amount of carbon dioxide in the air, while burning recently grown wood mostly recycles carbon that was already going round.

3

A friend says "the ozone hole is why the Earth is getting hotter". Explain what is wrong with this and give the correct cause of each problem.

Show answer

These are two separate problems. Ozone in the stratosphere blocks ultraviolet light. CFCs from old fridges and spray cans broke that ozone apart, so more ultraviolet light reaches the ground, which causes sunburn, skin cancer and eye damage.

The Earth getting hotter is global warming, caused by extra greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide and methane. These trap heat that is trying to escape to space.

One is about light coming in through a gap. The other is about heat being stopped from going out. Losing ozone does not warm the planet, and greenhouse gases do not damage the ozone layer.

4

A hillside forest is cleared for farming. Give two effects on the environment and explain the reason behind each.

Show answer

More carbon dioxide in the air. The trees were removing carbon dioxide by photosynthesis. With fewer trees, less is removed, so the amount in the air rises and the greenhouse effect is strengthened.

Soil erosion. Tree roots held the soil in place and the leaves broke the force of falling rain. Without them, rain runs straight down the bare hillside and carries the topsoil away, so the land becomes poor for farming anyway.

You could also say habitat loss: every animal that lived in the forest has nowhere to feed or shelter, so populations fall.

13.8

Section B practice

Use the shortcut from 13.6. Judge statement 1 first.

1

Which statements about the water cycle are correct?

  • 1Condensation is water changing from a liquid into a gas.
  • 2Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the leaves of plants.
  • 3Heat from the Sun is what drives evaporation.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: C

Statement 1 — wrong. Condensation is the other way round: gas turning into liquid. The statement has swapped it with evaporation. The moment statement 1 is wrong, the answer is C. You can stop here.

Checking anyway: statement 2 is the correct definition of transpiration, and statement 3 is right because the Sun's heat supplies the energy for water to escape as vapour. Both correct, consistent with C.

2

Which statements about the greenhouse effect and the ozone layer are correct?

  • 1The ozone layer protects living things by absorbing ultraviolet light.
  • 2Carbon dioxide and methane trap heat that would otherwise escape to space.
  • 3Damage to the ozone layer is the main cause of rising sea levels.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: B

Statement 1 — correct. That is exactly what ozone does. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — wrong. Sea level rises because ice melts, and ice melts because of heat trapped by greenhouse gases. Ozone damage lets ultraviolet light in, which has nothing to do with sea level. That rules out A.

Statement 2 — correct. Carbon dioxide and methane are greenhouse gases and trapping outgoing heat is what they do. So the answer is B.

3

Fertiliser from a farm washes into a nearby lake and the fish later die. Which statements are correct?

  • 1The fertiliser causes algae in the lake to grow rapidly.
  • 2The thick layer of algae blocks light from reaching plants below.
  • 3The fish die because decay of the dead algae uses up the oxygen in the water.
A · 1,2,3B · 1,2C · 2,3D · 1 only
Show answer

Answer: A

Statement 1 — correct. Fertiliser is plant food, and in water it feeds algae, which multiply into a bloom. Answer is A, B or D.

Statement 3 — correct. Decomposers breaking down the dead algae use dissolved oxygen, and the fish then suffocate. Correct statement 3 with a correct statement 1 means the answer must be A.

Statement 2 — correct. The bloom sits on the surface and shades everything under it. All three, so A is confirmed.

Watch for this trap: a tempting wrong version of statement 3 says the fish are poisoned by the fertiliser. They are not — they run out of oxygen.
