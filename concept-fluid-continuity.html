Concept

Why Fluids Speed Up in a Narrow Pipe

The same amount of water must pass every point of a pipe each second, so where the pipe narrows the water has no choice but to move faster.

Introduced in Chapter 5: Floating, Sinking and Density

1

What it means

Turn on a garden hose and water dribbles out in a soft arc. Put your thumb over most of the opening and the water shoots across the garden. You did not turn the tap up. You did not add energy with your thumb. The water simply came out faster. Why should squeezing the exit make water quicker rather than slower?

The answer is one of the tidiest arguments in physics, and it needs only counting. Water is a fluid, meaning anything that flows — liquids and gases both count. Water is also very hard to squash, so a litre of water stays a litre no matter what shape of pipe it is in. And water does not pile up inside a sealed hose, nor does it vanish.

Put those three facts together. If two litres of water go into the hose every second, then two litres must come out of the nozzle every second. Not one, not three. The same volume must cross every cross-section of the pipe in the same time. This rule is called continuity, from the idea that the flow is continuous and unbroken along the whole pipe.

Now the conclusion follows on its own. At the nozzle the opening is small. The same two litres per second have to get through a much smaller doorway. The only way is for each bit of water to travel further in that second — that is, to move faster.

2

How it works

Picture the volume passing a point each second as a cylinder of water. Its end face is the cross-section of the pipe, its length is how far the water travels in one second, which is the speed. So volume per second = area x speed. Continuity says this product is the same everywhere along the pipe. If the area shrinks, the speed must grow to keep the product unchanged.

Numbers make it obvious. Suppose a wide pipe has a cross-sectional area of 6 square centimetres and water moves through it at 2 cm/s. The volume passing each second is 6 x 2 = 12 cubic centimetres. The pipe then narrows to an area of 2 square centimetres. That same 12 cubic centimetres must still pass every second. So 2 x speed = 12, and the speed is 6 cm/s. The area fell to a third, the speed rose to three times. Whatever the area is divided by, the speed is multiplied by.

A crowd of people gives the same picture. A hundred people a minute walk down a broad corridor at a comfortable stroll. The corridor narrows to a single doorway. If a hundred people a minute are still to get through, everyone must hurry through that doorway. Nobody is pushing them faster; the geometry demands it.

Rivers do this at full scale. A river running across a wide flat plain moves slowly and looks lazy. The same river squeezed between the walls of a rocky gorge becomes a torrent. Not a drop more water is flowing — the gorge just gives it a far smaller channel, so the water races through. It also runs faster where the bed is shallow, since shallowness reduces the cross-section too.

The rule holds for gases in most everyday cases as well. Wind picks up speed as it funnels between two tall buildings, and a partly blocked chimney draws faster through the gap. Air can be compressed in a way water cannot, so the rule is not quite as exact for gases, but at ordinary speeds it works well.

Faster-moving fluid also turns out to press sideways more gently on the pipe walls. That second effect has its own name, the Bernoulli effect, and it explains things like why shower curtains blow inwards. It is a separate idea and is not needed here. What matters on this page is the counting argument: same volume per second, smaller opening, higher speed.

The heart of it

The same volume must pass every cross-section each second, so narrower always means faster.

3

Where the idea came from

Moving water was studied long before it was understood. Roman engineers built aqueducts across the empire from around 300 BC onwards, and they were superb at getting water to run where they wanted. But their method of charging customers reveals a gap: they measured supply by the width of the outlet pipe alone, as if the width by itself fixed how much water flowed. It does not, because speed matters just as much. Anyone who fitted a longer or steeper pipe could quietly draw more than they had paid for.

Careful measurement came in the 1600s. Benedetto Castelli, an Italian mathematician and a pupil of Galileo, published work on flowing water around 1628 in which he stated clearly that the amount of water passing a section depends on both the area and the speed. That is the continuity rule in its earliest recognisable form, and it corrected the Roman assumption directly.

The full mathematical treatment belongs to Daniel Bernoulli, a Swiss mathematician from a famous family of mathematicians, who published Hydrodynamica in 1738 while working in Russia and then Switzerland. The book pulled the behaviour of flowing fluids together into one framework, including the relationship between a fluid's speed and its pressure that now carries his name. His father Johann was also working on fluids at the time, and the two fell out bitterly over who deserved the credit, with the father publishing a rival book dated to appear earlier. Historians generally judge the son treated badly.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is assuming more water passes the wide part of the pipe than the narrow part. It feels right — a bigger pipe surely carries more. But in a steady, sealed flow it cannot. Water is not being created in the wide section or destroyed in the narrow one. Whatever goes in must come out, so the volume per second is identical everywhere. Past IMSO papers, including a 2019 one, have offered wrong answers built on exactly this assumption, pairing "more water at the wide end" with a guess about speed.

The second error is thinking the thumb over the hose adds energy or pressure to the water. Your thumb only reduces the opening. The speed increase follows from the geometry.

A third point worth separating: how fast a tap runs in a building is a different question, governed by depth of water above it rather than pipe width. A ground-floor tap runs harder than a top-floor tap because the water in the tank overhead is much deeper above it, and pressure in a liquid grows with depth. That is a pressure effect, not a continuity effect, and papers sometimes place the two side by side to see whether you can tell them apart.

Commonly missed

A pipe is wide at point A and narrow at point B. Water flows steadily from A to B. Compare the volume of water passing A each second with the volume passing B each second, and compare the speeds.

The trap is answering that more water passes A because A is wider. Two of the four possible answer combinations are built to catch that.

How to work it: ask where any extra water at A could possibly go. It cannot pile up inside the pipe, and it cannot leak away. So the volume passing each second is the same at A and at B. Then use volume per second = area x speed. The volume is equal but B's area is smaller, so B's speed must be larger. Answer: equal volumes per second, faster at B. Deal with the volumes first and let the speed follow — reaching for the speed first is what causes the error.

5

Connected ideas

Continuity rests on water having a fixed density, so a given mass always occupies the same volume no matter what shape of pipe holds it. The particle model explains why liquids flow to fill any channel yet resist being squashed. Pressure is the neighbouring idea, covering why deeper water pushes harder and why lower taps run faster, and the upward push of pressure on a submerged object is upthrust. Speed itself is best read off a speed-time graph.
