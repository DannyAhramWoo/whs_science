Concept

Hormones and Blood Sugar

Hormones are chemical messengers made by glands and carried in the blood, and the clearest example of them at work is the pair that keeps the amount of sugar in your blood steady.

Introduced in Chapter 17: The Human Body

1

What it means

A body needs to send messages to itself. Touch something hot and your arm pulls back before you have thought about it. That message went along a nerve, a living cable running from skin to spinal cord and back to the muscle, arriving in a fraction of a second at one particular place.

A second messaging system works completely differently. A hormone is a chemical made by a gland — an organ whose job is to produce and release a substance — and poured straight into the bloodstream. Blood goes everywhere, so a hormone reaches every part of the body. Only the organs built to respond to it do anything; the rest ignore it. Those are the target organs.

The contrast is worth setting out plainly, because it is regularly examined. Nerve messages are electrical, extremely fast, sent to one specific target, and stop almost as soon as the message stops. Hormone messages are chemical, slow — seconds to minutes, sometimes hours — spread widely by the blood, and long-lasting. Neither is better. You want the fast targeted system for pulling your hand off a stove, and the slow widespread one for growing, digesting and keeping conditions steady hour after hour.

The whole set of glands together is the endocrine system. It includes the pancreas, the thyroid in your neck, the adrenal glands sitting on top of the kidneys, and the pituitary at the base of the brain, which controls several of the others.

2

How it works

Take blood sugar as the worked example, because it shows the whole logic in one place.

Glucose is a simple sugar, the fuel cells burn in respiration to release energy, and the brain runs on almost nothing else. The amount dissolved in blood is the blood glucose concentration, and it must stay within a fairly narrow range. Too little and cells, especially brain cells, cannot get enough fuel. Too much, kept up for years, damages blood vessels, eyes and kidneys.

The problem is that glucose arrives in bursts. Eat a bowl of rice and within half an hour digestion has broken the starch into glucose and pushed a large load into the blood. Then go hours without eating and the supply stops while cells keep burning. Left alone the level would swing wildly. It does not, and the reason is the pancreas.

The pancreas is a soft organ behind the stomach. Besides making digestive juices, it holds clusters of cells that continuously sense how much glucose is in the blood flowing past. It is both the detector and the source of the response.

When blood glucose is too high, the pancreas releases insulin. Insulin travels in the blood to the liver and to the muscles. It makes the cells of those organs take glucose in from the blood, and it makes the liver and muscles join glucose molecules together into a large storage molecule called glycogen. Glycogen is a store, packed away and not dissolved in the blood. As glucose is removed and stored, the blood glucose concentration falls back towards normal. Insulin lowers blood sugar.

When blood glucose is too low, the pancreas releases a different hormone, glucagon. Glucagon travels to the liver and makes it do the reverse: break the stored glycogen back down into glucose and release that glucose into the blood. The blood glucose concentration rises back towards normal. Glucagon raises blood sugar.

So insulin and glucagon are a matched pair doing opposite jobs, made by the same organ. Such a pair is called antagonistic. The examined question is direct: if insulin in the blood rises, what happens to blood glucose concentration? It falls. If glucagon rises, blood glucose rises. IMSO papers from around 2019 have asked exactly that, and the marks go to whoever holds the two firmly as opposites rather than merely recognising both names.

The arrangement is negative feedback: a change in a quantity triggers a response that pushes that quantity back the other way. High glucose triggers insulin, which lowers glucose. Low glucose triggers glucagon, which raises it. "Negative" means opposing, not bad. A thermostat does the same — too cold turns the heater on, warming turns it off. The result is not a fixed value but a level wobbling gently around a set point. Keeping conditions steady this way is homeostasis.

Other hormones fill out the picture. Adrenaline, from the adrenal glands, is released when you are frightened or suddenly need to act. It raises heart and breathing rate and releases glucose from the liver, putting fuel into the blood for hard work. Thyroxine, from the thyroid in the neck, sets the metabolic rate — the overall speed at which the body runs its chemical reactions and uses energy. Too little and everything runs slowly; too much and the body burns through its reserves. Growth hormone, from the pituitary, controls how much a child grows.

The heart of it

Insulin lowers blood glucose by storing it as glycogen and glucagon raises blood glucose by releasing it again, which is negative feedback: each change triggers the response that reverses it.

3

Where the idea came from

Before around 1900, most scientists assumed the body coordinated itself entirely through nerves. In 1902 two physiologists in London, William Bayliss and Ernest Starling, tested that. It was known that acid arriving from the stomach makes the pancreas release digestive juice, and the accepted explanation was a nerve reflex. They cut the nerves to a stretch of gut and put acid into it. The pancreas responded anyway, so something must have travelled in the blood. They named it secretin. In 1905 Starling proposed the word hormone for messengers of this sort, and it stuck.

Insulin took longer. Doctors had long known of a disease in which people passed large volumes of sweet-tasting urine and wasted away, and by the 1890s experiments had shown that removing an animal's pancreas brought it on. Extracting the vital substance defeated people for years, because the pancreas's own digestive juices destroyed the very thing being hunted.

In 1921, at the University of Toronto, a young surgeon named Frederick Banting and a student assistant, Charles Best, worked around the problem. In a laboratory lent by J. J. R. Macleod, they tied off the ducts of dogs' pancreases so the digestive part wasted away, then extracted what remained. It lowered blood sugar in a diabetic dog. The biochemist James Collip purified it enough to be safe in people, and in January 1922 a teenager in Toronto was among the first treated. A disease that had been reliably fatal in children became one people could live with.

The 1923 Nobel Prize went to Banting and Macleod, and the omission of Best caused lasting argument. Banting was angry about it and shared his prize money with Best; Macleod shared his with Collip. The dispute is a useful reminder that discoveries usually have more hands on them than a prize can list.

4

What people get wrong

The first and by far the commonest mistake is swapping insulin and glucagon. The names sound alike and both come from the pancreas, so under pressure it is easy to write the wrong one. Two memory hooks help. "Glucagon" contains "gluc", and it puts glucose into the blood. Insulin is the one people with diabetes inject after eating, and you would only ever inject something to bring a high level down.

The second mistake is confusing the nerve and hormone systems, usually by describing a hormone as fast and aimed at one organ. A hormone goes everywhere the blood goes; what makes the response look targeted is that only certain organs are built to react.

The third is muddling glucose with glycogen. They differ by two letters. Glucose is the small sugar dissolved in blood; glycogen is the large storage molecule locked away in liver and muscle.

It is also worth being accurate rather than alarming about diabetes, since it is common and someone reading this may know a person who has it. In type 1 diabetes the pancreas makes little or no insulin, so it must be supplied from outside, usually by injection or a pump. In type 2 diabetes the pancreas still makes insulin but the body responds poorly to it, and it is generally managed with diet, activity and medicines. In both cases blood glucose rises higher than it should after meals, which is why the level is monitored. Both are manageable conditions, and people who have them go to school, work and play sport like anyone else.

Commonly missed

The level of glucagon in a person's blood rises sharply. What happens to the concentration of glucose in their blood, and why?

The trap is answering "it falls", because the question mentions a pancreas hormone and insulin is the pancreas hormone most people can name. The examiner has deliberately chosen the less familiar of the pair.

How to work it: Ask first when the body would ever release glucagon. It releases it when blood glucose has dropped, between meals or during exercise. The point of releasing it must therefore be to correct that drop, so the effect has to be a rise. Then give the mechanism: glucagon travels in the blood to the liver, the liver breaks its stored glycogen back down into glucose, and that glucose passes into the blood, so the concentration rises. Finish by naming the pattern: this is negative feedback, and insulin does the opposite job.

5

Connected ideas

Hormones only work because blood reaches every tissue, so this rests on circulation. The glucose being controlled arrives from digestion, and it is spent in respiration, which is why the three fit together. The kidney runs its own negative feedback on water, and the same messenger-and-target logic explains how a cell membrane decides what to let in.

In one line

Hormones are slow, blood-borne chemical messengers, and the pancreas holds blood glucose steady by negative feedback using insulin to store glucose as glycogen when it is high and glucagon to release it again when it is low.
