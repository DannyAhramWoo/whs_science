Concept

Power

Power is how much energy is transferred each second, so two machines can use the same energy and still differ hugely in power.

Introduced in Chapter 8: Energy and Its Changes

1

What it means

Two people carry the same heavy box up the same staircase. One strolls up in 60 seconds. The other runs up in 20 seconds. They have done the same job — same box, same height, same energy moved. But one of them is out of breath and the other is not. What differs is not the amount of energy. It is the rate.

Power is the amount of energy transferred per second. Written as a formula: power = energy / time. Energy is measured in joules (J). Time is in seconds. Power comes out in joules per second, and one joule per second is given the name watt (W). So a 60 W lamp turns 60 joules of electrical energy into light and heat every single second it is switched on.

The everyday sense of the word is the right one. Power means how fast energy is being used or delivered. A powerful machine is not one that has more energy stored up; it is one that shifts energy quickly. A kitchen kettle and a small phone charger might eventually pass the same number of joules, but the kettle does it in minutes and the charger takes hours, which is exactly why the kettle is the more powerful device.

2

How it works

Return to the staircase, with numbers. Suppose lifting the box up the stairs transfers 3000 J of energy. The stroller takes 60 s, so power = 3000 / 60 = 50 W. The runner takes 20 s, so power = 3000 / 20 = 150 W. Identical energy, three times the power. That single comparison is the whole idea. Going faster does not create extra energy; it packs the same energy into less time.

Rearrange the formula whenever the question needs it. Energy = power x time. A 2000 W kettle running for 90 s transfers 2000 x 90 = 180 000 J. And time = energy / power, so if a machine must deliver 180 000 J but is rated at only 500 W, it will need 180 000 / 500 = 360 seconds, which is six minutes.

Ratio problems are common, and they are easier than they look. Suppose engine A and engine B have powers in the ratio 2 : 1, meaning A is twice as powerful. Engine A does 800 J of work in 10 s, so its power is 800 / 10 = 80 W. Engine B must then be 40 W. How much work does engine B do in 30 s? Work = power x time = 40 x 30 = 1200 J. Notice engine B, the weaker one, ends up doing more total work simply because it was given three times as long. Being less powerful never means doing less work overall; it means taking longer per joule. An IMSO paper from 2014 used precisely this kind of two-engine ratio setup.

Everyday ratings make the scale concrete. An old filament light bulb is about 60 W. A modern LED giving the same brightness is about 8 W, which is why it costs less to run. A kettle is roughly 2000 to 3000 W. A microwave is around 800 W. A person walking upstairs manages perhaps 200 W. A family car engine at full effort produces around 75 000 W.

A person can be measured too. A child of mass 40 kg weighs about 400 N. Climbing 3 m of stairs transfers energy = force x distance = 400 x 3 = 1200 J. Do it in 6 s and power = 1200 / 6 = 200 W. Do it in 4 s and power = 1200 / 4 = 300 W. The same climb, a different rate. This is also how food energy connects: food labels often quote calories, and one food calorie is about 4200 J, so a 100 calorie snack holds roughly 420 000 J. Dividing that by the 1200 J of one flight of stairs shows why burning off a snack takes several hundred flights.

The heart of it

Power is energy divided by time, so it measures how fast energy is transferred, not how much there is.

3

Where the idea came from

Power became worth measuring when machines started competing with animals. In the 1770s and 1780s the Scottish engineer James Watt, working in Birmingham with his business partner Matthew Boulton, was selling improved steam engines. Watt had not invented the steam engine — Thomas Newcomen's engines had been pumping water out of mines since around 1712 — but Newcomen's design wasted enormous amounts of heat by cooling and reheating the same cylinder over and over. Watt's key improvement, from around 1765, was a separate condenser, so the main cylinder could stay hot. His engines used far less coal for the same job.

The selling problem was that customers had no unit to compare against. Their existing machinery was turned by horses walking in circles. So Watt measured what a horse could actually do — hauling on a rope over a pulley for a sustained period — and defined a unit he called horsepower, in the 1780s. He set it at a rate equivalent to about 746 watts in modern units. Whether his figure flattered or underrated real horses is still argued; some historians think he deliberately pitched it high so no customer could complain the engine underperformed. It was a commercial measure before it was a scientific one.

The scientific unit came much later. In 1889 the British Association for the Advancement of Science proposed naming the unit of power the watt in his honour, and it was formally adopted internationally in 1960. Watt himself never used the unit that carries his name.

4

What people get wrong

The classic confusion is treating power and energy as the same quantity. They are not, and questions are built on the gap. Energy is a total, measured in joules. Power is a rate, measured in watts. Saying a device "has a lot of power" tells you how quickly it draws energy, and nothing at all about how much it will use — that depends on how long it runs. A 2000 W kettle on for one minute uses less energy than a 20 W lamp left on all night.

The second mistake is assuming the more powerful machine always does more work. It does more work in the same time. Given different times, the weaker machine can easily do more in total, as the two-engine calculation above showed. Always check what time interval each machine is given.

A third slip is forgetting that time must be in seconds. A power quoted in watts is joules per second, so minutes and hours must be converted first, or the answer comes out wrong by a factor of 60 or 3600.

Commonly missed

Crane X lifts a load in 10 s. Crane Y lifts the identical load through the identical height in 40 s. Which crane transfers more energy to the load, and which has greater power?

The trap is answering "crane X" to both. Speed feels like it should mean more of everything.

How to work it: the energy given to a load lifted through a height depends only on the weight and the height. Same load, same height, so both cranes transfer exactly the same energy. Put in numbers: if the job needs 8000 J, then crane X has power 8000 / 10 = 800 W and crane Y has power 8000 / 40 = 200 W. Crane X is four times as powerful. Energy transferred: equal. Power: X wins. Whenever a question repeats "the same load through the same height", it is telling you the energy is fixed and only the time is changing.

5

Connected ideas

Power is built directly on energy and work, since the joules in the top of the fraction are the work done or the energy transferred. Knowing that energy is never created or destroyed is what lets you say two machines doing the same job must move the same joules. Kinetic and potential energy tell you where those joules go — a climb stores gravitational potential energy, and a speeding-up stores kinetic energy. Energy transformation chains show the path, and Newton's Second Law supplies the forces that do the work in the first place.
