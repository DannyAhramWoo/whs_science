Concept

The Ozone Layer

The ozone layer is a region of the upper atmosphere containing enough ozone gas to absorb most of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation before it reaches the ground.

Introduced in Chapter 13: Cycles and the Environment

1

What it means

Ordinary oxygen, the gas we breathe, is made of molecules containing two oxygen atoms joined together, written O2. Ozone is oxygen in a different form, with three atoms joined, written O3. Same element, different molecule, and very different behaviour — ozone is pale blue, has a sharp smell, and is chemically far more reactive.

The ozone layer sits in the stratosphere, roughly 15 to 35 km above the ground. Calling it a "layer" gives a misleading picture of something like a sheet of glass. Ozone is thinly scattered there — at most about 10 molecules of ozone per million molecules of air. Gathered together at ground-level pressure, the entire ozone layer would form a shell only about 3 mm thick. It is not a barrier. It is a haze that happens to be very good at absorbing one particular thing.

That thing is ultraviolet radiation, or UV: light with wavelengths shorter than violet, invisible to us, and energetic enough to break chemical bonds. This is why it damages living tissue — it can break the bonds in DNA, causing sunburn, eye damage and skin cancer, and it harms crops and plankton too.

2

How it works

Ozone is made and destroyed continuously, and the two processes are what does the shielding. High in the stratosphere, very energetic UV strikes an oxygen molecule and splits it into two separate oxygen atoms. Those loose atoms are highly reactive and quickly attach themselves to other oxygen molecules, making ozone. Meanwhile, slightly less energetic UV strikes an ozone molecule and breaks it apart again, into an oxygen molecule and a free atom. That cycle runs endlessly, and it maintains a steady amount of ozone.

The essential point is that both steps consume UV energy. Breaking a bond takes energy, so every split absorbs a packet of ultraviolet and converts it into heat. The ozone is not used up on balance; it acts rather like a catalyst, continually rebuilt while destroying UV. This is also why the stratosphere gets warmer with height instead of colder — the heat released by all this UV absorption warms the air there.

The damage came from a family of chemicals called chlorofluorocarbons, or CFCs, invented in the 1920s and used in fridges, aerosol sprays and foam. They were chosen precisely because they are unreactive, non-toxic and non-flammable, which made them seem ideal and completely safe. That very stability turned out to be the problem: because nothing at ground level breaks them down, they drift intact into the stratosphere over years. There, intense UV finally splits them, releasing chlorine atoms. A chlorine atom destroys an ozone molecule, and is then released again to destroy another, and another. A single chlorine atom can break down tens of thousands of ozone molecules before it is eventually removed. This is why very small quantities did so much damage.

The destruction is worst over Antarctica because of the extreme cold there. In the polar winter, temperatures below minus 78 degrees Celsius allow rare clouds of ice crystals to form in the stratosphere, and reactions on the surfaces of those crystals convert chlorine compounds into forms that are broken apart the instant sunlight returns in spring. That is why the hole appears in September and October rather than in midsummer.

The heart of it

Ozone protects us not by sitting still like a shield but by being repeatedly broken apart and rebuilt, absorbing ultraviolet energy each time.

3

Where the idea came from

Ozone in the upper atmosphere was identified in the early 1900s, and Gordon Dobson built instruments in the 1920s to measure it from the ground — the unit used for ozone amounts is still named after him. In 1974 Mario Molina and Frank Sherwood Rowland published a paper predicting, from chemistry alone, that CFCs would reach the stratosphere and destroy ozone. Their warning was disputed by industry for years. They shared the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1995, along with Paul Crutzen.

The hole itself was found in 1985 by a small British Antarctic Survey team — Joe Farman, Brian Gardiner and Jonathan Shanklin — using a Dobson instrument at Halley Bay that had been taking readings since 1957. Their equipment was old and their budget modest. What they saw was a decline in springtime ozone so steep that Farman initially suspected the instrument was faulty, and had a second instrument brought in to check. It was not faulty.

The most instructive part of the story is what had happened with the satellite data. NASA's Nimbus-7 satellite had been measuring atmospheric ozone since 1978, and had in fact recorded the low values. But the data-processing software had been written with a filter to discard readings that fell outside a plausible range, on the reasonable assumption that anything so extreme must be instrument error. The real measurements were being thrown away as nonsense. When the team went back and reprocessed the raw data after Farman's paper, the hole was there in the record. It is a lasting caution about building assumptions into your analysis: a filter that removes impossible results will also remove genuine discoveries.

What followed is unusual. Nations negotiated the Montreal Protocol in 1987, agreeing to phase out CFCs, and it was later strengthened and eventually ratified by every country in the world. Ozone-destroying chemicals in the atmosphere peaked in the late 1990s and have been falling since. The layer is slowly recovering, and current assessments expect it to return to 1980 levels around the middle of this century — the delay being simply how long CFCs already up there take to disappear. It stands as a rare example of a global environmental problem being identified and largely acted upon.

4

What people get wrong

The most common confusion is between the ozone hole and global warming. They are separate problems. The ozone hole is about CFCs letting ultraviolet in; global warming is about carbon dioxide stopping infrared getting out. Different chemicals, different radiation, different consequences. The confusion is understandable — both are atmospheric and both were in the news together — but mixing them leads to wrong answers, such as thinking the ozone hole causes the warming.

The second is imagining the "hole" as an actual gap with nothing in it. It is a region of severe thinning, typically losing about two thirds of its ozone at worst, not a puncture.

A third is confusing stratospheric ozone with ground-level ozone. High up it is protective; near the ground it is a pollutant formed in traffic fumes and sunlight, which irritates lungs. Same molecule, and whether it is helpful or harmful depends entirely on where it is.

Commonly missed

CFCs were chosen for use in fridges and sprays partly because they are extremely unreactive. Explain how this property, intended as a safety feature, is the reason they were able to damage the ozone layer.

The trap is that "unreactive" sounds like "harmless", so the question appears to contain a contradiction. It does not — it is asking you to follow the consequence of being unreactive over a long journey.

How to work it: Think about what normally happens to a gas released at ground level. Most substances react with something — water, oxygen, sunlight, living things — and are broken down or washed out within days or weeks, so they never travel far. CFCs do none of this, because they are so stable. Nothing in the lower atmosphere destroys them, so instead of being removed they simply drift, and over several years they are carried up into the stratosphere entirely intact. There they meet ultraviolet radiation far more intense than any that reaches the ground, and this finally splits them, releasing chlorine atoms among the ozone. So the stability did not prevent harm — it delivered the harmful part to precisely the place where it could do damage.

5

Connected ideas

The ozone layer is a part of the atmosphere that absorbs a particular band of radiation, and it is frequently and wrongly confused with the greenhouse effect. The story of the discarded satellite readings is a lesson about the difference between observation and inference, and about how accuracy in measurement can mislead when assumptions are built into the processing.
