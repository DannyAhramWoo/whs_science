Concept

Microorganisms

Microorganisms are living things too small to see without a microscope, and they belong to several completely different groups that happen to share a size.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

A microorganism, or microbe, is a living thing too small to see with the naked eye. A spoonful of soil holds more microbes than there are people on Earth.

The word describes a size, not a family. A bacterium and a mould are no more closely related than you are to an oak tree; they are grouped together only because both need a microscope. So the useful question is never "is it a microorganism" but "which sort": bacteria, fungi, viruses or protists.

One pair of words helps. A prokaryote is an organism whose cell has no nucleus, its genetic material lying loose in the cell. A eukaryote has a proper nucleus holding that material. Bacteria are prokaryotes; fungi, protists, plants and animals are eukaryotes.

2

How it works

Bacteria are single cells, and they are prokaryotes. A bacterium has no nucleus and no mitochondria — mitochondria being the structures inside plant and animal cells where most respiration happens. A bacterium still respires, using its cell membrane and the fluid inside the cell instead. It has a cell wall for support, reproduces by splitting in two, and lives everywhere: in soil, water, air, on your skin and in your gut.

Fungi are a different thing altogether. Yeasts are single-celled fungi; moulds grow as fine threads. Fungi are eukaryotes, so unlike bacteria they do have a nucleus and mitochondria. Their cell wall is chitin, the tough material of an insect's skeleton. They feed by releasing digesting chemicals onto food outside themselves and absorbing the result, which is why a mould spreads across bread as a stain. Many are decomposers.

Viruses are not cells at all. A virus is a package of genetic material in a protein coat, far smaller than a bacterium. It cannot feed or reproduce by itself; it must enter a living cell and use that cell's machinery to copy itself, usually destroying it. Whether a virus is alive is genuinely argued over, with no settled answer: it carries genetic material and evolves, but alone it does nothing.

Protists, by contrast, are ordinary eukaryotes, usually single-celled — amoeba, which flows around its food, and paramecium, covered in tiny beating hairs. "Protist" is a leftover label rather than a real family: it holds everything eukaryotic that is not a plant, animal or fungus.

Most microorganisms are useful. Lactobacillus is a bacterium that turns milk into yoghurt and curd by converting milk sugar into lactic acid, which sours the milk and sets its proteins. Yeast, a fungus, feeds on sugar in dough and gives off carbon dioxide, and bubbles of that gas make bread rise. Decomposers break down dead matter, releasing the carbon and nitrogen inside for plants to use again. A minority cause harm: some bacteria cause cholera and tuberculosis, and viruses cause influenza and measles.

Nitrogen-fixing bacteria matter most of all. The air is nearly four-fifths nitrogen, but plants cannot use the gas directly, and these bacteria convert it into compounds plants can absorb. Where each kind lives is the point to hold. Rhizobium lives inside root nodules on pea and bean roots: the plant feeds the bacteria, the bacteria feed the plant. Azotobacter and Clostridium are free-living, fixing nitrogen alone in soil. Anabaena is a cyanobacterium — a bacterium that photosynthesises — fixing nitrogen in thick-walled cells called heterocysts, and it lives mostly in fresh water rather than soil.

The heart of it

Bacteria, fungi, viruses and protists are grouped as microorganisms only because they are small; bacteria are prokaryotes with no nucleus and no mitochondria, while fungi and protists have both.

3

Where the idea came from

Nobody suspected microorganisms existed until someone could see them. In the 1670s Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, a cloth merchant in Delft who ground his own single-lens microscopes, examined pond water and scrapings from his teeth and reported vast numbers of moving creatures he called "animalcules". His lenses were better than anyone else's and he kept his method secret, so others struggled to confirm what he saw.

For two centuries most people believed small living things simply appeared in decaying matter — spontaneous generation. It was a reasonable belief, since meat left out really did fill with maggots. Louis Pasteur argued against it with the swan-neck flask experiments of about 1859 to 1861: broth was boiled in a flask whose neck bent into a long curve, so air still reached it but dust and microbes settled in the bend. The broth stayed clear; tip it so the liquid touched the neck and it clouded within days. In the 1880s Robert Koch found reliable ways to grow one microbe alone and show it caused a disease.

Martinus Beijerinck isolated Rhizobium from root nodules in 1888 and Azotobacter in 1901, which is how the difference between root-nodule and free-living fixers became known. In 1898 he found the cause of a tobacco plant disease passed through filters fine enough to trap any bacterium. That led to the concept of a virus, though what a virus was would not be understood for decades.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest error is treating "microorganism" as one group whose members share features, and mitochondria are where this shows. Because they are in plant, animal and fungal cells, it is tempting to say all cells have them. Bacteria do not, and neither do cyanobacteria, since they are bacteria as well. A cyanobacterium photosynthesises, which makes it feel plant-like, but it has no nucleus and no mitochondria.

The second error is answering "fungi" whenever a microbe changes food. Yeast is a fungus and makes bread rise, so people generalise. But milk into yoghurt or curd is bacteria — Lactobacillus producing lactic acid. A rough guide: soured and set means bacteria; risen or fermented to alcohol means yeast.

The third is mixing up the nitrogen fixers. Anabaena is the usual distractor, since it does fix nitrogen. The difference is what it is and where it lives: Anabaena is a photosynthesising cyanobacterium in water, while Azotobacter and Clostridium are ordinary soil bacteria. So when a question asks for a free-living nitrogen-fixing bacterium in soil, the answer wanted is Azotobacter. The one needing a partner is Rhizobium, in root nodules.

Commonly missed

Which of these lack mitochondria: yeast, amoeba, cyanobacteria, bacteria?

The trap is reaching for "all of them" because the whole list looks microscopic and primitive. Size has nothing to do with it.

How to work it: Ask of each whether it is a prokaryote or a eukaryote, since only prokaryotes lack mitochondria. Yeast is a fungus and amoeba a protist, so both are eukaryotes and both have them. Bacteria are prokaryotes: no nucleus, no mitochondria. Cyanobacteria are bacteria, whatever the photosynthesis suggests. So the answer is cyanobacteria and bacteria. One rule settles every question of this kind: mitochondria go with a nucleus, so no nucleus means no mitochondria.

5

Connected ideas

Microorganisms strain any system of classification, cutting across the familiar groups. As decomposers they keep food chains and the carbon cycle turning, and as nitrogen fixers they change what plants can grow.
