Concept

Pressure

Pressure is the force pressing on each unit of area, so the same force spread wider always produces less pressure.

Introduced in Chapter 5: Floating, Sinking and Density

1

What it means

Press the flat of your thumb against a table. Now press the point of a drawing pin with the same effort. The thumb barely registers; the pin hurts. The force was the same. What changed was the area it was concentrated into.

Pressure is force divided by the area it is spread over: pressure = force / area. Force is measured in newtons, area in square metres, and pressure in newtons per square metre, called the pascal. One pascal is one newton over one square metre, a very gentle press, so real pressures run into thousands.

Pressure is not a new force. It describes how concentrated an existing force is.

2

How it works

Work the numbers. A woman of mass 60 kg weighs about 600 N. In flat shoes the soles cover about 0.03 m2, so pressure is 600 divided by 0.03, which is 20 000 pascals. In high heels her weight rests on tips covering perhaps 0.0002 m2, so pressure is 600 divided by 0.0002, which is 3 000 000 pascals. Same weight, a hundred and fifty times more pressure. That is why heels dent a wooden floor and flat shoes do not.

Designers use the rule both ways. To get high pressure, shrink the area: a knife edge has almost no area, so a modest push is enough to part the material. To get low pressure, enlarge the area: a snowshoe spreads a walker's weight over a wide frame, so the pressure falls below what the snow can bear and the walker stays on top.

The formula rearranges to find a force: force = pressure times area. A suction cup of area 0.005 m2 has air pressing on it at about 100 000 pascals. The force holding it on is 100 000 times 0.005, which is 500 N.

Liquids differ from solids. A solid block presses only downwards. A liquid presses in all directions — sideways against its container and upwards on anything submerged, as well as down. That is why a hole in the side of a bucket squirts water sideways.

Pressure in a liquid also increases with depth, because more liquid is stacked above and its weight must be supported. Punch three holes down a tall bottle and the lowest jet shoots furthest. In a building, a ground-floor tap runs harder than a top-floor one.

Air does this too. The atmosphere is many kilometres deep and has weight, so it presses on everything beneath it. Atmospheric pressure at sea level is roughly 100 000 pascals, about 10 N on every square centimetre of you. You are not crushed because the pressure acts from every direction at once and the fluids inside you push back equally. Climb a mountain and it drops.

The heart of it

Pressure is force divided by area, so to press harder shrink the area and to press more gently spread it out.

3

Where the idea came from

For centuries suction was explained by saying "nature abhors a vacuum" — that empty space pulled things into itself. Then Italian pump-makers hit a wall: a pump refused to lift water more than about ten metres. A dislike of vacuum gave no reason for a limit.

Evangelista Torricelli, in Florence, solved it in 1643. He filled a long glass tube with mercury, sealed the end and upended it into a dish. The mercury fell only part way, leaving empty space at the top and a column about 76 cm high. Nothing pulled from above: the weight of the atmosphere pushed down on the mercury in the dish and held the column up. Mercury is far denser than water, so its column was far shorter, and the ten-metre water limit was the same effect. That apparatus is the barometer.

Blaise Pascal in France tested this in the 1640s. If air overhead held the column up, carrying the tube up a mountain should shorten it. He had it done on the Puy de Dôme in 1648, and the mercury fell as predicted. Through the 1640s and 1650s he also established that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted through all of it, the principle behind hydraulic jacks and car brakes. The unit is named after him.

4

What people get wrong

The first mistake is treating force and pressure as the same thing. Two objects can press with identical force and produce very different pressures. Whenever a question mentions area, or shows objects with different-sized feet or bases, it is asking about pressure.

The second is getting the division upside down. Area is on the bottom, so a bigger area gives a smaller pressure. Check it against the drawing pin: tiny area, huge pressure.

Past competition papers use this repeatedly. One IMSO paper shows birds with differently shaped feet and asks which exerts the least pressure on soft mud; another asks about water pressure changing with height in a building's pipes.

Commonly missed

Bird P has a mass of 2 kg and feet covering 0.004 m2 in total. Bird Q has a mass of 1 kg and feet covering 0.0005 m2. Which bird is more likely to sink into soft mud?

The trap is picking the heavier bird. But sinking depends on pressure, not force alone, and the question has quietly given very different foot areas.

How to work it: find the weight of each, then divide by area. Bird P weighs about 20 N, so its pressure is 20 divided by 0.004, which is 5000 pascals. Bird Q weighs about 10 N, so its pressure is 10 divided by 0.0005, which is 20 000 pascals. Bird Q presses four times as hard despite weighing half as much, because its feet are eight times smaller. Bird Q sinks. Broad feet are how wading birds stay on top of soft ground.

5

Connected ideas

Pressure is what a force becomes once you account for area, so it starts from force and weight. The rise of pressure with depth causes upthrust, since a submerged object is pushed up harder at the bottom than down at the top, which is why density decides whether something floats. A wide base also lowers the pressure on the ground.
