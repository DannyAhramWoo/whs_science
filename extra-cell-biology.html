Inside a Living Cell

Every living thing you have ever seen is built out of tiny boxes, most of them far too small for your eyes. Inside each box, a set of parts each does one job — and together they keep the whole thing alive.

Going further

This chapter goes further than the numbered ones. It reaches into what is normally taught a few years later, and everything in it is explained here from the beginning, so nothing is assumed.

It is here because everything alive is built from cells, so this is the floor that the rest of biology stands on. It is also here because a cell is a beautifully clear example of a general idea: seeing how each part does one job explains how a whole body works.

1

Everything is built from cells

A cell is the smallest thing that counts as alive. Cut a living thing into smaller and smaller pieces and the cell is where you have to stop. Below that you have parts, and parts are not alive on their own.

Almost all cells are far too small to see. A typical one is a few hundredths of a millimetre across, so you need a microscope. Your own body is made of something like tens of trillions of them — a number so large it is not really picturable. At the other end, plenty of living things are a single cell and nothing more. A bacterium is one cell. So is an amoeba. They feed, grow and reproduce with nothing else to help them.

How anyone found out

In 1665, an English scientist named Robert Hooke put a thin slice of cork under a microscope he had built himself. Cork is bark, and what he saw looked like rows of small empty boxes with walls between them. They reminded him of the little rooms that monks slept in, which were called cells, so that is the word he wrote down in his book Micrographia. The name stuck.

Be careful about what he actually saw, though. Cork is dead. Hooke was looking at the empty cell walls left behind by cells that had died long before — the boxes, not the living contents. In the 1670s a Dutch cloth merchant, Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, ground far better lenses and turned them on pond water, scrapings from his own teeth and drops of blood. He saw things moving. Those were living cells, and he was the first person to see them.

It took another century and a half for anyone to draw the obvious general conclusion. Around 1838 the German botanist Matthias Schleiden argued that all plants are made of cells, and in 1839 Theodor Schwann said the same of animals. Together that is the beginning of cell theory. In 1855 Rudolf Virchow added the missing third part: every cell comes from a cell that already existed. Cells are not built from scratch out of nothing — they are made by other cells dividing.

An honest footnote about credit

Virchow usually gets the credit for that third idea, but it is disputed. Robert Remak, a scientist working in Berlin at the same time, had published the same conclusion from his own careful work a few years earlier. Remak felt Virchow had read his work and then failed to credit him. Historians still argue about it. It is worth knowing that famous names in science are sometimes the ones who said a thing loudest, not the ones who said it first.

2

The parts of a cell and what each does

The parts inside a cell are called organelles — literally "little organs". The name is a good one, because they work the way organs do in a body. Each has one main job.

Nucleus. The control centre. It holds the cell's instructions and decides what the cell does and when. Nearly every cell has one.

Cell membrane. A very thin skin around the whole cell. It is not a sealed bag. It controls what gets in and what gets out, letting some substances through and holding others back.

Cytoplasm. The clear jelly that fills the cell. Everything else sits in it, and it is where most of the cell's chemical reactions actually happen.

Mitochondria. The place where respiration happens — the reaction that releases energy from food. A cell that needs a lot of energy, such as a muscle cell, has a lot of mitochondria. One of them on its own is a mitochondrion.

Ribosomes. Tiny specks scattered through the cytoplasm. They build proteins, which the cell needs both as building material and as tools.

There are two more parts you will meet by name. The endoplasmic reticulum and the Golgi body are, between them, the packing-and-delivery department: proteins are finished off, wrapped and sent where they are needed. That is enough about them for now.

The idea underneath the list

Do not learn this as five words with five definitions. Learn it as a workshop. There is an office holding the plans, a door with someone checking it, a floor where the work happens, a generator, a bench where things get built, and a despatch room. Division of labour is the point, and you will meet the same idea again at every larger scale.

3

Plant cells have three extras

A plant cell has everything an animal cell has — nucleus, membrane, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes — and then three more things on top.

A cell wall. Outside the membrane there is a stiff wall made of cellulose, a tough material built from sugar. It gives the cell a fixed boxy shape and holds it up. Read that first word carefully: the wall is in addition to the membrane, not instead of it. A plant cell has both. The wall is the rigid outer box; the membrane is still the thin skin just inside it, still doing the job of deciding what goes in and out. The wall itself is not fussy — water and dissolved substances pass straight through it.

Chloroplasts. Small green discs containing chlorophyll, the green substance that captures light. Photosynthesis happens inside them. This is why leaves are green and why roots, which never see light, are not.

A large permanent vacuole. A big bag in the middle of the cell filled with cell sap, which is mostly water with sugars and salts dissolved in it. It takes up most of the room, which is why the nucleus in a plant cell is usually squashed off to one side.

Why a thirsty plant droops

The vacuole does something you can watch happening. When it is full it swells and pushes outwards, pressing the rest of the cell hard against the cell wall. The wall pushes back and cannot stretch, so the whole cell becomes stiff, like a bicycle tyre pumped up hard. Millions of stiff cells side by side make a stem stand up straight.

Now let the plant go short of water. The vacuoles shrink. The pushing-out stops. Each cell goes floppy inside its wall, like a tyre losing air — and with nothing pressing outwards, the stem and leaves sag. That is wilting. Water the plant and, within an hour or two, the vacuoles refill, the pushing starts again and the plant stands back up. The recovery is the proof that this is what was going on.

4

Cells with a job

Not all cells look the same. In anything bigger than a speck, cells take on particular jobs, and a cell with a job usually has a shape built for it. These are called specialised cells, and the useful habit is always to ask the same question: what does this shape make easier?

Red blood cell. Shaped like a tiny disc with a dent in each side, rather like a doughnut that never quite got its hole. In mammals, including you, it also throws out its nucleus as it matures, leaving the inside almost entirely full of the substance that carries oxygen. Both features serve the same purpose: maximum room for oxygen and a large surface for taking it up quickly. Being flexible, it can also squeeze through the narrowest blood vessels. Worth saying plainly: losing the nucleus is a mammal feature. Birds and reptiles keep the nucleus in their red blood cells, and they manage perfectly well.

Root hair cell. A cell in a root that pulls out into one long thin hair, sticking out into the soil. A thin spike has an enormous surface for its size, and absorbing water is a job where surface is everything. More surface touching the soil means more water taken in.

Palisade cell. Tall, boxy cells packed tightly side by side just under the top surface of a leaf, each one crammed with chloroplasts. Being tall means more chloroplasts stacked in the path of the light. Being near the top means the light reaches them first, before anything else in the leaf has absorbed it.

Nerve cell. Very long and thin, sometimes astonishingly so — a single nerve cell can run most of the length of a leg. Its job is to carry a signal from one place to another, and a long cell carries a message a long way without having to hand it over. Branches at each end let it connect to many other cells.

Guard cells. These work in pairs. Two curved sausage-shaped cells sit facing each other in the surface of a leaf with a small gap between them. Change how much water is in them and they change shape, so the gap opens or closes. Those gaps are the tiny pores that let gases in and out of the leaf, so the guard cells are a door that the plant can shut when it needs to save water.

5

Water moving in and out

The membrane lets water through freely, but it is much fussier about the things dissolved in the water — salt and sugar, for instance, mostly cannot cross. That single fact produces a surprising amount of behaviour.

Here is the whole rule in one line. Water moves through the membrane from the side where there is more water to the side where there is less. That is osmosis. Nothing pushes it and nobody decides it. Water molecules jiggle about at random and bump across in both directions, but there are more of them on the watery side, so more cross that way than come back. The net effect is a steady drift towards the less watery side, and it carries on until both sides match.

Note what "less watery" means. A strong salt solution is less watery, because a lot of the room is taken up by salt. Pure water is as watery as it gets. So water always moves towards the saltier side.

Which way round — get this the right way up

Now put a cell in a liquid instead of a beaker. There are two cases and they are easy to memorise backwards, so read them slowly.

Cell in more watery surroundings, such as pure water. The scientific word is hypotonic, which simply means the surroundings are more dilute than the inside of the cell. Water moves in. The cell swells. If it keeps taking in water, it bursts.

Cell in saltier surroundings, such as strong salt water. The scientific word is hypertonic, which means the surroundings are more concentrated than the inside of the cell. Water moves out. The cell loses water and shrinks. It does not burst — there is nothing to burst it with. A cell losing water cannot swell.

Say it once more, because this is the one people get inverted: bursting happens in the watery liquid, shrinking happens in the salty one. If you ever find yourself about to write that a cell burst in salt water, stop and ask where the extra water would have come from.

Why a wall changes the ending

An animal cell has only its thin membrane. Put a red blood cell in pure water and it swells until the membrane cannot take any more, and it bursts. There is nothing to stop it.

A plant cell in pure water also takes in water and swells — but the cellulose wall will not stretch. The cell pushes hard against the wall, the wall pushes back, and the cell simply goes firm and stops. It does not burst. This is exactly the firmness described in section 3, and it is why a plant is happiest with plenty of water while an animal cell would be destroyed by the same conditions.

Two things this explains

Salt kills slugs. A slug's skin is wet and soft and its cells sit right at the surface. Pour salt on it and the outside becomes far saltier than the inside of those cells. Water rushes out of them, they shrink, and the slug dries out and dies. It is not poison. It is osmosis, and it is a fairly cruel way to do it.

You cannot drink seawater. Seawater is saltier than your body fluids. Drink it and, instead of watering your cells, it pulls water out of them. Your kidneys then have to make urine to get rid of the extra salt, and making that urine costs more water than the seawater gave you. Every mouthful leaves you thirstier than before. This is why the sea is the worst place in the world to be short of a drink.

6

From one cell to a whole body

One cell is not a body. Getting from one to the other goes up a ladder with five rungs, and each rung is just "a group of the rung below, working together".

Cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism.

A tissue is a group of similar cells doing the same job together. An organ is several tissues working together on one bigger job. An organ system is a set of organs that between them handle one whole task for the body. An organism is the complete living thing.

Follow one line all the way up. A muscle cell can shorten. Lots of muscle cells together make muscle tissue, which can pull. Muscle tissue plus nerve tissue plus blood vessels, arranged in the right shape, make the heart, which can pump. The heart plus the blood vessels plus the blood make the circulatory system, which delivers oxygen and food everywhere. And the circulatory system, together with the digestive system, the nervous system and all the rest, makes you.

This ladder is genuinely useful

Unlike some things worth learning once and forgetting, this one comes back constantly in ordinary biology. Whenever you meet a new body part, ask which rung it is on. It tells you immediately what kind of thing it is and what it is made of. Something new at each level appears that the level below did not have: a cell cannot pump, but a heart can.

7

Questions to think about

These have no marks attached and no single correct wording. Try answering out loud, in full sentences, before opening anything.

1

A red blood cell and a plant cell are both dropped into a beaker of pure water. Describe what happens to each, and explain why the endings are different.

Show answer

Both take in water, because pure water is more watery than the inside of a cell, so water moves in by osmosis in both cases. So far they behave identically.

The red blood cell has only a membrane. It swells, the membrane is stretched past what it can hold, and the cell bursts.

The plant cell has a cellulose wall outside its membrane. It swells too, but the wall does not stretch. The contents press outwards against the wall, the wall presses back, and the cell stops at firm rather than going any further. It survives.

The whole difference comes from one extra layer, which is a good illustration of how much a small structural feature can matter.

2

Someone writes: "In a salty solution, a cell bursts." What is wrong with this, and what is the correct version?

Show answer

It has the two cases swapped. In a salty solution the surroundings are less watery than the inside of the cell, so water moves out. The cell loses water and shrinks. It cannot burst, because bursting needs the cell to gain water and there is no water coming in.

The correct version: a cell bursts in a more watery solution such as pure water, and shrinks in a saltier one. The technical words are hypotonic for the watery case and hypertonic for the salty case.

A good way to keep it straight is to ask where the extra water would have to come from. If you cannot point at a source, nothing is going to burst.

3

Root hair cells are long and thin. Palisade cells are tall and boxy. Both shapes are described as helpful. Are they helpful for the same reason?

Show answer

Partly, but not entirely, and noticing the difference is the point of the question.

The root hair is thin so that it has a large surface for its size. Absorbing water happens at the surface, so more surface means faster absorption. The shape is about contact with the outside.

The palisade cell is tall so that more chloroplasts can be stacked in the path of the light coming down through the leaf, and it sits near the top so the light reaches it before anything else absorbs it. The shape is about volume and position, not surface.

Both are cases of shape suiting the job. They are not both cases of maximising surface area, which is the easy assumption to make.

4

Your red blood cells have no nucleus, but a pigeon's do. Does that mean one of the two arrangements is better?

Show answer

No, and this is worth being careful about. Mammals lose the nucleus from their red blood cells as those cells mature, which frees up room for carrying oxygen. Birds and reptiles keep it, and their blood carries oxygen perfectly well.

Both work. They are different solutions to the same problem, arrived at along different lines of descent. "Better" would need you to say better at what, and under which conditions.

The lesson worth taking away is smaller and more useful than the biology: when you read a fact about the body, check whether it is a fact about all animals or only about mammals. Textbooks often say "the red blood cell has no nucleus" when they mean "yours does not".

Why this is worth knowing

Nothing in this chapter will be tested, and none of it is needed for the numbered chapters. It is worth reading anyway for a straightforward reason: every living thing you will ever study is made of these. Leaves, blood, bark, bones, bacteria in a pond — all cells, all built from the same short list of parts. Learn the list once and it keeps paying you back.

There is also a way of thinking here that is bigger than cells. Twice in this chapter a structure explained a behaviour: a wall explained why a plant does not burst in water, and a shape explained why a root hair absorbs well. That habit — asking what a thing's structure allows it to do — works on organs, on machines, on almost anything built for a purpose. It is a better question than "what is this called".

And one small piece of care is worth carrying away on its own. Water moves towards the saltier side, so cells burst in watery surroundings and shrink in salty ones. That is exactly the sort of fact that gets remembered upside down, and the way to avoid it is not repetition but understanding why it has to be that way round.
