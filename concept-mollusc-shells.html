Concept

Molluscs and Their Shells

Molluscs are soft-bodied animals without a backbone, most of which build a hard shell around themselves, and the shape and markings of that shell are how the different kinds are told apart.

Introduced in Chapter 18: Sorting Living Things

1

What it means

Pick up a shell on a beach and you are holding the leftover of an animal. It was a mollusc, and the shell was its house, built by its own body.

Molluscs are one of the largest animal groups on Earth, with something like a hundred thousand living species, second only to the arthropods that hold insects and crabs. They are all invertebrates, animals with no backbone. Instead of an internal skeleton, most protect themselves with a shell on the outside.

Three features define the group. First, a soft body — the name comes from a Latin word meaning soft. Second, a muscular foot, a slab of muscle used for crawling, digging or grabbing. Third, a mantle, a fold of skin covering the body, and it is the mantle that secretes the shell. The shell is mainly calcium carbonate, the same chemical as chalk and limestone.

Not every mollusc has a shell, and that trips people up. Slugs have lost theirs or carry a remnant under the skin, and an octopus has none. They are still molluscs, because they still have the soft body, the mantle and the foot in modified form.

2

How it works

Three groups of molluscs come up again and again, and it helps to know each by its shell, its foot and how it feeds.

Gastropods are snails, slugs, whelks, limpets and cowries. The name means "stomach foot", because the animal glides along on a broad muscular foot lying under its body. Those with a shell have a single shell, and it is usually coiled, wound round in a spiral. Gastropods have a distinct head with eyes and feelers, and inside the mouth a radula — a strip of tissue covered in rows of tiny hard teeth, worked back and forth like a file to rasp food off a surface. That is how a garden snail shreds a leaf and how a periwinkle scrapes algae off a rock.

The vocabulary of a coiled shell is worth having, because keys use it. Each complete turn of the spiral is a whorl, and the line where one whorl meets the next is a suture. The opening the animal comes out of is the aperture, and its shape — round, oval or a narrow slit — is a useful character. The pointed end, made of the small whorls built when young, is the spire, its very tip the apex. If the coiling leaves a hole running up the centre of the underside, that is the umbilicus, and its width separates some species from others.

Bivalves are clams, mussels, oysters, scallops and cockles. A valve is one half of the shell. The two halves join along one edge at a hinge and are pulled shut by strong muscles. Bivalves have no head and no radula. They feed by filter feeding: drawing water in, straining out tiny floating plants and animals with their gills, and pushing the water out. The foot is a wedge for burrowing into sand or mud, though mussels glue themselves to rocks with tough threads.

Cephalopods are squid, octopuses, cuttlefish and the nautilus. The name means "head foot", the foot having become a ring of tentacles around the head. Only the nautilus keeps an external coiled shell. A cuttlefish has an internal chalky plate, the cuttlebone; a squid a thin internal rod; an octopus none. They have large sharp eyes, move by squirting a jet of water, and many change colour in a moment. They are the most intelligent invertebrates known: octopuses open jars, use coconut shells as portable shelters and learn their way through mazes.

Now a single bivalve shell, because a shell records its own history. Near the hinge sits a rounded bump, the umbo. This is the oldest part, the tiny shell the animal made as a larva, with everything else added later around its edges. Radiating out from it are the growth lines, curved ridges roughly parallel to the outer rim. Each marks a position the shell's edge once occupied, so the shell grew outwards like a spreading fan, always adding at the open rim, never in the middle. Where growth was fast the lines are widely spaced; where food was short or water cold they crowd together. You read them like tree rings, from the centre outwards, oldest first. Inside, the smooth pearly lining is nacre, or mother-of-pearl, laid in fine flat layers, and a pearl is the same material built around an irritating speck. Two dull patches mark where the closing muscles attached: the adductor muscle scars.

These terms are not decoration. They are the characters a dichotomous key uses — a key splitting a group in two at each step until one species is left. Because whole practical papers, including IMSO experimental papers from around 2019, have been built on sorting real shells down to genus and species, the terms have to be usable, not merely recognised.

The heart of it

A shell is built outwards from its oldest point, so the umbo on a bivalve and the apex of a snail's spire are the parts made first, and the growth lines between there and the rim are a record of the animal's life.

3

Where the idea came from

Shells were collected long before they were understood. Wealthy Europeans in the 1600s and 1700s kept cabinets of them, and rare specimens sold for the price of a house. What was missing was any orderly way to name them.

Carl Linnaeus supplied that in the 1750s with his two-word naming system, and many mollusc names still in use were set by him. But he and his contemporaries had the relationships badly wrong. Molluscs were lumped with other soft creatures under a general heading of worms, and shape was assumed to reveal kinship, so barnacles were counted as molluscs simply because they sit in hard plates on rocks. Barnacles are in fact crustaceans, relatives of crabs. Charles Darwin, who spent eight years from 1846 dissecting barnacles, helped settle where they belonged.

Working out what a shell records took similar unpicking. Naturalists in the 1800s noticed shells add lines in bands, and it took careful observation of living animals to establish that the mantle edge lays down new material at the rim and that a pause in growth leaves a line. Only then did the direction of growth become obvious.

Fossil molluscs mattered too. Ammonites, extinct relatives of the nautilus, are so common and varied in the rocks that geologists in the early 1800s used them to work out which rock layers were the same age across different countries.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is thinking a shell grows the way a jumper is knitted, evenly all over, or that a mollusc outgrows its shell and swaps it for a bigger one. Neither is right. The mollusc adds to the rim, so the shell enlarges without ever being replaced. The animal that does move house is the hermit crab, which is a crustacean living in an abandoned snail shell.

The second is deciding that anything with a shell must be a mollusc. Crabs, lobsters and barnacles have hard outer cases too, but theirs are a jointed exoskeleton of chitin which must be shed and regrown, and they have legs with joints. A mollusc shell is calcium carbonate, unjointed, and grows continuously.

The third is muddling the umbo with the middle of a shell. The umbo sits near the hinge, off to one side, not at the centre of the curve. Find the hinge and the oldest point is beside it.

Commonly missed

A bivalve shell has growth lines that are widely spaced near the umbo and crowded close together near the outer rim. What does this tell you about the animal?

The trap is reading the crowded lines as the start of life, on the reasonable-sounding grounds that a young animal is small so its lines must be close together. That gets the direction of time backwards.

How to work it: Fix the direction first. The umbo is oldest and the rim newest, so time runs from the umbo outwards. Widely spaced lines therefore came first and mean the edge advanced a long way between pauses: fast growth. Crowded lines came last and mean it advanced very little: slow growth. So this animal grew quickly when young and slowly when old. That is the ordinary pattern, and reasons include less food later, colder water, or energy going into eggs rather than shell. Say which way time runs before anything else, and the rest follows.

5

Connected ideas

Molluscs are a test case for classification, since they show how one group can hold animals as different as a limpet and an octopus. Their shell terms are exactly the sort of character a dichotomous key runs on, and the names produced by that key follow the two-word naming system. Comparing a mollusc shell with a crab's jointed case sharpens both.

In one line

Molluscs are soft-bodied invertebrates with a mantle and a muscular foot, split into gastropods with one coiled shell and a radula, bivalves with two hinged halves that filter feed, and cephalopods with tentacles and little or no shell.
