Concept

Trophic Levels and the Energy Pyramid

A trophic level is a feeding step in a food chain, and because roughly nine tenths of the energy is lost at every step, the steps stack into a narrow pyramid that runs out after four or five.

Introduced in Chapter 19: Ecosystems

1

What it means

Count how many meals away from a plant something is and you have its trophic level. Trophic simply means "to do with feeding". Grass is level one, the rabbit eating it level two, the fox eating the rabbit level three.

Each level has a name. The producer is level one: a plant or alga that builds its own food from sunlight. Everything above is a consumer, which must eat other living things. Level two is the primary consumer, level three the secondary consumer, level four the tertiary consumer. An apex predator sits at the top with nothing that regularly hunts it.

Papers use a second set of names, and you need both. A herbivore eats only plants, so it is a primary consumer; a carnivore eats only animals, so it is secondary or higher; an omnivore eats both. Herbivore and carnivore describe what an animal eats; primary and secondary describe where it sits in this chain. Both can be true at once.

Decomposers — bacteria and fungi — feed on dead bodies and waste, returning nutrients to the soil. They are drawn to one side rather than on a level, since they feed on material from every level.

2

How it works

The whole subject turns on one number. When energy passes from one trophic level to the next, only about 10 per cent of it is stored in the bodies of the level above. The other 90 per cent never makes it. This is the ten per cent rule — a rough guide rather than a law, since real transfers range from about 5 to 20 per cent, but close enough to reason with.

Where does the missing energy go? Three places. Not everything eaten is digested, so tough plant material leaves in the faeces. Staying alive costs energy — moving, growing, repairing — and that energy is released by respiration, the chemical process breaking down food inside cells, which always releases some as heat that escapes and cannot be recaptured. And some parts are never eaten: bones, fur, roots.

Now the arithmetic, which answers the classic question. If a meadow's plants capture 10,000 units of energy, the insects store about 1,000, the birds about 100, a hawk about 10. A fifth level would have about 1 unit — nowhere near enough to keep an animal alive. Food chains stop at four or five levels because the energy runs out. "Not enough species" and "the top predators went extinct" are distractors.

Stacking the levels as bars gives a pyramid of energy: each bar about a tenth of the one below, always wide at the bottom and narrow at the top, because energy can only be lost going up.

A pyramid of numbers counts individuals instead, and it can go wrong. One oak feeds thousands of caterpillars, so the producer bar is a single unit and the consumer bar enormous, and the pyramid comes out upside down — inverted. Nothing is broken; counting heads is the wrong measurement when producers are very large. The energy pyramid for that oak looks normal, because one tree holds a great deal of energy.

One more thing papers test: an animal can occupy several levels at once. A fox eating berries is a primary consumer; eating a rabbit that afternoon makes it secondary. Level is a position, not a fixed property.

The heart of it

Only about a tenth of the energy at one trophic level reaches the next, the rest lost as heat and in living, which is why chains are short and pyramids of energy always narrow upwards.

3

Where the idea came from

The British ecologist Charles Elton set out food chains and the pyramid of numbers in Animal Ecology in 1927. He had the pattern right and the measurement wrong, as the inverted oak-tree pyramid shows: counting bodies treats a giant tree and a single caterpillar as one unit each.

The fix came from a young American ecologist, Raymond Lindeman, working on a shallow Minnesota lake. In a paper published in 1942 he proposed measuring the energy flowing through each feeding level rather than counting individuals. Do that and the pyramid stands up every time. His paper is usually treated as the point where ecology became quantitative. Lindeman died in 1942 at twenty-seven, before it appeared in print.

The 10 per cent figure attached itself afterwards, though Lindeman did not present it as a constant and later measurements gave a wide spread. Treat it as a rule of thumb, not a law.

4

What people get wrong

The first error is believing the energy is destroyed. It is transferred into a form the next animal cannot eat, chiefly heat spreading into the surroundings. Say "lost as heat", not "used up and gone".

The second is treating an inverted pyramid of numbers as a drawing mistake. It is a correct count, showing that number is a poor measure when organism sizes differ wildly. The third is assuming each animal has one fixed level: work it out from the chain in front of you.

This family of ideas recurs in past IMSO papers, usually as a "why do food chains stop" question with energy loss as the correct option among tempting alternatives.

Commonly missed

A heron eats fish, frogs and water beetles. The fish eat water beetles, and the beetles eat pondweed. At how many trophic levels does the heron feed, and what happens to frog numbers if all the fish are removed?

The trap is giving the heron a single level, then treating fish removal as a one-step change.

How to work it: Trace each route. Pondweed → beetle → heron makes the heron secondary; pondweed → beetle → fish → heron makes it tertiary. So it feeds at more than one level, which is normal in a web. For the frogs: with no fish the heron has fewer prey choices and eats more frogs, pushing numbers down; but beetles rise, so frogs have more food, pushing numbers up. The honest answer names both effects and says we cannot be sure which wins.

5

Connected ideas

Trophic levels are the labelled version of a food chain, and the energy entering level one arrives through photosynthesis. The losses happen through respiration, which releases heat, and the whole picture obeys the rule that energy is transferred rather than created. The numbers at each level are populations, shaped by predator and prey pressure.

In one line

A trophic level is one feeding step above the producers, and because only about a tenth of the energy survives each step, chains stay short and a pyramid of energy always narrows towards the top, even when a pyramid of numbers turns upside down.
