Concept

Rotation and Revolution

Rotation is the Earth spinning on its own axis, which gives us day and night; revolution is the Earth travelling round the Sun, which gives us the year.

Introduced in Chapter 10: Earth, Moon and Sun

1

What it means

Two different motions are happening at once, and because they share a rather similar name they are easily muddled. It helps to picture a spinning top being carried slowly around a room. The top spins on the spot — that is rotation. It also travels around the room — that is revolution. The Earth is doing both at the same time.

Rotation is turning about a line through yourself. The Earth's axis is an imaginary line running through the North Pole and the South Pole, and the Earth turns once about it every 24 hours. Nothing outside the Earth is needed for this; a lone planet in empty space would still rotate.

Revolution is travelling in a path around something else. The Earth revolves around the Sun once every 365 and a quarter days, along a path called its orbit. Revolution needs something to go around, and it needs a force to keep the Earth curving instead of flying off in a straight line. That force is gravity.

A simple test: if the effect repeats every day, rotation is responsible. If it repeats every year, revolution is responsible.

2

How it works

Rotation causes day and night because the Earth is a ball and the Sun can only light one side of it at a time. The lit half has day, the unlit half has night, and as the Earth turns, each place is carried from the lit side into the dark side and back again. Sunrise is not the Sun coming up; it is your part of the Earth rotating into the light. The Earth turns from west to east, and that is precisely why the Sun appears to move the other way, rising in the east and setting in the west. If you turn on the spot to your left, the whole room appears to sweep to your right.

The speed involved is startling. The Earth's circumference at the equator is about 40,000 km and it turns once in 24 hours, so a person standing at the equator is being carried east at roughly 1,700 km per hour. You feel nothing, because everything around you — the ground, the air, your bicycle — is moving with you at the same steady speed. You only feel motion when it changes.

Revolution causes the year, and together with the tilt of the axis it causes the seasons. The Earth's axis is not upright relative to its orbit; it leans by about 23.5 degrees, and it keeps pointing the same way as the Earth goes round. So for part of the orbit the northern half leans towards the Sun and gets summer, and half a year later it leans away and gets winter. Revolution alone would give no seasons at all. It is revolution plus a fixed tilt.

Revolution also explains why different stars are visible at different times of year. At midnight you look outwards, away from the Sun — and six months later the Earth is on the other side of its orbit, so "away from the Sun" points at a completely different part of space.

The heart of it

Rotation is spinning on the spot and makes the day; revolution is going round the Sun and makes the year.

3

Where the idea came from

For most of history the sensible view was that the Earth stands still and the sky turns. This was not stupidity. It matches what you see, it matches what you feel, and it accounted for the observations well enough to predict eclipses. A few Greek thinkers, notably Aristarchus of Samos around 270 BC, suggested the Earth moved round the Sun, but the idea did not take hold — partly because if the Earth really moved, the nearer stars ought to shift against the further ones, and nobody could detect any such shift. That objection was reasonable. The stars are simply far further away than anyone imagined, and the shift was not measured until 1838.

In 1543 the Polish astronomer Nicolaus Copernicus published a full mathematical system with the Sun at the centre and the Earth both rotating and revolving. He got a good deal right and some things wrong: he kept perfect circles for the orbits, which forced him to keep awkward extra circles to make the predictions fit. Johannes Kepler fixed that in 1609 by showing the orbits are ellipses, slightly squashed circles.

Galileo Galilei's telescope observations from 1610 — the moons of Jupiter circling a body that was not the Earth, and the full set of phases of Venus — made the Sun-centred picture far more convincing. His public advocacy brought him into conflict with the Church, and in 1633 he was tried and placed under house arrest for the rest of his life. The often-repeated story that he muttered "and yet it moves" at his trial appears only long afterwards and is probably embroidered.

It is worth being honest that the Earth's rotation was genuinely hard to prove from the ground. The first simple, direct demonstration came only in 1851, when Léon Foucault hung a long pendulum in Paris and showed that its swing slowly turned during the day — not because the pendulum was pushed, but because the floor beneath it was rotating.

4

What people get wrong

The commonest slip is assigning day and night to revolution. It sounds plausible — the Earth goes round, so surely it faces the Sun for part of the trip. But the orbit takes a year, so if that were the cause, a day would last six months of light followed by six months of dark. Day and night take 24 hours because the Earth spins in 24 hours.

A second slip is the reverse: blaming rotation for the seasons. Rotation is the same in June and December, so it cannot explain a difference between them.

Commonly missed

If the Earth stopped rotating but continued to revolve around the Sun, what would happen to day and night, and to the seasons?

The trap is answering with one effect and forgetting the other, or assuming that stopping one motion stops everything.

How to work it: Take the two motions separately. Day and night come from rotation, so without rotation a place would face the Sun for a long stretch and away from it for a long stretch — one full cycle of light and dark per orbit, so a "day" lasting a year. The seasons come from revolution combined with the tilt, and both of those still remain, so the seasons would carry on much as before. One motion stopping changes only what that motion caused.

5

Connected ideas

Revolution only happens because gravity keeps pulling the Earth towards the Sun, curving what would otherwise be a straight-line path — which is inertia at work. The tilt combined with revolution produces the seasons, and the same pair of motions in the Earth–Moon system gives the phases of the Moon and, occasionally, eclipses.
